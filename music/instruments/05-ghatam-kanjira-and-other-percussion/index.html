5. Ghaṭam, Kañjīra, and Other Auxiliary Percussion

Beyond the mṛdaṅgam (Chapter 4), a Carnatic ensemble frequently includes one or more auxiliary (upa-pakkavādyam) percussion instruments, adding further rhythmic and timbral variety to a performance's rhythmic accompaniment.

Ghaṭam

Definition - Ghaṭam

The ghaṭam is a specially crafted clay pot, played by striking its surface with the hands, fingers, and sometimes the whole body of the player (the pot itself is sometimes lifted and struck against the player's bare abdomen for particular tonal effects) - capable of a surprisingly wide range of pitches and timbres for what is, at its core, an ordinary pottery vessel purpose-built for musical use.

Kañjīra

Definition - Kañjīra

The kañjīra is a small frame drum, similar in basic construction to a tambourine but without the jingles, traditionally headed with monitor lizard skin (though synthetic alternatives are increasingly used) - played with the fingers and palm of one hand while the other hand's wrist and fingers modulate the drumhead's tension to bend pitch, a highly virtuosic instrument in skilled hands.

Morsing

The morsing (a type of jaw harp) is held against the teeth or lips and plucked, with the player's mouth cavity shaping the resulting sound's overtones - a much quieter, subtler instrument than the mṛdaṅgam, ghaṭam, or kañjīra, typically used to add a distinctive, buzzing timbral layer rather than to carry primary rhythmic responsibility.

Auxiliary Percussion in Ensemble

Remark - Layered Percussion Texture

When present, these auxiliary instruments generally follow the mṛdaṅgam's rhythmic lead (Chapter 4) rather than acting as independent primary timekeepers, adding timbral layering and additional improvisational voices - particularly prominent during an extended tani āvartanam (Chapter 6 of Musical Forms & Compositions), where the different percussion instruments often engage in call-and-response passages with one another as well as with the mṛdaṅgam.
