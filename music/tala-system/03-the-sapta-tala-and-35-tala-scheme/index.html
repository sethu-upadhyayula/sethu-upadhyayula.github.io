3. The Sapta Tāla and 35 Tāla Scheme

Just as the melakarta scheme (Chapter 2 of The Rāga System) systematically classifies rāgas, Carnatic music theory has a comparably systematic scheme for classifying tālas - the sapta (seven) tālas, multiplied by five possible jātis into a full 35-tāla system.

The Seven Sūlādi Tālas

The Seven Base Tāla Patterns
  • Dhruva tāla - laghu, druta, laghu, laghu.
  • Mātya tāla - laghu, druta, laghu.
  • Rūpaka tāla - druta, laghu.
  • Jhampā tāla - laghu, anudruta, druta.
  • Triputa tāla - laghu, druta, druta.
  • Aṭa tāla - laghu, laghu, druta, druta.
  • Eka tāla - laghu (alone).

Jāti: The Laghu's Variable Count

Definition - Jāti

Recall from Chapter 2 that a laghu's akṣara count (its jāti) can vary - the five possible jātis are tiśra (3), caturaśra (4), khaṇḍa (5), miśra (7), and saṅkīrṇa (9). Applying each of these five jātis to each of the seven sūlādi tālas above produces $7 \times 5 = 35$ distinct tālas - the complete 35 tāla system, directly paralleling how the melakarta scheme's combinatorics produce exactly 72 rāgas.

Computing a Tāla's Total Akṣara Count

Since anudruta is always 1 akṣara and druta is always 2, a tāla's total length depends only on how many laghus it contains and the chosen jāti - for instance, Ādi tāla (caturaśra-jāti Triputa: one caturaśra laghu of 4 akṣaras, plus two drutas of 2 each) totals $4 + 2 + 2 = 8$ akṣaras per cycle, the most common tāla length in practice (Chapter 5).

Theoretical Completeness versus Practical Use

Remark - Most-Used Tālas

As with the 72 melakarta rāgas, only a small subset of the 35 tālas are in regular practical use - Ādi tāla and Rūpaka tāla (Chapter 5) dominate the standard concert repertoire, with several others appearing occasionally and the remainder largely of theoretical or historical interest rather than active performance practice.
