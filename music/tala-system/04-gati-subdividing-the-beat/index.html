4. Gati (Naḍai): Subdividing the Beat

Beyond how many akṣaras a tāla cycle contains (Chapters 2–3), each individual akṣara can itself be subdivided into a fixed number of smaller pulses - this subdivision, called gati (or naḍai), adds an entirely independent second dimension to Carnatic rhythm.

Gati: Subdivision Within the Beat

Definition - Gati

Gati (also called naḍai) specifies how many equal smaller pulses each akṣara of a tāla is subdivided into - conceptually similar to choosing whether a Western musical beat "feels" like it divides into two, three, or some other number of subdivisions. Confusingly, gati uses the same five names as jāti (Chapter 3) - tiśra (3), caturaśra (4), khaṇḍa (5), miśra (7), saṅkīrṇa (9) - but describes a completely independent structural choice, applied to subdividing the beat rather than to counting a laghu's akṣaras.

Gati Is Independent of Tāla

Because gati and tāla (and jāti) are independent choices, the same tāla (e.g., Ādi tāla, Chapter 5) can be performed in caturaśra gati (the most common, each beat subdividing into four) or, for variety and rhythmic interest, in tiśra, khaṇḍa, or another gati - changing the felt subdivision and character of the underlying beat pattern without altering the tāla's own overall cycle length or structure (Chapter 3) in any way.

Gati in Composition and Improvisation

Example - Gati Changes Within a Performance

Skilled performers, particularly percussionists (Chapter 4 of Instruments) and in advanced rhythmic improvisation (such as extended tani āvartanam solo passages), can shift gati mid-performance - momentarily reorganizing the felt subdivision of the beat while the underlying tāla cycle continues unbroken - a sophisticated rhythmic technique that showcases both individual virtuosity and deep internalization of the tāla-gati distinction covered in this chapter.

Gati and Konnakol

Gati is most directly and precisely practiced and demonstrated through konnakol (Chapter 6), whose spoken syllable patterns map naturally onto a chosen subdivision - reciting a fixed set of syllables per akṣara is a direct, embodied way of internalizing a given gati before applying it to actual musical performance.
