6. A Survey of Common Rāgas

This closing chapter surveys a handful of rāgas that appear especially often in the Carnatic concert repertoire, illustrating the concepts covered earlier in this subject with concrete, recognizable examples.

Śankarābharanam

Example - Śankarābharanam

A melakarta rāga (Chapter 2) using all seven svaras straightforwardly in both directions, equivalent to the Western major scale. Its familiarity to ears trained on Western music makes it a common starting point for describing rāga concepts to newcomers, though its actual performance practice - jīva svara emphasis, characteristic gamaka - is distinctly Carnatic rather than a simple transplant of Western major-key melody.

Kalyāṇi

Kalyāṇi is another melakarta rāga, using a raised fourth (Ma2) in place of Śankarābharanam's natural fourth - a single svara difference that nonetheless produces a distinctly different, often described as brighter or more expansive, character, illustrating how much a rāga's felt identity can hinge on one changed note.

Tōḍi

Tōḍi is a melakarta rāga built almost entirely from the "flattened" variants of its variable svaras, giving it a distinctive, often described as deeply emotive or serious, character - frequently cited as one of the rāgas whose full aesthetic depth takes considerable listening experience to fully appreciate.

Mōhanam

Example - Mōhanam, an Audava-Audava Janya Rāga

Mōhanam is a janya rāga (Chapter 3) derived from Śankarābharanam, using only five notes in both ascent and descent (an audava-audava rāga) - omitting Ma and Ni entirely. Its pentatonic structure gives it a simple, immediately accessible character, and closely related pentatonic scales appear in musical traditions worldwide, making Mōhanam a frequently used example when introducing rāga to listeners from other musical backgrounds.

Bhairavi

Bhairavi is a widely performed janya rāga known for its considerable melodic flexibility and emotional range, traditionally associated with concluding a concert (Chapter 8 of Concert Practice covers the conventions around this) - often described as one of the more accessible yet still deeply expressive rāgas in regular performance.

Hamsadhvani

Hamsadhvani is a compact, five-note (audava-audava) janya rāga with a bright, energetic character, frequently used to open a concert or as the basis for shorter, lively compositions - its combination of simplicity and immediate melodic appeal has also made it one of the Carnatic rāgas most often adapted or referenced in music outside the tradition itself.
