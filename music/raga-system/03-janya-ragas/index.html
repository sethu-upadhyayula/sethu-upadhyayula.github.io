3. Janya Rāgas

Far more rāgas are actually performed in practice than just the 72 melakartas (Chapter 2) - the great majority are janya ("derived" or "born") rāgas, each traceable back to one melakarta parent but differing from it in specific, named ways.

Definition and Relationship to Melakartas

Definition - Janya Rāga

A janya rāga derives its svara set from one specific melakarta parent, but departs from that parent's strict, complete, serial seven-note scale in some defined way - omitting notes, using a different order in ascent versus descent, or otherwise deviating from the "straight scale" pattern that defines a melakarta.

Varjya Rāgas: Omitted Notes

Definition - Varjya Rāga

A varjya rāga omits one or more of the seven svaras. If a rāga uses only five svaras, it is called audava; six svaras, ṣāḍava; and different combinations of five/six/seven notes in ascent versus descent give rise to further named categories (e.g. an audava-ṣāḍava rāga uses five notes ascending and six descending).

Vakra Rāgas: Zigzag Movement

Definition - Vakra Rāga

A vakra ("crooked" or "zigzag") rāga does not move strictly serially through its svaras in ascent or descent - a phrase might briefly dip down or skip ahead before continuing in the expected direction, an intentional, defining structural feature of the rāga rather than a performer's ornamental choice.

Bhāṣāṅga Rāgas: Borrowed Notes

Definition - Bhāṣāṅga Rāga

A bhāṣāṅga rāga incorporates a svara variant from outside its designated parent melakarta's own note set - typically used sparingly, in specific characteristic phrases (Chapter 5) - as opposed to a vakra/audava/ṣāḍava rāga (collectively sometimes called upāṅga rāgas), which stays strictly within its parent's note set even while varying the order or completeness of that set.

Why So Many Janya Rāgas Exist

Because each of the 72 melakartas can, in principle, give rise to numerous distinct janya rāgas through different combinations of omission, zigzag movement, and borrowed notes, the total number of janya rāgas in active or historical use vastly exceeds 72 - the melakarta scheme (Chapter 2) functions as an organizing reference framework for this much larger, practically performed repertoire, not as an exhaustive list of every rāga a musician will actually encounter.
