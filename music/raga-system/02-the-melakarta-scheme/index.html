2. The Melakarta Scheme

Carnatic music organizes its full universe of possible seven-note "parent" scales into a single, exhaustive, systematically numbered scheme - the melakarta system - covered in this chapter.

Melakarta Rāgas

Definition - Melakarta Rāga

A melakarta rāga (also called a janaka, or "parent," rāga) is a rāga using all seven svara positions in both ascent and descent, in strictly serial (non-zigzagging) order - a complete, "straight" seven-note scale. There are exactly 72 such scales possible given the combinatorics of the svara variants covered in Chapter 2 of Fundamentals, and Carnatic theory names and numbers every single one, whether or not it is ever actually used in practice.

Why Exactly 72

Since Ri and Ga together, and Da and Ni together, combine (given the fixed positions of Sa and Pa, and the constraint that Ri must lie below Ga and Da below Ni) into 6 possible combinations each, and Ma has 2 possible positions (Chapter 2 of Fundamentals), the total number of valid combinations works out to $6 \times 6 \times 2 = 72$ - the combinatorial origin of the scheme's exact size.

The Katapayādi Numbering System

Definition - Katapayādi Sankhya

Each of the 72 melakarta rāgas has a name constructed so that its first two syllables, decoded via the katapayādi numeral-to-consonant convention, directly encode its own melakarta number - meaning a practitioner who knows this convention can determine a melakarta rāga's exact svara composition purely from its name, without needing to memorize a separate lookup table.

The Two Chakras

The 72 melakartas are further organized into 12 groups of 6 ("chakras"), based on the Ma variant and Ri/Ga combination used - the first 36 melakartas use Ma1 (śuddha madhyama) and the second 36 use Ma2 (prati madhyama), with otherwise identical Ri/Ga/Da/Ni patterns repeating across the two halves - a structural symmetry that makes the scheme considerably easier to learn and internalize than 72 entirely unrelated scales would be.

Example - Well-Known Melakartas

Some melakarta rāgas are extremely commonly performed in their own right - Śankarābharanam (equivalent to the Western major scale) and Kalyāṇi (major scale with a raised fourth) are among the most familiar - while many of the other 72 are primarily of theoretical interest, rarely or never performed as standalone rāgas.
