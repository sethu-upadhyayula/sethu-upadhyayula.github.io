1. What Is a Rāga?

A rāga is often loosely described as a "scale" or "mode," but this undersells it considerably - a rāga is closer to a complete melodic framework with its own permitted movements, emphasis, and characteristic identity, within which endless individual melodies can be composed or improvised.

Rāga as More Than a Scale

Definition - Rāga

A rāga is a melodic framework defined by a specific selection of svaras (Chapter 2 of Fundamentals) from the twelve available, specific rules governing how they may ascend and descend, and a body of characteristic phrases (Chapter 5) that together give it a distinct, recognizable identity beyond just "which notes are allowed." Two rāgas can share the exact same set of permitted svaras and yet sound completely different, because their characteristic phrasing and emphasis differ.

Arohana and Avarohana

Definition - Arohana and Avarohana

A rāga's arohana (ascending pattern) and avarohana (descending pattern) specify which svaras are used, and in what order, when moving up versus down in pitch. In many rāgas these two patterns use the identical set of notes in strictly reversed order, but - as covered further in Chapter 4 - this is by no means universal; the two directions can genuinely differ.

Svara Function Within a Rāga

Not every svara in a rāga carries equal melodic weight. Some are emphasized and lingered on (jīva svara, the rāga's "life note"), some serve as natural resting points at phrase endings (nyāsa svara), and some appear only fleetingly in passing - a rāga's actual character rests substantially on this internal hierarchy of emphasis, not merely on which raw svara set is permitted (Chapter 5 develops this in depth).

Rāga Bhāva

Remark - Rāga Bhāva (Mood)

Each rāga is traditionally associated with a characteristic bhāva - an emotional mood or aesthetic quality - that experienced listeners and performers recognize, informing when and in what context a rāga is considered appropriate to perform (some rāgas, for instance, are traditionally associated with particular times of day, though this convention is treated more strictly in Hindustani than in Carnatic practice).
