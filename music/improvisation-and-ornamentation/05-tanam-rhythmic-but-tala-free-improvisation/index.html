5. Tānam: Rhythmic-but-Tāla-Free Improvisation

Tānam occupies a distinctive middle ground between ālāpana (Chapter 2, unmetered and rhythmically free) and niraval/kalpana svaram (Chapters 3–4, tightly bound to a fixed tāla) - pulsed and rhythmically energetic, yet still without any fixed tāla cycle.

What Tānam Is

Definition - Tānam

Tānam is an improvisational style built around a distinctive, syllabically pulsed melodic texture (traditionally associated with softly vocalized syllables such as "tam," "anantam," or similar), giving it a clear rhythmic pulse and forward momentum that ālāpana deliberately lacks - but, like ālāpana, still without any fixed tāla cycle constraining it (The Tāla System).

Tānam's Place Between Ālāpana and Pallavi

In an RTP (Chapter 6 of Musical Forms & Compositions), tānam is performed after the rāgam (ālāpana) section and before the pallavi is introduced - serving as a rhythmically energized continuation of the rāga exploration begun in the rāgam, building momentum and anticipation before the tāla cycle is finally established with the pallavi line.

Tānam on the Vīṇā and Other Instruments

Example - Instrumental Tānam

Tānam is particularly well developed as an instrumental improvisational style, especially on the vīṇā (Chapter 2 of Instruments) and violin, where its plucked or bowed pulse can be articulated with particular rhythmic clarity - though it is performed vocally as well, using the syllabic vocalization mentioned above in place of an instrument's naturally percussive attack.

Tānam versus Gati-Based Rhythm

Remark - Tānam's Pulse Is Not a Gati

Although tānam has a clear, steady rhythmic pulse, this pulse is not organized according to a formal gati (Chapter 4 of The Tāla System) governing a tāla cycle - it is a free rhythmic energy without the cyclical, beat-position-tracking structure that true tāla-bound performance (Chapters 3–4) requires, which is precisely what distinguishes tānam from the tāla-bound pallavi improvisation that follows it in an RTP.
