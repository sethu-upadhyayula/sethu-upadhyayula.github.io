5. Concert Etiquette

Carnatic concerts follow a set of largely unwritten but well-understood conventions, for both performers and audience members - this chapter covers the etiquette a newcomer to Carnatic concerts benefits from knowing in advance.

Announcing Rāga and Tāla

Before or during a piece, the lead performer (or occasionally an announcer) typically states the rāga, tāla, and composer of the upcoming item - information that a knowledgeable audience uses to actively follow the performance (recognizing the rāga's characteristic phrases, Chapter 5 of The Rāga System, or tracking the tāla cycle, Chapter 5 of The Tāla System) rather than listening passively.

Applause Conventions

When Audiences Applaud

Carnatic concert audiences frequently applaud not only at the end of a full item but at specific moments within a performance - a particularly effective ālāpana phrase (Chapter 2 of Improvisation & Ornamentation), a well-executed korvai (Chapter 4 of Improvisation & Ornamentation and Chapter 4 of this subject), or a skillfully landed tani āvartanam conclusion (Chapter 4) may all draw spontaneous applause mid-piece, reflecting an audience actively following the music's structure rather than reserving all response for a piece's formal conclusion.

Keeping Tāla in the Audience

It is common and welcomed, rather than distracting, for audience members familiar with tāla (The Tāla System) to visibly keep the beat themselves during a performance - a quiet hand gesture tracking the tāla cycle is generally read as a sign of genuine engagement and knowledge, not an interruption or unwelcome display.

Etiquette for Performers

Remark - Mutual Respect Among Performers

Performers are expected to acknowledge accompanists' contributions (a brief nod or gesture after a particularly strong response, for instance) and to give accompanists genuine space to contribute during kalpana svaram exchanges and the tani āvartanam (Chapters 3–4) - a lead performer who consistently crowds out or ignores accompanists' contributions is generally regarded poorly, regardless of their own individual technical skill.

Requests and Song Suggestions

In some contexts, particularly smaller or more informal concerts, audience members may request specific compositions or rāgas, which a performer may or may not accommodate depending on how the request fits the concert's overall programming (Chapter 2) already underway - a practice more common in some settings and regions than others, and generally handled with a considerable degree of performer discretion.
