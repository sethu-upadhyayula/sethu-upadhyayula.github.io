1. Kutcheri Paddhati: The Standard Concert Format

As Chapter 6 of History & Composers noted, the modern Carnatic concert follows a fairly standardized structure - kutcheri paddhati - that took its now-familiar shape substantially during the early-20th-century sabhā era. This chapter covers that structure in detail.

Opening: Varṇam

A concert traditionally opens with a varṇam (Chapter 2 of Musical Forms & Compositions), its brisk tempo and technical demands serving to settle the ensemble into a shared rhythmic footing and establish the performer's technical command before the more melodically expansive items that follow.

Building Through Kṛtis

The Main Body of a Concert

Following the varṇam, a concert typically presents a series of kṛtis (Chapter 3 of Musical Forms & Compositions) of gradually increasing length and improvisational elaboration - early kṛtis might be performed relatively simply, while later ones incorporate more substantial ālāpana (Chapter 2 of Improvisation & Ornamentation), niraval (Chapter 3), and kalpana svaram (Chapter 4), building toward the concert's central, most substantial item.

The Main Piece and RTP

A concert's central, most substantial item - often called the "main" piece - typically features the most extensive improvisation of the performance, frequently taking the form of an RTP (Chapter 6 of Musical Forms & Compositions), or at minimum an extensive ālāpana-kṛti-niraval-kalpana svaram sequence around a major kṛti, and is usually where the tani āvartanam (Chapter 4) is placed.

Closing: Lighter Pieces and Tillānā

Remark - Winding Down

After the main piece, a concert typically shifts toward shorter, lighter items - padams or jāvaḷis (Chapter 4 of Musical Forms & Compositions) for emotional contrast, devotional pieces, and usually a tillānā (Chapter 5 of Musical Forms & Compositions) to close on an energetic note - often ending with a brief devotional shloka or mangalam, a short auspicious closing verse.

Flexibility Within the Standard Format

While kutcheri paddhati provides a strong default structure, performers exercise real discretion in choosing specific rāgas, tālas, and compositions within it - the format constrains the overall shape and pacing of a concert, covered further in Chapter 2, without dictating its exact content.
