2. Programming a Concert

Within the overall structure covered in Chapter 1, a performer must still make deliberate choices about which specific rāgas, tālas, and compositions to actually include - this chapter covers the considerations that typically guide that programming.

Rāga Variety and Balance

Balancing Rāga Choice Across a Concert

Performers generally aim for variety across a concert's rāga choices - avoiding excessive repetition of similar-sounding or allied rāgas (Chapter 4 of The Rāga System) in close succession, and balancing well-known, immediately accessible rāgas (Chapter 6 of The Rāga System) against rarer or more demanding ones, so the concert as a whole offers both familiarity for the audience and a demonstration of the performer's broader rāga knowledge.

Tāla Variety

Similarly, a well-programmed concert typically varies its tāla choices across items (The Tāla System) rather than repeatedly using the same tāla, giving the percussion accompanist (Chapter 3) opportunities to demonstrate rhythmic variety and giving the concert as a whole a more varied rhythmic character.

Composer Balance

Programming also typically balances compositions across different composers (History & Composers) - a concert might include Trinity kṛtis alongside works by other composers, reflecting both reverence for the core historical repertoire and an interest in showcasing the tradition's broader compositional range.

Choosing the Main Piece

Remark - Selecting the Central Item

The choice of rāga and tāla for a concert's main item (Chapter 1) - particularly if it is to be an RTP (Chapter 6 of Musical Forms & Compositions) - is typically made with particular care, since this item bears the most weight in showcasing a performer's improvisational depth (Improvisation & Ornamentation) and is often what a concert is most remembered for afterward.

Adapting to the Audience and Occasion

Programming also responds to context - a concert accompanying dance, a devotional occasion at a temple, or a formal sabhā concert (Chapter 5 of History & Composers) may each call for somewhat different rāga, tāla, and repertoire choices, adapting the general kutcheri paddhati framework (Chapter 1) to the specific performance context at hand.
