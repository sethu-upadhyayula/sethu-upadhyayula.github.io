4. Tani Āvartanam: The Percussion Solo

The tani āvartanam is the one part of a standard concert where percussion, rather than melody, takes complete centre stage - an extended rhythmic solo passage, typically following the main piece (Chapter 1), that this chapter covers in detail.

What a Tani Āvartanam Is

Definition - Tani Āvartanam

A tani āvartanam is an extended, largely improvised percussion solo passage, performed by the mṛdaṅgam (Chapter 4 of Instruments) alone or in conversation with any auxiliary percussion present (Chapter 5 of Instruments), typically inserted after the main item's melodic content has concluded - melody pauses almost entirely (aside from the tāla-establishing melodic phrase the percussionists build from) while the percussionists take an extended improvisational turn.

Structure of a Tani Āvartanam

A tani āvartanam typically begins with each percussionist present introducing their own rhythmic ideas somewhat independently, before moving into call-and-response exchanges between the different percussion instruments, and finally builds toward a korvai (a precisely calculated rhythmic phrase, Chapter 4 of Improvisation & Ornamentation) that resolves exactly back to the point in the tāla cycle where the melodic performer will re-enter.

Coordinating the Return to Melody

Remark - Landing the Return

The tani āvartanam's concluding korvai must land precisely on the correct beat of the tāla cycle (typically the samam, Chapter 1 of The Tāla System) so the melodic performer can re-enter exactly where the piece requires - a moment of real technical and musical significance in the concert, since a mistimed or poorly executed landing is immediately obvious to anyone tracking the tāla, in sharp contrast to melodic errors, which can sometimes be more easily absorbed or disguised.

Audience Engagement During Tani Āvartanam

Example - An Interactive Moment

Knowledgeable audience members often follow a tani āvartanam particularly closely, sometimes visibly keeping tāla themselves (Chapter 5) and reacting audibly to especially skillful rhythmic passages or a well-landed korvai - making the tani āvartanam one of the more overtly interactive and audience-engaging moments of a typically otherwise more contemplatively received concert.
