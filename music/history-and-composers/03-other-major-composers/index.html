3. Other Major Composers

While the Trinity (Chapter 2) looms especially large in the Carnatic repertoire, they represent only one, particularly concentrated, period within a much longer and continuing history of composition - this chapter surveys several other composers whose work remains widely performed.

Svāti Tirunāḷ

Svāti Tirunāḷ (19th century), the Maharaja of Travancore, was himself a prolific composer and patron of the arts, known for compositions spanning multiple languages and a wide stylistic range, reflecting his court's cosmopolitan cultural environment - his works remain a significant and distinct presence in the repertoire alongside the Trinity's.

Composers of the Bhakti Era Before the Trinity

Beyond Purandara Dāsa (Chapter 1), other devotional poet-composers - including Annamācārya, whose vast output of devotional songs to Lord Venkateswara predates the Trinity by roughly two to three centuries - contributed substantially to the devotional song tradition (Chapter 1) that later fed into the mature kṛti form.

Papanāsam Śivan and 20th-Century Composers

Example - Papanāsam Śivan

Papanāsam Śivan (20th century) is often described as a composer in the direct devotional and stylistic lineage of the Trinity, prolific in Tamil-language devotional composition, and representative of how the kṛti tradition continued to develop actively well into the 20th century rather than ending with the Trinity's own era (Chapter 6 covers this continuation in more depth).

Composers of Padams, Jāvaḷis, and Tillānās

Distinct composers are also particularly associated with the lighter forms covered in Chapters 4–5 of Musical Forms & Compositions - padam composition, for instance, has its own distinguished lineage of specialist composers historically associated with the courtly and temple-dance performance contexts those forms grew out of, separate from the primarily kṛti-focused output of the Trinity and their contemporaries.

A Continuing Tradition

Remark - Composition as an Ongoing Practice

Carnatic composition has never stopped - performers and composers continue creating new kṛtis, varṇams, and other forms today, even as the concert repertoire remains heavily weighted toward the Trinity and other historical composers, reflecting both genuine reverence for that established repertoire and composition's status as a living, ongoing practice rather than a closed historical chapter.
