1. Origins and Early History

Carnatic music as a distinct classical tradition, with its own theoretical scheme (The Rāga System, The Tāla System) and characteristic performance style, took shape over many centuries, growing out of a broader shared South Asian musical and devotional heritage.

Shared Roots with Hindustani Music

Carnatic and Hindustani classical music (the classical tradition of North India) share deep historical roots in the same ancient Sanskrit musical theory, and both use closely analogous concepts of rāga and tāla, but the two traditions diverged into distinct systems over time, particularly after the medieval period, developing along largely separate lines with their own distinct repertoire, terminology, and performance conventions.

The Bhakti Movement

Definition - The Bhakti Movement's Influence

The bhakti (devotional) movement - a wave of vernacular, emotionally direct devotional poetry and music across medieval India - profoundly shaped Carnatic music's characteristic content: the great majority of the tradition's core compositions (kṛtis, padams, and others, Musical Forms & Compositions) are devotional in subject matter, a direct legacy of this movement's emphasis on personal, musically expressed devotion to a chosen deity.

Purandara Dāsa: The "Father of Carnatic Music"

Example - Purandara Dāsa

Purandara Dāsa (15th–16th century) is traditionally honoured as the "father of Carnatic music," credited with systematizing the pedagogical sequence still used to teach beginners today - including the sarali svara exercises (Chapter 4 of Fundamentals) and the gītam form (Chapter 1 of Musical Forms & Compositions) - and with composing a very large body of devotional songs (kīrtanas) that remain part of the active repertoire.

Codification of Theory

Alongside this practical and devotional development, Carnatic music's formal theoretical apparatus - including the systematic melakarta scheme covered in Chapter 2 of The Rāga System - was progressively codified by music theorists over subsequent centuries, culminating in the more standardized theoretical framework still taught today, setting the stage for the flourishing of composition associated with the Trinity (Chapter 2).
