5. Institutions and Transmission

Beyond individual composers and performing lineages (Chapters 2–4), Carnatic music's continuity has depended on a broader set of institutions - courts, temples, and later dedicated music organizations - this chapter covers how the tradition has been supported and transmitted at this institutional level.

Temple and Court Patronage

Historically, Carnatic music was substantially sustained through temple institutions (where devotional music, Chapter 1, was performed as part of religious practice) and royal court patronage (such as the Travancore court associated with Svāti Tirunāḷ, Chapter 3) - both providing musicians with institutional support and a steady performance context well before the modern public concert format (Concert Practice) became the tradition's dominant public venue.

The Guru-Śiṣya Paramparā

Definition - Guru-Śiṣya Paramparā

The guru-śiṣya paramparā (teacher-student lineage) is the traditional mode of transmitting Carnatic music - a student learns directly and extensively from an individual teacher over an extended period, absorbing not just specific compositions and technique but also gamaka (Chapter 1 of Improvisation & Ornamentation), baaṇi (Chapter 4), and broader musical values largely by direct example and imitation, consistent with the oral-transmission emphasis covered in Chapter 5 of Fundamentals.

The Rise of Sabhās and the Modern Concert Format

From roughly the early 20th century onward, dedicated music organizations called sabhās emerged, particularly in Madras (present-day Chennai), organizing public ticketed concerts and establishing much of the concert structure and etiquette still followed today (Concert Practice) - shifting Carnatic music's primary public context from temple and court patronage toward organized public performance for a paying, dedicated concert-going audience.

Formal Music Education

Remark - Institutions Alongside the Guru-Śiṣya Tradition

Alongside the continuing guru-śiṣya paramparā, formal institutions - dedicated music colleges, structured graded examination systems, and, more recently, online instruction - have expanded access to Carnatic training considerably, without fully displacing the direct teacher-student relationship that remains central to how gamaka and baaṇi in particular are still most effectively transmitted.
