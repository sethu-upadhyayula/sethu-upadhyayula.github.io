2. Varṇam

The varṇam occupies an unusual dual role in Carnatic music - simultaneously one of the most technically demanding forms a student practices, and a staple concert opener performed by advanced artists throughout their careers.

Structure of a Varṇam

Definition - Varṇam Structure

A varṇam is built from several sections: the pallavi (opening refrain), anupallavi (second section), muktāyi svaram (a concluding passage of pure svara passages, no lyrics), and - after returning to the pallavi and anupallavi - a series of caraṇam and caraṇa svaram sections, each caraṇa svaram typically increasing in rhythmic density and complexity as the piece progresses.

Why Varṇams Are Demanding

A varṇam is deliberately constructed to comprehensively exercise a rāga's characteristic phrases (Chapter 5 of The Rāga System) across all three registers (Chapter 3 of Fundamentals), while simultaneously demanding precise tāla control (The Tāla System) through its increasingly intricate svara passages - making thorough mastery of a single varṇam a substantial technical achievement in its own right, not merely a stepping-stone piece to be quickly moved past.

Tāna Varṇam and Pada Varṇam

Two Types of Varṇam
  • Tāna varṇam - the type described above, primarily a vehicle for melodic and rhythmic technique, with comparatively simple, often devotional lyrics.
  • Pada varṇam - associated more closely with dance (particularly Bharatanatyam), typically slower and with more elaborated, often romantic or devotional narrative lyrics suited to being expressed through dance gesture and expression alongside the music.

The Varṇam as a Concert Opener

Remark - Why Concerts Open with a Varṇam

A varṇam's brisk tempo, rhythmic energy, and technical demands make it an effective concert opener (Chapter 8 of Concert Practice) - it immediately establishes the performer's technical command and settles the ensemble into a shared tempo and rhythmic footing before the more melodically expansive and improvisation-heavy forms (Chapters 3–6, and Improvisation & Ornamentation) that typically follow later in a concert.
