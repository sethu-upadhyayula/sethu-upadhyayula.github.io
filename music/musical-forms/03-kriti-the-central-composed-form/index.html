3. Kṛti: The Central Composed Form

The kṛti is, by volume and by centrality to the concert repertoire, the single most important compositional form in Carnatic music - the form the great composers (History & Composers) are most closely associated with, and the form around which a typical concert's main pieces are organized.

The Three-Part Kṛti Structure

Definition - Pallavi, Anupallavi, Caraṇam

A standard kṛti has three sections: the pallavi (the opening line or two, functioning as the piece's central refrain, returned to repeatedly), the anupallavi (a contrasting second section, often rising in pitch and elaborating on the pallavi's theme), and the caraṇam (one or more further verses, typically ending with a melodic phrase that leads naturally back into the pallavi, closing the form's structural loop).

Sāhitya: Lyrical Content and Composer's Signature

Kṛti lyrics are considerably more developed than a gītam's (Chapter 1) - typically devotional, addressed to a specific deity, and often incorporating the composer's mudra (a signature word or phrase, frequently a play on the composer's own name or a related word) somewhere within the caraṇam, a convention that both signs the composition and helps confirm attribution to a specific composer (Chapter 2 of History & Composers).

Kṛti as a Vehicle for Improvisation

Remark - Kṛtis Rarely Stand Alone

In performance, a kṛti is rarely rendered as a purely fixed, unchanging piece - it is typically preceded by an ālāpana (Chapter 2 of Improvisation & Ornamentation) exploring the kṛti's rāga, and may be followed by niraval (melodic improvisation on a specific line, Chapter 3 of Improvisation & Ornamentation) and kalpana svaram (Chapter 4 of Improvisation & Ornamentation) built from the kṛti's own pallavi or a chosen caraṇam line - meaning a single kṛti can anchor an extended performance segment lasting anywhere from a few minutes to well over half an hour, depending on how much improvisation a performer builds around it.

The Kṛti's Historical Development

The kṛti form as widely performed today owes much of its standardization and its central place in the repertoire to the composers of the late 18th and early 19th centuries, particularly the Trinity of Carnatic music (Chapter 1 of History & Composers), whose kṛtis remain among the most frequently performed compositions in the tradition today.
