6. Śruti Instruments and Tuning

This closing chapter covers the instruments responsible for actually sustaining the continuous drone introduced in Chapter 1, and the practical process of tuning a performance to a chosen śruti.

The Tambūra

Definition - Tambūra

The tambūra (tanpura) is a long-necked, unfretted plucked string instrument whose strings are tuned to the śruti and its supporting intervals (typically the tonic Sa, the fifth Pa or fourth Ma, and a lower octave Sa) and played with a continuous, rhythmically unstructured plucking pattern throughout a performance - producing the sustained drone that anchors every other instrument and voice.

The Electronic Śruti Box

Modern performances frequently substitute an electronic śruti box (or a tambūra sample/app) for a physical tambūra - offering more consistent tuning and requiring no dedicated accompanying musician to play it, at some cost to the subtle, continuously varying overtone-rich texture a physical, hand-plucked tambūra produces.

Choosing and Setting the Śruti

Before a performance, the lead performer selects their śruti (Chapter 1) and the tambūra or śruti box is tuned accordingly; accompanying instruments (mṛdaṅgam aside, since it is not a pitched instrument in the same sense, Chapter 4 of Instruments) then tune themselves to match this same reference, ensuring the entire ensemble operates against one shared, unified pitch anchor for the full performance.

Tuning Precision and Intonation

Remark - Just Intonation and Ear Training

Carnatic tuning is generally oriented toward acoustically pure (just) intervals relative to the drone rather than the equal-tempered tuning standard in most Western instruments - meaning Carnatic musicians train their intonation by continuous reference to the actual sounding drone (Chapter 1) rather than to a fixed, pre-calculated frequency table, reinforcing why the drone's constant presence throughout a performance is so structurally important, not merely a background convenience.
