1. Śruti: The Foundation of Pitch

Unlike Western classical music, which is generally anchored to a fixed, universal pitch standard (A440), Carnatic music is built around a performer-chosen reference pitch - śruti - that everything else in the performance is defined relative to.

Śruti as a Reference Pitch

Definition - Śruti

Śruti is the fixed drone pitch (functioning as the tonic, "Sa") that a performer selects before a concert, based on their vocal or instrumental range, and sustains continuously throughout the performance as an unchanging reference. Every other pitch, phrase, and rāga performed is understood and perceived relative to this one fixed point - melody in Carnatic music is fundamentally relational, not tied to any absolute frequency the way a fixed concert pitch would be.

Choosing a Śruti

A performer's chosen śruti is a personal, practical decision - generally the pitch at which their voice or instrument sounds fullest and most comfortable across the range a performance demands - rather than a fixed convention shared by every performer. This is why two Carnatic vocalists can perform the identical composition in the identical rāga while singing at entirely different absolute pitches, without this being considered a "different" rendition in any meaningful sense.

The Continuous Drone

Remark - Why the Drone Never Stops

The śruti drone plays continuously throughout a performance - not merely as a starting reference that is then abandoned. This constant presence gives the ear a fixed anchor against which every svara (Chapter 2) can be judged for intonation, and is part of why Carnatic musicians cultivate an extremely precise, drone-referenced sense of pitch rather than relying on fixed, memorized absolute frequencies.

Śruti versus Śruti (Microtones)

Confusingly, the word "śruti" is also used in Indian music theory for the 22 microtonal intervals said to subdivide the octave - a separate, older theoretical concept distinct from the practical "drone pitch" sense of the word used throughout the rest of this subject. Chapter 2 covers how the twelve practically used svara positions relate to this finer microtonal backdrop.
