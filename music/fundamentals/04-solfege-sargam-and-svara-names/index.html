4. Solfège: Sargam and Svara Names

Beyond simply naming pitch positions, the seven svara names (Chapter 2) form Carnatic music's spoken and sung solfège system - sargam - used both pedagogically and as a performance practice in its own right.

Sargam as Solfège

Definition - Sargam

Sargam (from Sa-Ri-Ga-Ma, the first four svara names) refers to singing or reciting a melody using the svara names themselves, rather than composed lyrics - the Carnatic equivalent of Western solfège ("do-re-mi") singing. It serves both as a teaching tool (making the underlying svara structure of a phrase explicit to a learner) and as a performed improvisational element in its own right (kalpana svaram, Chapter 4 of Improvisation & Ornamentation).

Sargam in Teaching

Carnatic pedagogy typically introduces a new composition or rāga phrase first in sargam, so a student directly perceives and internalizes its svara structure, before attaching the actual composed lyrics (sāhitya) - separating the melodic/svara content of a piece from its textual content as two distinct layers to be learned somewhat independently before being combined.

Fixed-Do versus Movable-Do

Remark - Sargam Is Movable-Do

Because Sa is always the performer's own chosen śruti (Chapter 1) rather than a fixed absolute pitch, sargam functions as a movable-do system - analogous to movable-do solfège in Western pedagogy, where "do" always means "the tonic," whatever its absolute pitch happens to be, rather than a "fixed-do" system where note names are tied to specific fixed frequencies (as in French/Italian-style fixed solfège).

Basic Melodic Exercises

Foundational Carnatic training begins with structured svara exercises - sarali svaras (simple ascending/descending patterns), janta svaras (patterns with repeated notes), and similar graded exercises - that build facility with the twelve svarasthānas (Chapter 2) and the three registers (Chapter 3) before a student moves on to actual rāgas and compositions (The Rāga System and Musical Forms & Compositions).
