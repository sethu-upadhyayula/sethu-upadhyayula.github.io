3. The Saptaka and Octave Registers

A Carnatic performance typically spans more than a single octave above the śruti - this chapter covers the three-register system used to describe and notate that wider range.

The Saptaka

Definition - Saptaka

A saptaka is an octave - the span from one Sa to the next Sa at double (or half) its frequency, containing the twelve svarasthānas covered in Chapter 2. The term is used interchangeably with "octave" in most practical discussion, though it literally emphasizes the "seven" (sapta) named svaras that structure it.

The Three Registers

Mandra, Madhya, and Tāra Sthāyi
  • Mandra sthāyi - the lower octave register, below the primary reference octave.
  • Madhya sthāyi - the middle register, the primary octave in which the tonic Sa (matching the chosen śruti, Chapter 1) sits, and where most melodic movement is centred.
  • Tāra sthāyi - the upper (higher) register, above the madhya sthāyi octave.

Skilled performers move fluidly across all three registers within a single rāga rendition (Chapter 2 of The Rāga System and Chapter 2 of Improvisation & Ornamentation), with the specific registers emphasized often depending on the rāga's own character and the performer's vocal or instrumental range.

Marking Registers in Notation

Remark - Notating Register

In written notation (Chapter 5), madhya sthāyi svaras are written plain, mandra sthāyi svaras are marked with a dot below the letter, and tāra sthāyi svaras are marked with a dot above the letter - allowing a single set of seven svara letters to unambiguously specify pitch across all three registers rather than needing separate symbols for each octave.

Registers and Vocal/Instrumental Range

A given performer's or instrument's practical range typically spans parts of all three sthāyis but rarely the complete span of all three at full comfort - part of why śruti selection (Chapter 1) matters so much: it determines exactly which portion of this three-register span a given rāga's melodic movement will actually sit within for that particular performer.
