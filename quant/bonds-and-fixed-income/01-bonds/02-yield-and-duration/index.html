1.2 Yield, Duration and DV01

Once we can price a bond, the natural next questions are: how sensitive is the price to changes in interest rates, and how do we measure that sensitivity? The answers are duration and DV01 - the workhorses of interest rate risk management on every fixed income desk.

Yield to Maturity

The yield to maturity $y$ of a bond is its internal rate of return: the single flat discount rate that equates the present value of all future cashflows to the observed market dirty price $P$.

Definition - Yield to Maturity

For a bond with cashflows $C_i$ at times $t_i$ (coupons) and principal $F$ at maturity $T$, the yield $y$ satisfies under continuous compounding: $$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} C_i\, e^{-y\,t_i} + F\,e^{-y\,T}$$ With semi-annual compounding ($m=2$): $$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{C/m}{(1+y/m)^{m\,t_i}} + \frac{F}{(1+y/m)^{m\,T}}$$ The yield is found by numerical root-finding (Newton-Raphson). There is no closed-form solution in general.

Macaulay Duration

Duration measures the weighted average time until a bondholder receives cashflows, where weights are proportional to the present value of each cashflow.

Definition - Macaulay Duration

The Macaulay duration $D_{\text{Mac}}$ is: $$D_{\text{Mac}} = \frac{1}{P}\sum_{i=1}^{n} t_i \cdot PV(C_i) = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} t_i\, C_i\,e^{-y\,t_i} + T\,F\,e^{-y\,T}}{P}$$ For a zero-coupon bond, $D_{\text{Mac}} = T$ exactly. For a coupon bond, $D_{\text{Mac}} < T$ because cashflows received before maturity reduce the effective average timing.

Modified Duration

Modified duration converts Macaulay duration into a direct measure of price sensitivity to yield changes.

Definition - Modified Duration

$$D_{\text{mod}} = -\frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dy} = \frac{D_{\text{Mac}}}{1 + y/m}$$ where $m$ is the compounding frequency. For continuous compounding, $D_{\text{mod}} = D_{\text{Mac}}$. The first-order price change approximation for a parallel yield shift $\Delta y$ is: $$\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{mod}}\cdot P\cdot \Delta y$$

Theorem - Duration as Price-Yield Sensitivity

Under continuous compounding, differentiating $P = \sum_i C_i e^{-y t_i} + F e^{-yT}$ with respect to $y$: $$\frac{dP}{dy} = -\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} t_i\,C_i\,e^{-y t_i} + T\,F\,e^{-yT}\right) = -D_{\text{Mac}}\cdot P$$ Hence $D_{\text{mod}} = D_{\text{Mac}}$ under continuous compounding, and the approximation $\Delta P \approx -D_{\text{Mac}} \cdot P \cdot \Delta y$ holds to first order.

DV01 and PVBP

The DV01 (Dollar Value of a Basis Point) or PVBP (Price Value of a Basis Point) is the change in bond price for a 1 basis point (0.0001) parallel shift in yields.

Definition - DV01

$$\text{DV01} = -\frac{dP}{dy}\times 0.0001 = D_{\text{mod}}\cdot P \cdot 0.0001$$ For a notional of $N$, the full DV01 is $D_{\text{mod}} \cdot P \cdot N \cdot 0.0001$. DV01 is quoted in currency units per basis point per unit notional and is the primary tool for hedging and risk aggregation on rates desks.

Example - Duration and DV01

Consider a 5-year 4% semi-annual bond priced at par ($P = 100$, $y = 4\%$, $m = 2$). Macaulay duration: $D_{\text{Mac}} \approx 4.49$ years.

Modified duration: $D_{\text{mod}} = 4.49\, /\, (1 + 0.04/2) = 4.49\, /\, 1.02 \approx 4.40$ years.

DV01 $= 4.40 \times 100 \times 0.0001 = \$0.044$ per \$100 notional. A 10 bp yield rise reduces the price by approximately $\$0.44$ per \$100 face value.

Convexity

Duration is a linear (first-order) approximation. Convexity captures the curvature - the second-order correction to the price-yield relationship, which matters for large yield moves.

Definition - Convexity

$$\mathcal{C} = \frac{1}{P}\frac{d^2 P}{dy^2} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} t_i^2\,C_i\,e^{-y t_i} + T^2\,F\,e^{-yT}}{P}$$ The second-order price approximation is: $$\frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -D_{\text{mod}}\,\Delta y + \frac{1}{2}\mathcal{C}\,(\Delta y)^2$$ Convexity is always positive for plain vanilla fixed-rate bonds. This means a bond gains more when yields fall than it loses when yields rise by the same amount - a desirable property investors pay for.

Remark - Portfolio Duration and Rate Hedging

The DV01 of a bond portfolio is the sum of individual DV01s (linearity of the first-order approximation). To hedge $\$H$ of DV01 exposure using a government bond future with DV01 $d$ per contract, one sells (or buys) $H/d$ contracts. This duration-neutral construction is the foundation of all parallel-shift hedging in fixed income.
