1.1 Bond Pricing

A bond is a debt instrument in which the issuer promises to pay the holder a series of fixed cashflows. The standard fixed-rate bond pays periodic coupons $C$ and returns the face value (principal) $F$ at maturity $T$. Pricing a bond means computing the present value of all these cashflows.

Definition — Bond Price (Discrete Compounding)

For a bond with $n$ coupon periods, coupon $C$ per period, face value $F$, yield to maturity $y$, and coupon frequency $m$ (periods per year), the (dirty) price is: $$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{C}{(1+y/m)^{i}} + \frac{F}{(1+y/m)^{n}}$$ The yield $y$ is the single discount rate that equates this sum to the observed market price.

Using the geometric series formula this simplifies to:

$$P = \frac{C}{y/m}\left[1 - \frac{1}{(1+y/m)^n}\right] + \frac{F}{(1+y/m)^n}$$
Definition — Bond Price (Continuous Compounding)

Under continuous compounding with flat yield $y$, the bond price is: $$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} C\,e^{-y\,t_i} + F\,e^{-y\,T}$$ where $t_i$ are the coupon dates. For a zero-coupon bond ($C=0$): $$P = F\,e^{-yT}$$

Day Count Conventions

The fraction of a year between two dates depends on the day count convention, which affects accrued interest and coupon amounts.

Remark — Common Day Count Conventions

Act/365 (Actual/365 Fixed): divide the actual number of calendar days by 365. Common for GBP bonds and money markets.

Act/360: divide actual days by 360. Used for USD SOFR and many money market instruments.

30/360: assumes each month has 30 days and each year 360 days. Standard for US corporate and agency bonds.

Act/Act (ICMA): actual days divided by actual days in the coupon period times frequency. Used for government bonds in many markets.

Clean Price, Dirty Price and Accrued Interest

In practice bonds are quoted as clean prices (flat prices) which exclude accrued interest. The dirty price (full price) is what the buyer actually pays.

Definition — Accrued Interest

Let $t_{prev}$ be the last coupon date, $t$ the settlement date, and $t_{next}$ the next coupon date. Then: $$AI = C \cdot \frac{t - t_{prev}}{t_{next} - t_{prev}}$$ and $\text{Dirty Price} = \text{Clean Price} + AI$.

Example

A 5% annual bond ($F = 100$) with 3 years to maturity trades at a yield of 4%. Using annual compounding:

$$P = \frac{5}{1.04} + \frac{5}{1.04^2} + \frac{105}{1.04^3} = 4.808 + 4.623 + 93.388 = 102.82$$

The bond trades above par because its coupon rate (5%) exceeds the yield (4%).

Zero-Coupon Bonds and Discount Factors

A zero-coupon bond pays only $F$ at maturity with no intermediate coupons. Its price directly reveals the market discount factor:

$$P(0, T) = F \cdot e^{-r(T) \cdot T}$$

where $r(T)$ is the continuously compounded spot rate for maturity $T$. Discount factors $P(0,T)$ are the fundamental building blocks for pricing all fixed income instruments.

Remark — Bond Price Relationships

When $y = $ coupon rate, $P = F$ (priced at par). When $y >$ coupon rate, $P < F$ (discount bond). When $y <$ coupon rate, $P > F$ (premium bond). As $T \to 0$, $P \to F$ regardless of yield (pull-to-par).
