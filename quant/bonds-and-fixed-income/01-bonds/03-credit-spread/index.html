1.3 Credit Spread

Government bonds are treated as risk-free: the state can always print money to repay in its own currency. Corporate bonds and bonds from riskier sovereigns trade at higher yields to compensate investors for credit risk. The credit spread is this excess yield, and its precise measurement is the foundation of relative value analysis in credit markets.

I-Spread (Interpolated Spread)

Definition - I-Spread

The I-spread is the difference between the bond's yield to maturity and the linearly interpolated swap rate (or government benchmark yield) at the same maturity: $$s_I = y_{\text{bond}} - y_{\text{swap}}(T)$$ It is the simplest spread measure and widely quoted for investment grade corporates. Its limitation is that it uses a single benchmark rate and ignores the shape of the yield curve.

Z-Spread

The Z-spread (zero-volatility spread) uses the full swap discount curve rather than a single benchmark rate, giving a more accurate measure of the credit premium.

Definition - Z-Spread

The Z-spread $z$ is the constant spread added to each point of the risk-free discount curve such that the discounted cashflows equal the observed dirty price. Under continuous compounding: $$P = \sum_{i=1}^{n} C_i\,e^{-(r(t_i)+z)\,t_i} + F\,e^{-(r(T)+z)\,T}$$ where $r(t)$ is the continuously compounded spot rate from the OIS/swap curve at time $t$. The Z-spread $z$ is found by numerical root-finding and is the standard credit spread measure for fixed-rate bonds.

Remark - Z-Spread vs I-Spread

The Z-spread properly accounts for the term structure of rates, making it more accurate than I-spread when the yield curve is steep. For bullet bonds with moderate maturities in a near-flat curve environment, the two are close. The Z-spread is always well-defined and preferred for risk-adjusted comparisons across maturities.

Asset Swap Spread (ASW)

An asset swap packages a fixed-rate bond with an interest rate swap (IRS) that converts the fixed coupons into floating payments. The asset swap spread is the floating spread over benchmark that makes this package trade at par.

Definition - Asset Swap Spread

For a bond with dirty price $P_d$ and clean price $P_c$, the par asset swap spread $s_{\text{ASW}}$ satisfies: $$(100 - P_d) + \text{PV(fixed bond cashflows discounted at swap curve)} = s_{\text{ASW}} \times \text{Annuity}$$ Approximately: $$s_{\text{ASW}} \approx \text{coupon} - \text{par swap rate} + \frac{(100 - P_c)}{\text{Annuity}}$$ where Annuity $= \sum_i DF(t_i)/m$. The ASW spread is widely used for relative value between bonds and CDS.

Option-Adjusted Spread (OAS)

For bonds with embedded options (callables, putables) or path-dependent cashflows (MBS prepayment), the Z-spread conflates credit risk with option value. The OAS strips out the embedded option.

Definition - Option-Adjusted Spread

The OAS is the constant spread added to the short-rate model discount curve such that the model price - averaged over all simulated interest rate paths - equals the market price: $$P = \mathbb{E}^Q\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} C_i\,e^{-\int_0^{t_i}(r(s)+\text{OAS})\,ds} + F\,e^{-\int_0^T (r(s)+\text{OAS})\,ds}\right]$$ For a callable bond, OAS $<$ Z-spread because the embedded call option has positive value (benefits the issuer). For a putable bond, OAS $>$ Z-spread.

CDS-Bond Basis

Definition - CDS-Bond Basis

The CDS-bond basis is the difference between the CDS spread and the bond Z-spread (or ASW spread) for the same reference entity and tenor: $$\text{Basis} = s_{\text{CDS}} - s_Z$$ In theory the basis should be zero by no-arbitrage. In practice it reflects: cheapest-to-deliver (CTD) optionality in CDS, repo specialness, funding and balance-sheet costs, liquidity differences between the bond and CDS markets, and counterparty risk. A persistently negative basis (bond spread $>$ CDS spread) typically indicates funding or supply-demand distortions.

Example - Z-Spread Calculation

A 3-year semi-annual 5% corporate bond is priced at 98.50. The swap spot rates are: 0.5yr = 3.6%, 1yr = 3.8%, 1.5yr = 3.9%, 2yr = 4.0%, 2.5yr = 4.1%, 3yr = 4.2%.

The Z-spread $z$ solves: $$98.50 = \sum_{i=1}^{6} \frac{2.5}{(1+(r(0.5i)+z)/2)^{i}} + \frac{100}{(1+(4.2\%+z)/2)^{6}}$$ Solving numerically gives $z \approx 112$ bps. This is the credit and liquidity premium investors demand above the risk-free swap curve.
