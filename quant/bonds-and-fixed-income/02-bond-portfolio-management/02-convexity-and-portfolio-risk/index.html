2.2 Convexity and Portfolio Risk

Section 1.2 introduced convexity $\mathcal{C}$ as the second-order term in the price-yield relationship. At the single-bond level it is a minor refinement; at the portfolio level, convexity becomes a tool that portfolio managers actively trade — and the difference between two portfolios with identical duration but different convexity can be the dominant driver of relative performance in a large rate move.

Portfolio Convexity

Just as portfolio duration is the value-weighted average of the constituent durations (Section 2.1), portfolio convexity is the value-weighted average of the constituent convexities.

Definition — Portfolio Convexity

$$\mathcal{C}_{\text{portfolio}} = \sum_{i=1}^N w_i\, \mathcal{C}_i, \qquad w_i = \frac{P_i}{\sum_{j=1}^N P_j}$$ and the portfolio's second-order price approximation under a parallel shift $\Delta y$ is: $$\frac{\Delta P_{\text{portfolio}}}{P_{\text{portfolio}}} \approx -D_{\text{portfolio}}\,\Delta y + \frac{1}{2}\mathcal{C}_{\text{portfolio}}\,(\Delta y)^2$$

Barbell vs Bullet Portfolios

Consider two portfolios with the same market value and the same modified duration, but constructed differently:

Remark — Bullet and Barbell

Bullet portfolio: all the capital is invested in bonds clustered around a single maturity (e.g. all 7-year bonds). Its cashflows are concentrated in time.

Barbell portfolio: the capital is split between short-dated and long-dated bonds (e.g. half in 2-year bonds, half in 15-year bonds) chosen so the value-weighted duration equals the same 7 years. Its cashflows are spread across the two ends of the curve.

Theorem — Barbells Have Higher Convexity

For two portfolios with equal value and equal duration $D$, the one whose cashflows are more dispersed in time has higher convexity. Formally, since convexity involves a weighted average of squared times-to-cashflow ($\mathcal{C} \propto \sum_i w_i t_i^2$, roughly), and variance is non-negative: $$\mathcal{C}_{\text{barbell}} \geq \mathcal{C}_{\text{bullet}}$$ with equality only in the degenerate case where the barbell collapses to the bullet's maturity. Intuitively, a portfolio with cashflows at $t=2$ and $t=15$ averaging to duration $7$ has more "spread" in $t_i$ around the mean than one with all cashflows near $t=7$, and $\mathcal{C}$ grows with the second moment of that distribution.

Convexity's Effect on P&L for Large Rate Moves

The second-order term $\tfrac{1}{2}\mathcal{C}(\Delta y)^2$ is always non-negative (since $\mathcal{C} > 0$ for vanilla bonds and $(\Delta y)^2 \geq 0$), regardless of the direction of the rate move. This means that for two portfolios with the same duration but different convexity, the higher-convexity portfolio:

Remark — The Convexity Trade-Off

Outperforms the lower-convexity portfolio whenever yields move by a large amount in either direction (gains more when rates fall, loses less when rates rise). The duration-matched first-order effects are identical by construction, so any difference in realized P&L for a large parallel move is attributable entirely to the convexity gap. This benefit is not free: in a market priced by no-arbitrage, higher-convexity portfolios (like barbells) typically offer a lower yield than lower-convexity portfolios (bullets) of the same duration — the yield give-up is the market price of the convexity. This trade-off between yield pickup and convexity is a recurring theme across fixed income relative value (Chapter 4).

Worked Example — Barbell vs Bullet Comparison

Example — P&L Under a Large Parallel Shift

Two portfolios, each worth $P = 100$, have the same modified duration $D = 7$. The bullet portfolio (all 7-year bonds) has convexity $\mathcal{C}_{\text{bullet}} = 55$. The barbell portfolio (split between 2-year and 15-year bonds, weighted to the same duration) has convexity $\mathcal{C}_{\text{barbell}} = 95$.

Suppose yields rise by $\Delta y = +200\text{bp} = 0.02$. The second-order approximation gives:

Bullet: $\dfrac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -7(0.02) + \tfrac{1}{2}(55)(0.02)^2 = -0.14 + 0.011 = -0.129$, i.e. $-12.9\%$.

Barbell: $\dfrac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -7(0.02) + \tfrac{1}{2}(95)(0.02)^2 = -0.14 + 0.019 = -0.121$, i.e. $-12.1\%$.

Now suppose instead yields fall by $\Delta y = -0.02$. Both portfolios gain from the linear term, but the convexity term is again positive (since $(\Delta y)^2$ is the same):

Bullet: $\dfrac{\Delta P}{P} \approx +0.14 + 0.011 = +0.151$, i.e. $+15.1\%$. Barbell: $\dfrac{\Delta P}{P} \approx +0.14 + 0.019 = +0.159$, i.e. $+15.9\%$.

In both directions the barbell outperforms the bullet by roughly $0.8$ percentage points — the convexity advantage of $40$ units ($\mathcal{C}_{\text{barbell}} - \mathcal{C}_{\text{bullet}} = 40$) times $\tfrac{1}{2}(\Delta y)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.0004) = 0.0002$, giving $40 \times 0.0002 = 0.008$, i.e. $0.8\%$. For a $200$bp move this convexity premium is meaningful; for a $10$bp move it would be negligible ($40 \times \tfrac{1}{2}(0.001)^2 \approx 0.002\%$), which is why convexity differences matter primarily in volatile rate environments or for large stress scenarios.
