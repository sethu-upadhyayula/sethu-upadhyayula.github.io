3.1 Primary Markets and Auctions

Every bond outstanding in the secondary market was, at some point, sold for the first time in the primary market. For sovereign issuers, this happens through a regular cycle of auctions; for corporate issuers, through a syndication process run by underwriting banks. The mechanics of issuance shape the supply that the secondary market must absorb, and the timing of auctions is itself a source of short-term price pressure that relative value traders track closely.

Government Bond Auctions

Most developed-market governments fund themselves through periodic auctions of standardized securities (e.g. US Treasury bills, notes, and bonds; UK Gilts; German Bunds). An auction calendar is published in advance, specifying the maturity, target issue size, and date for each auction. Investors submit bids specifying a price (or yield) and quantity, and the issuer allocates the announced amount among bidders according to a defined rule.

Definition — Competitive and Non-Competitive Bids

Competitive bids specify both a yield (or price) and a quantity. The issuer ranks competitive bids from lowest to highest yield (i.e. highest to lowest price) and accepts them in that order until the announced issue size is reached.

Non-competitive bids specify only a quantity (typically capped at a small amount per bidder) and are guaranteed to be filled in full at the auction's clearing yield, regardless of where that yield falls. Non-competitive bids are designed to give smaller investors guaranteed access without requiring them to take a view on the clearing level.

Single-Price (Dutch) vs Multiple-Price Auctions

Having ranked the competitive bids, the issuer must decide what price each successful bidder actually pays. Two conventions dominate.

Definition — Single-Price and Multiple-Price Auctions

In a single-price (Dutch) auction, the issuer determines the highest yield $y^*$ (the "stop-out yield") at which the cumulative quantity of accepted bids exactly equals the announced issue size. Every successful bidder — including those who bid more aggressively (lower yield) than $y^*$ — pays the same price corresponding to $y^*$.

In a multiple-price (discriminatory) auction, each successful bidder pays the price corresponding to their own submitted yield. Bidders who bid more aggressively pay a higher price (receive a lower yield) than those who bid closer to the margin.

Remark — Why Single-Price Auctions Dominate

Most major sovereign issuers (including the US Treasury for notes and bonds) have moved to single-price auctions. The argument is that multiple-price auctions create a "winner's curse": a bidder who wins at an aggressive price relative to the eventual stop-out has effectively overpaid, which encourages bidders to shade their bids away from their true valuation to avoid this risk — reducing aggregate demand and raising the issuer's funding cost. Single-price auctions remove this disincentive because every winning bidder receives the same, uniform clearing price, encouraging bidders to bid their true valuation.

The Bid-to-Cover Ratio and Tail

Two statistics summarize how an auction went and are watched closely by the market immediately after results are released.

Definition — Bid-to-Cover and Tail

The bid-to-cover ratio is the total quantity bid divided by the quantity actually issued: $$\text{Bid-to-Cover} = \frac{\text{Total bids submitted}}{\text{Amount issued}}$$ A high ratio (e.g. above 2.5) signals strong demand; a low ratio signals weak demand and is often followed by underperformance of the new issue in secondary trading ("tailing").

The tail is the difference between the stop-out yield and the yield implied by the when-issued market just before the auction (the "pre-auction" level): $$\text{Tail} = y^* - y_{\text{WI}}$$ A positive tail (auction clears at a higher yield / lower price than the market expected) is read as a sign of weak demand.

When-Issued (WI) Trading

Between the announcement of an auction and its settlement, the security trades on a when-issued (WI) or "grey market" basis — investors agree to buy or sell the security for forward settlement on the issue date, even though it has not yet been issued. WI trading performs two important functions:

Remark — Roles of the WI Market

First, it provides price discovery: the WI yield just before the auction is the market's best estimate of where the auction will clear, and the auction "tail" is measured relative to it. Second, it allows dealers and investors to pre-position — a dealer who expects to win a large allocation can sell WI to lock in a distribution price before the auction even occurs, transferring inventory risk forward in time.

Because the WI security does not yet exist, WI trades are effectively short sales that must be covered either by the auction allocation itself or by borrowing the security in the repo market (Section 3.2) until the new issue settles.

Corporate Bond Issuance and Syndication

Corporate issuers do not typically use auctions. Instead, a new bond is brought to market through a syndicate of underwriting banks (bookrunners) who manage the process on the issuer's behalf.

Remark — The Syndication Process

The process typically follows these stages:

1. Mandate and price talk: The issuer mandates one or more banks as bookrunners. The banks gauge investor appetite and circulate initial "price talk" — an indicative spread range over a benchmark (e.g. government bond yield or swap rate) — often set deliberately wide to attract orders.

2. Bookbuilding: Investors submit indications of interest (IOIs) specifying the size they would buy at given spread levels. As the order book builds, the bookrunners tighten price talk — moving the spread guidance lower as demand proves strong, a process often called "tightening through the talk."

3. Pricing and allocation: Once the book is sufficiently oversubscribed, the final spread and size are fixed, and bonds are allocated to investors — typically with some discretion by the bookrunners to favor accounts likely to hold the bonds long-term ("real money") over those likely to flip them immediately. A new issue that prices with a new issue concession (a small yield pickup versus where the issuer's existing curve trades) often tightens in secondary trading shortly after — a pattern relative value desks try to anticipate.
