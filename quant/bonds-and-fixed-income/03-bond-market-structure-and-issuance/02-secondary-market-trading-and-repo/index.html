3.2 Secondary Market Trading and Repo

Once issued, a bond's life in the secondary market is supported by two interlocking pieces of infrastructure: the venues and counterparties through which it is bought and sold, and the repo market through which it is financed. A trader who wants to hold a bond position rarely funds it entirely with cash - instead the bond itself is used as collateral to borrow the cash needed to buy it, and the cost of that financing is a first-order input to any relative value trade.

Secondary Market Structure

Government and corporate bonds trade predominantly in dealer (over-the-counter) markets rather than on centralized exchanges like equities. A small number of dealer banks make markets in each security, quoting bid and offer prices to clients and to each other.

Remark - Dealer Markets vs Electronic Platforms

In the traditional model, an investor wanting to trade calls (or messages) one or more dealers for a quote ("request for quote", RFQ) and trades bilaterally at the agreed price. Dealers hold inventory, taking the other side of client trades and managing the resulting risk on their own book - this is why dealer balance sheet capacity is itself a scarce resource that affects liquidity, especially for less-liquid (off-the-run) bonds.

Electronic trading platforms (e.g. multi-dealer RFQ systems, and for the most liquid government bonds, central limit order books similar to equity exchanges) have grown rapidly, particularly for the most recently issued, most liquid securities ("on-the-run" bonds). On-the-run bonds typically trade with tighter bid-offer spreads and deeper electronic liquidity than "off-the-run" bonds of similar maturity, even though their fundamental cashflows may be nearly identical - a liquidity premium that itself becomes a relative value opportunity (Chapter 4).

Repurchase Agreements (Repo)

A repurchase agreement (repo) is a transaction in which one party sells a security to another party with an agreement to repurchase it at a specified future date and price. Economically, it is a collateralized loan: the seller obtains cash and pays interest (the repo rate), while the buyer earns that interest and holds the security as collateral.

Definition - Repo Mechanics

In a repo with start date $t_0$, end date $t_1$, security market value (price) $P$, and repo rate $r$, the cash exchanged is:

At $t_0$: the cash lender pays $P$ (often with a small haircut, i.e. lends slightly less than $P$) and receives the security as collateral.

At $t_1$: the cash borrower repays $P\left(1 + r \cdot \dfrac{t_1-t_0}{360}\right)$ and receives the security back. The difference is the repo interest, $P \cdot r \cdot \dfrac{t_1-t_0}{360}$.

General Collateral vs Special Repo

Repo rates are quoted for two broad categories of transaction, and the gap between them is a key signal about supply and demand for an individual security.

Definition - General Collateral (GC) and Special Repo

General collateral (GC) repo allows the cash borrower to deliver any security from an acceptable, broadly-defined basket (e.g. any on-the-run Treasury of a similar maturity bucket). The GC rate trades close to the prevailing short-term policy/money-market rate, since the collateral itself is interchangeable and not the object of demand.

Special repo occurs when a specific security is in high demand - typically because many market participants need to borrow that exact bond (e.g. to cover a short sale, or because it is cheapest-to-deliver into a futures contract). A bond "on special" trades at a repo rate below GC: the holder of the bond can lend it out and earn a below-market borrowing rate on the cash they receive, i.e. effectively earn extra yield by lending the bond rather than the cash. The gap $r_{GC} - r_{\text{special}} > 0$ is the "specialness."

Remark - Why a Bond Goes Special

A bond typically goes special around its auction date (heavy short-covering demand from dealers who sold it forward in the when-issued market, Section 3.1) and when it is the cheapest-to-deliver bond into a bond futures contract near delivery. Specialness directly affects relative value: a bond that is persistently special will tend to trade at a richer (lower yield) price than its curve-implied fair value, because holding it generates additional repo income.

Worked Example - Financing a Bond Position with Repo

Example - Repo Carry on a Levered Bond Position

A trader buys $\$10{,}000{,}000$ face value of a bond at a (dirty) price of $P = 99.50$ (i.e. $\$9{,}950{,}000$) and finances the purchase via a 1-month repo at $r_{GC} = 4.20\%$, with a $2\%$ haircut (the trader must post $2\%$ of the position in cash, borrowing the remaining $98\%$).

Cash borrowed: $0.98 \times \$9{,}950{,}000 = \$9{,}751{,}000$.

Repo interest for 30 days: $\$9{,}751{,}000 \times 4.20\% \times \dfrac{30}{360} = \$34{,}128.50$.

Suppose the bond's coupon accrues at an annualized rate equivalent to $\$9{,}950{,}000 \times 5.00\% \times \dfrac{30}{360} = \$41{,}458.33$ over the same 30 days.

The net carry on the position - coupon income minus financing cost - is $\$41{,}458.33 - \$34{,}128.50 = \$7{,}329.83$ for the month, before any price change in the bond itself. If the bond subsequently goes special and the trader can lend it out at, say, $r_{\text{special}} = 1.50\%$ instead of paying GC, the financing cost falls to $\$9{,}751{,}000 \times 1.50\% \times \dfrac{30}{360} = \$12{,}188.75$, and the net carry rises to $\$41{,}458.33 - \$12{,}188.75 = \$29{,}269.58$.

This illustrates why repo specialness is not a curiosity but a direct, quantifiable contributor to a position's profitability - and why relative value traders model financing costs explicitly rather than assuming a generic funding rate, a theme that recurs throughout Chapter 4.
