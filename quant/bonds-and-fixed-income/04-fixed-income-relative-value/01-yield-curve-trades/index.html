4.1 Yield Curve Trades

A yield curve trade takes a view on the shape of the yield curve - how yields at different maturities move relative to each other - while remaining largely neutral to the overall level of rates. By combining a long position in one maturity with a short position in another, sized so that the net exposure to a parallel shift is approximately zero, a trader isolates exposure to changes in the curve's slope or curvature.

Steepeners and Flatteners

The simplest curve trade involves two points on the curve: a short maturity and a long maturity.

Definition - Steepener and Flattener

A steepener is a trade that profits when the spread between a long-maturity yield $y_L$ and a short-maturity yield $y_S$ widens - i.e. when $y_L - y_S$ increases (the curve becomes more steeply upward-sloping, or less inverted). It is implemented by being long the short-maturity bond and short the long-maturity bond, in duration-weighted amounts.

A flattener is the opposite: it profits when $y_L - y_S$ narrows, implemented by being short the short-maturity bond and long the long-maturity bond, again duration-weighted.

Duration-Neutral Weighting

For a curve trade to express a pure view on slope rather than on the overall level of rates, the legs must be sized so that the portfolio's net DV01 is (approximately) zero - a parallel shift in yields produces no first-order P&L.

Definition - DV01-Neutral Curve Trade

For a two-leg trade with face amounts $N_S$ (short maturity, DV01 per unit $\text{DV01}_S$) and $N_L$ (long maturity, DV01 per unit $\text{DV01}_L$), DV01-neutrality requires: $$N_S \cdot \text{DV01}_S = N_L \cdot \text{DV01}_L$$ so that the hedge ratio is: $$\frac{N_L}{N_S} = \frac{\text{DV01}_S}{\text{DV01}_L}$$ Since long-maturity bonds have higher DV01 per unit face value, a duration-neutral curve trade requires a smaller face amount of the long-maturity bond than the short-maturity bond.

Remark - P&L of a DV01-Neutral Steepener

With the hedge ratio above, a parallel shift $\Delta y$ in both yields produces approximately zero net P&L, because the dollar gain on one leg offsets the dollar loss on the other. If instead the curve steepens - $y_L$ rises by more than $y_S$ (or falls by less) - the short leg in the long-maturity bond gains more than the long leg in the short-maturity bond loses, producing a net profit proportional to $\text{DV01}_S \times \Delta(y_L - y_S)$ (using the short leg's DV01 as the common scaling, since the position is sized in those units).

Butterfly Trades

A butterfly trade extends the curve trade to three points on the curve - a short, a middle ("belly"), and a long maturity - and takes a view on the curve's curvature rather than its slope.

Definition - Butterfly Spread

The butterfly spread is defined as: $$\text{Fly} = 2y_M - y_S - y_L$$ where $y_S, y_M, y_L$ are the yields at the short, middle, and long maturities. A long belly (or "long the butterfly") position profits when $\text{Fly}$ decreases - the middle of the curve richens (its yield falls) relative to the wings - and is implemented by being long the belly and short the wings, with weights chosen so the trade is DV01-neutral against both a parallel shift and a change in slope.

Remark - Why Trade Butterflies

A butterfly is doubly hedged: properly weighted, it has near-zero exposure to both the level and the slope of the curve, leaving exposure only to curvature - changes in how "bowed" the curve is. This makes butterflies attractive when a trader believes a specific maturity is mispriced relative to its neighbors (e.g. due to a large issuance concentrated at that point, Section 3.1) without taking a view on the broader direction or steepness of the curve.

Worked Example - Constructing a DV01-Neutral Steepener

Example - 2s10s Steepener

A trader believes the curve will steepen between the 2-year and 10-year points (a "2s10s steepener"). The 2-year bond has $\text{DV01}_2 = \$0.018$ per $\$100$ face value; the 10-year bond has $\text{DV01}_{10} = \$0.085$ per $\$100$ face value.

The trade is: short $\$100\text{mm}$ face of the 10-year bond, and long enough of the 2-year bond to match its DV01. The hedge ratio is: $$\frac{N_2}{N_{10}} = \frac{\text{DV01}_{10}}{\text{DV01}_2} = \frac{0.085}{0.018} \approx 4.72$$ So the trader buys $\$100\text{mm} \times 4.72 \approx \$472\text{mm}$ face value of the 2-year bond against the $\$100\text{mm}$ short in the 10-year bond.

Check: 2-year leg DV01 $= \$472\text{mm} \times 0.018/100 = \$84{,}960$. 10-year leg DV01 $= \$100\text{mm} \times 0.085/100 = \$85{,}000$. The two are approximately equal (small residual due to rounding the hedge ratio), so a parallel 1bp move in both yields produces close to zero net P&L.

If instead the 2-year yield falls by 5bp while the 10-year yield is unchanged (curve steepens by 5bp), the long 2-year leg gains $\$84{,}960 \times 5 \approx \$424{,}800$, while the short 10-year leg has no P&L - a net gain of approximately $\$424{,}800$ from the steepening, with negligible exposure to the parallel level of rates.
