Rates Fundamentals

Interest rates are the price of time - the compensation required for deferring consumption or lending capital. These notes develop the mathematical objects that all of fixed income builds upon: discount factors, spot rates, and forward rates, and the practical machinery used to construct a yield curve from observable market instruments.

We begin with the foundational objects: discount factors, spot rates, and forward rates. These three representations of the yield curve are mathematically equivalent but each illuminates different aspects of the market. The discount factor $P(0,T)$ is the present value of one unit of currency paid at $T$; the spot rate $r(0,T)$ is the yield implied by that discount factor; and the instantaneous forward rate $f(0,T)$ is the marginal cost of borrowing at $T$ as seen from today.

The second chapter covers yield curve construction - bootstrapping from observable market instruments and interpolation between pillar dates - the practical machinery that turns quoted swap rates and deposit rates into a continuous discount curve.

The third chapter turns to curve risk: how a curve's sensitivity to market rates is measured and hedged via DV01 and key rate durations. The fourth chapter addresses practical issues that arise when building curves in production - calibration algorithms and the turn-of-year and seasonality effects that real curve-building systems must account for. Together these four chapters provide the indispensable foundation for everything that follows in interest rate swaps, options, and term structure modelling.
