2.2 Interpolation Methods

Bootstrapping (Section 2.1) determines discount factors only at a discrete set of pillar dates — the maturities of the input instruments. To price a cash flow on any other date, and to compute forward rates between pillars, the curve must be interpolated. The choice of interpolation scheme is far from a cosmetic detail: it determines the shape of the forward rate curve, which directly affects the pricing of any instrument with cash flows between pillars.

Definition — Interpolation on a Curve

Given pillar dates $T_0 < T_1 < \cdots < T_n$ with known discount factors $P(0,T_i)$, an interpolation scheme defines a continuous (and ideally differentiable) curve $T \mapsto P(0,T)$ for $T \in (T_{i-1}, T_i)$. Equivalently, since $P$, the spot rate $r(0,T) = -\ln P(0,T)/T$, and instantaneous forward rates $f(0,T) = -\partial_T \ln P(0,T)$ are related bijectively, interpolation may be applied to any of these representations — and the choice of which quantity is interpolated linearly materially changes the result.

Linear Interpolation: Rates vs. Log-Discount Factors vs. Forward Rates

The simplest schemes are piecewise linear, but "linear" must specify linear in what. Three common choices give materially different curves between pillars.

Remark — Three Linear Schemes

Linear in zero rates. Interpolate $r(0,T)$ linearly between $r(0,T_{i-1})$ and $r(0,T_i)$: $$r(0,T) = r(0,T_{i-1}) + \frac{T - T_{i-1}}{T_i - T_{i-1}}\left(r(0,T_i) - r(0,T_{i-1})\right)$$ then recover $P(0,T) = e^{-r(0,T)T}$. Simple and intuitive, but produces discontinuous (jumpy) instantaneous forward rates at pillar dates.

Linear in log-discount factors (log-linear). Interpolate $\ln P(0,T)$ linearly: $$\ln P(0,T) = \ln P(0,T_{i-1}) + \frac{T-T_{i-1}}{T_i-T_{i-1}}\left(\ln P(0,T_i) - \ln P(0,T_{i-1})\right)$$ This is equivalent to assuming a constant instantaneous forward rate within each pillar interval — the forward curve becomes a step function. Widely used because it guarantees $P(0,T) > 0$ and is simple, but produces a discontinuous (piecewise constant) forward curve.

Linear in (instantaneous or simple) forward rates. Interpolate the forward rate $f(0,T)$ directly and linearly between pillars, then integrate to obtain $\ln P(0,T) = -\int_0^T f(0,s)\,ds$. This produces a continuous forward curve (piecewise linear), at the cost of a slightly more involved discount factor formula (piecewise quadratic in $\ln P$).

Example — Log-Linear Implies Flat Forwards

Suppose $P(0,1) = 0.96$ and $P(0,2) = 0.91$. Under log-linear interpolation, for $T \in (1,2)$: $$\ln P(0,T) = \ln(0.96) + (T-1)\big[\ln(0.91)-\ln(0.96)\big]$$ The instantaneous forward rate $f(0,T) = -\partial_T \ln P(0,T) = \ln(0.96) - \ln(0.91) = \ln(0.96/0.91) \approx 5.35\%$ is constant over $(1,2)$ — a step in the forward curve at $T=1$ and $T=2$.

By contrast, linear-in-zero-rates interpolation would give a forward rate that varies continuously but jumps in slope at each pillar — neither scheme produces a smooth forward curve.

Cubic Spline Methods

To obtain a smooth (continuously differentiable, or even twice-differentiable) curve, cubic splines fit a piecewise cubic polynomial through the pillar points, with continuity conditions imposed on the function value, first derivative, and second derivative at each pillar.

Definition — Natural Cubic Spline

A cubic spline on $\{T_0,\ldots,T_n\}$ is a function $g(T)$ that is a cubic polynomial $g_i(T)$ on each interval $[T_{i-1},T_i]$, satisfies $g_i(T_i) = g_{i+1}(T_i)$ (continuity), $g_i'(T_i) = g_{i+1}'(T_i)$ (smooth first derivative), and $g_i''(T_i)=g_{i+1}''(T_i)$ (smooth second derivative) at every interior pillar. The natural spline additionally imposes $g''(T_0) = g''(T_n) = 0$ at the endpoints. Applied to $\ln P(0,T)$ or to zero rates, the spline produces a smooth, twice-differentiable curve and hence a continuous (though not necessarily monotone or well-behaved) forward curve.

Remark — The Problem with Splines for Rates

Cubic splines are global: a change in one input quote can perturb the curve — and therefore forward rates — far away from that pillar, which is undesirable for hedging (a trader hedging the 5-year point should not see their 30-year forward rate exposure change). Splines can also produce oscillations ("wiggles") between widely-spaced pillars, and worse, can imply negative forward rates even when all input par rates are positive and increasing — an arbitrage-free curve should not exhibit this.

Monotone Convex Interpolation (Hagan-West)

The Hagan-West monotone convex method, introduced specifically for interest rate curves, was designed to address the shortcomings above: it produces a continuous forward curve, is local (a change at one pillar affects only neighbouring segments), and — crucially — guarantees that the forward curve does not introduce spurious negative rates or oscillations beyond what the input data implies.

Definition — Monotone Convex Interpolation (Hagan-West)

The Hagan-West method interpolates the instantaneous forward rate $f(0,T)$ as a piecewise quadratic function of $T$ within each pillar interval, with the quadratic coefficients on interval $(T_{i-1},T_i)$ chosen so that: (i) the average forward rate over the interval equals the discrete forward rate implied by $P(0,T_{i-1})$ and $P(0,T_i)$ (consistency with bootstrapped discount factors); (ii) the forward curve is continuous at pillar boundaries; and (iii) within each interval, the quadratic is constrained — via a case-by-case adjustment of its coefficients — to be either monotone or to have a single extremum of controlled sign, preventing the wide oscillations that an unconstrained quadratic or cubic spline can produce.

Remark — Why Monotone Convex Became Standard

Monotone convex interpolation is now the de facto standard for building rates curves at major dealers and in open-source quant libraries (e.g. QuantLib). It achieves a forward curve that is continuous and visually smooth like a spline, while remaining local (good hedging properties) and shape-preserving — if the discrete forward rates implied by the pillars are positive and increasing, the interpolated continuous forward curve will not dip negative or overshoot between pillars. This combination — smoothness, locality, positivity, and exact repricing of inputs — is difficult to achieve with classical spline or polynomial methods.

Trade-offs: Smoothness, Locality, and Forward Rate Stability

Choosing an interpolation method is an exercise in balancing competing, and partially incompatible, desiderata.

Remark — Comparing the Methods
MethodForward curveLocal?Arbitrage-free forwards?
Linear zero ratesDiscontinuous, jumpyYesNot guaranteed
Log-linear discount factorsPiecewise constant (step function)YesYes (if positive)
Natural cubic splineSmooth but globalNoNot guaranteed (can go negative)
Monotone convex (Hagan-West)Continuous, smooth, shape-preservingYesYes

Log-linear interpolation remains popular for its simplicity and arbitrage-free property despite its unrealistic step-function forward curve — many short-dated instruments are insensitive to this. Where the shape of the forward curve matters directly — e.g. pricing instruments with cash flows densely spaced between pillars, or risk-managing forward-starting structures — monotone convex (or another shape-preserving local method) is preferred. The choice of interpolation scheme is itself a modelling assumption that should be disclosed and consistently applied across the discounting and projection curves of the multi-curve framework.
