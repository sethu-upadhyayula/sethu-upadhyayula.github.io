1.1 Discount Factors and Spot Rates

The central question of fixed income is: what is a future cash flow worth today? The answer is given by the discount factor — the present value of one unit of currency to be received at a specified future date. Discount factors are the primitive objects of interest rate theory; all other rates are derived from them.

Definition — Discount Factor

The discount factor $P(t, T)$ is the value at time $t$ of a contract that pays one unit of currency at time $T \geq t$ with certainty. In particular, $P(t,t) = 1$ for all $t$. The function $T \mapsto P(0, T)$ is called the discount curve or term structure of discount factors. It must be a positive, decreasing function with $P(0,0) = 1$ and $P(0,\infty) = 0$ (assuming positive interest rates).

A zero-coupon bond is the traded instrument that directly realises the discount factor: a zero-coupon bond maturing at $T$ with face value 1 has price $P(0,T)$ today. In practice, pure zero-coupon bonds are not traded at all maturities, so discount factors are inferred by stripping (bootstrapping) from prices of coupon-bearing instruments — a process we address in Chapter 2.

Compounding Conventions

Given the discount factor $P(0,T)$, the associated interest rate depends on the compounding convention chosen. Different conventions are standard in different markets and for different instruments, so it is essential to be fluent in all of them.

Definition — Compounding Conventions

Continuous compounding. The continuously compounded spot rate $r(0,T)$ satisfies: $$P(0,T) = e^{-r(0,T)\cdot T} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad r(0,T) = -\frac{\ln P(0,T)}{T}$$ This is the most natural convention for theoretical work as it simplifies many formulas.

Annual compounding. The annually compounded spot rate $y(0,T)$ satisfies: $$P(0,T) = \frac{1}{(1+y(0,T))^T} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y(0,T) = P(0,T)^{-1/T} - 1$$

Simple (linear) compounding. The simply compounded rate $L(0,T)$ satisfies: $$P(0,T) = \frac{1}{1 + L(0,T) \cdot T}$$ Simple compounding is standard for short maturities (money market instruments, LIBOR, SOFR term rates) where $T \leq 1$ year.

$m$-times per year compounding. At frequency $m$: $$P(0,T) = \left(1 + \frac{r_m(0,T)}{m}\right)^{-mT}$$ Semi-annual ($m=2$) compounding is standard for US Treasury yields and US dollar swap rates quoted as bond yields.

Remark — Equivalence of Conventions

All conventions encode the same information. Given a discount factor $P(0,T)$, one can convert freely between conventions. For small rates and short maturities all conventions give nearly identical results. The differences become significant at longer maturities or higher rates. In quantitative work, continuous compounding is usually preferred for its analytic convenience; practitioners in specific markets use the relevant market convention for quoting and communication.

The Spot Rate Curve

The spot rate curve (or zero curve) is the function $T \mapsto r(0,T)$ — the continuously compounded yield on a zero-coupon bond of each maturity. It encodes the market's current pricing of all future cash flows.

Definition — Present Value of a Cash Flow Stream

The present value of a deterministic cash flow stream $(C_1, t_1), \ldots, (C_n, t_n)$ is: $$PV = \sum_{i=1}^n C_i \cdot P(0, t_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n C_i \cdot e^{-r(0,t_i)\,t_i}$$ Each cash flow is discounted at its own maturity-specific rate, not a single flat rate. Using a flat yield — as in the yield-to-maturity of a coupon bond — is an approximation that conflates rates at different maturities.

Par Rate

The par rate for maturity $T$ is the fixed coupon rate that makes a fixed-coupon bond (paying coupons at times $t_1, \ldots, t_n = T$ with face value 1) worth exactly par (price = 1) today.

Definition — Par Rate

Let $\delta_i = t_i - t_{i-1}$ be the day count fraction for coupon period $i$. The par rate $c$ satisfies: $$1 = c \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i P(0, t_i) + P(0, t_n)$$ Solving: $$c = \frac{1 - P(0,t_n)}{\sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i P(0,t_i)}$$ The denominator $A = \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_i P(0,t_i)$ is called the annuity factor or DV01 per unit notional. Par rates are the market-quoted rates for vanilla interest rate swaps.

Example — Par Rate from Discount Factors

Suppose we observe the following discount factors for annual maturities:

$T$ (years)$P(0,T)$$r(0,T)$ (cont.)
10.95244.88%
20.90574.93%
30.86384.96%

The 3-year annual par rate is: $$c = \frac{1 - 0.8638}{0.9524 + 0.9057 + 0.8638} = \frac{0.1362}{2.7219} = 5.00\%$$

The par rate (5.00%) lies slightly above the 3-year spot rate (4.96%) because the coupon payments in years 1 and 2 are discounted at lower rates, pulling the effective yield up marginally.

Day Count Conventions in Rates Markets

Interest accrual in rates markets depends on day count conventions, which determine the year fraction $\delta$ between two dates. The conventions differ by instrument and currency.

Remark — Common Rates Day Count Conventions

Act/360: standard for USD money market instruments, SOFR, Euribor, and most floating rate legs. The year fraction between dates $d_1$ and $d_2$ is (actual calendar days) / 360.

Act/365 Fixed: standard for GBP SONIA and sterling money markets. Year fraction = (actual days) / 365.

30/360 (Bond Basis): standard for the fixed legs of USD interest rate swaps and US corporate bonds. Assumes each month has 30 days and each year has 360 days.

Act/Act (ISDA): used for US Treasury bonds and German Bunds. The year fraction correctly accounts for leap years by splitting periods that span a year boundary.
