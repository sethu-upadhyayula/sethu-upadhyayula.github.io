1.2 Forward Rates

A forward rate is an interest rate, agreed today, that applies to a future period. While the spot rate $r(0,T)$ describes the rate for borrowing or lending from today until $T$, a forward rate describes the rate for a loan that begins at some future date $t_1 > 0$ and ends at a later date $t_2 > t_1$. Forward rates are not predictions of future spot rates - they are rates that can be locked in today using only instruments observable today, via a replication (no-arbitrage) argument.

Defining the Forward Rate

Consider an investor who, at time $0$, wants to guarantee a fixed rate of return on money invested between times $t_1$ and $t_2$. This can be achieved by combining two zero-coupon bond positions: buy one unit of the $t_1$-maturity zero-coupon bond's worth of the $t_2$-maturity bond... more precisely, the replication is: short $P(0,t_2)/P(0,t_1)$ units of the $t_1$-bond's notional position and go long the $t_2$-bond, structured so that the net cash flow at time $0$ is zero. This produces a known cash outflow of $1$ at $t_1$ and a known cash inflow of $P(0,t_1)/P(0,t_2)$ at $t_2$ - a forward loan.

Definition - Simple Forward Rate

The simply-compounded forward rate $f(t_1, t_2)$, set at time $0$ for the period $[t_1, t_2]$, is the rate such that investing $1$ unit at $t_1$ and receiving $1 + f(t_1,t_2)(t_2 - t_1)$ at $t_2$ has the same value as the zero-coupon replication above. By no-arbitrage: $$\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)} = 1 + f(t_1,t_2)(t_2-t_1)$$ so that $$f(t_1,t_2) = \frac{1}{t_2-t_1}\left(\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)} - 1\right)$$ When $t_1 = 0$, this reduces to the spot simple rate $L(0,t_2)$, consistent with the definition of discount factors under simple compounding.

Theorem - Continuously Compounded Forward Rate

Under continuous compounding, the forward rate $f(t_1,t_2)$ for the period $[t_1,t_2]$ satisfies: $$P(0,t_2) = P(0,t_1)\, e^{-f(t_1,t_2)(t_2-t_1)}$$ so that $$f(t_1,t_2) = \frac{\ln P(0,t_1) - \ln P(0,t_2)}{t_2-t_1} = \frac{r(0,t_2)\,t_2 - r(0,t_1)\,t_1}{t_2-t_1}$$ The continuously compounded forward rate is the slope of the log-discount curve over $[t_1,t_2]$, and equivalently a weighted difference of the spot rates at $t_1$ and $t_2$.

Proof

Substitute $P(0,t_1) = e^{-r(0,t_1)t_1}$ and $P(0,t_2) = e^{-r(0,t_2)t_2}$ into $f(t_1,t_2) = \frac{\ln P(0,t_1)-\ln P(0,t_2)}{t_2-t_1}$: $$f(t_1,t_2) = \frac{-r(0,t_1)t_1 - (-r(0,t_2)t_2)}{t_2-t_1} = \frac{r(0,t_2)t_2 - r(0,t_1)t_1}{t_2-t_1}.\qquad\blacksquare$$

The Instantaneous Forward Rate

Letting the forward period shrink to zero, $t_2 \to t_1$, the forward rate converges to the instantaneous forward rate - the annualised rate, agreed today, for an infinitesimally short loan starting at $t_1$. This is one of the most fundamental objects in interest rate modelling, since the entire discount curve can be recovered by integrating it.

Definition - Instantaneous Forward Rate

The instantaneous forward rate at time $t$, observed at time $0$, is: $$f(t) = \lim_{\Delta \to 0} f(t, t+\Delta) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\ln P(0,T)\bigg|_{T=t} = -\frac{\partial_T P(0,T)}{P(0,T)}\bigg|_{T=t}$$ Conversely, the discount curve can be recovered from the instantaneous forward curve by: $$P(0,T) = \exp\left(-\int_0^T f(u)\,du\right)$$ and the spot rate is the average of instantaneous forwards over $[0,T]$: $$r(0,T) = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f(u)\,du$$

Remark - Spot, Forward, and Instantaneous Forward Curves

The spot rate curve is a moving average of the instantaneous forward curve, which is why the spot curve is always smoother than the forward curve. When the spot curve is upward-sloping (normal), the instantaneous forward curve lies above the spot curve; when the spot curve is downward-sloping (inverted), the forward curve lies below it. The forward curve crosses the spot curve at the point where the spot curve is locally flat.

Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs)

A Forward Rate Agreement is the over-the-counter instrument that directly trades forward rates. It is the building block from which the no-arbitrage forward rate formula derives its market relevance.

Definition - Forward Rate Agreement

An FRA with notional $N$, fixing date $t_1$, payment/maturity date $t_2$, and contract rate $K$ pays, at time $t_2$ (or, more commonly in practice, discounted at $t_1$): $$N \cdot (L(t_1,t_2) - K)\cdot(t_2-t_1)$$ where $L(t_1,t_2)$ is the realised simply-compounded reference rate (e.g. SOFR or term IBOR) observed at $t_1$ for the period $[t_1,t_2]$. The FRA's fair or par rate, set at inception so the contract has zero value, is exactly the no-arbitrage forward rate: $$K^* = f(t_1,t_2) = \frac{1}{t_2-t_1}\left(\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)}-1\right)$$

Proof - Why the Par FRA Rate is the Forward Rate

Consider the static replication: at time $0$, buy $\frac{1}{P(0,t_2)}$ units of the $t_2$-bond's worth of zero-coupon bonds maturing at $t_1$ for cost $\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)}$, financed by issuing one zero-coupon bond maturing at $t_2$ (raising $P(0,t_2)$, costing $1$ at $t_2$). At $t_1$, the $t_1$-bonds pay $\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)}$, which is reinvested at the realised rate $L(t_1,t_2)$ to $t_2$, yielding $\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)}\left(1 + L(t_1,t_2)(t_2-t_1)\right)$ at $t_2$. The net payoff at $t_2$ is this amount minus the $1$ owed on the issued bond. If $K = f(t_1,t_2)$ as defined above, $\frac{P(0,t_1)}{P(0,t_2)} = 1 + K(t_2-t_1)$, so the strategy was set up at zero net cost at time $0$ and produces a payoff of $(L(t_1,t_2)-K)(t_2-t_1)$ at $t_2$ - exactly the FRA payoff. Since the replication costs nothing today, no-arbitrage requires the fair FRA rate to equal $f(t_1,t_2)$. $\blacksquare$

No-Arbitrage Relationship: Spot, Forward, Discount Factors

Spot rates, forward rates, and discount factors are three equivalent representations of the same information - the term structure - connected by no-arbitrage identities. Given any one, the other two are determined.

Theorem - Equivalence of Representations

For $0 < t_1 < t_2$, the following identities hold and are mutually consistent: $$P(0,t_2) = P(0,t_1)\cdot \frac{1}{1+f(t_1,t_2)(t_2-t_1)} = P(0,t_1)\,e^{-f(t_1,t_2)(t_2-t_1)}$$ $$r(0,t_2) = \frac{r(0,t_1)t_1 + f(t_1,t_2)(t_2-t_1)}{t_2}$$ The second identity shows the spot rate to $t_2$ is a time-weighted average of the spot rate to $t_1$ and the forward rate over $[t_1,t_2]$. Chaining this relationship across consecutive periods $[0,t_1], [t_1,t_2], \ldots, [t_{n-1},t_n]$ allows the entire discount curve to be reconstructed from a sequence of forward rates and an initial discount factor - the basis of curve bootstrapping (Chapter 2).

Worked Example

Example - Computing a Forward Rate from Discount Factors

Suppose the 1-year and 2-year discount factors are $P(0,1) = 0.9524$ and $P(0,2) = 0.9057$.

Simple forward rate for the period $[1,2]$: $$f(1,2) = \frac{1}{2-1}\left(\frac{0.9524}{0.9057}-1\right) = \frac{0.9524}{0.9057}-1 = 1.05156 - 1 = 5.156\%$$

Continuously compounded forward rate for the same period: $$f(1,2) = \frac{\ln(0.9524) - \ln(0.9057)}{1} = (-0.04875) - (-0.09907) = 5.03\%$$

As expected, the simple and continuous forward rates are close but not identical - the simple rate is always slightly higher than the continuous rate for positive rates, since $\ln(1+x) < x$. An FRA on the 1-year-into-2-year period (a "$1\times2$ FRA") would be quoted at a fair rate close to $5.16\%$ on an Act/360 simple basis, consistent with the simple forward rate computed above (subject to day-count adjustments).
