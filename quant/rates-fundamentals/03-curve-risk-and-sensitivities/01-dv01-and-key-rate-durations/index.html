3.1 DV01 and Key Rate Durations

The price of any rates instrument is a function of the discount curve, $PV = PV(P(0,T_1), \ldots, P(0,T_n))$, or equivalently of the underlying zero or par rates. DV01 ("dollar value of a basis point", also called PV01 or BPV) measures how much that present value changes for a one basis point ($0.0001$, or $1\text{bp}$) move in rates. It is the single most widely used risk number in fixed income, because it converts an abstract pricing function into a single dollar figure that can be compared, aggregated, and hedged across instruments.

Definition - DV01 (Parallel)

The (parallel) DV01 of a position with present value $PV(r)$, where $r$ denotes a uniform shift applied to every point of the zero curve, is: $$\text{DV01} = -\frac{\partial PV}{\partial r} \times 0.0001 \approx -\big(PV(r+0.0001) - PV(r)\big)$$ The minus sign convention is chosen so that DV01 is positive for a position that loses value when rates rise (e.g. a fixed-rate bond holder, or the receiver of fixed in a swap). DV01 is typically quoted per unit notional or per the actual notional of the trade, in currency units (e.g. "$1,250 per bp").

For a fixed-rate bond or the fixed leg of a swap with cash flows $C_i$ at times $t_i$, the present value is $PV = \sum_i C_i P(0,t_i)$. Differentiating with respect to a parallel shift in continuously compounded zero rates, $P(0,t_i) = e^{-(r(0,t_i)+r)t_i}$, gives a clean closed form.

Theorem - DV01 of a Fixed Cash Flow Stream

For a fixed cash flow stream $\{(C_i, t_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ discounted at continuously compounded zero rates, $$\text{DV01} = 0.0001 \sum_{i=1}^n t_i\, C_i\, P(0,t_i)$$ Each cash flow contributes its present value weighted by its time to payment $t_i$ - longer-dated cash flows contribute disproportionately more to DV01 for the same present value, because their discount factor is more sensitive to a given change in rates.

Example - DV01 of a Receiver Swap

Consider a 2-year annual-pay receiver swap (receive fixed $4.50\%$, pay floating) on notional $N=100$, with $P(0,1)=0.96154$ and $P(0,2)=0.91553$ (the curve from Section 2.1). At inception the swap is at par, so its value is driven entirely by the fixed leg's annuity: $$A = \sum_{i=1}^2 \delta_i P(0,t_i) = 0.96154 + 0.91553 = 1.87707$$ The DV01 of the fixed leg (and hence of the swap, to first order) is approximately: $$\text{DV01} \approx 0.0001 \times N \times \big(t_1 P(0,t_1) + t_2 P(0,t_2)\big) = 0.0001 \times 100 \times (1\times 0.96154 + 2\times 0.91553)$$ $$= 0.0001 \times 100 \times 2.79260 = 0.027926 \approx \$0.0279 \text{ per } \$100 \text{ notional, per bp}$$

For a swap with $N = \$10{,}000{,}000$ notional this is approximately $\$2{,}793$ per basis point - meaning a 1bp parallel rise in rates moves the swap's value by roughly $\$2{,}793$ (a loss for the fixed-rate receiver).

Key Rate Durations and the Risk Ladder

A parallel DV01 answers "what happens if every rate moves by the same amount?" - but real curve moves are rarely parallel: the short end and long end of the curve often move by different amounts (steepening or flattening). Key rate durations (also called partial DV01s or bucketed DV01s) decompose the total sensitivity into contributions from each pillar of the curve.

Definition - Key Rate '01

Given pillar maturities $T_1 < T_2 < \cdots < T_n$ used in curve construction, the key rate '01 at pillar $T_k$ is the change in present value resulting from a 1bp bump applied only to the rate (or quote) at pillar $T_k$, holding all other pillar quotes fixed and re-interpolating the curve between pillars: $$\text{KR01}_k = -\big(PV(\text{curve with } T_k \text{ bumped by } 1\text{bp}) - PV(\text{base curve})\big)$$ The bump at $T_k$ is typically applied as a "tent" or "hat" function - full weight at $T_k$, decaying linearly to zero at the neighbouring pillars $T_{k-1}$ and $T_{k+1}$ - so that the bumps at all pillars sum exactly to a parallel 1bp shift everywhere.

Theorem - Key Rate '01s Sum to Parallel DV01

If the tent-function bumps at each pillar partition a parallel shift exactly (i.e. they sum to $1$ at every maturity), then to first order: $$\sum_{k=1}^n \text{KR01}_k \approx \text{DV01}$$ This decomposition is the basis of the risk ladder: a table showing the position's sensitivity to each individual point on the curve (e.g. 1m, 3m, 1y, 2y, 5y, 10y, 30y), whose row sum reproduces the parallel DV01.

Example - Risk Ladder for a Barbell vs. a Bullet

Two portfolios can have the same total DV01 but completely different risk ladders. A "bullet" position concentrated in a 5-year swap has nearly all of its KR01 in the 5y bucket. A "barbell" combining a 2-year and a 10-year swap, sized so the total DV01 matches the bullet, instead has KR01 split between the 2y and 10y buckets, with little or no 5y exposure.

Although both portfolios have identical parallel DV01 and would show zero P&L under a parallel shift hedge, they respond very differently to a steepening or flattening of the curve - the barbell gains (or loses) on a curve twist that the bullet is largely insensitive to. This is why parallel DV01 alone is an incomplete risk measure, and why trading desks monitor the full key rate ladder.

Convexity

DV01 and key rate durations are first-order (linear) sensitivities. For large rate moves, the second-order term - convexity - becomes significant, particularly for instruments with optionality (callable bonds, swaptions, mortgage-backed securities) or long-dated cash flows.

Definition - Convexity

The convexity of a position is the second derivative of present value with respect to a parallel rate shift: $$\text{Convexity} = \frac{\partial^2 PV}{\partial r^2}$$ A second-order Taylor approximation of the P&L from a parallel shift $\Delta r$ is: $$\Delta PV \approx -\text{DV01}\times \frac{\Delta r}{0.0001} + \frac{1}{2}\text{Convexity}\times(\Delta r)^2$$ Plain-vanilla fixed cash flow streams (bonds, vanilla swaps) have positive convexity - their value rises more for a given rate decrease than it falls for an equal rate increase - which is favourable to the holder and is itself priced into instruments with embedded optionality.
