3.2 Correlation Swaps and Products

While dispersion trades express a view on correlation indirectly through a basket of variance positions, correlation swaps and related products provide direct exposure to realized correlation. These instruments isolate the correlation component that is otherwise bundled together with volatility in dispersion trades, but they come with their own pricing and hedging challenges — most notably, the absence of a simple, model-free static replication strategy analogous to the one available for variance swaps.

Correlation Swap Payoff

Definition — Correlation Swap

A correlation swap on $k$ underlyings pays, at maturity $T$, the difference between a realized average pairwise correlation and a fixed strike correlation $K_\rho$, multiplied by a notional $N$: $$\text{Payoff} = N \times \left(\rho_{\text{realized}} - K_\rho\right)$$ where the realized correlation is typically computed as the equally-weighted average of pairwise realized correlations between the daily (or other periodic) log-returns of each pair of underlyings over $[0,T]$: $$\rho_{\text{realized}} = \frac{2}{k(k-1)}\sum_{i

Realized vs. Implied Correlation

Remark — Two Different Numbers

Realized correlation is a backward-looking statistical quantity computed directly from a historical (or, at maturity, the full) sample of returns, as in the formula above. It is observable only in arrears and is subject to sampling noise — over short windows or with few constituents, realized correlation estimates can be noisy even if the "true" underlying correlation structure is stable.

Implied correlation is forward-looking and is backed out from the prices of index and single-stock options (or variance swaps), as in the implied correlation formula of Section 3.1 — it represents the market's risk-neutral expectation (under the relevant measure) of average pairwise correlation over the relevant horizon, embedded in the relative pricing of index vs. single-stock volatility. Implied correlation is observable in real time (it is just a different way of expressing the index/single-stock volatility ratio) but is not a direct forecast of realized correlation — it includes a risk premium, analogous to the volatility risk premium, that has historically made implied correlation trade above subsequently realized correlation on average.

Remark — Why Correlation Swaps are Hard to Hedge

Unlike a variance swap, which can be statically replicated (up to discrete-monitoring and jump effects) by a log-contract position in vanilla options across all strikes — giving the seller a model-independent hedge — there is no equivalent static replication for a correlation swap. A correlation swap's payoff depends on the realized covariance structure across all pairs of $k$ assets, a $\binom{k}{2}$-dimensional object, whereas the instruments available to hedge it (index and single-stock variance swaps or options) provide only an aggregate, single-number view of correlation through the implied correlation relationship. As a result, correlation swap desks are typically left with substantial residual basis risk between the correlation swap payoff and whatever combination of index/single-stock variance positions they use as a proxy hedge — dispersion trades (Section 3.1) are, in effect, the closest liquid proxy hedge for a correlation swap, but the relationship between the two is only approximate.

Correlation Skew

Definition — Correlation Skew

Just as implied volatility varies with strike (the volatility smile/skew) for a single underlying, implied correlation backed out from basket or worst-of option prices varies with the strike (or moneyness) of the basket option used to infer it. This phenomenon is called correlation skew: implied correlation extracted from out-of-the-money basket puts is typically higher than implied correlation extracted from at-the-money or out-of-the-money basket calls.

Remark — Why Correlation Skew Exists

Downside correlation is structurally higher: in market sell-offs, individual stock returns become more correlated as systemic, market-wide factors dominate idiosyncratic factors — a phenomenon sometimes summarized as "correlations go to one in a crash." A basket put (which pays off in exactly these scenarios) is therefore more valuable than a simple constant-correlation model would suggest, because the relevant correlation in the down-scenarios that drive the put's payoff is higher than the correlation relevant for an at-the-money or upside scenario. Backing out a single implied correlation from the put price (as if correlation were constant) therefore yields a higher number for OTM puts than for OTM calls.

Consequence for worst-of and autocallable pricing: the worst-of structures discussed in Chapter 1 (autocallables) and Chapter 2 (rainbow options) have payoffs concentrated in downside scenarios — exactly where correlation skew implies the relevant correlation is highest. A desk pricing these structures off a single, constant correlation assumption (e.g., an average implied correlation from at-the-money basket options) will systematically misprice the embedded worst-of optionality, typically underestimating the value of the embedded downside put — and therefore underestimating the issuer's short-put risk on autocallable books. Sophisticated desks therefore use local correlation models, or stress the correlation assumption directly when reserving for worst-of risk, rather than relying on a single flat correlation number.
