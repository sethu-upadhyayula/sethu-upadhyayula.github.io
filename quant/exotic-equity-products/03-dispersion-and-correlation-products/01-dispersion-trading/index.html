3.1 Dispersion Trading

A dispersion trade is a relative-value position that exploits the relationship between an index's implied volatility and the implied volatilities of its individual constituent stocks. The trade is most commonly expressed as a short position in index volatility against a long position in single-stock volatility, sized so as to be approximately vega-neutral at inception. Its P&L is, to a first approximation, a direct bet on realized correlation between the index constituents.

The Variance Decomposition

Result - Index Variance as a Function of Constituent Variances and Correlation

For an index $I$ composed of $k$ constituents with weights $w_j$ (such that $I_t \approx \sum_j w_j S_t^{(j)}$ in return terms), the instantaneous variance of the index return relates to the constituent variances and pairwise correlations by: $$\sigma_I^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k}\sum_{j=1}^{k} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} = \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_j^2 \sigma_j^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij}$$ If all pairwise correlations are equal to a single value $\bar{\rho}$ (the simplifying assumption used to define a single "implied correlation" number), this becomes: $$\sigma_I^2 = \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_j^2\sigma_j^2 + \bar{\rho}\sum_{i\neq j} w_iw_j\sigma_i\sigma_j$$ Solving for $\bar{\rho}$ given an observed index volatility $\sigma_I$ and the constituent volatilities $\sigma_j$ defines the implied correlation - the single correlation value that reconciles index-level implied volatility with the weighted-average single-stock implied volatilities.

Constructing a Dispersion Trade

Definition - Classic Dispersion Trade

A dispersion trade consists of:

Short index variance: sell a variance swap (or a delta-hedged straddle/strangle position) on the index, taking in the index's implied variance.

Long single-stock variance: buy variance swaps (or delta-hedged straddles) on each of the index constituents, sized by $w_j^2$ (or, in practice, by vega-weighting so the position is vega-neutral at inception) - i.e., the position is long the weighted sum $\sum_j w_j^2 \sigma_{j,\text{realized}}^2$ of single-stock realized variances and short $\sigma_{I,\text{realized}}^2$ of index realized variance.

Relationship to Implied Correlation

Remark - Dispersion is a Short-Correlation Trade

Rearranging the variance decomposition, the cross-term involving correlation can be written as: $$\sum_{i\neq j} w_iw_j\sigma_i\sigma_j\rho_{ij} = \sigma_I^2 - \sum_{j} w_j^2\sigma_j^2$$ A short-index/long-single-stock dispersion position has a payoff that is, to leading order, proportional to $-\left(\sigma_{I,\text{realized}}^2 - \sum_j w_j^2 \sigma_{j,\text{realized}}^2\right)$, i.e., it profits when this cross-correlation term is smaller than what was priced in at inception - that is, when realized correlation comes in lower than implied correlation. The trade is therefore fundamentally a short position in implied correlation, expressed via the variance/volatility surface rather than via a direct correlation product.

Why dispersion is structurally attractive to sell-side desks: implied correlation has historically tended to trade above subsequently realized correlation on average (an analogue of the well-documented variance risk premium for single-asset implied vs. realized volatility), making the short-correlation dispersion trade a persistent (though not riskless) source of premium - compensation for the tail risk that correlation spikes sharply higher in a market-wide selloff, exactly when the short-index-variance leg of the trade is also losing money.

Risks of Dispersion Trades

Remark - Correlation Spikes and Crowding

Correlation regime risk: in market-wide stress events, correlations across single names tend to rise sharply toward $1$ (the "all correlations go to one" phenomenon in a crash) - precisely when a short-correlation dispersion position loses the most, since realized correlation jumps far above the implied level the trade was put on at. This makes dispersion trades behave like a short-volatility, short-tail-risk position overall, despite being vega-neutral at the index level.

Rebalancing and basis risk: maintaining vega-neutrality (or variance-notional neutrality) requires the relative weights between the index leg and the $k$ single-stock legs to be rebalanced as implied vols and correlations drift, and as the index composition itself changes (constituent additions, deletions, and weight changes). Dividend effects, single-stock corporate actions (M&A, spin-offs), and liquidity differences between index options (very liquid) and single-stock options (often much less liquid, especially for smaller index members) all introduce basis risk that a simplified "decomposition" framework ignores.

Worked Example - Dispersion Trade P&L

Example - Realized Correlation Below Implied

Consider a simplified index of $k=4$ equally-weighted stocks ($w_j = 0.25$), each with implied volatility $\sigma_j = 30\%$ at inception, and an index implied volatility of $\sigma_I = 22\%$. Using the equal-correlation formula with $w_j=0.25$ for all $j$: $$\sigma_I^2 = \sum_j w_j^2\sigma_j^2 + \bar\rho\sum_{i\neq j}w_iw_j\sigma_i\sigma_j$$ Here $\sum_j w_j^2\sigma_j^2 = 4\times(0.25)^2\times(0.30)^2 = 0.0225$, and $\sum_{i\neq j}w_iw_j\sigma_i\sigma_j = 12\times(0.25)^2\times(0.30)^2 = 0.0675$. Solving $0.22^2 = 0.0484 = 0.0225 + \bar\rho \times 0.0675$ gives $\bar\rho_{\text{implied}} \approx 0.384$, roughly $38\%$ implied correlation.

Now suppose over the life of the trade, each stock realizes its implied volatility of $30\%$ exactly, but the realized pairwise correlation comes in at $\bar\rho_{\text{realized}} = 0.20$ rather than $38\%$. The realized index variance would then be $\sigma_{I,\text{realized}}^2 = 0.0225 + 0.20\times 0.0675 = 0.0360$, i.e., $\sigma_{I,\text{realized}} \approx 19.0\%$ - well below the $22\%$ implied level the index variance was sold at.

The short-index-variance leg therefore pays out less than it took in (a gain to the dispersion trader of roughly $\sigma_I^2 - \sigma_{I,\text{realized}}^2 = 0.0484 - 0.0360 = 0.0124$ in variance terms, scaled by the variance notional), while the long-single-stock legs realize exactly their implied levels (roughly breakeven on those legs in isolation). The net effect is a profit driven almost entirely by the gap between implied correlation ($38\%$) and realized correlation ($20\%$) - illustrating why dispersion is, in practice, best understood as a correlation trade wrapped in a volatility-instrument structure.
