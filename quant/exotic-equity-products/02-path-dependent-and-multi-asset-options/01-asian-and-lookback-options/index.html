2.1 Asian and Lookback Options

Asian and lookback options are the canonical examples of strong path dependency: their payoffs depend on the entire trajectory of the underlying over the option's life, not merely on its terminal value. Asian options replace $S_T$ in a vanilla payoff with an average of observed prices, while lookback options replace it with the running maximum or minimum. Both are widely used - Asians especially in commodity and FX markets to reduce the impact of single-day price manipulation or settlement-date noise, and lookbacks as a stylized way to express "perfect timing" payoffs.

Asian Option Payoffs

Definition - Arithmetic and Geometric Average Options

An Asian (average-rate) call option pays off based on the average of the underlying's price over a set of observation dates $t_1 < t_2 < \dots < t_n = T$, rather than on $S_T$ alone. The arithmetic average is: $$A_{\text{arith}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} S_{t_i}$$ and the arithmetic Asian call payoff is $\max(A_{\text{arith}} - K, 0)$. The geometric average is: $$A_{\text{geo}} = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{n} S_{t_i}\right)^{1/n}$$ with payoff $\max(A_{\text{geo}} - K, 0)$. A continuous-time version replaces the discrete sum/product with a time integral, $A_{\text{arith}} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T S_t\,dt$ and $A_{\text{geo}} = \exp\left(\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \ln S_t\,dt\right)$.

"Average strike" Asians instead use the average to set the strike, with payoff $\max(S_T - A, 0)$ - these behave somewhat differently, but the averaging mechanics are the same.

Remark - Why Geometric Averages Have Closed-Form Prices

Under Black-Scholes, $\ln S_t$ is normally distributed for each $t$, and a sum of jointly normal random variables is itself normal. The geometric average $A_{\text{geo}}$ is the exponential of an average of $\ln S_{t_i}$ terms - i.e., $\ln A_{\text{geo}}$ is normally distributed (a weighted sum of correlated normals). This means $A_{\text{geo}}$ is itself lognormally distributed, so a geometric Asian option can be priced with a Black-Scholes-style closed-form formula using an adjusted volatility and drift. The arithmetic average, by contrast, is a sum of lognormal random variables, which has no closed-form distribution - arithmetic Asians are generally priced via Monte Carlo, moment-matching approximations (e.g., approximating $A_{\text{arith}}$'s distribution with a lognormal matched on its first two moments, the Turnbull-Wakeman approach), or PDE methods.

Why Asians Have Lower Volatility Exposure Than Vanillas

The defining economic feature of an Asian option is that averaging reduces the effective volatility of the payoff-relevant quantity relative to the terminal price alone.

Result - Variance of an Average is Lower

For the continuous geometric average under Black-Scholes with constant volatility $\sigma$, the quantity $\ln A_{\text{geo}} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \ln S_t\,dt$ has variance: $$\text{Var}\left[\ln A_{\text{geo}}\right] = \frac{\sigma^2 T}{3}$$ compared to $\text{Var}[\ln S_T] = \sigma^2 T$ for the terminal price. The averaging reduces the effective variance by a factor of $3$, which corresponds to an effective volatility of $\sigma_{\text{eff}} = \sigma/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.577\sigma$. Intuitively, an average of many correlated-but-not-identical draws of $\ln S_t$ has less dispersion than any single draw - extreme moves late in the path are partially offset by the accumulated average from earlier, less extreme observations. As the number of averaging dates $n \to \infty$ with fixed total horizon $T$, the discrete arithmetic average's variance converges to a similar reduced figure, though not identical to the geometric case.

Remark - Practical Consequences

Lower premium: because the effective volatility entering the option's value is lower, an Asian option is always cheaper than the corresponding vanilla option on the same underlying, strike, and maturity - for a call, $C_{\text{Asian}} \leq C_{\text{vanilla}}$, with the gap widening as $\sigma$ and $T$ increase (more time for averaging to matter) and narrowing as the number of averaging dates shrinks toward one (an Asian with a single observation date is a vanilla option).

Vega and the "vega ramp-down": as the averaging period progresses and more fixings are locked in, the remaining unobserved portion of the average shrinks, and the option's vega declines towards zero even before expiry - a feature absent in vanilla options, whose vega is driven purely by time-to-maturity. This makes Asian options attractive to commodity hedgers, who often want exposure to an average price over a delivery period (e.g., monthly average jet fuel price) rather than a single spot fixing, and reduces the "pin risk" associated with a single terminal observation.

Lookback Options

Definition - Floating-Strike and Fixed-Strike Lookbacks

Define the running maximum and minimum of the underlying over $[0,T]$ as $M_T = \max_{0\leq t\leq T} S_t$ and $m_T = \min_{0\leq t\leq T} S_t$.

Floating-strike lookback call: pays $S_T - m_T$ - the holder buys at the lowest price observed over the life of the option and sells at the terminal price. A floating-strike lookback put pays $M_T - S_T$.

Fixed-strike lookback call: pays $\max(M_T - K, 0)$ - a vanilla call payoff but evaluated at the best (highest) price reached during the option's life rather than at $S_T$. A fixed-strike lookback put pays $\max(K - m_T, 0)$.

Remark - Lookbacks as "Perfect Timing" Options and Their Cost

A floating-strike lookback call effectively guarantees the holder the best possible entry price retroactively - it can never be worth less than a vanilla at-the-money option, and is typically substantially more expensive, since $M_T \geq S_T \geq m_T$ always holds and $M_T - m_T$ can be large for volatile underlyings. Lookback options are therefore among the most expensive exotic structures relative to vanillas, and are priced via the joint distribution of $(S_T, M_T)$ or $(S_T, m_T)$, which - like the barrier option case - can be obtained in closed form under Black-Scholes using the reflection principle, since $M_T$ and $m_T$ are running extrema of (drifted) Brownian motion.

Worked Example - Asian Option Pricing Intuition

Example - Comparing an Asian Call to a Vanilla Call

Consider a stock at $S_0 = 100$, with $\sigma = 30\%$, $r=0$, $T=1$ year, and a strike $K=100$. A vanilla at-the-money call with these parameters is worth roughly $11.9$ (using $C \approx S_0 \cdot \sigma\sqrt{T/2\pi}$ as a quick ATM approximation).

For a geometric Asian call observed continuously over the year, the effective volatility is $\sigma_{\text{eff}} = \sigma/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.30/1.732 \approx 17.3\%$. Plugging this reduced volatility into the same ATM approximation gives a price of roughly $100 \times 0.173 \times \sqrt{1/(2\pi)} \approx 6.9$ - about $58\%$ of the vanilla price, purely as a consequence of the variance-reduction from averaging.

An arithmetic Asian with monthly (12-date) averaging would price slightly higher than the continuous geometric case (the arithmetic average has marginally higher variance than the geometric average, and fewer averaging dates means less variance reduction than the continuous limit), but the order of magnitude - roughly half to two-thirds of the vanilla premium - is the key practical takeaway: an Asian structure is a cost-effective way to express a long-volatility view over a period, at a fraction of the premium of buying a strip of vanilla options.
