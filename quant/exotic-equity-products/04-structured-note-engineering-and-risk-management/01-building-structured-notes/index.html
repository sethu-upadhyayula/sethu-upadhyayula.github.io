4.1 Building Structured Notes

From the investor's perspective, a structured note appears as a single instrument with a defined payoff at maturity (or upon early redemption). From the issuer's perspective, however, every structured note is engineered as a combination of a fixed-income component - a zero-coupon or coupon-paying bond providing some or all of the capital repayment - and one or more derivative components that generate the note's variable payoff. Understanding this decomposition is essential both for pricing the note and for understanding where the issuer's margin is embedded.

The Bond-Plus-Option Decomposition

Definition - Decomposing a Structured Note

A structured note with notional $N$ and maturity $T$ can generally be written as: $$\text{Note Value} = \underbrace{N \times P(0,T) \times \alpha}_{\text{bond component}} + \underbrace{\text{(option component)}}_{\text{derivative payoff}}$$ where $P(0,T)$ is the risk-free discount factor to maturity and $\alpha \in [0,1]$ is the fraction of notional that is principal-protected (for a fully capital-protected note, $\alpha=1$; for a capital-at-risk note such as the autocallable of Section 1.2, $\alpha$ may be $0$ or reflect only a partial floor). The option component is whatever combination of vanilla and exotic options replicates the note's variable payoff - a call spread for a simple "growth" note, a down-and-in put for the embedded risk in an autocallable, a basket or rainbow option for a multi-asset note, and so on.

Pricing and Issuer Margin

Remark - Where the Margin Lives

The fair value identity: at issuance, the theoretical fair value of the bond-plus-option decomposition should equal $100\%$ of the issue price if the note were priced with zero margin - i.e., $N\times P(0,T)\times\alpha + V_{\text{option}} = N$ at the par issue price. In practice, structured notes are issued at par (e.g., $\$1{,}000$ per note) while the fair value of the decomposition is typically less than par by some amount - this gap is the issuer's margin, covering distribution costs, hedging costs, and profit.

Where the gap comes from: the issuer can fund the bond component at its own (often sub-Treasury, reflecting issuer credit spread) funding rate rather than the risk-free rate - issuing the bond component "cheaper" than its theoretical risk-free value, which directly funds part of the margin. Separately, the option component is typically priced by the issuer's derivatives desk using bid-side (i.e., favorable to the issuer) volatility, correlation, and skew assumptions relative to where the desk could actually transact in the market, embedding additional margin in the option leg. The combination of issuer credit spread (funding benefit) and a bid/offer-favorable option valuation typically accounts for a total margin of roughly $1\%$-$3\%$ of notional for a standard 1- to 3-year retail note, though this varies widely by product complexity and distribution channel.

Issuer Credit Risk Embedded in the Bond Component

Remark - The Note is Unsecured Issuer Debt

A critical feature often underappreciated by retail investors: the "bond component" of a structured note is not a segregated, risk-free deposit - it is an unsecured claim on the issuing bank (or a special-purpose vehicle guaranteed by the issuer). If the issuer becomes insolvent, the investor's claim - including any "principal protection" - ranks alongside other unsecured creditors and may be worth significantly less than par, regardless of how the underlying equity performed. This was a key lesson of the 2008 financial crisis, when investors in "principal-protected" notes issued by failed institutions discovered that the protection was a promise from the issuer, not a guarantee independent of the issuer's solvency. The bond component's true value is therefore $N\times P_{\text{issuer}}(0,T)\times\alpha$, where $P_{\text{issuer}}$ is a discount factor reflecting the issuer's own credit spread - not the risk-free rate $P(0,T)$ used in the idealized decomposition above.

Worked Example - Decomposing a Capital-at-Risk Autocallable

Example - Decomposition of the 1-Year Autocallable from Section 1.2

Recall the 1-year autocallable note from Section 1.2: notional $N=100$, quarterly autocall at $100\%$, coupon $2\%$ per quarter (paid if autocalled or if $S_{t_i}\geq 70$), and knock-in barrier at maturity $B_{KI}=60$. Assume a 1-year risk-free rate of $4\%$, so $P(0,1\text{yr}) \approx 0.9608$, and assume issuer credit spread adds roughly $1\%$ to the discount rate, giving $P_{\text{issuer}}(0,1\text{yr}) \approx 0.9518$.

Bond component: the capital-at-risk feature means the investor's downside is not protected - there is no $\alpha=1$ zero-coupon floor. Instead, the "bond-like" component here is better thought of as the present value of the worst-case (knock-in, full decline) payoff, $N\times P_{\text{issuer}}(0,1\text{yr})\times(S_T/S_0)$ in the worst scenario - but since this depends on $S_T$, it is not a pure bond at all. The cleanest decomposition for a capital-at-risk autocallable is therefore: long a zero-coupon bond paying $N$ at $T$ (funded at issuer credit), minus a short down-and-in put struck at $S_0$ with barrier $B_{KI}=60$ (the investor has effectively sold this put to the issuer), plus a strip of digital coupon options (one per observation date, paying the $2\%$ coupon conditional on $S_{t_i}\geq B_C=70$ and not yet autocalled), plus an autocall feature that can be valued as a strip of "one-touch" / digital barrier options that, when triggered, terminate all subsequent components.

Putting illustrative numbers on this: suppose the zero-coupon bond component (at issuer credit) is worth $95.2$ (i.e., $100\times P_{\text{issuer}}$). The short down-and-in put, given the deep $40\%$ knock-in barrier and moderate single-stock volatility (say $25\%$), might be valued by the derivatives desk at around $3.5$ (a relatively cheap put given how far out-of-the-money the knock-in level is). The coupon strip plus autocall feature - which together approximate the expected value of receiving $2\%$ per quarter, weighted by the probability of reaching each observation date without prior autocall - might be valued at around $7.0$ in aggregate (reflecting a high probability of autocalling early and collecting at least one or two coupons).

Summing: $95.2 - 3.5 + 7.0 = 98.7$. If the note is issued at par ($100$), the issuer's margin on this note is approximately $100 - 98.7 = 1.3$, or $1.3\%$ of notional - consistent with the typical range cited above, and consisting of a small funding benefit from issuer credit plus a modest spread embedded in the derivatives desk's valuation of the put and coupon/autocall components relative to where those exposures could actually be hedged in the market.
