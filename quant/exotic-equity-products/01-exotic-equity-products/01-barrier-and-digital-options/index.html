1.1 Barrier and Digital Options

Barrier and digital (binary) options are among the simplest path-dependent and discontinuous-payoff exotics, and serve as building blocks for more complex structured products such as autocallables. Both can be priced in closed form under Black-Scholes assumptions using extensions of the reflection principle for Brownian motion, but their hedging behavior near the barrier or strike is markedly different from vanilla options.

Barrier Options

Definition — Knock-Out and Knock-In Options

A barrier option is a vanilla call or put whose existence depends on whether the underlying price touches a pre-specified barrier level $H$ during the option's life.

Knock-out: the option is extinguished (becomes worthless) if $S_t$ touches $H$ at any time before expiry. An up-and-out option knocks out if $S_t \geq H$ for some $t \in [0,T]$ (with $H > S_0$); a down-and-out option knocks out if $S_t \leq H$ (with $H < S_0$).

Knock-in: the option only comes into existence (becomes a vanilla option) if the barrier is touched. Up-and-in and down-and-in are defined analogously. A useful identity: a knock-in plus the corresponding knock-out (same strike, barrier, type) is exactly equal to the vanilla option, since exactly one of the two conditions must occur: $$\text{Vanilla} = \text{Knock-In} + \text{Knock-Out}$$

Barrier options are cheaper than the corresponding vanilla option (an out option can only have a payoff less than or equal to the vanilla, and an in option requires an additional event to occur), making them attractive for investors who want to express a directional view while reducing premium — at the cost of giving up the payoff entirely if the barrier is breached (for knock-outs) or if it is never breached (for knock-ins).

Pricing via the Reflection Principle

Under the Black-Scholes assumption that $\ln S_t$ follows a (drifted) Brownian motion, the joint distribution of the terminal value $S_T$ and the running minimum or maximum of $S_t$ over $[0,T]$ can be computed in closed form using the reflection principle: for a driftless Brownian motion $W_t$ started at $0$, the probability that $W$ reaches a level $H>0$ before time $T$ and finishes at some value below $H$ equals the probability of finishing above the "reflected" value $2H - W_T$.

Theorem — Down-and-Out Call (No Dividends, $H \le K$)

For a down-and-out call with barrier $H < S_0$ and $H \leq K$, under Black-Scholes with $r$, $\sigma$, the price is: $$C_{\text{DO}} = C_{\text{BS}}(S_0,K) - \left(\frac{H}{S_0}\right)^{2\lambda}C_{\text{BS}}\!\left(\frac{H^2}{S_0},K\right)$$ where $\lambda = \frac{r+\sigma^2/2}{\sigma^2}$ and $C_{\text{BS}}(S,K)$ denotes the standard Black-Scholes call price as a function of spot $S$. The second term is the "reflected" or "image" option: it subtracts exactly the probability mass corresponding to paths that would have crossed the barrier, using the reflection of the spot price about the barrier, $H^2/S_0$.

Remark — Hedging Barrier Options

Discontinuous payoff at the barrier: for a knock-out option, the value drops discontinuously to zero (in the continuous-monitoring idealization) as the spot crosses $H$. This produces extreme gamma and vega near the barrier as expiry approaches — small moves in the underlying can cause large swings in the hedge ratio.

Discrete monitoring and barrier shifting: in practice, barriers are often monitored at discrete times (e.g., daily closes) rather than continuously, which makes the option less likely to knock out than the continuous-monitoring formula assumes. Practitioners commonly apply a "barrier shift" — adjusting $H$ by a small amount (related to $\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}$, where $\Delta t$ is the monitoring interval) — to approximately correct continuous-monitoring formulas for discrete monitoring, an application of the Broadie-Glasserman-Kou correction.

Digital (Binary) Options

Definition — Cash-or-Nothing Digital Option

A cash-or-nothing digital call pays a fixed amount $\$1$ if $S_T > K$ and zero otherwise: $$\text{Payoff} = \mathbb{1}\{S_T > K\}$$ Under Black-Scholes, its price is the discounted risk-neutral probability of finishing in the money: $$D_C = e^{-rT}N(d_2), \qquad d_2 = \frac{\ln(S_0/K)+(r-q-\sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$$ A digital put pays $\$1$ if $S_T < K$, with price $D_P = e^{-rT}N(-d_2) = e^{-rT} - D_C$.

Static Replication of Digitals via Call Spreads

Because a digital option's payoff is discontinuous, it cannot be replicated by a finite position in a single vanilla option. However, it can be approximated arbitrarily well — and replicated in the limit — by a call spread of vanishing width.

Replication — Tight Call Spread

Consider a call spread: long $\frac{1}{\epsilon}$ calls struck at $K-\epsilon$ and short $\frac{1}{\epsilon}$ calls struck at $K$, for small $\epsilon > 0$. The payoff of this position is: $$\frac{1}{\epsilon}\big[\max(S_T-(K-\epsilon),0) - \max(S_T-K,0)\big]$$ For $S_T \leq K-\epsilon$, this is $0$. For $S_T \geq K$, both calls are in the money and the payoff is $\frac{1}{\epsilon}\cdot\epsilon = 1$. For $K-\epsilon < S_T < K$, the payoff ramps linearly from $0$ to $1$. As $\epsilon \to 0$, this ramp converges to the digital's discontinuous step function, and the price of the call spread converges to: $$\lim_{\epsilon \to 0}\frac{C(K-\epsilon)-C(K)}{\epsilon} = -\frac{\partial C}{\partial K} = e^{-rT}N(d_2)$$ which is exactly the digital call price — recovering the result above directly from static replication, without reference to the risk-neutral probability interpretation.

Remark — Skew and Digital Pricing

In practice, $-\partial C/\partial K$ must be evaluated using the market's volatility smile $\sigma_{\text{imp}}(K)$, not a constant volatility — this introduces an additional term: $$-\frac{\partial C}{\partial K} = e^{-rT}N(d_2) - \frac{\partial C}{\partial \sigma}\frac{\partial \sigma_{\text{imp}}}{\partial K}$$ The first term is the "sticky" Black-Scholes digital price; the second term is a skew correction (vega times the slope of the smile). Because index skew is typically negative (downward sloping), this correction increases the price of digital puts and decreases the price of digital calls relative to the flat-volatility Black-Scholes value — a market maker who prices digitals off a single implied volatility (rather than the local slope of the skew) will be systematically mispriced, which is why digitals are usually hedged with the actual call/put spread implied by the skew rather than relying on the closed-form formula alone.
