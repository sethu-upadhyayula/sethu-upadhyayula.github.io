Foreign Exchange

The foreign exchange market is the world's largest and most liquid financial market, with daily turnover exceeding $7 trillion. It encompasses spot transactions, forwards, swaps, and a rich options market with conventions and quoting standards that are unique among asset classes. These notes develop the quantitative framework for understanding FX products from the ground up.

We begin with the FX spot market — the mechanics of quoting conventions, the distinction between base and quote currencies, and the structure of the interbank market. From there we build the FX forward curve through covered interest rate parity, the no-arbitrage relationship that links FX rates to the interest rate differential between two currencies. FX swaps and cross-currency basis swaps, which are the instruments through which banks and corporates manage multi-currency funding, are covered alongside the empirically important cross-currency basis — the deviation from textbook interest rate parity that persists in real markets due to balance sheet constraints and funding pressures.

The options section develops the Garman-Kohlhagen model — the Black-Scholes framework adapted for FX, where both currencies earn interest — and then addresses the market-specific conventions around delta quoting, premium currency, and the parametrisation of the volatility surface through risk reversals and butterflies. These conventions are non-trivial and essential for practitioners working with FX vol.

The final chapter covers exotic FX products with emphasis on barrier options — knock-ins and knock-outs — whose hedging near the barrier presents distinctive challenges, and Target Redemption Forwards (TARFs), which are widely used in emerging market corporate hedging and exhibit complex path-dependent payoffs.
