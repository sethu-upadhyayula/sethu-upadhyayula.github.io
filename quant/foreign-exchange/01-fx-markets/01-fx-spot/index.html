1.1 FX Spot

An FX spot transaction is an agreement to exchange one currency for another at an agreed rate for delivery on the spot date — conventionally two business days after the trade date (T+2) for most currency pairs, though USD/CAD settles T+1 and some pairs involving emerging market currencies have different conventions. The spot exchange rate is the fundamental price in all of FX.

Definition — Exchange Rate

An exchange rate $S$ for a currency pair $\text{CCY1}/\text{CCY2}$ is the number of units of CCY2 required to purchase one unit of CCY1. CCY1 is called the base currency and CCY2 is called the quote currency (or price currency or terms currency).

For example, EUR/USD = 1.0850 means one euro costs 1.0850 US dollars. EUR is the base currency; USD is the quote currency.

The choice of which currency is base and which is quote is a market convention, not a mathematical necessity. The conventions have evolved historically and are now standardised. EUR, GBP, AUD, and NZD are typically quoted as base currencies against USD. USD is quoted as the base currency against most other currencies (USD/JPY, USD/CHF, USD/CAD). Cross rates (e.g., EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY) follow from the major pairs.

Bid, Offer, and Spread

Definition — Bid-Offer Spread

A market maker quotes two prices for a currency pair:

  • The bid $S^{\text{bid}}$: the rate at which the market maker will buy the base currency (i.e., sell the quote currency). The customer sells base at the bid.
  • The offer (or ask) $S^{\text{ask}}$: the rate at which the market maker will sell the base currency. The customer buys base at the offer.

The spread $S^{\text{ask}} - S^{\text{bid}} > 0$ is the market maker's compensation for providing liquidity and bearing inventory risk.

Spreads in FX spot are quoted in pips. A pip (percentage in point) is one unit of the last quoted decimal place — conventionally the fourth decimal place for most pairs (so 1 pip = 0.0001), and the second decimal place for JPY pairs (1 pip = 0.01). In practice, electronic platforms quote to a fifth decimal place (a "pipette" or "fractional pip").

Example — EUR/USD Bid-Offer

A market maker quotes EUR/USD as 1.08472 / 1.08480. The spread is 0.8 pips.

A client wishing to buy €10,000,000 will pay $10,000,000 × 1.08480 = $10,848,000.

A client wishing to sell €10,000,000 will receive $10,000,000 × 1.08472 = $10,847,200.

Market Microstructure

The FX spot market is a decentralised over-the-counter (OTC) market with no central exchange. Trading occurs across three main tiers:

Remark — FX Market Tiers

Tier 1 — Interbank market: large commercial and investment banks trade directly with each other or via interdealer brokers (electronic platforms such as EBS and Reuters Matching). Spreads are extremely tight (often sub-pip) and minimum transaction sizes are large (typically $5–10m notional).

Tier 2 — Prime brokerage: hedge funds and other non-bank financial institutions access interbank liquidity through prime brokerage arrangements, allowing them to trade at near-interbank spreads.

Tier 3 — Retail and corporate: non-financial corporates, asset managers, and retail clients access FX through their banking relationships. Spreads are wider and execution may be via request-for-quote (RFQ) or multi-dealer platforms.

Cross Rates

A cross rate is an exchange rate between two currencies neither of which is USD, derived from the rates of each against USD. Given EUR/USD and USD/JPY, the EUR/JPY cross is:

$$S_{\text{EUR/JPY}} = S_{\text{EUR/USD}} \times S_{\text{USD/JPY}}$$
Proposition — Cross Rate Arbitrage

In the absence of transaction costs, cross rates must satisfy the triangular arbitrage condition. If $S_{A/B}$ denotes the rate of currency pair A/B, then for any three currencies A, B, C: $$S_{A/C} = S_{A/B} \times S_{B/C}$$ Any deviation from this relationship allows a riskless profit by cycling through the three currencies. In practice, deviations within the bid-offer spread do not generate arbitrage opportunities after costs.

Example — Triangular Arbitrage

Suppose EUR/USD = 1.0850, USD/JPY = 149.50, and EUR/JPY is quoted at 162.20 (instead of the no-arbitrage value of 1.0850 × 149.50 = 162.21). An arbitrageur could:

  1. Buy EUR/USD: spend $1,085,000 to receive €1,000,000.
  2. Sell EUR/JPY: sell €1,000,000 at 162.20 to receive ¥162,200,000.
  3. Sell USD/JPY: sell ¥162,200,000 at 149.50 to receive $1,085,016.72.

The $16.72 riskless profit per $1,085,000 (about 1.5 pips) would quickly be arbitraged away in liquid markets.

Value Date and Settlement

Settlement of an FX spot transaction requires the physical exchange of the two currency amounts. The value date (settlement date) must be a business day in both currency centres. This creates complications around holidays: a trade on Monday in a pair where Tuesday is a holiday in one of the currency centres will not settle on the normal T+2 schedule.

Remark — Settlement Risk (Herstatt Risk)

Because the two legs of an FX settlement occur in different time zones, there is a window during which one party has delivered its currency but has not yet received the other. This settlement risk — known as Herstatt risk after the German bank that failed mid-settlement in 1974 — is mitigated by continuous linked settlement (CLS), a payment-versus-payment system operated by CLS Bank that synchronises the two payment legs and eliminates the settlement exposure for participating currencies.
