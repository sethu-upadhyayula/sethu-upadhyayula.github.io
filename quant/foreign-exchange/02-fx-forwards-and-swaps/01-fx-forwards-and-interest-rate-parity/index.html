2.1 FX Forwards and Interest Rate Parity

An FX forward is an agreement to exchange two currencies at a rate fixed today, for delivery at a specified future date $T$. Unlike spot, where the rate is set by current supply and demand, the forward rate is pinned down (in a frictionless market) by a no-arbitrage relationship to the spot rate and the interest rates in the two currencies: covered interest rate parity.

Covered Interest Rate Parity

Definition — Forward Rate

The forward rate $F(T)$ for a currency pair is the rate, agreed today, at which one unit of the base currency will be exchanged for $F(T)$ units of the quote currency at time $T$. The forward rate generally differs from the spot rate $S$; the difference $F(T) - S$ is the forward points.

Theorem — Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIP)

Let $S$ be the spot exchange rate (units of domestic currency per unit of foreign currency), $r_d$ the domestic risk-free interest rate, $r_f$ the foreign risk-free interest rate, and $T$ the time to maturity (in years). Under simple compounding, the no-arbitrage forward rate is:

$$F(T) = S \cdot \frac{1 + r_d T}{1 + r_f T}$$

Under continuous compounding, this becomes:

$$F(T) = S \, e^{(r_d - r_f) T}$$

No-Arbitrage Proof via Covered Carry Trade

Proof — Covered Carry Trade

Consider two strategies for converting 1 unit of domestic currency into foreign currency and back over horizon $T$, both fully hedged ("covered") against FX risk.

Strategy A (domestic deposit): Invest 1 unit of domestic currency at rate $r_d$. At time $T$ this is worth $1 + r_d T$ units of domestic currency.

Strategy B (foreign deposit, FX-hedged):

  1. Convert 1 unit of domestic currency to $1/S$ units of foreign currency at the spot rate.
  2. Invest $1/S$ at the foreign rate $r_f$, growing to $\frac{1}{S}(1 + r_f T)$ units of foreign currency at time $T$.
  3. Today, simultaneously enter a forward contract to sell this known foreign-currency amount at time $T$ at the forward rate $F(T)$, locking in $\frac{1}{S}(1 + r_f T) \cdot F(T)$ units of domestic currency at $T$.

Both strategies require zero net investment beyond the initial 1 unit and have a deterministic, riskless payoff at $T$ (Strategy B's foreign-currency risk is fully hedged by the forward). By the law of one price, their payoffs must be equal:

$$1 + r_d T = \frac{1}{S}(1 + r_f T) \cdot F(T)$$

Solving for $F(T)$ gives $F(T) = S \cdot \dfrac{1 + r_d T}{1 + r_f T}$. If this equality failed — say the left side exceeded the right — an arbitrageur could borrow domestic currency, execute Strategy B, and repay the loan from Strategy A's smaller deposit, pocketing the riskless difference; the reverse trade applies if the inequality went the other way. Either direction would be exploited until the relationship holds. $\blacksquare$

Forward Points

In practice, the forward market does not directly quote $F(T)$; instead it quotes forward points (or "swap points"), the difference between the forward and spot rate, scaled to the same pip convention as the spot quote. The trader adds (or subtracts) the forward points to the spot rate to obtain the outright forward rate.

Definition — Forward Points

The forward points for maturity $T$ are defined as $\text{Points}(T) = F(T) - S$, usually quoted in pips (i.e., multiplied by $10^4$ for most pairs or $10^2$ for JPY pairs). From CIP, $\text{Points}(T) = S \left( \dfrac{1+r_dT}{1+r_fT} - 1 \right) = S \cdot \dfrac{T(r_d - r_f)}{1+r_fT}$, which is approximately $S \cdot T (r_d - r_f)$ for short maturities and small rates. The sign of the points is positive when the domestic rate exceeds the foreign rate (the base currency is at a "forward premium" if it earns the higher rate... more precisely, the quote currency's higher rate makes the base currency cheaper forward, i.e., points are negative — the exact sign depends on which currency is base; the key relationship is that the higher-yielding currency trades at a forward discount to the lower-yielding currency).

Worked Example

Example — Computing Forward Points for EUR/USD

Suppose EUR/USD spot $S = 1.0850$, the 3-month USD deposit rate is $r_d = 5.30\%$, and the 3-month EUR deposit rate is $r_f = 3.50\%$, with $T = 0.25$ (3 months, simple compounding, act/360 conventions ignored for simplicity).

$$F(T) = 1.0850 \times \frac{1 + 0.0530 \times 0.25}{1 + 0.0350 \times 0.25} = 1.0850 \times \frac{1.01325}{1.00875} = 1.0850 \times 1.004461 = 1.08984$$

The forward points are $F(T) - S = 1.08984 - 1.0850 = 0.00484$, or about $48.4$ pips. Since USD rates exceed EUR rates, EUR (the base currency) trades at a forward premium to USD — you receive more dollars per euro forward than spot, compensating for the lower euro deposit rate.

A trader would quote this as "EUR/USD 3M points: 48.4" and the outright 3-month forward would be obtained by adding the points to the spot rate.

The Post-2008 CIP Basis

Remark — Breakdown of CIP Since the Global Financial Crisis

Before 2007–08, CIP held to within a few basis points across major currency pairs — deviations were arbitraged away almost instantly by bank balance sheets with abundant capacity. Since the crisis, however, persistent and economically significant deviations from CIP have been observed, commonly quantified as the cross-currency basis:

$$\text{basis} = (r_d - r_f) - \left(\frac{1}{T}\ln\frac{F(T)}{S}\right)$$

i.e., the gap between the interest rate differential implied by money markets and the differential implied by FX forward points. This basis is typically negative for USD against most other G10 currencies, meaning it is more expensive to borrow dollars synthetically (via FX swaps) than to borrow them directly in the cash market.

The widely cited explanations include: (i) post-crisis bank balance sheet costs and regulatory capital charges (e.g., leverage ratio requirements under Basel III) that make balance-sheet-intensive arbitrage trades like the covered carry trade above expensive even when "free money" appears available; (ii) persistent demand imbalances for dollar funding from non-US banks and institutions with USD-denominated liabilities or hedging needs; and (iii) reduced arbitrage capital and risk appetite among the dealer banks that historically enforced CIP. The basis widens sharply during periods of stress (e.g., the 2008 crisis, the 2020 COVID shock, and quarter-end "turn" effects), making it an important real-time gauge of dollar funding conditions. The cross-currency basis swap, covered in Section 2.3, is the instrument used to trade this basis directly.
