4.1 Mandatory Convertibles, PEPS, and Exchangeables

The standard convertible bond of Chapters 1–3 gives the holder the option to convert. A mandatory convertible removes that optionality: conversion into equity occurs automatically at maturity, with the holder's only choice having been made at issuance — to accept a payoff profile that is capped on the upside in exchange for a higher coupon than a comparable common-stock dividend yield. PEPS instruments are a closely related US variant, and exchangeable bonds retain the holder's conversion option but convert into shares of a company other than the issuer.

Mandatory Convertible Structure and Payoff

Definition — Mandatory Convertible

A mandatory convertible is a security that pays a fixed coupon until maturity $T$, at which point it converts automatically into a variable number of shares of the issuer's common stock. The number of shares delivered per unit of face value depends on the stock price $S_T$ at maturity according to a conversion ratio $CR(S_T)$ that is itself piecewise-defined by two threshold prices, the initial price $S_0$ (set at issuance) and the threshold appreciation price $S_{\text{cap}} > S_0$: $$CR(S_T) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{100}{S_0}, & S_T \leq S_0 \\[2mm] \dfrac{100}{S_T}, & S_0 < S_T < S_{\text{cap}} \\[2mm] \dfrac{100}{S_{\text{cap}}}, & S_T \geq S_{\text{cap}} \end{cases}$$ where $100$ is the face/par value per unit. The value delivered at maturity, $CR(S_T) \times S_T$, is therefore floored at $100 \times (S_T/S_0)$ for $S_T \leq S_0$, equal to $100$ in the middle region, and capped at $100 \times (S_T/S_{\text{cap}})$ for $S_T \geq S_{\text{cap}}$.

Remark — The Mandatory as a Collar Between Two Conversion Ratios

The structure can equivalently be described as a collar between two fixed conversion ratios: a maximum ratio $CR_{\max} = 100/S_0$ (applied when the stock has fallen or stayed flat) and a minimum ratio $CR_{\min} = 100/S_{\text{cap}}$ (applied when the stock has risen above $S_{\text{cap}}$). Between $S_0$ and $S_{\text{cap}}$, the conversion ratio adjusts continuously so that the delivered value tracks par ($100$) — equivalently, the holder's payoff in this middle region is fixed at $100$ regardless of $S_T$. From an option perspective, the mandatory's payoff is equivalent to holding $CR_{\max}$ shares, short a call struck at $S_0$ with that same notional, and long a call struck at $S_{\text{cap}}$ with notional $CR_{\min}$ — i.e., the holder is long the stock down to $S_0$, flat (paid at par) between $S_0$ and $S_{\text{cap}}$, and participates again, at a reduced ratio, above $S_{\text{cap}}$.

Example — Payoff at Three Stock Levels

Suppose $S_0 = \$50$ and $S_{\text{cap}} = \$60$ (a typical 20% conversion premium), so $CR_{\max} = 100/50 = 2.0$ shares per $100$ face and $CR_{\min} = 100/60 \approx 1.667$ shares.

If $S_T = \$40$ (stock fell 20%): $CR = CR_{\max} = 2.0$, delivered value $= 2.0 \times 40 = \$80$ — the holder participates fully in the downside below $S_0$, receiving less than par.

If $S_T = \$55$ (stock rose 10%, within the collar): $CR = 100/55 \approx 1.818$, delivered value $= 1.818 \times 55 = \$100$ — par, regardless of where exactly $S_T$ sits between $S_0$ and $S_{\text{cap}}$.

If $S_T = \$72$ (stock rose 44%, above $S_{\text{cap}}$): $CR = CR_{\min} \approx 1.667$, delivered value $= 1.667 \times 72 = \$120$ — the holder participates in the upside above $S_{\text{cap}}$, but only at the reduced ratio $CR_{\min}$, so the $\$120$ delivered value is well below the $\$144$ a holder of $2.0$ shares outright would have received.

PEPS: Preferred Equity Participation Securities

Definition — PEPS

PEPS (Preferred Equity Participation Securities, also issued under names such as PERCS, ACES, or DECS depending on the bank) are a family of mandatory convertible preferred structures, most common in the US market, that implement the same collared conversion-ratio mechanic as the generic mandatory convertible above, but issued in preferred stock form rather than as debt. Holding preferred rather than debt form affects the issuer's accounting and regulatory capital treatment — mandatory convertible preferreds are often counted as equity (or equity-like) capital by rating agencies and regulators, well before the conversion date, because the obligation to deliver shares (rather than cash) at maturity is contractually fixed.

From a holder's perspective, the economics of a PEPS are essentially identical to the generic mandatory convertible: a high fixed dividend (in lieu of a bond coupon) until the mandatory conversion date, and the same collared payoff at conversion. The distinction is primarily one of legal form and balance-sheet treatment for the issuer — a PEPS issuance is often used specifically because the issuer needs to raise capital that counts toward equity ratios without immediately diluting existing shareholders, deferring the dilution until the conversion date while still receiving favourable capital treatment from issuance.

Exchangeable Bonds

Definition — Exchangeable Bond

An exchangeable bond is structurally identical to the convertible bond of Section 1.1 — a coupon-bearing bond with a holder's option to convert into a fixed number of shares at a conversion price $K$ — except that the shares deliverable on conversion are shares of a company different from the issuer of the bond. The issuer is typically a parent company, a large shareholder, or a holding vehicle that owns a stake in the "reference company" whose shares are delivered.

Remark — Why Issue an Exchangeable Rather Than a Convertible

A company holding a large equity stake in another company (e.g., from a past acquisition, spin-off, or strategic investment) can monetise part of that stake without an outright sale by issuing bonds exchangeable into the held shares. This achieves several things at once: the issuer raises cash immediately (the bond proceeds), retains voting rights and dividends on the underlying shares until and unless bondholders convert, defers any capital gains tax on the stake until conversion actually occurs, and — because the embedded call option on the reference company's stock has value — can issue the bond at a lower coupon than an unsecured bond of the same issuer would carry. All of the pricing machinery of Chapters 1–3 (decomposition into bond floor plus conversion option, the three regimes, lattice/PDE valuation, and credit-spread effects) applies directly to an exchangeable, with the caveat that the relevant credit spread for the bond-floor discounting is the issuer's spread, while the relevant volatility and dividend yield for the conversion option are those of the reference company's stock — two different names, which is itself a source of basis risk for an exchangeable arbitrage position relative to a standard convertible arbitrage trade.
