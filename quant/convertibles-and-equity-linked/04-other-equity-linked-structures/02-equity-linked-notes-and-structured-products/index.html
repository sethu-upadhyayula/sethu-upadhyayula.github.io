4.2 Equity-Linked Notes and Structured Products

The instruments of Section 4.1 — mandatory convertibles, PEPS, and exchangeables — are primarily issuer-driven: a corporation or large shareholder structures the security to achieve a financing or monetisation objective, and investors buy it as a fixed-income-like instrument with equity upside. Equity-linked notes and structured products invert this relationship: a bank packages an equity-linked payoff specifically to meet an investor's stated risk/return preference, typically retail or private-banking demand for enhanced yield or principal protection, with the issuing bank manufacturing the payoff from vanilla and exotic options.

Reverse Convertibles

Definition — Reverse Convertible

A reverse convertible is a short-dated note that pays a high fixed coupon, and at maturity returns either par in cash or a fixed number of shares of a reference stock, at the issuer's choice (in practice, determined mechanically by where the stock price $S_T$ sits relative to a strike $K$ set at issuance): $$\text{Payoff at } T = \begin{cases} 100, & S_T \geq K \\[1mm] 100 \times \dfrac{S_T}{K}, & S_T < K \end{cases}$$ plus the fixed coupon in either case. The name "reverse" reflects that, unlike a standard convertible bond where the holder has the conversion option (and so pays for it via a lower coupon), here the issuer effectively holds an option to "put" the depreciated stock to the investor, and the investor is compensated for selling that option via the enhanced coupon.

Remark — Reverse Convertible as Bond Plus Short Put

The payoff decomposes into a zero-coupon bond paying $100$ at $T$, minus a short position in a put option on the stock with strike $K$ and notional $100/K$ shares: if $S_T \geq K$ the put expires worthless and the investor receives the full $100$; if $S_T < K$ the investor's payoff is reduced by $100 \times (K - S_T)/K = 100 - 100\,S_T/K$, exactly the loss on a short put of that notional. The enhanced coupon is therefore the bank's way of passing through to the investor the premium income from selling that put — the investor is, economically, short downside volatility on the reference stock.

Barrier and Knock-In Features

Definition — Knock-In Barrier

Many reverse convertibles and related notes add a knock-in barrier $B < K$: the embedded short put only becomes "active" if the stock price touches or breaches $B$ at any point during the note's life (an American-style, continuously-monitored barrier), or is below $B$ at a specific observation date (a European-style barrier). If the barrier is never breached, the investor receives par at maturity regardless of where $S_T$ ultimately ends up — even if $S_T < K$ at maturity. If the barrier is breached, the put becomes live and the maturity payoff reverts to the standard reverse-convertible formula above.

From the bank's hedging perspective, a knock-in put is cheaper than a vanilla put with the same strike (because it only pays out in the subset of paths that also breach $B$), which allows the structurer to set $B$ comfortably below $K$ — giving the investor a buffer against moderate declines — while still funding an attractive coupon from the (smaller) premium of the knock-in put. The trade-off is that once $B$ is breached the investor's protection disappears entirely and the full reverse-convertible downside (as if there were no barrier) applies.

Equity-Linked Notes: Principal Protected vs. Not

Definition — Principal-Protected Equity-Linked Note

A principal-protected equity-linked note guarantees return of (at least) the original investment at maturity, plus a participation in the upside of a reference index or basket: $$\text{Payoff at } T = 100 + \alpha \times 100 \times \max\!\left(0, \frac{S_T - S_0}{S_0}\right)$$ where $\alpha$ is the participation rate. The structure is decomposed into a zero-coupon bond that, discounted at the issuer's funding rate, accretes to $100$ at $T$ (providing the principal guarantee), plus $\alpha$ units of an at-the-money call option on $S$ with notional matched so its payoff funds the upside participation. The participation rate $\alpha$ is determined by how much budget remains after buying the zero-coupon bond: if rates are high, the bond is cheap to buy (more residual budget for the call, so $\alpha$ can be higher); if rates are low, less budget remains for the call and $\alpha$ is correspondingly lower — principal-protected notes are structurally less attractive (lower $\alpha$ for the same maturity) in low-rate environments.

Remark — Non-Principal-Protected Notes

Dropping the principal guarantee frees up the entire budget for optionality, allowing either a higher participation rate $\alpha > 1$ (leveraged upside participation, but with the investor's principal now at risk on the downside, typically with some buffer or barrier as in the reverse convertible above), or the addition of an enhanced fixed coupon funded by selling downside protection (as in the reverse convertible). The general design space spans a continuum from fully principal-protected, low-participation notes at one end to reverse-convertible-style notes with full downside exposure and enhanced coupons at the other — the bank's structuring desk allocates the fixed "options budget" implied by the note's economics between buying upside calls (for participation) and selling downside puts (for coupon enhancement) according to the targeted payoff profile.

Worked Example: Pricing a Reverse Convertible's Budget

Example — One-Year Reverse Convertible on a $\$100$ Stock

Consider a one-year reverse convertible on a stock currently at $S_0 = \$100$, with strike $K = \$100$ (at-the-money) and a knock-in barrier at $B = \$70$ (a 30% buffer), issued at par ($100$).

Suppose the one-year zero-coupon bond (at the issuer's funding rate) is worth $97.50$ per $100$ face — i.e., investing $97.50$ today returns $100$ in one year with certainty. This leaves a budget of $100 - 97.50 = 2.50$ to fund the enhanced coupon, available from selling the knock-in put.

If the one-year, $30\%$-out-of-the-money knock-in put (strike $\$100$, barrier $\$70$) on this stock is worth $6.00$ per $100$ notional in the options market, the bank can sell this put, add its $6.00$ premium to the $2.50$ residual budget, giving $8.50$ of total budget — minus the bank's structuring margin (say $1.50$), leaving $7.00$ available to pay out as a coupon over the note's one-year life: a $7\%$ annual coupon.

At maturity: if $S_T$ never touched $\$70$ during the year, the investor receives $100 + 7 = 107$ regardless of where $S_T$ ended up. If the barrier was touched at some point and $S_T = \$80$ at maturity (a $20\%$ decline from $S_0$), the investor receives $100 \times (80/100) + 7 = 80 + 7 = 87$ — the coupon is still paid, but the principal is reduced in proportion to the stock's decline, illustrating that the $7\%$ coupon compensates the investor for bearing exactly this knock-in downside risk.
