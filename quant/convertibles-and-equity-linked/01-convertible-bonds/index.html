1. Convertible Bonds

A convertible bond is a corporate bond that gives the holder the right to convert the bond into a fixed number of shares of the issuer's common stock, instead of receiving the bond's redemption value in cash. It is therefore a hybrid security: a straight bond combined with an embedded equity call option, and its value reflects both the credit and interest-rate sensitivity of the bond component and the equity sensitivity of the conversion option.

Basic Structure

Definition — Conversion Ratio and Conversion Price

A convertible bond with face value $F$ and conversion ratio $C_r$ (the number of shares received per bond upon conversion) has an implied conversion price:

$$P_{\text{conv}} = \frac{F}{C_r}$$

The conversion price is typically set at a premium to the stock price prevailing at issuance — a 20-30% "conversion premium" is common — meaning the stock must rise by that amount before conversion becomes attractive relative to par redemption.

Decomposition: Bond Floor and Conversion Option

A convertible bond's value can be decomposed, approximately, into two components:

Definition — Bond Floor and Conversion Value
  • Bond floor — the present value of the bond's coupon and principal cash flows, discounted at the issuer's credit-risky yield, ignoring the conversion option entirely. This represents the minimum value of the convertible if the stock price falls to zero (the bond would never be converted, and behaves like a straight corporate bond).
  • Conversion value — the value of the shares received upon immediate conversion, $C_r \times S_t$, where $S_t$ is the current stock price.

The convertible's market price is always at least $\max(\text{bond floor}, \text{conversion value})$, with the excess over this floor representing the value of the optionality to convert at a more advantageous future date — analogous to the time value of an American call option.

The Three Regimes

Remark — Bond-Like, Hybrid, and Equity-Like Convertibles

Convertibles are often classified by how far the stock price is from the conversion price:

  • Out-of-the-money ("bond-like") — the stock price is well below the conversion price. The convertible trades close to its bond floor, and its price is dominated by interest rate and credit risk, with low sensitivity to the stock price.
  • At-the-money ("hybrid") — the stock price is near the conversion price. The convertible exhibits the most pronounced optionality, with significant sensitivity to both credit/rates and the stock, and is the regime where convertible arbitrage strategies are most active.
  • In-the-money ("equity-like") — the stock price is well above the conversion price. The convertible trades close to its conversion value (effectively, $C_r$ shares plus a small residual bond value), moving almost one-for-one with the stock.

Convertible Arbitrage

A classic strategy involving convertibles is convertible arbitrage: buying the convertible bond and simultaneously selling short the underlying stock in a ratio determined by the convertible's delta (its sensitivity to the stock price), aiming to profit from the convertible's embedded volatility being underpriced relative to the stock's realised volatility, while remaining largely hedged against directional moves in the stock.

Example — Delta Hedging a Convertible

Suppose a convertible has a conversion ratio of $C_r = 20$ shares per bond and a delta of $\Delta = 0.5$ (meaning the convertible's price moves by approximately $0.5 \times C_r = 10$ shares' worth of stock price for a small move in the stock). A convertible arbitrageur holding one bond would short approximately $10$ shares to be delta-neutral. As the stock price moves, the delta changes (gamma), requiring the hedge to be rebalanced — and these rebalancing trades, on average, generate a profit if the stock's realised volatility exceeds the volatility implied by the convertible's price, analogous to gamma-scalping a long-volatility option position.

Issuer Call and Investor Put Features

Many convertibles include additional embedded options that affect their valuation:

  • Issuer call — the issuer may have the right to redeem the bond early (often subject to a "soft call" condition that the stock trades above a trigger level for a sustained period), which caps the convertible's upside by forcing conversion or redemption before the conversion option has fully matured.
  • Investor put — investors may have the right to sell the bond back to the issuer at par (or a premium) on specified dates, providing a floor on the bond's value independent of the bond floor implied by discounting to final maturity, effectively shortening the bond's duration from the investor's perspective.

Because of these embedded American-style features, convertible bonds are typically valued using a binomial or trinomial lattice (or a finite-difference PDE) that jointly models the evolution of the stock price and the issuer's credit spread, with the conversion, call, and put decisions evaluated at each node — a direct extension of the lattice methods used for American options, augmented with a credit-risky discounting of the bond cash flows.
