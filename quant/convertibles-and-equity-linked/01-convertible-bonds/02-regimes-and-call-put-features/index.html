1.2 The Three Regimes and Call/Put Features

The decomposition introduced in Section 1.1 - a convertible's price as the sum of a bond floor and an embedded conversion option - implies that the convertible's sensitivities to the stock price, interest rates, and credit spreads vary continuously as the stock price moves relative to the conversion price. Market practitioners classify convertibles into three regimes based on this "moneyness," and these regimes determine which risk factors dominate and which trading strategies are appropriate.

The Three Regimes

Remark - Bond-Like, Hybrid, and Equity-Like Convertibles

Define the ratio $S_t / P_{\text{conv}}$ as a measure of moneyness. Convertibles are classified as follows:

  • Out-of-the-money ("bond-like" or "busted") - $S_t \ll P_{\text{conv}}$. The convertible trades close to its bond floor $B_t$, and $V_t \approx B_t \gg \text{Parity}_t$. Its price is dominated by interest-rate and credit risk, behaving much like a straight corporate bond, with low sensitivity (delta) to the stock price. The embedded conversion option is far out-of-the-money and contributes little value.
  • At-the-money ("hybrid") - $S_t \approx P_{\text{conv}}$. The convertible exhibits the most pronounced optionality: $B_t$ and $\text{Parity}_t$ are comparable in magnitude, the embedded option's gamma is highest, and the convertible has significant sensitivity to both credit/rates and the stock. This is the regime where convertible arbitrage strategies (Chapter 3) are most active, since the convertible's implied volatility is most exposed.
  • In-the-money ("equity-like") - $S_t \gg P_{\text{conv}}$. The convertible trades close to its conversion value, $V_t \approx \text{Parity}_t \gg B_t$, moving almost one-for-one with the stock (delta near $C_r$). The bond floor becomes largely irrelevant - credit and rate risk are a small component of the convertible's total risk.
Example - Regime Transition as the Stock Price Moves

Continuing the bond from Section 1.1 ($F=1{,}000$, $C_r=25$, $P_{\text{conv}}=\$40$, bond floor $B_t \approx 87.0\%$ of face), consider the convertible's approximate price at three stock prices:

$S_t$Parity (% face)RegimeApprox. $V_t$ (% face)
$2255.0%Bond-like~88% (near bond floor)
$40100.0%Hybrid~112% (significant premium)
$60150.0%Equity-like~152% (near parity)

At $S_t = \$22$, parity ($55\%$) is well below the bond floor ($87\%$), so the convertible trades close to $B_t$ with a small premium for the far-out-of-the-money option. At $S_t = \$40$ (at the conversion price), the convertible carries the largest premium over both $B_t$ and parity, reflecting maximal optionality. At $S_t=\$60$, parity ($150\%$) dominates the bond floor, and the convertible trades close to parity with only a small residual premium - the bond component contributes little since redemption is essentially irrelevant once conversion is so deep in-the-money.

Issuer Call Provisions

Many convertibles grant the issuer the right to redeem the bond early at a specified call price (often par or a small premium to par), typically after an initial non-call period.

Definition - Hard Call and Soft Call
  • Hard call - the issuer may call the bond at the call price at any time after the non-call period, unconditionally.
  • Soft call - the issuer may call only if a trigger condition is satisfied, most commonly that the stock price has closed above a specified level (e.g. $130\%$ of the conversion price) for a minimum number of trading days within a trailing window (e.g. 20 of the preceding 30 trading days).
Remark - Why Issuers Call: Forced Conversion

If $\text{Parity}_t$ exceeds the call price $K_{\text{call}}$ when the issuer calls the bond, a rational holder will convert into shares rather than accept the call price in cash - this is a "forced conversion." The issuer's call right therefore effectively caps the convertible's upside: once parity rises far enough above the call price (and the soft-call trigger, if any, is satisfied), the issuer can force holders to convert, eliminating any further time value in the embedded option. The call provision is economically similar to the issuer holding a call option on the convertible itself, exercised once the embedded option is deep enough in-the-money that little optionality remains to be lost.

Investor Put Features

Convertibles frequently include "hard put" dates - specific dates on which the holder may sell the bond back to the issuer at a pre-specified price, usually at or near par.

Definition - Investor Put

An investor put at date $t_p$ with put price $K_{\text{put}}$ gives the holder the right (not the obligation) to receive $K_{\text{put}}$ in cash at $t_p$, regardless of the bond's remaining cash flows or the prevailing stock price. Economically, this truncates the relevant horizon of the bond floor calculation: for valuation purposes the bond's cash flows beyond $t_p$ are only relevant if the holder chooses not to exercise the put, so the put effectively places a floor of (the present value of) $K_{\text{put}}$ on the convertible's value at $t_p$, shortening the bond's effective duration from the investor's perspective relative to the bond floor computed to final maturity in Section 1.1.

Remark - American-Style Optionality and Valuation Implications

A convertible with issuer call and investor put features embeds three distinct American-style decisions, each potentially exercisable at any time (or on specified dates): the holder's conversion right, the issuer's call right (subject to any soft-call trigger), and the holder's put right. None of these can be valued by a simple closed-form decomposition into "bond floor plus a European option," because the optimal exercise of each right depends on the joint evolution of the stock price and the issuer's credit spread over time, and the rights interact (e.g. the issuer's call decision depends on whether holders would convert or accept cash). Valuing convertibles with these features therefore requires a numerical lattice or finite-difference PDE approach that evaluates all three decisions at every node - the subject of Chapter 2.
