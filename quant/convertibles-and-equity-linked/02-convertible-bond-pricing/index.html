2. Convertible Bond Pricing

Chapter 1 established that a convertible bond embeds several American-style decisions — the holder's right to convert, the issuer's right to call, and (often) the holder's right to put the bond back — whose optimal exercise depends on the joint evolution of the stock price and the issuer's credit standing. No closed-form formula captures this jointly; instead, convertibles are valued numerically using a binomial (or trinomial) lattice or, equivalently, a finite-difference solution of a Black-Scholes-type PDE, with the conversion, call, and put decisions evaluated at every node or grid point. This chapter develops that lattice and PDE machinery from the ground up, beginning with the mechanics of building and backward-inducting through a tree that incorporates the embedded optionality.

The second part of the chapter addresses the central modelling challenge specific to convertibles: the issuer's credit risk is not independent of its stock price, and a convertible's bond component must be discounted at a credit-risky rate that itself depends on the same underlying process driving the conversion option. We introduce the hazard-rate (reduced-form) approach to credit risk, show how it is incorporated into the discounting of the bond component, and discuss the resulting "credit-equity coupling" that links a firm's stock price to its bond's discount rate — a defining feature of convertible bond valuation that distinguishes it from valuing a plain equity option or a plain corporate bond in isolation.
