3.2 Vega, Credit Spread Risk, and Cross-Greeks

Delta and gamma (Section 3.1) describe the convertible's sensitivity to the stock price, but a convertible's value also depends on volatility and on the issuer's credit spread - both of which a delta-hedged book remains exposed to. This section develops vega and a credit-spread analogue of CS01, then examines the cross-greeks that couple these exposures to the equity delta, before discussing how these residual risks can compound in stress, using the 2008 convertible arbitrage unwind as a conceptual case study.

Vega: Sensitivity to Volatility

Definition - Vega

The vega of a convertible bond is its sensitivity to the volatility input $\sigma$ used in the lattice or PDE of Chapter 2: $$\nu = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}$$ Because the convertible embeds a long call option on the stock (Section 1.1), $\nu > 0$: a higher assumed volatility increases the value of the embedded optionality and hence the convertible's price, holding the stock price, credit spread, and interest rates fixed. Vega is largest in the hybrid regime, where the embedded option is closest to at-the-money and its time value is most sensitive to $\sigma$, and smallest in the deeply bond-like regime, where the option is far out-of-the-money and contributes little to $V$.

For a convertible arbitrage book, vega is the other side of the gamma-scalping thesis from Section 3.1. The remark there showed that gamma-scalping P&L depends on realised volatility exceeding the volatility implied by the convertible's price. Vega measures the book's direct exposure to that implied volatility itself: if the implied volatility used to mark the convertible falls - even with no change in the stock price or realised volatility - a long-convertible, delta-hedged position with $\nu > 0$ loses value. A pure gamma-scalping thesis is therefore implicitly also a long-vega position, and the two exposures are usually managed together rather than separately.

Credit Spread Risk: CS01

Section 2.2 introduced the hazard-rate model, in which the issuer's credit spread $h$ (or equivalently its hazard rate $\lambda$) enters the discounting of the bond-floor component of $V$. A convertible's sensitivity to this spread is the equity-market analogue of the DV01 introduced in Section 3.1 of Rates Fundamentals, applied to credit rather than rate risk.

Definition - CS01

The credit spread sensitivity of a convertible, denoted $\text{CS01}$, is the change in the convertible's value for a one-basis-point parallel widening of the issuer's credit spread $h$: $$\text{CS01} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} \times 0.0001$$ Because widening the credit spread reduces the present value of the bond floor (Section 1.1) without directly affecting the conversion option, $\text{CS01} > 0$ for a long convertible position - a spread widening reduces $V$, so $\text{CS01}$ (defined with the leading minus sign so that it is positive for a typical long position) represents a loss. $\text{CS01}$ is largest for bond-like convertibles, where the bond floor dominates $V$ and credit spread changes pass through to price almost one-for-one (discounted), and smallest for equity-like convertibles, where $V$ is dominated by the conversion value and largely insensitive to $h$.

Example - CS01 of a Bond-Like Convertible

Consider a convertible trading deep in the bond-like regime, priced at $V = 92$ (per 100 face) with the issuer's credit spread at $h = 400\text{bp}$ and an effective remaining maturity of $6$ years. Approximating the bond-floor component as a defaultable zero-coupon-like sensitivity, a 1bp widening of $h$ reduces the present value of the bond floor by roughly $0.0001 \times 6 \times (\text{bond-floor value})$.

If the bond floor accounts for $85$ of the $92$ price, the convertible's $\text{CS01} \approx 0.0001 \times 6 \times 85 \approx 0.051$ - about $5.1$ cents per 100 face for each 1bp of spread widening. For a $10$ million face position, this is roughly $\$5{,}100$ of P&L per basis point - a meaningful exposure that the delta hedge in Section 3.1 does nothing to offset, since shorting stock has no credit-spread sensitivity.

Cross-Greeks: Delta's Dependence on Credit Spread

Section 2.2 established that the stock price and the issuer's credit spread are coupled - through a structural-model-style relationship, a falling stock price tends to coincide with a widening credit spread, since both reflect deteriorating issuer fundamentals. This coupling means the convertible's delta itself depends on the level of the credit spread, giving rise to a cross-greek.

Definition - Delta-Credit Cross-Greek

The cross-greek measuring how delta changes with the credit spread is the mixed second partial derivative: $$\frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial h} = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S \,\partial h}$$ Holding $S$ fixed, a widening of $h$ reduces the bond-floor's contribution to $V$ relative to the conversion option's, which - for a convertible already in the hybrid regime - tends to push delta higher (the convertible behaves more equity-like) even though $S$ itself hasn't moved. Equivalently, as $h$ widens, the effective "kink" between the bond-floor-dominated and conversion-value-dominated regions of $V(S)$ shifts, changing the slope $\partial V/\partial S$ at the current stock price.

Remark - Why the Cross-Greek Matters for Hedging

A delta hedge set at time $t_0$ using $\Delta(S_0, h_0)$ becomes stale not only when $S$ moves (Section 3.1's gamma) but also when $h$ moves with $S$ held fixed - a scenario that the stock-price hedge alone cannot capture. Because $S$ and $h$ are themselves correlated (a falling stock typically accompanies a widening spread), the total change in delta following a credit event is the sum of the direct effect $\Gamma \, dS$ and the cross effect $\frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial h}\,dh$ - and in a sharp credit deterioration, the cross term can be comparable in magnitude to the gamma term, meaning a book hedged only against $\Gamma$ can find itself significantly off-delta purely from a spread move at an unchanged stock price.

Convertible Arbitrage Risk in Stress: The 2008 Unwind

The Greeks above are all local sensitivities - first and second derivatives evaluated at current market levels, assuming the position can be re-hedged continuously and that the hedging instruments (the stock, and in some books, CDS on the issuer) remain liquid. The 2008 convertible arbitrage unwind illustrates how these assumptions can fail simultaneously, even for books that were "delta-, vega-, and CS01-hedged" on a model basis.

Remark - The 2008 Convertible Arbitrage Unwind (Conceptual)

Funding shock. Convertible arbitrage funds typically run leveraged books, financing long convertible positions via repo or prime-broker leverage and shorting stock through borrowed shares. In late 2008, prime-broker balance sheets contracted sharply - margin requirements rose and stock-borrow became scarcer and more expensive - forcing funds to reduce leverage and unwind positions regardless of whether the underlying Greeks still looked attractive.

Cross-greek realised all at once. Credit spreads widened dramatically across nearly all issuers simultaneously, while equity markets fell sharply - exactly the correlated $(dS, dh)$ move described in the cross-greek remark above, but at a magnitude far outside the range over which the local Greeks remain a good approximation. Convertibles' bond floors collapsed in value as spreads widened, even as their conversion options were also falling in value as stocks fell - both legs of $V$ moved against long-convertible positions at once.

Crowded unwind and liquidity feedback. Because many funds ran similar delta-hedged, long-gamma, long-credit strategies, simultaneous forced selling of convertibles (to deleverage) overwhelmed the limited number of natural buyers. Convertible prices fell to levels implying volatilities and credit spreads far above what any fundamental model would justify - i.e., convertibles became cheap to "fair value" by a wide margin, but funds were forced sellers at exactly the wrong time, realising losses that no static Greek hedge could have prevented.

Lesson. A position that is delta-, gamma-, vega-, and CS01-neutral at current market levels is hedged against small, independent moves in each risk factor. It is not hedged against a large, simultaneous, correlated move in several factors at once combined with a funding shock that forces the position to be closed at distressed prices rather than held to recovery - a combination of risks that sits outside the Greek framework entirely but is precisely what materialises in a systemic deleveraging event.
