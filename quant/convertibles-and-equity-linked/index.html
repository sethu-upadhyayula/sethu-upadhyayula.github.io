Convertibles and Equity-Linked Products

Convertible bonds and their relatives sit at the intersection of fixed income, equity derivatives, and credit. A convertible is, at its core, a corporate bond with an embedded call option on the issuer's stock - but pricing and hedging that embedded option correctly requires a model that couples equity, interest-rate, and credit risk, because the issuer's creditworthiness and its stock price are not independent. These notes build up the theory of convertible bonds and equity-linked structures from first principles: their structure and decomposition into bond and option components, the lattice and PDE methods used to value them with their embedded American-style features, the Greeks that describe their risk and the arbitrage strategies built around them, and the broader family of mandatory convertibles, exchangeables, and structured equity-linked notes.

The first chapter introduces the basic structure of a convertible bond - conversion ratio, conversion price, and parity - and decomposes it into a bond floor and an embedded conversion option, before examining the three pricing regimes (bond-like, hybrid, equity-like) and the issuer call and investor put features that complicate valuation. The second chapter develops the binomial and PDE machinery used to value convertibles with these American-style features, and shows how issuer credit risk is incorporated through a hazard-rate model that couples the bond and equity components.

The third chapter covers the Greeks of a convertible - delta, gamma, vega, and credit-spread sensitivity - and the convertible arbitrage strategies built around delta-hedging and gamma trading, including the cross-greeks that made convertible arbitrage funds so vulnerable during the 2008 unwind. The fourth chapter surveys other equity-linked structures: mandatory convertibles and PEPS, exchangeable bonds, and the broader family of equity-linked notes including reverse convertibles and principal-protected structures.
