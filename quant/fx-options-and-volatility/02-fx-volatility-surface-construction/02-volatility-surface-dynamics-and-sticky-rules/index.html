2.2 Volatility Surface Dynamics and Sticky Rules

Sections 2.1 and 1.3 describe how to construct the volatility surface at a snapshot in time. But a trading book is repriced continuously as spot moves, and the volatility surface must move with it - the question is how. Different assumptions about how the surface re-anchors itself as spot changes are called "sticky rules," and the choice between them is not a modelling detail buried in the appendix: it directly determines the realized P&L of a vega-hedged book and the effective delta of an option position.

Three Sticky Rules

Definition - Sticky Strike, Sticky Delta, Sticky Moneyness
  • Sticky strike: the implied volatility for a given absolute strike $K$ stays fixed as spot moves. $\sigma(K)$ is unchanged; $\sigma(\Delta)$ for a fixed delta level changes because the strike corresponding to that delta shifts with spot.
  • Sticky delta (sticky moneyness in delta space): the implied volatility for a given delta (e.g. the 25-delta call) stays fixed as spot moves. $\sigma(\Delta)$ is unchanged; $\sigma(K)$ for a fixed strike changes because that strike now corresponds to a different delta.
  • Sticky moneyness (in strike space): implied volatility is fixed as a function of $K/S_0$ (or $K/F_0$) - i.e. the smile "rides along" with spot when plotted against moneyness, similar in spirit to sticky delta but defined directly via the strike-to-spot ratio rather than delta.
Remark - Which Rule Does the Market Follow?

No single rule holds exactly in practice. Empirically, FX volatility surfaces tend to behave closer to sticky delta (or sticky moneyness) over short horizons and for liquid G10 pairs - the smile shifts with spot so that a given delta point retains roughly the same implied volatility - though the relationship is regime-dependent and can shift toward sticky strike during periods of acute stress when the market re-prices the entire surface around the new spot level rather than smoothly translating it. Many desks calibrate a "sticky-ness" parameter empirically from historical co-movements of spot and the vol surface, or use a local/stochastic volatility model whose dynamics interpolate between the two extremes.

Implications for Delta Hedging

The sticky rule assumption changes the effective delta of an option - the total sensitivity of the option price to spot, including the indirect effect of spot on the implied volatility used to price it.

Proposition - Effective Delta Under Different Sticky Rules

For an option priced at implied volatility $\sigma(K, S_0)$, the total (effective) delta is:

$$\Delta_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S_0}\bigg|_{\sigma \text{ fixed}} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}\cdot\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial S_0} = \Delta_{\text{BS}} + \text{Vega}\cdot\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial S_0}$$

Under sticky strike, $\sigma(K)$ does not depend on $S_0$ for fixed $K$, so $\partial\sigma/\partial S_0 = 0$ and $\Delta_{\text{eff}} = \Delta_{\text{BS}}$ - the plain Black-Scholes delta is the correct hedge ratio. Under sticky delta, as spot rises the smile shifts so that the volatility at the (now higher) original strike changes according to the local slope of the smile in delta space; for a smile with negative skew (puts richer than calls, as is typical with a negative risk reversal), a spot increase typically decreases the volatility at a fixed strike, so $\partial\sigma/\partial S_0 < 0$, and $\Delta_{\text{eff}} \neq \Delta_{\text{BS}}$.

Remark - The Sign and Size of the Correction

For an OTM call on a currency pair with negative skew (negative risk reversal - puts richer than calls), under sticky delta a spot rally tends to reduce the implied volatility at the option's strike (the strike becomes "less OTM" on the call side, moving toward the cheaper part of the smile). Since $\text{Vega} > 0$ always, and $\partial\sigma/\partial S_0 < 0$ in this case, the correction term $\text{Vega}\cdot\partial\sigma/\partial S_0$ is negative - the sticky-delta effective delta is smaller than the plain Black-Scholes delta for this call. A hedger who uses the Black-Scholes delta under a sticky-delta market is therefore over-hedged, and will see a P&L drag from carrying excess spot exposure relative to the option's true sensitivity.

P&L of a Vega-Hedged Book

A book that is vega-hedged - net vega approximately zero across the surface - is not automatically immune to surface dynamics. The realized P&L from a spot move depends on how the surface actually moves relative to the assumption embedded in the hedge.

Example - Sticky-Delta Book, Sticky-Strike Realization

Suppose a trading desk hedges a short risk-reversal position (short OTM call, long OTM put, both 25-delta) assuming sticky-delta dynamics: as spot moves, they re-strike their delta hedge assuming the 25-delta implied volatilities stay constant. Vega exposure to the ATM bucket is hedged separately and assumed flat.

Now suppose spot rallies sharply, but the market in fact realizes sticky-strike dynamics - the implied volatility at each absolute strike stays fixed, meaning the volatility at the (now more OTM, on a delta basis) original call strike has not fallen as the sticky-delta assumption would predict. The desk's short call position, marked under the realized sticky-strike surface, is now more expensive than the sticky-delta hedge assumed - generating a mark-to-market loss on the vega/vanna exposure of the risk reversal that the delta hedge alone cannot offset.

This is the essence of "vanna P&L": even a book with zero net vega and a delta hedge appropriate for one sticky rule will show P&L when the realized surface dynamics differ from the assumed rule, because the cross-sensitivity of vega to spot (vanna) was hedged under the wrong assumption. Quantifying and managing this exposure - vanna and volga risk explicitly - is the subject of Chapter 4.
