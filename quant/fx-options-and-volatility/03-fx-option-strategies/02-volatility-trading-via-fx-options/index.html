3.2 Volatility Trading via FX Options

Beyond static combinations held to expiry, FX options are used to take active positions on volatility itself — trading the gap between the realized volatility that the underlying actually exhibits and the implied volatility paid (or received) when the option was transacted. The key mechanism is the delta-hedged option position, continuously rebalanced, whose P&L decomposes cleanly into a realized-vs-implied volatility spread.

Delta-Hedged Options and the Realized-Implied Spread

Consider buying an option and continuously delta-hedging the position by trading the underlying spot to keep total delta at zero. The P&L of this hedged position over the life of the option, in the idealized continuous-hedging limit, depends only on the difference between realized and implied variance.

Result — P&L of a Delta-Hedged Long Option Position

For a long option continuously delta-hedged, financed at the risk-free rate, the cumulative P&L at expiry (in the Black-Scholes/Garman-Kohlhagen setting) is approximately:

$$\text{P\&L} \approx \frac{1}{2}\int_0^T \Gamma_t S_t^2 \left(\sigma_{\text{realized},t}^2 - \sigma_{\text{implied}}^2\right) dt$$

where $\Gamma_t$ is the option's gamma at time $t$, $\sigma_{\text{implied}}$ is the (constant) volatility at which the option was purchased and hedged, and $\sigma_{\text{realized},t}$ is the instantaneous realized volatility of the underlying. A long delta-hedged option position profits if realized volatility exceeds the implied volatility paid, and loses if realized volatility is lower — weighted at each instant by the gamma-dollar exposure $\Gamma_t S_t^2$.

Remark — Why Gamma Weighting Matters

Gamma is largest for options near the money and close to expiry, so the realized-implied spread matters most precisely when the option is ATM and close to maturity — exactly when delta-hedging activity (and hedging cost from bid-ask spreads on each rebalance) is also highest. This creates a tension: the P&L driver (gamma-weighted variance spread) and the hedging cost driver (rebalancing frequency near expiry) are concentrated in the same region, so the realized P&L of a "long vol" position is sensitive to the discreteness of the hedging schedule, not just the average realized-implied spread.

Gamma Scalping

"Gamma scalping" is the practical implementation of the long-volatility delta-hedging strategy: a trader holding a long-gamma position (e.g., a long straddle) rebalances the delta hedge as spot moves, systematically buying low and selling high relative to the position's evolving delta.

Definition — Gamma Scalping Mechanics

A long straddle has positive gamma: as spot rises, its delta becomes more positive (the call delta rises faster than the put delta falls in magnitude... more precisely the net delta moves from ~0 toward +1), so a delta-neutral hedger must sell spot to re-flatten delta — selling into the rally. As spot falls, the position's delta becomes more negative, so the hedger must buy spot — buying into the decline. Each rebalance, in isolation, is a small profitable trade (sell high, buy low) whose aggregate size over the life of the position approximates the realized-variance term in the P&L formula above.

Remark — Scalping in FX Specifically

FX spot markets are deep and trade continuously (barring weekend closures), making gamma scalping operationally easier in FX than in many equity or commodity markets — rebalancing can happen at any time, in large size, with tight bid-ask spreads for G10 pairs. This is one reason FX volatility is often considered a "cleaner" expression of a realized-vs-implied view than markets where hedging is constrained by trading hours or liquidity. However, the gamma-scalping P&L is path-dependent in practice (not just dependent on total realized variance) because of the discreteness of hedging — high-frequency "whipsaw" moves that revert can produce more scalping P&L than the same total variance realized as one smooth trend, all else equal.

Calendar Spreads and the Term Structure of Volatility

Where a straddle or strangle expresses a view on the level of volatility at a single tenor, a calendar spread — long an option at one maturity and short an option (often at the same strike/delta) at a different maturity — expresses a view on the term structure of implied volatility, i.e., how implied volatility differs across tenors.

Definition — Calendar Spread

A long calendar spread is long an option with maturity $T_2$ and short an option with maturity $T_1 < T_2$, both at the same (or matched) delta. The position's net vega is the difference between the vegas of the two legs; because vega generally increases with maturity (for ATM options, vega scales roughly with $\sqrt{T}$), a long calendar spread is typically net long vega, with the magnitude depending on the relative vegas of the two legs.

Example — Trading a Term-Structure View

Suppose the 1-month ATM implied volatility for USD/JPY is $\sigma_1 = 9.0\%$ and the 3-month ATM implied volatility is $\sigma_2 = 8.0\%$ — an inverted term structure, often seen when the market is pricing elevated near-term event risk (e.g., an upcoming central bank meeting) that is expected to fade.

A trader who believes the near-term elevated volatility is overpriced relative to the medium term — i.e., that the 1-month/3-month spread will normalize (1-month vol will fall, or 3-month vol will rise, or both) — can sell the 1-month ATM straddle and buy the 3-month ATM straddle in vega-weighted proportions, constructing a position that is approximately vega-neutral overall but profits if the term structure flattens or inverts less.

The key risk to this trade is that it is not gamma-neutral: the short-dated leg has higher gamma per unit of vega, so the position has negative net gamma even while being vega-neutral. If the near-term event causes a large realized spot move (rather than just an implied-vol repricing), the gamma-scalping losses on the short 1-month leg can dominate the intended term-structure P&L — a reminder that "vega-neutral" does not mean "risk-neutral" across all Greeks, a theme developed further in Chapter 4.
