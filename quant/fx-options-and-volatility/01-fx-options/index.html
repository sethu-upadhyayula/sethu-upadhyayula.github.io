1. FX Options

This chapter covers the pricing and risk management of vanilla FX options. Unlike equity options, FX options involve two interest rates — domestic and foreign — and the underlying is itself an exchange rate, so a "call" in one currency pair is simultaneously a "put" in the inverted pair. We begin with the Garman-Kohlhagen model, the FX analogue of Black-Scholes, then turn to the delta and hedging conventions used to quote options in the FX market, and finally to the construction of the implied volatility surface from at-the-money, risk reversal, and butterfly quotes.

These conventions are not arbitrary: they reflect the practical reality that FX options are quoted and traded by delta rather than by strike, and that the volatility surface is parameterised in a way that directly encodes the market's view on skew (directional bias) and smile (convexity) for each currency pair and tenor.
