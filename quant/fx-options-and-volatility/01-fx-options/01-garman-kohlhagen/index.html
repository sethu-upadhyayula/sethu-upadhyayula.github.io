1.1 Garman-Kohlhagen Model

The Garman-Kohlhagen model (1983) extends the Black-Scholes framework to options on exchange rates. The key insight is that holding the foreign currency is analogous to holding a stock that pays a continuous dividend yield: the dividend yield is simply the foreign risk-free rate $r_f$, since an investor holding one unit of the foreign currency earns interest on it at rate $r_f$. The domestic risk-free rate $r_d$ plays the role of the discount rate, exactly as $r$ does in Black-Scholes.

Definition — Garman-Kohlhagen Formula

Let $S_0$ be the spot exchange rate (units of domestic currency per unit of foreign currency), $K$ the strike, $T$ the time to expiry, $r_d$ the domestic risk-free rate, $r_f$ the foreign risk-free rate, and $\sigma$ the volatility of the exchange rate. The price of a European call option to buy one unit of foreign currency at strike $K$ is:

$$c = S_0 e^{-r_f T} N(d_1) - K e^{-r_d T} N(d_2)$$

and the price of the corresponding put is:

$$p = K e^{-r_d T} N(-d_2) - S_0 e^{-r_f T} N(-d_1)$$

where

$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + (r_d - r_f + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$

This is structurally identical to the Black-Scholes-Merton formula for a stock paying a continuous dividend yield $q$, with the substitution $q \to r_f$. Both legs of the formula carry an explicit discount factor: $e^{-r_f T}$ discounts the foreign-currency notional (which itself earns $r_f$ until delivery), and $e^{-r_d T}$ discounts the domestic-currency strike payment.

Remark — Forward Formulation

Recalling covered interest rate parity, the FX forward rate is $F_0 = S_0 e^{(r_d-r_f)T}$. Substituting gives the equivalent Black-76 style form:

$$c = e^{-r_d T}\left[F_0 N(d_1) - K N(d_2)\right], \qquad d_1 = \frac{\ln(F_0/K) + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$$

This form is often more convenient in practice because $F_0$ is directly observable from the forward market, avoiding the need to separately specify $S_0$, $r_d$, and $r_f$.

Put-Call Parity for FX Options

Put-call parity follows from a simple static replication argument: a long call plus a short put with the same strike and maturity replicates a forward contract to buy the foreign currency at $K$.

Theorem — FX Put-Call Parity

For European call and put options with the same strike $K$ and maturity $T$ on the same currency pair:

$$c - p = S_0 e^{-r_f T} - K e^{-r_d T}$$

Equivalently, $c - p = e^{-r_d T}(F_0 - K)$, the discounted value of the forward contract struck at $K$.

Currency Pair Symmetry

An exchange rate is a relative price, and every FX option can be viewed from either currency's perspective. A call option giving the right to buy one unit of foreign currency (CCY1) for $K$ units of domestic currency (CCY2) is, from the perspective of a CCY2-based investor, a put option giving the right to sell CCY2 for CCY1 — but the notional and strike must be re-expressed in the inverted quoting convention.

Proposition — Call/Put Duality (Numéraire Symmetry)

A EUR call / USD put on EUR/USD with strike $K$ (USD per EUR) and notional $N$ EUR, struck so the payoff is $N\max(S_T - K, 0)$ in USD, is equivalent to a position of $NK$ USD-denominated USD calls / EUR puts on USD/EUR struck at $1/K$, with payoff:

$$NK \max\left(\frac{1}{K} - \frac{1}{S_T}, 0\right) \text{ EUR}$$

This identity — that "a call on X/Y is a put on Y/X" — is fundamental to FX options trading, because it allows a single book of vanilla options to be quoted, hedged, and risk-managed consistently regardless of which currency is treated as the "base" for pricing purposes. The Garman-Kohlhagen formula applied to USD/EUR (swapping the roles of $r_d$ and $r_f$, and using $1/S_0$ and $1/K$) reproduces the same value, converted at the appropriate rate.

Worked Example

Example — Pricing a EUR/USD Call

Consider a 6-month European call option on EUR/USD with the following inputs:

  • Spot $S_0 = 1.0850$
  • Strike $K = 1.1000$
  • Domestic (USD) rate $r_d = 5.00\%$
  • Foreign (EUR) rate $r_f = 3.50\%$
  • Volatility $\sigma = 9.0\%$
  • Maturity $T = 0.5$

First compute $d_1$ and $d_2$:

$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(1.0850/1.1000) + (0.05 - 0.035 + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.09)^2)(0.5)}{0.09\sqrt{0.5}} = \frac{-0.01373 + 0.00951}{0.06364} \approx -0.0664$$ $$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T} = -0.0664 - 0.0636 \approx -0.1300$$

Using $N(-0.0664) \approx 0.4735$ and $N(-0.1300) \approx 0.4483$:

$$c = 1.0850\,e^{-0.035(0.5)}(0.4735) - 1.1000\,e^{-0.05(0.5)}(0.4483)$$ $$c \approx 1.0660(0.4735) - 1.0728(0.4483) \approx 0.5048 - 0.4810 \approx 0.0238$$

The call is worth approximately 0.0238 USD per EUR of notional — i.e. about 238 pips, or for a notional of €10,000,000, a premium of approximately $238,000.
