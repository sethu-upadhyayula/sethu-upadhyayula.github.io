3.1 Black-76 Model

Most exchange-traded commodity options are written not on the spot commodity itself, but on a futures contract. A crude oil call option, for example, gives the holder the right to enter a long futures position at the strike price, not to buy a barrel of oil for physical delivery. This single feature — the underlying being a futures price rather than a spot price — is what makes the Black-76 model (also called the Black model) the standard pricing framework across commodity options markets.

The Futures Price as the Underlying

Recall that the futures price $F(t,T)$ for delivery at $T$ already incorporates the cost-of-carry relationship to the spot price $S_t$: financing costs, storage costs, and convenience yield are all embedded in $F(t,T)$. An option on the futures contract therefore does not need to separately model these carry terms — they are already priced into the underlying.

Definition — Futures Price Dynamics under the Forward Measure

Under the $T$-forward (or futures) measure $\mathbb{Q}^T$, the futures price $F(t,T)$ for a contract maturing at $T$ is a martingale: $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^T}[F(t_2,T) \mid \mathcal{F}_{t_1}] = F(t_1,T)$ for $t_1 < t_2 \le T$. Assuming $F(t,T)$ follows geometric Brownian motion with zero drift and constant volatility $\sigma$, $$dF(t,T) = \sigma F(t,T)\, dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}^T}.$$

This is the key simplification relative to the Black-Scholes setting for equities: there is no risk-free rate $r$ or dividend yield $q$ appearing in the drift of $F$, because the futures price is already a "pre-discounted, carry-adjusted" quantity. The only role of the interest rate is in discounting the option's terminal payoff back to today.

The Black-76 Formula

Theorem — Black-76 Pricing Formula

Consider a European call option with strike $K$ and maturity $T$ on a futures contract with current price $F = F(t,T)$, where the futures price has volatility $\sigma$ and $r$ is the continuously-compounded risk-free rate to maturity $T$. The price of the call is

$$c = e^{-rT}\left[F\,N(d_1) - K\,N(d_2)\right]$$

and the corresponding European put is

$$p = e^{-rT}\left[K\,N(-d_2) - F\,N(-d_1)\right]$$

where

$$d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(F/K) \pm \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}.$$

Derivation Sketch

The derivation mirrors the Black-Scholes argument but is carried out under the $T$-forward measure, in which the discount bond $P(t,T)$ is the numéraire. Because $F(t,T)$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}^T$ with zero drift, the call payoff $\max(F(T,T)-K,0) = \max(F_T - K, 0)$ has expected value (under $\mathbb{Q}^T$) given exactly by the Black-Scholes formula applied to a "stock" with current price $F$, zero dividend yield, and zero risk-free rate (since the drift is zero under this measure):

Proof — Sketch via Change of Measure

The price of the call today is $c = P(t,T)\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^T}\left[\max(F_T - K, 0)\right]$. Since $F_T = F\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 T + \sigma\sqrt{T}Z\right)$ for $Z \sim N(0,1)$ under $\mathbb{Q}^T$, the expectation $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^T}[\max(F_T-K,0)]$ is computed exactly as in the Black-Scholes derivation, yielding $F\,N(d_1) - K\,N(d_2)$ with $d_{1,2}$ as above.

Multiplying by the discount factor $P(t,T) = e^{-rT}$ (assuming a flat continuously-compounded rate) gives $c = e^{-rT}[F\,N(d_1) - K\,N(d_2)]$, as claimed. $\blacksquare$

Put-Call Parity for Futures Options

Theorem — Put-Call Parity (Black-76)

For European call and put options on the same futures contract with identical strike $K$ and maturity $T$: $$c - p = e^{-rT}(F - K).$$ This follows because a long call and short put with the same strike replicate a long forward position on the futures contract struck at $K$, whose value is $e^{-rT}(F-K)$ — the discounted difference between the current futures price and the strike, since entering a futures position itself requires no upfront payment.

Comparison with Black-Scholes

Remark — Black-76 vs. Black-Scholes

The Black-Scholes formula for a European call on a non-dividend-paying stock is $c = S_0 N(d_1) - Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)$ with $d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + (r \pm \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}$. Black-76 can be obtained from Black-Scholes by the substitution $S_0 \to F e^{-rT}$ (i.e., treating the futures price as if it were a forward price on a stock paying a continuous dividend yield equal to $r$, so that the prepaid forward price is $Fe^{-rT}$).

Practically, the advantage of Black-76 is that it requires only the observable futures price $F$, not the spot price $S_0$ together with carry assumptions ($r$, storage costs, convenience yield). Since futures prices trade liquidly and transparently while spot prices for many commodities are illiquid or non-existent as tradeable instruments, Black-76 sidesteps the need to model the spot-futures relationship explicitly when pricing options.

Worked Example: Crude Oil Call Option

Example — Pricing a WTI Crude Oil Call

Consider a European call option on the WTI crude oil futures contract with the following parameters:

$F = \$80.00$ per barrel (current futures price), $K = \$85.00$ (strike), $T = 0.25$ years (3 months to expiry), $\sigma = 35\%$ (annualised implied volatility), $r = 5\%$ (continuously-compounded risk-free rate).

First compute $d_1$ and $d_2$:

$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(80/85) + \frac{1}{2}(0.35)^2(0.25)}{0.35\sqrt{0.25}} = \frac{-0.06062 + 0.01531}{0.175} = \frac{-0.04531}{0.175} \approx -0.2589$$

$$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T} = -0.2589 - 0.175 \approx -0.4339$$

From the standard normal CDF: $N(d_1) \approx N(-0.2589) \approx 0.3979$ and $N(d_2) \approx N(-0.4339) \approx 0.3322$.

The call price is then:

$$c = e^{-0.05 \times 0.25}\left[80 \times 0.3979 - 85 \times 0.3322\right] = e^{-0.0125}\left[31.832 - 28.237\right]$$

$$c \approx 0.9876 \times 3.595 \approx \$3.55 \text{ per barrel}.$$

This call is out-of-the-money ($F < K$), so its value reflects purely time value driven by volatility — there is meaningful probability that the futures price will rise above $85 by expiry given a 35% annualised volatility over three months. Note that the discount factor $e^{-rT}$ plays a relatively minor role compared to the spread between $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$, which captures the option's moneyness and the volatility-driven probability of exercise.
