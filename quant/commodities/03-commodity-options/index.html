3. Commodity Options

Options on commodity futures are among the most actively traded derivatives in energy, metals, and agricultural markets. Producers use them to set price floors on future production, consumers use them to cap input costs, and speculators use them to express directional or volatility views with defined risk.

The crucial pricing distinction in commodity options markets is that the underlying instrument is almost always a futures contract, not the physical spot commodity. Because a futures price already embeds the cost-of-carry relationship between spot and forward prices, the option pricing problem simplifies: under the appropriate forward measure, the futures price is a martingale with no drift term to worry about. This is precisely the insight behind the Black-76 model, which has become the market standard for pricing options on futures across virtually every commodity asset class — from crude oil and natural gas to gold and agricultural products.

This chapter develops the Black-76 framework from first principles, examines how it relates to the Black-Scholes model for equity options, and then turns to the empirical reality of commodity options markets: implied volatility surfaces that exhibit pronounced skew, seasonality, and shapes that are often very different from those observed in equity or interest rate markets.
