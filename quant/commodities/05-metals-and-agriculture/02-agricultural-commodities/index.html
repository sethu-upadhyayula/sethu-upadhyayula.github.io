5.2 Agricultural Commodities

Agricultural commodities — grains, oilseeds, and "softs" such as coffee, sugar, and cotton — are produced through biological processes governed by planting and harvest cycles, and are therefore subject to a degree of seasonality and weather sensitivity unmatched by metals or even most energy commodities. This section ties together the seasonal storage dynamics introduced in the convenience yield discussion with the specific institutional features of grain and soft commodity markets.

Grain Markets: Corn, Wheat, and Soybeans

Definition — The Major Row Crops

Corn is used primarily as livestock feed and, in the U.S., as a feedstock for ethanol production — a use that links corn prices to energy markets via biofuel mandates and the relative economics of ethanol versus gasoline.

Wheat is grown in multiple varieties (winter wheat, spring wheat, hard red, soft red, etc.) across many growing regions globally, making global wheat supply highly dependent on weather conditions in several major producing countries (U.S., Russia, Ukraine, EU, Australia, Argentina) simultaneously.

Soybeans are crushed into soybean meal (animal feed) and soybean oil (cooking oil and biodiesel feedstock); the relationship between the soybean price and the combined value of its crush products is captured by the "crush spread," analogous in spirit to the crack spread in oil refining.

Seasonality: Planting and Harvest Cycles

Unlike oil or metals, where production can in principle be adjusted relatively continuously, grain production occurs in discrete annual (or, in the case of some Southern Hemisphere crops, semi-annual) cycles dictated by growing seasons. This produces a highly characteristic seasonal pattern in futures curves and price volatility.

Remark — The Annual Crop Cycle

Planting season: prices are sensitive to planting intentions and early-season weather, since adverse conditions can reduce planted acreage or delay planting, with knock-on effects for the eventual harvest.

Growing season: this is typically the period of highest price volatility, since the size of the eventual harvest remains uncertain and is highly sensitive to weather (drought, excess rainfall, temperature extremes during critical pollination or flowering windows).

Harvest: as the crop is harvested and supply uncertainty resolves, prices typically decline (the "harvest low"), and futures curves often exhibit a seasonal pattern where new-crop contracts (for delivery after harvest) trade at a discount to old-crop contracts (for delivery before the new harvest is available), reflecting the step-change in available supply at harvest time.

Storage Costs and Convenience Yield Revisited

The convenience yield framework introduced for general commodities applies with particular force to grains, where the entire futures curve within a crop year reflects the cost of carrying inventory from harvest until the next harvest.

Example — Carry Within a Crop Year

Immediately after a large harvest, grain elevators are full and the convenience yield on holding additional inventory is low — there is little benefit to holding extra physical grain when supply is abundant. The futures curve for contracts maturing later in the crop year typically reflects close to full carry: $F(t,T) \approx S_t e^{(r+u)(T-t)}$, where $u$ is the physical storage cost (elevator fees), since $y \approx 0$.

As the crop year progresses and stocks are drawn down toward the next harvest, the convenience yield $y$ rises — holders of physical grain value having supply on hand to meet processing or feeding needs without relying on the (increasingly uncertain) availability of new-crop supply. If stocks become tight enough late in the crop year (a "old-crop/new-crop" supply squeeze), the old-crop contract can move into backwardation relative to the new-crop contract, reflecting $y > r + u$.

Weather Risk

Remark — Weather as the Dominant Supply Shock

Because there is essentially one opportunity per year (per hemisphere) to produce a given crop, weather events during critical growth windows — drought during pollination, excess rain delaying harvest, an early frost — can have outsized and immediate effects on price, since there is no possibility of a rapid supply response analogous to, say, OPEC+ adjusting output or a shale producer drilling additional wells. This makes agricultural futures and options markets highly responsive to weather forecasts during the growing season, and explains the prominence of weather-related news in driving short-term grain price volatility.

Softs: Coffee, Sugar, and Cotton

Remark — Characteristics of Soft Commodities

Coffee (arabica traded on ICE, robusta on ICE Europe) is produced primarily in tropical regions (Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia) and is particularly sensitive to frost events in Brazil's growing regions, which can damage trees and affect production for multiple subsequent harvests due to the multi-year growth cycle of coffee plants.

Sugar markets are heavily influenced by Brazilian production decisions, since Brazilian mills can flexibly allocate sugarcane between sugar production and ethanol production depending on relative prices — linking sugar to both food and energy markets.

Cotton demand is tied to textile manufacturing and apparel demand globally, with China, India, and other major textile-producing countries representing key sources of demand, while production is concentrated among a smaller number of major growing countries (U.S., India, China, Brazil).

USDA Reports as Market-Moving Events

Definition — Key USDA Reports

WASDE (World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates): a monthly report providing the U.S. Department of Agriculture's estimates of supply, demand, and ending stocks for major U.S. and global agricultural commodities. WASDE releases are among the most closely watched scheduled events in agricultural markets and routinely produce sharp price moves when actual figures deviate from analyst expectations.

Acreage and Planting Intentions reports: released in spring, these surveys of farmer planting intentions provide the market's first concrete estimate of how much land will be devoted to each crop in the coming season, directly affecting expectations for the size of the eventual harvest.

Crop Progress reports: released weekly during the growing season, these report the percentage of the crop at each stage of development (planted, emerged, in critical growth stages, harvested) relative to historical averages, allowing the market to track whether the season is running ahead of or behind a normal pace.

Because these reports are released on a fixed schedule and contain information that can materially shift supply/demand balances, options markets often price elevated implied volatility in the days leading up to a major USDA release, with volatility collapsing ("crushing") once the report is published and its information is incorporated into prices.
