4.2 Volatility Surface Calibration and Risk

The vega introduced in Section 4.1 is computed against the implied volatility of a single option, but the inputs to a desk's pricing of caps, floors, and swaptions are not individual volatilities - they are the parameters of a calibrated model, most commonly SABR (Section 1.3), fit across the entire cap/floor and swaption volatility cube (Section 1.2). This closing section covers how that calibration is performed across the surface, how the resulting vega risk is bucketed for reporting and hedging, and the model risk inherent in relying on a calibrated parametric model for both pricing and risk.

These three topics - surface-wide calibration, vega bucketing, and model risk - are where the pricing models of Chapters 1-2, the exotic structures of Chapter 3, and the Greeks of Section 4.1 all come together in the daily operation of a real interest rate options book: every price, every Greek, and every hedge ultimately traces back to a calibrated surface that must be refit, monitored, and stress-tested continuously.

Calibrating SABR Across the Cube

Section 1.3 described SABR calibration for a single expiry/tenor slice: given market vols at several strikes for fixed $(f,T)$, find $(\alpha,\rho,\nu)$ (with $\beta$ fixed) that best fit Hagan's formula. A full surface or cube calibration repeats this independently for every $(T_0,\tau)$ pair in the grid.

Definition - Slice-by-Slice Calibration

For each expiry/tenor pair $(T_0^{(j)}, \tau^{(k)})$ in the quoted grid, with forward $f_{jk}$ and market smile $\{\sigma_{\text{mkt}}(K_i; T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)})\}_i$, calibrate independent SABR parameters: $$(\hat\alpha_{jk},\hat\rho_{jk},\hat\nu_{jk}) = \arg\min_{\alpha,\rho,\nu}\sum_i w_i\left(\sigma_B(K_i;f_{jk},T_0^{(j)};\alpha,\beta,\rho,\nu)-\sigma_{\text{mkt}}(K_i;T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)})\right)^2$$ with $\beta$ typically fixed at a single value across the entire cube (chosen as discussed in Section 1.3's backbone remark). The result is a grid of $(\hat\alpha_{jk},\hat\rho_{jk},\hat\nu_{jk})$ triples, one per $(T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)})$ cell, from which the full continuous surface $\sigma(T_0,\tau,K)$ is obtained by interpolating the parameters (or the resulting implied vols) smoothly between grid points.

Remark - Smoothness Across Slices

Independent per-slice calibration can produce SABR parameters that jump discontinuously between adjacent $(T_0,\tau)$ cells even when the underlying market quotes change smoothly - an artefact of the per-slice optimisation landing in different local optima, or of small calibration noise being amplified by the nonlinear map from quotes to SABR parameters. Production calibration routines typically add a regularisation term penalising large parameter differences between neighbouring cells, or calibrate a small number of global parameters (e.g. a single $\nu(\tau)$ term-structure shared across expiries for each tenor) on top of per-cell $\alpha$, trading off a marginally worse fit to today's quotes for a much more stable, better-behaved surface - which matters enormously for the vega bucketing described next, since a noisy surface produces noisy (and untradeable) bucketed vega numbers.

Vega Bucketing Across Expiry and Tenor

A book's aggregate vega (Section 4.1) is a single number, but a single number cannot tell a trader whether the book's volatility exposure is concentrated in, say, 1-year options on 10-year swaps versus 10-year options on 2-year swaps - two very different risks that might net to a similar aggregate vega while requiring entirely different hedges.

Definition - Vega Bucket

For a book of options with positions $\{V_p\}$, the vega bucket at grid point $(T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)})$ is: $$\text{VegaBucket}(T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)}) = \sum_p \frac{\partial V_p}{\partial \sigma(T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)})}$$ i.e. the sensitivity of the book's total value to a bump of the implied volatility at that single grid point (with all other grid points held fixed, and the SABR parameters at that cell re-implied to match). Summing $\text{VegaBucket}(T_0^{(j)},\tau^{(k)})$ over all $(j,k)$ recovers (approximately) the aggregate vega, but the bucketed view shows the expiry/tenor distribution of that exposure.

Remark - Bucketing in Practice

Computing a full bucketed vega grid by direct bump-and-reprice - bumping each of, say, 100 grid points one at a time and repricing the entire book each time - is computationally expensive for a large book. In practice, desks often compute vega with respect to the underlying SABR parameters $(\alpha_{jk},\rho_{jk},\nu_{jk})$ at each cell (a smaller set of bumps), then use the chain rule through the calibration to translate parameter sensitivities back into implied-vol-bucket sensitivities. Alternatively, adjoint algorithmic differentiation (AAD) computes all bucketed sensitivities in roughly the cost of a small constant multiple of one full repricing, regardless of the number of buckets - a technique now standard in large rates books precisely because the number of vega buckets (expiry $\times$ tenor $\times$ strike, potentially hundreds of points) makes naive bump-and-reprice infeasible to run intraday.

The bucketed vega grid is the primary input to the desk's hedging decisions: a concentration of vega in the 5y10y cell, for example, would be hedged by trading 5y10y swaptions (or a closely correlated neighbouring cell) specifically, rather than by an instrument that only moves the aggregate level of volatility.

Model Risk in Calibration

SABR (or any parametric smile model) is chosen because it fits today's market prices well with few parameters - but "fits today's prices" is a backward-looking criterion, and the risk numbers (Greeks, including vega buckets) computed from the calibrated model are forward-looking statements about how the book will behave as the market moves. The gap between these two roles is the essence of model risk.

Remark - Sources of Model Risk in a Calibrated Surface

Smile dynamics risk. As discussed in Section 1.3, the choice of $\beta$ (and the resulting backbone) determines how the model predicts the smile will move when the forward rate moves. If the realised dynamics differ from what the calibrated model predicts - e.g. the market exhibits more "sticky-strike" behaviour than the calibrated $\beta$ implies - then delta-hedges computed from the model will be systematically biased, showing up as a recurring P&L drag (or gain) that is not captured by the model's own Greeks.

Calibration instability. As noted above, independently calibrated parameters can be noisy from day to day even when underlying quotes move smoothly, especially for less liquid cells (long expiries, extreme tenors, or far-from-the-money strikes where few or no market quotes exist and the calibration is essentially extrapolating). Vega buckets computed from an unstable calibration can show large day-to-day swings that reflect calibration noise rather than genuine changes in market risk - a problem that regularisation (above) mitigates but does not eliminate.

Extrapolation and arbitrage. Section 1.3 noted that Hagan's formula can produce arbitrageable (non-monotonic) implied densities for extreme strikes or expiries. A book with exotic positions (Chapter 3) whose payoffs depend on tail strikes or on the joint behaviour of multiple rates (CMS spread correlation, Bermudan exercise boundaries) is especially exposed to whatever the calibrated model assumes in regions where it has little or no market data to calibrate against - model risk here is less about a single mispriced vega and more about the entire shape of the model's extrapolation being essentially a modelling choice, dressed up as a calibration result. Robust risk management requires stress-testing positions against plausible alternative model choices (different $\beta$, different extrapolation schemes, different assumed correlations) rather than relying on a single calibrated surface as ground truth.
