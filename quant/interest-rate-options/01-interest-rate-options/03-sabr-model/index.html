1.3 SABR Model

Black's model prices every option on a given underlying with a single volatility parameter, but market prices for caps, floors, and swaptions exhibit a volatility smile: implied volatility varies systematically with strike. The SABR ("Stochastic Alpha, Beta, Rho") model, introduced by Hagan, Kumar, Lesniewski, and Woodward (2002), is the industry-standard parametric model for capturing this smile in a way that is simple to calibrate and yields an analytic approximation for implied volatility.

SABR Dynamics

SABR models the joint evolution of a forward rate $F(t)$ (e.g., a forward LIBOR/SOFR rate or forward swap rate) and its instantaneous volatility $\alpha(t)$ as a pair of correlated stochastic processes under the relevant forward/annuity measure.

Definition - SABR Model

Under the appropriate forward measure, the forward rate $F_t$ and stochastic volatility $\alpha_t$ evolve as: $$dF_t = \alpha_t \, F_t^{\beta} \, dW_t^{(1)}$$ $$d\alpha_t = \nu \, \alpha_t \, dW_t^{(2)}$$ $$dW_t^{(1)} dW_t^{(2)} = \rho \, dt$$ with initial conditions $F_0 = f$ (today's forward rate) and $\alpha_0 = \alpha$. The model has four parameters:

  • $\alpha > 0$: the initial level of volatility (sets the overall ATM vol level);
  • $\beta \in [0,1]$: the CEV exponent, controlling the "backbone" - how the ATM volatility itself moves as $F$ moves;
  • $\rho \in [-1,1]$: correlation between rate moves and volatility moves, controlling the skew (asymmetry of the smile);
  • $\nu > 0$: the "vol-of-vol", controlling the curvature (smile convexity).
Remark - Special Cases of $\beta$

$\beta = 1$ recovers lognormal (Black-type) dynamics for $F_t$ in the absence of stochastic vol; $\beta = 0$ recovers normal (Bachelier-type) dynamics. $\beta = 0.5$ is a common choice for rates (a "square-root" CEV process, similar to CIR). In practice $\beta$ is often fixed exogenously (e.g., $\beta=0.5$ or $\beta=1$) based on historical analysis of how ATM vols move with rate levels, and $\alpha, \rho, \nu$ are calibrated to the smile, since $\beta$ and $\rho$ are not jointly identifiable from a single smile snapshot - both primarily control skew.

Hagan's Asymptotic Implied Volatility Formula

The SABR SDEs do not admit a closed-form density in general, but Hagan et al. derived a highly accurate asymptotic expansion for the Black implied volatility produced by the model, valid for short maturities and parameters not too far from typical market values.

Theorem - Hagan's SABR Implied Volatility (Lognormal)

For strike $K$, forward $f$, and time to expiry $T$, the Black implied volatility under SABR is approximately: $$\sigma_B(K,f) = \frac{\alpha}{(fK)^{(1-\beta)/2}\left[1 + \frac{(1-\beta)^2}{24}\ln^2(f/K) + \frac{(1-\beta)^4}{1920}\ln^4(f/K)\right]} \cdot \frac{z}{\chi(z)} \cdot \left[1 + \left(\frac{(1-\beta)^2\alpha^2}{24(fK)^{1-\beta}} + \frac{\rho\beta\nu\alpha}{4(fK)^{(1-\beta)/2}} + \frac{2-3\rho^2}{24}\nu^2\right)T\right]$$ where $$z = \frac{\nu}{\alpha}(fK)^{(1-\beta)/2}\ln(f/K), \qquad \chi(z) = \ln\left(\frac{\sqrt{1-2\rho z + z^2}+z-\rho}{1-\rho}\right)$$ For the at-the-money case $K=f$, this simplifies to: $$\sigma_B(f,f) \approx \frac{\alpha}{f^{1-\beta}}\left[1 + \left(\frac{(1-\beta)^2\alpha^2}{24f^{2-2\beta}} + \frac{\rho\beta\nu\alpha}{4f^{1-\beta}} + \frac{2-3\rho^2}{24}\nu^2\right)T\right]$$

The formula expresses the implied volatility of any strike directly in terms of the four SABR parameters and the model inputs $(f,T)$, allowing the entire smile for a given expiry/tenor to be generated from just $(\alpha,\beta,\rho,\nu)$.

Fitting SABR to the Volatility Smile

Calibration takes observed market implied volatilities at several strikes for a given expiry/tenor and finds the SABR parameters that best reproduce them.

Definition - SABR Calibration

Given market quotes $\{\sigma_{\text{mkt}}(K_j)\}_{j=1}^m$ at strikes $K_1,\ldots,K_m$ for fixed $(f,T)$, and with $\beta$ typically fixed exogenously, calibration solves: $$(\hat\alpha,\hat\rho,\hat\nu) = \arg\min_{\alpha,\rho,\nu} \sum_{j=1}^m w_j \left(\sigma_B(K_j; \alpha,\beta,\rho,\nu) - \sigma_{\text{mkt}}(K_j)\right)^2$$ using a nonlinear least-squares routine (e.g., Levenberg-Marquardt), with weights $w_j$ often chosen to emphasise near-the-money strikes where liquidity is highest. A common refinement is to calibrate $\alpha$ analytically by inverting the ATM formula given a target ATM vol, then optimise only $(\rho,\nu)$ over the off-ATM strikes - this guarantees an exact fit to the (most liquid and important) ATM point.

Example - Typical SABR Smile Shapes

For a swap rate near $f = 4\%$ with $\beta = 0.5$ fixed: a positive $\rho$ (e.g., $\rho = 0.3$) produces a smile that is upward-sloping in strike (call skew) - typical of some commodity or inflation markets. A negative $\rho$ (e.g., $\rho=-0.3$, more typical of rates markets, reflecting that vol tends to rise when rates fall) produces a downward-sloping smile - higher implied vols for low-strike (receiver/floor) options than for high-strike (payer/cap) options.

Increasing $\nu$ (vol-of-vol) steepens the curvature on both wings symmetrically, producing a more pronounced "smile" (U-shape) regardless of the sign of $\rho$. $\alpha$ primarily shifts the overall level of the curve up or down without much affecting its shape.

The Backbone and Smile Dynamics

A crucial practical consideration is not just how SABR fits today's smile, but how the fitted smile is predicted to move when the underlying forward rate $f$ itself moves - this is the model's "smile dynamics," governed primarily by the exponent $\beta$.

Definition - Backbone

The backbone is the locus traced out by the ATM volatility $\sigma_B(f,f)$ as $f$ varies, holding $(\alpha,\beta,\rho,\nu)$ fixed. From the ATM formula, $\sigma_B(f,f) \approx \alpha f^{\beta-1}$ to leading order, so:

  • $\beta = 1$: ATM vol is roughly constant as $f$ moves (lognormal-type backbone) - "sticky strike"-like behaviour for the ATM point.
  • $\beta = 0$: ATM vol scales like $1/f$ (normal-type backbone) - ATM vol rises sharply as $f \to 0$.
  • $0 < \beta < 1$: intermediate behaviour.
Remark - Managing Smile Dynamics in Practice

The choice of $\beta$ has direct hedging implications: it determines how much of the change in an option's value, when the forward moves, is attributed to delta (movement along a fixed smile) versus a shift of the entire smile (vega-like effects from the backbone). A poorly chosen $\beta$ can cause a SABR-based hedge to systematically mis-predict P&L when the underlying rate moves. Desks typically choose $\beta$ based on a historical regression of changes in ATM implied vol against changes in the forward rate, and re-calibrate $(\alpha,\rho,\nu)$ daily while holding $\beta$ fixed over longer periods for hedging consistency.

Limitations of SABR

Remark - Known Limitations

Hagan's formula is an asymptotic expansion and can produce arbitrage (e.g., a non-monotonic or even negative implied density) for very low strikes, very long expiries, or extreme parameter combinations - particularly when $f$ is close to zero and $\beta < 1$, since $(fK)^{(1-\beta)/2}$ and the $\ln(f/K)$ terms become numerically unstable. Practitioners address this with "free" or "shifted" SABR variants (shifting $F$ and $K$ by a constant before applying the formula) or by switching to an arbitrage-free numerical PDE-based SABR implementation for extreme regimes.

SABR is a single-expiry, single-underlying smile model - it does not by itself describe the joint dynamics of the entire forward curve or provide a consistent framework for pricing path-dependent, multi-rate products such as Bermudan swaptions. For these, term-structure models are required: short-rate models, the HJM framework, or the LIBOR Market Model, often combined with SABR-style dynamics for individual forward rates (SABR-LMM) - the subject of Chapter 5.
