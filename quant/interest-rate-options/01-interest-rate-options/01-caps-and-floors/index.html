1.1 Caps and Floors

Caps and floors are the most basic interest rate options. A cap protects a floating-rate borrower against rising rates by paying out whenever a reference rate exceeds a fixed strike; a floor protects a floating-rate lender against falling rates by paying out whenever the reference rate falls below a strike. Each is a portfolio of simpler building blocks — caplets and floorlets — each of which is an option on a single forward rate.

Caplets and Floorlets

Consider a future accrual period $[T_i, T_{i+1}]$ with day count fraction $\delta_i = T_{i+1} - T_i$, governed by the forward rate $F_i(t) = F(t; T_i, T_{i+1})$ observed at time $t \leq T_i$ and set (fixed) at $T_i$.

Definition — Caplet and Floorlet

A caplet on the period $[T_i,T_{i+1}]$ with strike $K$ pays at time $T_{i+1}$ the amount: $$\text{Caplet payoff} = \delta_i \cdot N \cdot \max(F_i(T_i) - K, 0)$$ where $N$ is the notional. A floorlet pays: $$\text{Floorlet payoff} = \delta_i \cdot N \cdot \max(K - F_i(T_i), 0)$$ A caplet is therefore a call option on the forward rate $F_i$, struck at $K$, with the option payoff scaled by the accrual factor $\delta_i$ and paid in arrears at $T_{i+1}$. A floorlet is the corresponding put.

Definition — Cap and Floor

A cap with strike $K$, notional $N$, and tenor structure $T_0 < T_1 < \cdots < T_n$ is a portfolio of caplets, one for each accrual period $[T_{i},T_{i+1}]$, $i = 0, \ldots, n-1$: $$\text{Cap} = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \text{Caplet}_i(K)$$ A floor is the analogous portfolio of floorlets. The first caplet (covering $[T_0,T_1]$) is sometimes excluded if $F_0(T_0)$ is already known at trade inception — such a cap is called a "cap excluding the first caplet."

Remark — Interpretation

A borrower paying a floating rate plus a spread can buy a cap to put a ceiling on their effective borrowing cost: if the floating rate exceeds $K$, the caplet payoff offsets the excess interest. Symmetrically, an investor receiving floating can buy a floor to guarantee a minimum return. Caps and floors are therefore the interest-rate analogue of a strip of European call/put options on a sequence of forward rates.

Black's Model for Caplet Pricing

The market-standard pricing model for caplets treats the forward rate $F_i(t)$ as a lognormal martingale under the $T_{i+1}$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_{i+1}}$ (the measure associated with the numeraire $P(t,T_{i+1})$). Under this measure, $F_i(t)$ is a martingale by construction since it is itself a forward price.

Definition — Black's Model Dynamics

Under $\mathbb{Q}^{T_{i+1}}$, the forward rate follows driftless geometric Brownian motion: $$dF_i(t) = \sigma_i F_i(t)\, dW_t^{T_{i+1}}$$ where $\sigma_i$ is the (Black) volatility of the forward rate, assumed constant. This implies $F_i(T_i)$ is lognormally distributed conditional on $F_i(t)$.

Theorem — Black's Caplet Formula

The time-$t$ price of a caplet with strike $K$ on the period $[T_i,T_{i+1}]$ is: $$\text{Caplet}(t) = \delta_i \, N \, P(t,T_{i+1}) \left[ F_i(t)\, \Phi(d_1) - K \, \Phi(d_2) \right]$$ where $$d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(F_i(t)/K) \pm \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_i^2 (T_i - t)}{\sigma_i \sqrt{T_i - t}}$$ and $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. The corresponding floorlet price follows from put-call parity: $$\text{Floorlet}(t) = \delta_i \, N \, P(t,T_{i+1}) \left[ K\, \Phi(-d_2) - F_i(t)\, \Phi(-d_1) \right]$$ The cap (floor) price is the sum of the constituent caplet (floorlet) prices, each priced off its own forward rate $F_i(t)$, discount factor $P(t,T_{i+1})$, and (potentially distinct) implied volatility $\sigma_i$.

Notice the structural similarity to the Black-Scholes formula for an equity option: $F_i(t)$ plays the role of the forward stock price, $P(t,T_{i+1})$ plays the role of the discount factor, and $\sigma_i\sqrt{T_i-t}$ is the total volatility to expiry.

Example — Pricing a Single Caplet

Consider a caplet on a 3-month forward rate, $\delta_i = 0.25$, with $F_i(t) = 4.50\%$, $K = 4.50\%$ (at-the-money), $\sigma_i = 35\%$, time to fixing $T_i - t = 1$ year, $P(t,T_{i+1}) = 0.955$, and notional $N = 10{,}000{,}000$.

Compute $d_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_i\sqrt{T_i-t} = 0.175$ and $d_2 = -0.175$ (since $\ln(F/K)=0$ at the money). Then $\Phi(d_1) \approx 0.5695$, $\Phi(d_2) \approx 0.4305$.

$$\text{Caplet} = 0.25 \times 10{,}000{,}000 \times 0.955 \times 0.045 \times (0.5695 - 0.4305) \approx \$14{,}940$$ The ATM caplet value is driven almost entirely by $\sigma_i \sqrt{T_i-t}$ — for ATM options $F\Phi(d_1) - K\Phi(d_2) \approx F \cdot \sigma\sqrt{T-t}/\sqrt{2\pi}$ to leading order.

Cap-Floor Parity with Swaps

Caps, floors, and swaps are linked by a static no-arbitrage relationship analogous to put-call parity for equity options.

Theorem — Cap-Floor-Swap Parity

For a cap and floor with the same strike $K$, notional, and tenor structure, written on the same floating index: $$\text{Cap}(K) - \text{Floor}(K) = \text{Value of receive-floating-pay-fixed-at-}K\text{ swap}$$ Equivalently, if $K = R_{\text{swap}}$ (the par swap rate for that tenor structure), then $\text{Cap}(R_{\text{swap}}) = \text{Floor}(R_{\text{swap}})$ — an at-the-money cap and floor (struck at the par rate) have equal value. This follows because $\max(F-K,0) - \max(K-F,0) = F - K$ for each underlying forward rate, so summing caplet-minus-floorlet payoffs reproduces the floating-minus-fixed cash flows of the swap.

Remark — Practical Use

Cap-floor parity gives a useful pricing check and also explains why a "collar" (long cap, short floor at a lower strike) is a popular zero-cost or low-cost hedge: the premium received from selling the floor partially or fully offsets the premium paid for the cap, while still bounding the effective rate paid within a band $[K_{\text{floor}}, K_{\text{cap}}]$.

Normal vs. Lognormal Volatility Quoting

Black's model assumes $F_i(t)$ is lognormal, which requires $F_i(t) > 0$. After the global rates environment of the 2010s, when many government bond yields and even some swap rates traded at or below zero, lognormal volatility became ill-defined for at-the-money-or-lower strikes. The market responded by adopting an alternative quoting convention based on the Bachelier (normal) model.

Definition — Bachelier (Normal) Model

Under the Bachelier model, the forward rate follows arithmetic (not geometric) Brownian motion: $$dF_i(t) = \sigma_i^N \, dW_t^{T_{i+1}}$$ where $\sigma_i^N$ is the normal volatility, quoted in basis points per $\sqrt{\text{year}}$ (e.g., "65 bps normal vol"). The corresponding caplet price is: $$\text{Caplet}(t) = \delta_i \, N \, P(t,T_{i+1}) \left[ (F_i(t)-K)\,\Phi(d) + \sigma_i^N\sqrt{T_i-t}\,\phi(d) \right], \qquad d = \frac{F_i(t)-K}{\sigma_i^N\sqrt{T_i-t}}$$ where $\phi$ is the standard normal density. Unlike lognormal vol, $\sigma_i^N$ remains well-defined for negative $F_i(t)$ or $K$.

Remark — Converting Between Quoting Conventions

Normal and lognormal (Black) volatilities for the same option price are related approximately by $\sigma^N \approx \sigma^{\text{Black}} \cdot F$ near the money (since $dF = \sigma^{\text{Black}} F\,dW$ vs. $dF = \sigma^N dW$). Trading desks today often quote cap/floor and swaption volatilities natively in normal (bps) terms across the entire surface, including strikes below zero, while still using Black's lognormal formula for legacy systems via a shifted-lognormal (displaced diffusion) transformation: replace $F_i(t) \to F_i(t) + s$ and $K \to K + s$ for a positive shift $s$ large enough to keep both arguments positive.
