3.2 CMS and CMS Spread Options

A constant maturity swap (CMS) rate is a periodically reset reference rate equal to the par swap rate of a fixed tenor (e.g. the 10-year swap rate), observed and paid on dates that do not coincide with that swap's own natural payment schedule. This mismatch between the rate's "natural" measure and the measure under which it is actually paid introduces a systematic bias — the CMS convexity adjustment — that must be added to the forward swap rate to obtain the correct expected payoff. CMS spread options extend this idea to payoffs depending on the difference between two CMS rates of different tenors, introducing an additional dependence on the correlation between those two rates.

Constant Maturity Swap Rates

Definition — CMS Rate

A CMS rate $R^{(\tau)}(T)$ is the par fixed rate, as of time $T$, of a swap with tenor $\tau$ (e.g. $\tau=10$ years) starting at $T$. A CMS leg pays, on a sequence of payment dates $T_1 < T_2 < \cdots$, the cash flow $\delta_i \cdot R^{(\tau)}(T_i)$ — i.e. it pays out the prevailing $\tau$-year swap rate observed at $T_i$, accrued over the period $\delta_i$, on the payment date $T_i$ itself (rather than on the dates that would naturally correspond to a $\tau$-year swap starting at $T_i$).

Remark — Why CMS Legs Exist

A standard floating leg of a swap pays a short-tenor rate (e.g. 3-month SOFR) reset and paid in a way that matches the natural fixing/payment structure of that rate. A CMS leg instead lets one party receive exposure to a long-tenor rate (e.g. the 10-year swap rate) without entering into an actual 10-year swap at each reset — useful for investors who have a view on the level or shape of the long end of the curve but want cash flows on a short, regular schedule. This convenience is exactly what creates the convexity adjustment: the rate being paid ($R^{(\tau)}(T_i)$, naturally associated with a $\tau$-year annuity) is paid on a date/measure that doesn't match its natural annuity.

The CMS Convexity Adjustment

The forward swap rate $R^{(\tau)}(t)$ is a martingale under its own annuity measure $\mathbb{Q}^{A^{(\tau)}}$ (Section 1.2) — but a CMS leg pays $R^{(\tau)}(T_i)$ discounted under the ordinary $T_i$-forward measure $\mathbb{Q}^{T_i}$, the measure natural to a single payment date $T_i$, not under $\mathbb{Q}^{A^{(\tau)}}$. Changing measure from $\mathbb{Q}^{A^{(\tau)}}$ to $\mathbb{Q}^{T_i}$ introduces a Radon-Nikodym derivative that depends on $R^{(\tau)}$ itself, so $R^{(\tau)}$ is generally not a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}^{T_i}$ — its expectation under $\mathbb{Q}^{T_i}$ differs from today's forward value $R^{(\tau)}(t)$.

Theorem — CMS Convexity Adjustment

The fair value of a single CMS payment $\delta_i \cdot R^{(\tau)}(T_i)$, paid at $T_i$, is: $$\text{PV} = P(t,T_i)\cdot\delta_i\cdot\mathbb{E}^{T_i}\!\left[R^{(\tau)}(T_i)\right] = P(t,T_i)\cdot\delta_i\cdot\left(R^{(\tau)}(t) + \text{CA}(T_i)\right)$$ where $\text{CA}(T_i) = \mathbb{E}^{T_i}[R^{(\tau)}(T_i)] - R^{(\tau)}(t) > 0$ is the CMS convexity adjustment. The adjustment is positive (for the typical case of paying the rate, as opposed to receiving a CMS-linked floor) because the change of measure effectively weights high-rate (low-annuity, since annuities fall as rates rise) outcomes more heavily — a manifestation of Jensen's inequality applied to the convex relationship between a swap rate and its annuity. The size of $\text{CA}(T_i)$ grows with the volatility of $R^{(\tau)}$, the time to $T_i$, and the tenor $\tau$ (longer tenors have more convex annuity-vs-rate relationships).

Remark — Static Replication

In practice, $\text{CA}(T_i)$ is computed via a static replication argument: the CMS payoff $R^{(\tau)}(T_i)$ can be written as a linear function of $R^{(\tau)}(T_i)$ plus an integral over a continuum of European payer and receiver swaption payoffs on $R^{(\tau)}$ at every strike (a result of the same flavour as the Carr-Madan / Breeden-Litzenberger replication of a general payoff by a strip of options). Because this replication uses the entire swaption smile for tenor $\tau$ — not just the ATM volatility — the convexity adjustment depends directly on the SABR-calibrated smile (Section 1.3) for that tenor: a steeper or more curved smile produces a larger $\text{CA}(T_i)$, since the replicating strip is more heavily weighted toward away-from-the-money strikes where the smile diverges most from a flat (Black-constant-vol) assumption.

CMS Spread Options

A CMS spread option has a payoff depending on the difference between two CMS rates of different tenors — most commonly a "curve" trade such as the 10-year minus 2-year swap rate spread, used to express a view on the slope of the yield curve.

Definition — CMS Spread Option

A CMS spread call with strike $K$ and payment date $T_i$ pays: $$\text{Payoff} = N\cdot\delta_i\cdot\max\left(\left(R^{(\tau_1)}(T_i) - R^{(\tau_2)}(T_i)\right) - K, 0\right)$$ for two tenors $\tau_1 > \tau_2$ (e.g. $\tau_1=10$, $\tau_2=2$). Pricing requires the joint distribution of $R^{(\tau_1)}(T_i)$ and $R^{(\tau_2)}(T_i)$ — each individually has its own SABR smile and its own convexity adjustment (as above), but the spread option's value additionally depends on how the two rates co-move.

Correlation Sensitivity

Remark — The Central Role of Correlation

If $R^{(\tau_1)}$ and $R^{(\tau_2)}$ were perfectly correlated (moving in lockstep), the spread $R^{(\tau_1)}-R^{(\tau_2)}$ would have very low volatility — a CMS spread option would be far out of the money most of the time and cheap. If the two rates were uncorrelated, the spread would inherit nearly the full combined volatility of both legs, making the spread option considerably more expensive for the same strike. Real long-tenor and short-tenor swap rates are highly, but not perfectly, correlated (typically $\rho \approx 0.6$-$0.9$ depending on the tenor pair and market regime), and this correlation is itself not directly observable from vanilla swaption prices — it must be inferred or assumed, often via a model such as SABR-LMM (Section 1.3's closing remark) that specifies the joint dynamics of multiple forward rates with a calibrated correlation structure.

This makes CMS spread options a correlation product: their price is highly sensitive to an input — the correlation between two swap rates of different tenors — that is not directly hedgeable with vanilla instruments. A desk holding a book of CMS spread options therefore carries "correlation risk" in addition to the more familiar delta, vega, and convexity risks of the individual CMS legs, and typically manages this risk by monitoring the realized and implied correlation across a range of historical regimes and stress scenarios, since no single liquid instrument provides a direct hedge.
