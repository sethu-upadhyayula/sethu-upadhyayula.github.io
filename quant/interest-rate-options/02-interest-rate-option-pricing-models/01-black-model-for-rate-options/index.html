2.1 Black Model for Rate Options

The Black-76 model (often simply called "Black's model" in rates contexts) is the market-standard pricing model for caps, floors, and swaptions. It is a direct adaptation of the Black-Scholes formula to a world where the underlying is a forward price — a forward rate or forward swap rate — rather than a spot asset, and was first introduced in Section 1.1 and Section 1.2 for caplets and swaptions respectively. This section consolidates the model, makes explicit its core assumption, and works through a full pricing example.

The Lognormal Forward Rate Assumption

The defining feature of the Black-76 model is that it specifies dynamics directly for the forward quantity that the option is written on — the forward rate $F_i(t)$ for a caplet, or the forward swap rate $R(t)$ for a swaption — rather than for a spot price that must then be projected forward.

Definition — Black-76 Dynamics

Under the appropriate forward (or annuity) measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the underlying forward quantity $F(t)$ follows driftless geometric Brownian motion: $$dF(t) = \sigma F(t)\, dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$ with constant volatility $\sigma$. Because $F(t)$ is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$ by construction (it is itself a forward price, or a ratio of tradeable assets), no drift term is needed — unlike the Black-Scholes equation for a spot price, which requires a risk-free drift adjustment. The terminal value $F(T)$ is lognormally distributed conditional on $F(t)$, with $$\ln F(T) \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\ln F(t) - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2(T-t),\ \sigma^2(T-t)\right)$$

Theorem — Black-76 Pricing Formula

The time-$t$ price of a European call on $F$ with strike $K$, expiry $T$, and numeraire value $A(t)$ (a discount factor $P(t,T)$ for a caplet, or an annuity for a swaption) is: $$C(t) = A(t)\left[F(t)\,\Phi(d_1) - K\,\Phi(d_2)\right], \qquad d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(F(t)/K) \pm \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2(T-t)}{\sigma\sqrt{T-t}}$$ and the corresponding put (floor/receiver) price is: $$P(t) = A(t)\left[K\,\Phi(-d_2) - F(t)\,\Phi(-d_1)\right]$$

The formula is identical in form across instruments — only the interpretation of $F(t)$, $A(t)$, and $\sigma$ changes: $F(t)$ is a forward LIBOR/SOFR rate and $A(t)=\delta\,P(t,T_{i+1})$ for a caplet (Section 1.1); $F(t)$ is the forward swap rate and $A(t)$ is the annuity for a swaption (Section 1.2). This uniformity is precisely why Black-76 became the universal quoting convention: a single number, the Black implied volatility $\sigma$, summarises the market price of any of these instruments.

Why Lognormality Is a Convenient (but Imperfect) Choice

Lognormal dynamics guarantee $F(t) > 0$ for all $t$, which is a natural property for an interest rate in a world where rates are economically bounded below by zero. The Black formula is also analytically tractable and produces a single closed-form expression that is fast to evaluate and easy to invert for implied volatility — essential for a market that quotes thousands of cap/floor and swaption prices daily.

Remark — The Single-Volatility Limitation

Black's model, with one constant $\sigma$ per underlying, cannot reproduce the volatility smile observed across strikes for a given expiry/tenor — this is the motivation for the SABR model of Section 1.3, which generates a strike-dependent Black implied volatility $\sigma_B(K)$ from a small number of parameters. In practice, $\sigma$ in the Black-76 formula above should be read as $\sigma_B(K)$, the SABR-implied (or market-quoted) volatility appropriate for that particular strike — Black-76 remains the pricing formula, while SABR (or an equivalent smile model) supplies the volatility input.

Worked Example: Pricing a 2y5y Payer Swaption with Black-76

Example — Black-76 Payer Swaption

Consider a 2-year-into-5-year payer swaption. Suppose the forward swap rate is $R(t) = 3.80\%$, the strike is $K = 4.00\%$, the Black implied volatility is $\sigma = 28\%$, time to expiry is $T-t=2$ years, and the annuity is $A(t) = 4.55$ (per unit notional).

First compute the total volatility, $\sigma\sqrt{T-t} = 0.28\sqrt{2} \approx 0.3960$. Next, $\ln(R/K) = \ln(3.80/4.00) = \ln(0.95) \approx -0.0513$. Then: $$d_1 = \frac{-0.0513 + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.3960)^2}{0.3960} \approx \frac{-0.0513+0.0784}{0.3960} \approx 0.0684, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - 0.3960 \approx -0.3276$$

Using standard normal CDF values $\Phi(0.0684)\approx 0.5273$ and $\Phi(-0.3276)\approx 0.3717$: $$C(t) = A(t)\left[R(t)\Phi(d_1) - K\Phi(d_2)\right] = 4.55\times\left[0.0380\times0.5273 - 0.0400\times0.3717\right]$$ $$= 4.55 \times \left[0.02004 - 0.01487\right] = 4.55 \times 0.00517 \approx 0.02352$$

For a notional of $N=\$20\text{m}$, the payer swaption is worth approximately $\$20\text{m}\times0.02352 \approx \$470{,}400$, or about 23.5 bps of notional. Note that because $K > R(t)$, this payer swaption is out of the money — its value comes entirely from the time value implied by $\sigma\sqrt{T-t}$.

Limitations Motivating Chapter 2.2

The Black-76 framework above implicitly assumes $F(t) > 0$ at all times — both because $\ln F(t)$ must be well-defined and because a lognormal variable cannot take non-positive values. For most of financial history this was an innocuous assumption for nominal interest rates. The next section examines what happens when this assumption fails, and how the market adapted.
