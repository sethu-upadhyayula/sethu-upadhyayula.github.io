2.2 Normal vs Lognormal Models and Negative Rates

The Black-76 model of Section 2.1 assumes the underlying forward rate or swap rate is lognormally distributed, which requires $F(t) > 0$ at all times and for $\ln(F(t)/K)$ to be well-defined. From roughly 2012 onward, EUR, CHF, JPY, and (later) other government bond yields and swap rates traded persistently at or below zero, and the Black formula - and the entire concept of "lognormal volatility" for these rates - became mathematically inapplicable at the relevant strikes. This section covers the Bachelier (normal) model that the market adopted in response, and the shifted-lognormal compromise that bridges the two worlds.

The Bachelier (Normal) Model

The Bachelier model, originally proposed by Louis Bachelier in 1900 (predating Black-Scholes by 73 years), assumes the underlying follows arithmetic - not geometric - Brownian motion.

Definition - Bachelier Dynamics

Under the relevant forward/annuity measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the forward rate or swap rate $F(t)$ follows: $$dF(t) = \sigma^N \, dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$$ where $\sigma^N$ is the normal volatility, constant, with units of rate per $\sqrt{\text{year}}$ - quoted in practice as basis points per $\sqrt{\text{year}}$ (e.g. "62bp normal vol"). Since $F(T) = F(t) + \sigma^N(W_T - W_t)$ is Gaussian, $F(T)$ can take any real value, including negative ones, with positive probability - there is no constraint $F(t)>0$.

Theorem - Bachelier Pricing Formula

The time-$t$ price of a European call (e.g. a caplet or payer swaption) with strike $K$, expiry $T$, and numeraire $A(t)$ is: $$C(t) = A(t)\left[(F(t)-K)\,\Phi(d) + \sigma^N\sqrt{T-t}\,\phi(d)\right], \qquad d = \frac{F(t)-K}{\sigma^N\sqrt{T-t}}$$ and the corresponding put is: $$P(t) = A(t)\left[(K-F(t))\,\Phi(-d) + \sigma^N\sqrt{T-t}\,\phi(d)\right]$$ where $\phi$ and $\Phi$ are the standard normal density and CDF. Both formulas remain well-defined for any real $F(t)$ and $K$, including negative values - the only requirement is $\sigma^N > 0$.

Crucially, the formula's structure - $(F-K)\Phi(d) + \sigma^N\sqrt{T-t}\,\phi(d)$ - has no logarithm and no ratio $F/K$, so it never encounters the $\ln(\cdot)$ singularity that breaks the Black-76 formula when $F(t)$ or $K$ is non-positive.

Why Negative Rates Broke Lognormal Models

Remark - The Mechanics of the Breakdown

Two distinct problems arise once $F(t)$ or $K$ can be zero or negative under Black-76. First, $\ln(F(t)/K)$ in $d_{1,2}$ is undefined if either argument is non-positive - the formula simply cannot be evaluated. Second, even where $F(t) > 0$ but a strike $K \le 0$ is quoted (e.g. a "$-10$bp" floor strike, which became economically meaningful once rates went negative), the formula breaks for that specific option even if other options on the same underlying are fine.

Beyond the formula itself, the underlying economic assumption was simply wrong: lognormal dynamics imply $F(t)$ can never reach zero, let alone go negative, with positive probability - yet EUR and CHF short rates did exactly that for an extended period. A model whose state space excludes outcomes the market is actively trading is not just inconvenient, it is structurally mis-specified.

The market's response was swift: by the mid-2010s, EUR and CHF cap/floor and swaption volatilities were quoted natively in normal (bps) terms using the Bachelier formula above, sidestepping the issue entirely. Many other markets (including USD) followed during and after the COVID-era rate cuts of 2020, when even USD short rates approached zero and market participants wanted volatility surfaces that remained well-defined however far rates might fall.

Shifted-Lognormal as a Workaround

Many legacy pricing systems, risk models, and regulatory frameworks were built around the Black-76 formula and lognormal volatility as the fundamental unit of volatility. Rather than rewrite this infrastructure, a common workaround is the shifted (or displaced-diffusion) lognormal model.

Definition - Shifted-Lognormal Model

Choose a positive shift $s$ large enough that $F(t) + s > 0$ and $K + s > 0$ for all rates and strikes of interest (e.g. $s = 3\%$ or $s=4\%$, large enough to cover plausible negative excursions). Then assume the shifted variable $\tilde{F}(t) = F(t) + s$ follows Black-76 lognormal dynamics: $$d\tilde{F}(t) = \sigma^{\text{shift}}\,\tilde{F}(t)\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}, \qquad \tilde{F}(t) = F(t)+s > 0$$ Option prices are then computed by applying the ordinary Black-76 formula to $\tilde{F}(t)$ and $\tilde{K} = K+s$: $$C(t) = A(t)\left[\tilde{F}(t)\,\Phi(d_1) - \tilde{K}\,\Phi(d_2)\right], \qquad d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln(\tilde{F}(t)/\tilde{K}) \pm \tfrac{1}{2}(\sigma^{\text{shift}})^2(T-t)}{\sigma^{\text{shift}}\sqrt{T-t}}$$ Since $(F(t)-K) = (\tilde{F}(t)-\tilde{K})$, the option payoff is unchanged by the shift - only the internal mechanics of the formula are affected.

Remark - Trade-offs of Shifting

Shifted-lognormal lets existing Black-76 infrastructure, Greeks formulas, and implied-vol conventions continue to function with minimal code changes - only $F$ and $K$ need to be offset by $s$ before calling the existing routines. The cost is an extra free parameter (the shift $s$) that must be agreed upon across counterparties and risk systems, and that effectively changes the "units" of lognormal volatility: a $\sigma^{\text{shift}}$ quoted with $s=3\%$ is not directly comparable to one quoted with $s=4\%$, even for the same option. As a pure quoting convention, normal (Bachelier) volatility avoids this ambiguity entirely, which is why it has become the dominant convention for ATM and near-the-money rates volatility despite the continued use of shifted-lognormal in some legacy contexts.

Worked Comparison: Pricing the Same Option Three Ways

Example - Normal vs Shifted-Lognormal for a Near-Zero Forward

Suppose a 1-year caplet has forward rate $F(t) = 0.30\%$, strike $K=-0.20\%$ (a negative strike, common in EUR markets circa 2019), $A(t) = 0.995$, and $T-t=1$.

Bachelier: with normal volatility $\sigma^N = 45$bp $=0.0045$, $d = \frac{0.0030-(-0.0020)}{0.0045} = \frac{0.0050}{0.0045}\approx 1.111$. Using $\Phi(1.111)\approx0.8667$ and $\phi(1.111)\approx 0.2155$: $$C(t) = 0.995\left[(0.0050)(0.8667) + 0.0045(0.2155)\right] = 0.995\left[0.004334+0.000970\right] \approx 0.995\times0.005304 \approx 0.005277$$

Shifted-lognormal with $s=3\%$: $\tilde{F}=3.30\%$, $\tilde{K}=2.80\%$. Suppose the market quotes $\sigma^{\text{shift}}=15\%$ for this shift convention. Then $\ln(\tilde{F}/\tilde{K})=\ln(3.30/2.80)\approx0.1640$, $\sigma^{\text{shift}}\sqrt{T-t}=0.15$, so $d_1=\frac{0.1640+0.5(0.15)^2}{0.15}\approx1.1683$, $d_2\approx1.0183$. With $\Phi(d_1)\approx0.8787$ and $\Phi(d_2)\approx0.8457$: $$C(t) = 0.995\left[0.0330\times0.8787 - 0.0280\times0.8457\right] = 0.995\left[0.02900-0.02368\right]\approx0.995\times0.00532\approx0.005294$$

Both approaches give essentially the same price ($\approx0.00528$ vs $\approx0.00529$) - as expected, since $\sigma^{\text{shift}}$ was chosen to roughly match the Bachelier price for this $(F,K,T)$. The point is not that one number is "more correct," but that the Bachelier price required no auxiliary parameter $s$ and remains valid however negative $K$ or $F$ become, whereas the shifted-lognormal price is only valid as long as $F+s>0$ and $K+s>0$, and its $\sigma^{\text{shift}}$ is specific to the chosen $s$.
