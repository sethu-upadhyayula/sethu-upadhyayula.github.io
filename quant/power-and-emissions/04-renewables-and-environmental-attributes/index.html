4. Renewables, PPAs, and Environmental Attributes

The growth of wind and solar generation has created a market not just for the electricity these resources produce, but for the environmental attributes associated with that generation - claims about the source of the power that can be bought, sold, and retired independently of the underlying electrons. This chapter covers how those attributes are unbundled and traded, and the contractual structures (power purchase agreements) that link renewable generators to the corporate and utility buyers who want to claim their output.

Renewable Energy Certificates

Definition - Renewable Energy Certificate (REC)

A Renewable Energy Certificate (REC), also called a Guarantee of Origin (GO) in European markets, represents the environmental, social, and other non-power attributes of one MWh of electricity generated from a renewable source. When a renewable generator produces electricity, it earns one REC per MWh, which can be sold separately from the electricity itself. A buyer that retires a REC can claim to have "used" renewable electricity, regardless of the physical source of the power actually delivered to its premises.

This unbundling is the key conceptual point: the physical electron flowing through the grid is indistinguishable regardless of its generation source - electrons from a wind farm and a coal plant mix together on the grid and cannot be separately routed to a specific consumer. RECs solve this by creating a separate, tradable instrument for the claim of renewable sourcing, decoupled from the physical commodity (electricity) discussed in Section 1.

Remark - Decoupling from the Underlying Power

Because RECs are unbundled from electricity, their price is determined by a largely separate supply-and-demand balance: the supply of RECs is driven by renewable generation volumes (themselves driven by build-out, weather, and capacity factors), while demand is driven by regulatory requirements (e.g., state-level Renewable Portfolio Standards, which mandate that utilities source a minimum percentage of their power from renewables and can satisfy this requirement via REC purchases) and voluntary corporate demand (e.g., companies with renewable energy procurement targets).

A direct consequence is that REC prices and power prices can move largely independently - a period of high wind generation can simultaneously depress wholesale power prices (more supply of a non-storable commodity, Section 1) and depress REC prices (more supply of RECs), but the two price moves are not mechanically linked and can diverge sharply depending on the relative tightness of each market. A generator's total revenue is therefore the sum of its energy-market revenue (sold at the local hub or node price, subject to the basis risk discussed in Section 3) and its REC revenue (sold into whichever compliance or voluntary REC market it is eligible for) - two largely decorrelated streams.

Power Purchase Agreements

A power purchase agreement (PPA) is a long-term contract between a renewable generator (or developer) and an offtaker - historically a utility, but increasingly a corporate buyer seeking to procure renewable energy directly - under which the offtaker agrees to purchase the output (and often the associated RECs) of a specific generating asset, typically at a fixed price, for a term of 10-20 years. PPAs are the primary financing mechanism for new utility-scale wind and solar projects, providing the long-term revenue certainty that lenders require.

Definition - Physical vs Virtual (Synthetic) PPA

Physical PPA: the offtaker takes physical or contractual delivery of the electricity itself, typically requiring the offtaker to be in the same market/balancing area as the generator and to have the retail relationships needed to actually consume or resell the power.

Virtual (or synthetic) PPA: a purely financial contract-for-differences. The generator continues to sell its physical output into the wholesale market at the prevailing local price, while the offtaker (typically a corporate buyer who is not itself an electricity market participant) and the generator settle the difference between a fixed contract price and the floating market price. The offtaker pays the generator if the market price is below the contract price, and receives a payment if the market price is above it - economically equivalent to the offtaker owning a fixed-for-floating swap on the project's power output. The corporate buyer separately purchases its actual electricity at retail rates from its normal supplier, and typically receives the RECs associated with the project's generation as part of the arrangement, which it can use toward its own renewable energy claims.

Example - Virtual PPA Cash Flows

A corporate buyer signs a 15-year virtual PPA with a wind farm at a fixed price of $\$35$/MWh. In a given month, the wind farm generates 100,000 MWh and the average wholesale price at the farm's local node is $\$45$/MWh.

The generator sells its output into the wholesale market for $100{,}000 \times \$45 = \$4{,}500{,}000$. Under the swap, the generator owes the buyer the difference: $100{,}000 \times (45 - 35) = \$1{,}000{,}000$. Net, the generator effectively realises $\$35$/MWh ($\$3{,}500{,}000$ total) regardless of the wholesale price - and the buyer's net cost of the financial hedge, combined with whatever it pays its retail supplier for actual power, determines its all-in cost of electricity. If wholesale prices had instead fallen to $\$25$/MWh, the buyer would owe the generator $100{,}000 \times (35-25) = \$1{,}000{,}000$, again leaving the generator with $\$35$/MWh.

Basis Risk in Virtual PPAs

Remark - Shape and Location Basis

Shape risk: a virtual PPA typically settles against the floating price at the project's node for the hours the project actually generates - which, for wind and solar, is an inherently variable and weather-dependent "as-generated" shape, not a flat or peak/off-peak block (Section 3). The contract price the buyer locks in is therefore implicitly a price for this specific, intermittent generation shape, which can be worth more or less than a flat-block price for the same average $/MWh depending on how the shape correlates with the broader price curve - a wind farm that tends to generate during low-price overnight hours produces a "shape" that is worth less than its simple average MWh-weighted price would suggest (the so-called "shape" or "profile" discount).

Location (basis) risk: the corporate buyer's own electricity consumption is typically in a different location - often a different grid or balancing area entirely - from the renewable project. The buyer is hedged against the project's local node price, not against the price in its own consumption zone, so the buyer retains the basis risk between the two locations (the same locational basis risk discussed for power hubs in Section 3, now sitting on the corporate buyer's balance sheet rather than a generator's or trader's). Together, shape and location basis mean a virtual PPA is rarely a perfect hedge of a buyer's actual electricity costs - it is best understood as a financial instrument whose value is correlated with, but not identical to, the buyer's underlying exposure.

Additionality and Broader Environmental Attribute Markets

A recurring theme across the environmental attribute markets discussed in this chapter and in Section 2 (carbon allowances and voluntary carbon credits) is the question of additionality: does the purchase of the attribute cause an environmental outcome that would not otherwise have occurred, or does it merely provide revenue to a project that would have been built (or an emissions reduction that would have happened) regardless?

Remark - Additionality in Practice

For RECs, the additionality debate centres on whether REC purchases from existing, already-profitable renewable projects represent a meaningful environmental claim, versus RECs (or PPAs) that provide the financing certainty needed to bring a new project online - the latter is generally considered to have a stronger additionality case, and has driven the growth of corporate PPAs (which directly fund new build) relative to simple REC purchases (which can be satisfied by existing generation).

More broadly, environmental attribute markets - carbon allowances and offsets (Section 2), RECs, and other emerging instruments such as biodiversity credits and water rights - share a common structure: a regulator or standard-setter defines a unit representing some environmental outcome, the unit is issued (by allocation, auction, or certification of a project), and the unit then trades as a financial instrument whose price reflects the market's valuation of that outcome. The credibility and price of any such market rests on the rigor of its issuance and verification standards, and weaknesses in those standards - overcrediting, double-counting, or weak additionality requirements - are a recurring source of price volatility and reputational risk for participants across all of these markets, from compliance carbon to voluntary RECs to PPA-backed renewable claims.
