2. Carbon and Emissions Markets

Carbon emissions allowances are tradable instruments created by cap-and-trade regulatory schemes, which set a declining limit ("cap") on total greenhouse gas emissions from covered industries and allow regulated entities to buy and sell ("trade") allowances to meet their individual obligations at the lowest aggregate cost.

Cap-and-Trade Mechanics

Definition — Allowances and Compliance

Under a cap-and-trade scheme such as the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), regulators issue a fixed number of emissions allowances (e.g., EU Allowances, or EUAs, each representing the right to emit one tonne of CO₂-equivalent) each compliance period. Covered installations (power plants, industrial facilities, and increasingly aviation and shipping) must surrender one allowance for every tonne of CO₂ they emit. A facility that emits less than its allocation can sell surplus allowances; a facility that emits more must buy additional allowances on the market. The cap is reduced over time, mechanically tightening supply and — all else equal — pushing the allowance price higher to incentivise emissions reductions.

Price Drivers

The price of carbon allowances reflects the marginal cost of abatement across the regulated economy: it should settle at roughly the cost of the cheapest emissions-reduction technology that has not yet been adopted. In practice, allowance prices are driven by:

  • Fuel switching economics — in power generation, a higher carbon price raises the cost of coal-fired generation relative to gas-fired generation more than proportionally (coal emits roughly twice the CO₂ per unit of electricity generated as gas), so the carbon price needed to make gas cheaper than coal on a total-cost basis is a key reference level, often called the "switching price."
  • Macroeconomic activity — industrial output and electricity demand directly drive emissions, so allowance prices are pro-cyclical.
  • Policy interventions — mechanisms such as the EU's Market Stability Reserve (MSR), which removes allowances from auction supply when there is a surplus in circulation, directly affect the supply side and have caused significant price moves on announcement.
  • Free allocation phase-outs — many schemes initially gave allowances to industry free of charge to ease the transition, with free allocation progressively reduced over time, increasing the share of allowances that must be purchased and supporting demand.
Example — The Coal-to-Gas Switching Price

Suppose a coal plant has a heat rate (efficiency) requiring $2.0$ tonnes of CO₂ per MWh generated, while a gas plant requires $0.4$ tonnes of CO₂ per MWh. If gas costs $\$8$/MWh more than coal on a pure fuel-cost basis, the carbon price $P_{CO_2}$ at which the two become equally costly satisfies:

$$P_{CO_2} \times (2.0 - 0.4) = 8 \implies P_{CO_2} = \frac{8}{1.6} = \$5/\text{tonne}$$

Above this switching price, gas generation becomes cheaper than coal once carbon costs are included, and grid operators have an economic incentive to dispatch gas plants ahead of coal plants — directly linking the carbon market to the structure of power markets discussed in Natural Gas and Electricity.

Voluntary Carbon Markets

Remark — Compliance vs Voluntary Markets

Distinct from regulated "compliance" markets like the EU ETS, voluntary carbon markets allow companies to purchase carbon credits — typically generated by projects that avoid or remove emissions (reforestation, renewable energy projects in developing countries, methane capture) — to offset their own emissions on a voluntary basis, often as part of corporate net-zero commitments. Voluntary credits are generally far less liquid and more heterogeneous than compliance allowances, with prices varying enormously by project type, vintage, and the certification standard (e.g., Verra, Gold Standard) used to verify the underlying emissions reduction. Concerns about the additionality and permanence of some offset projects — whether the claimed emissions reduction would not have happened anyway, and whether it is durable — have been a recurring source of scrutiny and price volatility in voluntary markets.

Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanisms

As carbon pricing schemes raise production costs for domestic industry, policymakers have introduced carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAMs) — tariffs applied to imports based on the embedded carbon emissions of the imported goods — to prevent "carbon leakage," where production simply relocates to jurisdictions without a carbon price. The EU's CBAM, phased in from 2026, requires importers of certain goods (steel, cement, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, hydrogen) to purchase certificates corresponding to the carbon price that would have applied had the goods been produced under the EU ETS — effectively extending the EU carbon price to imported goods and linking emissions market dynamics directly to international trade flows.
