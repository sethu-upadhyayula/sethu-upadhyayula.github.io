1. Natural Gas and Electricity

Natural gas and electricity markets exhibit some of the most extreme seasonality and volatility in the commodity universe. Natural gas is storable but its storage capacity is finite and regionally constrained, producing a strongly seasonal futures curve. Electricity, by contrast, is effectively non-storable at scale, which fundamentally changes how its forward curve and spot prices behave.

Henry Hub and the Natural Gas Curve

Definition — Henry Hub

Henry Hub, located in Erath, Louisiana, is a major pipeline interconnection point and the delivery point for the NYMEX natural gas futures contract, making it the primary price benchmark for North American natural gas. Prices are quoted in dollars per million British thermal units (\$/MMBtu).

Natural gas demand is strongly seasonal: heating demand peaks in winter and, in markets with significant gas-fired air conditioning load, a secondary demand peak occurs in summer. Production, by contrast, is relatively stable year-round (subject to longer-term decline curves and drilling activity). The mismatch between steady production and seasonal consumption is absorbed by underground storage.

Remark — Injection and Withdrawal Seasons

Injection season (roughly April through October) is when production exceeds demand and the surplus is injected into underground storage facilities (depleted reservoirs, salt caverns, aquifers).

Withdrawal season (roughly November through March) is when winter heating demand exceeds production and the deficit is met by withdrawing gas from storage. The futures curve reflects this cycle: winter contracts (particularly January and February) typically trade at a premium to shoulder-month contracts, reflecting the value of gas delivered when storage is being drawn down and the risk of cold-weather demand spikes. Weekly EIA storage reports are a key market-moving release.

Weather Derivatives

Because weather is the dominant driver of short-term natural gas (and electricity) demand, a market in weather derivatives has developed to allow utilities, energy traders, and other weather-sensitive businesses to hedge volumetric risk that price hedges alone cannot address.

Definition — Heating and Cooling Degree Days

A heating degree day (HDD) for a given day is defined as $\max(65°F - T_{\text{avg}}, 0)$, and a cooling degree day (CDD) as $\max(T_{\text{avg}} - 65°F, 0)$, where $T_{\text{avg}}$ is the average daily temperature. Cumulative HDDs and CDDs over a season are strong predictors of heating and cooling fuel demand respectively.

Weather derivatives — traded on exchanges (e.g., CME) and in the OTC market — pay out based on cumulative HDD or CDD indices over a specified period, allowing a utility to hedge the volumetric risk that a mild winter (low HDDs) reduces gas sales volumes, complementing price hedges that address commodity price risk but not volume risk.

Electricity: The Non-Storable Commodity

Electricity is fundamentally different from every other commodity discussed in this text: at the wholesale level, it cannot be economically stored in bulk (grid-scale battery storage remains a small fraction of total capacity in most markets). Supply and demand on the electrical grid must balance instantaneously, every second, or the system becomes unstable.

Remark — Distinguishing Features of Power Markets

Non-storability: because power cannot be banked, the standard cost-of-carry relationship between spot and forward prices breaks down entirely — there is no arbitrage linking today's spot price to tomorrow's forward price via storage.

Load curves: electricity demand ("load") varies predictably within a day (higher during business hours and evening peak, lower overnight) and across seasons (driven by heating and cooling demand), and grid operators must have generation available to match load at every point on this curve.

The generation stack and merit order: at any moment, the marginal generating unit needed to meet load — the most expensive unit dispatched — sets the wholesale price. As load rises through the day, progressively more expensive generation (from baseload nuclear/coal, to mid-merit gas, to peaking units such as gas turbines or even diesel generators) is dispatched, producing sharp price increases at the margin.

Day-ahead vs. real-time markets: most organised power markets operate a day-ahead market, where participants submit bids and offers for each hour of the next day and a market-clearing price is determined, alongside a real-time (spot) market that balances actual deviations from day-ahead schedules. The difference between real-time and day-ahead prices reflects forecast errors in load and renewable generation and is itself a source of risk and arbitrage.

The Spark Spread

For gas-fired generators, the analogue of the crack spread is the spark spread: the margin earned by converting natural gas into electricity.

Definition — Spark Spread

The spark spread for a gas-fired generator with heat rate $H$ (measured in MMBtu of fuel required per MWh of electricity produced) is $$\text{Spark Spread} = P_{\text{power}} - H \times P_{\text{gas}}$$ where $P_{\text{power}}$ is the wholesale electricity price (\$/MWh) and $P_{\text{gas}}$ is the natural gas price (\$/MMBtu). The heat rate $H$ depends on the generator's efficiency: a modern combined-cycle gas turbine might have a heat rate around 7 MMBtu/MWh, while a less efficient simple-cycle peaker might have a heat rate of 10-12 MMBtu/MWh. The spark spread approximates the gross margin per MWh generated, before fixed costs, emissions costs, and other variable costs.

Example — Spark Spread Calculation

A combined-cycle gas plant with a heat rate of $H = 7$ MMBtu/MWh operates when natural gas trades at $P_{\text{gas}} = \$3.50$/MMBtu and the day-ahead power price is $P_{\text{power}} = \$45$/MWh.

$$\text{Spark Spread} = 45 - 7 \times 3.50 = 45 - 24.50 = \$20.50 \text{ per MWh}.$$ A positive and sufficiently large spark spread incentivises the plant to run; a negative or thin spark spread (relative to the plant's fixed/start-up costs) means the plant should remain offline. The spark spread is therefore a key driver of which generating units are "in the money" at any given time, and of natural gas demand from the power sector.

Price Spikes and Volatility

Remark — Why Power Prices Spike

Because electricity demand is highly inelastic in the short run (consumers cannot easily reduce usage in response to a price signal within minutes or hours) and supply is capacity-constrained, small changes in load or available generation near the top of the merit order can produce enormous price spikes. A heatwave that pushes air-conditioning load to near the grid's capacity limit can cause prices to spike from tens of dollars per MWh to hundreds or even thousands of dollars per MWh, as the most expensive (and scarce) peaking capacity is called upon.

This combination of non-storability, inelastic demand, and capacity constraints gives electricity price distributions extreme positive skewness and kurtosis (fat right tails) relative to other commodities — a feature that heavily influences the design and pricing of electricity derivatives, capacity markets, and demand-response programmes.
