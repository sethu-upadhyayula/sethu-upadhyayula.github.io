4. Commodity Portfolio and Risk Management

The first three chapters built up the pricing and risk machinery for individual commodity futures and options. In practice, however, most exposure to commodities as an asset class is held not through bespoke single-commodity positions but through diversified portfolios — commodity index funds, multi-commodity hedge programs, and cross-asset risk books. This final chapter steps back from single-instrument pricing to the portfolio level, where the dominant concerns become how a passive long-only allocation is constructed and rolled, and how exposures across related but imperfectly correlated commodities are hedged against one another.

We begin with commodity index investing: the construction methodologies of major benchmark indices, the mechanics of rolling futures positions from one expiry to the next, and the roll yield that can make a meaningful contribution — positive or negative — to total return independent of any change in spot prices. We then turn to cross-commodity hedging, addressing the common situation where a firm's exposure does not have a liquid, directly-matching futures contract, and a hedge must instead be constructed using a related commodity whose price co-moves imperfectly with the exposure being hedged. Both topics share a common thread: in commodity markets, the difference between a position's nominal exposure and its realised economic outcome is often driven as much by curve shape and cross-asset correlation as by the outright direction of spot prices.
