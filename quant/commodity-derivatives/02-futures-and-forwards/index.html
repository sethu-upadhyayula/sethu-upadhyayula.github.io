2. Futures and Forwards

Having established the role of spot prices and convenience yield in physical commodity markets, this chapter develops the framework for pricing and interpreting commodity futures and forward contracts. The cost-of-carry model extends the familiar no-arbitrage pricing of financial forwards to incorporate storage costs and convenience yield, producing the relationship $F(t,T) = S_t e^{(r+u-y)(T-t)}$ that governs the link between spot and futures prices.

We examine why commodities can violate the textbook cost-of-carry identity that holds for financial assets - because physical commodities cannot generally be sold short, only an upper bound on the forward price is guaranteed by arbitrage. We then study the shape of the futures curve itself: contango and backwardation, their connection to inventory levels and roll yield, and the historical theory of normal backwardation. Finally, we turn to basis risk - the residual risk that remains when a hedger's physical exposure does not exactly match the deliverable grade, location, or timing of the futures contract used to hedge it.

Together, these topics form the analytical backbone for understanding how commodity derivatives are priced, how their curves behave, and how they are used - and sometimes only imperfectly used - to manage physical price risk.
