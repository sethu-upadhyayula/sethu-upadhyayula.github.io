3.2 Volatility Surfaces

The Black-76 formula takes a single volatility $\sigma$ as input, but in practice, options on the same underlying futures contract trade at different implied volatilities depending on strike $K$ and maturity $T$. The function $(K,T) \mapsto \sigma_{\text{imp}}(K,T)$ is the implied volatility surface, and its shape encodes the market's view of the distribution of future price moves — including fat tails, skewness, and term-structure effects that a constant-volatility lognormal model cannot capture.

Skew and the "Smirk"

Definition — Volatility Skew

For a fixed maturity $T$, the volatility skew is the variation of implied volatility $\sigma_{\text{imp}}(K,T)$ across strikes $K$. A smile refers to a U-shaped pattern (higher implied vol for both deep in- and out-of-the-money options relative to at-the-money). A skew or smirk refers to a monotonic or asymmetric tilt, where implied volatility is systematically higher on one side of at-the-money than the other.

In equity index options markets, the well-known pattern is a downward skew: out-of-the-money puts (low strikes) trade at higher implied volatility than out-of-the-money calls (high strikes), reflecting the market's pricing of crash risk — large downside moves are perceived as more likely (or at least more costly to hedge) than large upside moves.

Remark — Commodity Skew Often Runs the Other Way

For many energy commodities — most notably crude oil and natural gas — the skew can be inverted relative to equities: out-of-the-money calls (high strikes) trade at higher implied volatility than out-of-the-money puts. This reflects the asymmetric risk of supply shocks: a sudden disruption (geopolitical conflict, OPEC+ production cut, refinery outage, extreme weather) can cause prices to spike sharply higher, while downside moves driven by demand destruction tend to be comparatively gradual.

The direction and steepness of the skew is not fixed, however — it varies with market conditions. During periods dominated by demand-side fears (e.g., a global recession scare), the skew can flatten or even revert toward the equity-like downward-sloping shape, as downside price risk becomes the more salient tail.

Seasonality in the Volatility Surface

For commodities with strongly seasonal demand or supply patterns, the term structure of implied volatility reflects the calendar. Natural gas is the canonical example.

Example — Natural Gas Volatility Seasonality

Natural gas demand in the United States is dominated by winter heating demand and, increasingly, summer cooling demand for power generation. As a result:

Options expiring in or just before peak winter months (December through February) typically carry materially higher implied volatility than options expiring in the "shoulder" months (April-May, September-October), because winter weather is the dominant source of demand uncertainty and price spikes.

This produces a term structure of implied volatility that is not monotonic but instead exhibits a sawtooth or seasonal pattern, with local peaks around winter-month expiries. A trader pricing a calendar spread option or a strip of monthly options must account for this seasonality explicitly — a single flat volatility assumption across the strip would misprice the relative value of winter versus summer optionality.

Why Commodity Surfaces Are Hard to Fit

Remark — Practical Challenges

Limited strike liquidity: unlike equity index options, where hundreds of strikes trade across many expiries with deep liquidity, commodity options often have only a handful of liquidly-traded strikes per expiry, with wide bid-ask spreads away from at-the-money. Interpolating and extrapolating the surface from sparse data is inherently noisier.

Expiry-specific delivery contracts: each futures expiry corresponds to a distinct delivery month with its own supply/demand dynamics (e.g., a January natural gas contract reflects peak winter conditions, while a July contract does not). This means each point on the volatility surface is, in a sense, tied to a different underlying asset — there is no single continuous "stock price process" underlying the whole surface, complicating the application of standard arbitrage-free surface construction techniques designed for a single underlying.

Roll and calendar effects: as the prompt futures contract approaches expiry and trading rolls to the next contract, implied volatilities can jump discontinuously, reflecting the change in underlying delivery characteristics rather than a genuine change in the volatility of "the commodity" in the abstract.

Models Used for Commodity Volatility Surfaces

Definition — SABR and Local Volatility Models

SABR (Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho) model: a stochastic volatility model in which the futures price $F$ and its volatility $\alpha$ both follow correlated diffusions, $$dF = \alpha F^\beta\, dW_1, \qquad d\alpha = \nu\alpha\, dW_2, \qquad dW_1\, dW_2 = \rho\, dt.$$ SABR is popular because it has a closed-form approximation for implied volatility as a function of strike, allowing fast calibration to the observed skew. The parameter $\rho$ controls the skew's sign and steepness, while $\nu$ (vol-of-vol) controls the curvature (smile).

Local volatility models: specify $\sigma = \sigma_{\text{loc}}(F,t)$ as a deterministic function of the futures price and time, calibrated (via Dupire's formula or its discrete analogue) to exactly reproduce observed option prices across all strikes and maturities. Local vol surfaces fit the current smile perfectly by construction but can produce unrealistic dynamics for how the smile evolves as $F$ moves — a known limitation that is often addressed by blending local and stochastic volatility ("local-stochastic volatility" models).

Example: The Shape of a Crude Oil Volatility Surface

Example — Stylised WTI Crude Oil Vol Surface

A typical WTI crude oil implied volatility surface might exhibit the following stylised features:

Strike dimension (skew): at the front-month expiry, implied volatility for an out-of-the-money call struck 20% above the current futures price might be around 38%, compared to roughly 33% at-the-money and 35% for an equivalent out-of-the-money put — an upward-sloping ("call skew") consistent with supply-shock risk.

Term structure dimension: implied volatility tends to be higher for near-dated expiries and decline for longer-dated ones (a downward-sloping term structure), reflecting the well-documented "Samuelson effect" — futures contracts become more volatile as they approach expiry, since news has a more concentrated impact on the price of an asset closer to delivery.

Combining these two dimensions produces a surface that slopes upward with strike (call skew) at every maturity, but where the overall level of volatility decays as maturity lengthens — a shape distinct from the classic equity index "volatility smile that flattens with maturity but tilts the other way."
