1.1 Market Structure

Commodity markets differ from equity and fixed income markets in a fundamental respect: the underlying asset is a physical good that must be produced, stored, transported, and consumed. These physical constraints shape every aspect of market structure, from the roles played by different participants to the relationship between prices at different maturities.

Market Participants

Definition - Market Participants

Producers (oil companies, mining firms, farmers) are naturally long the physical commodity and use derivatives to hedge price risk on future output.

Consumers (airlines, utilities, food manufacturers) are naturally short and hedge against rising input costs.

Merchants and traders intermediate between producers and consumers, profiting from geographic, temporal, and quality arbitrage. They maintain physical storage and logistics infrastructure.

Financial participants (banks, hedge funds, commodity trading advisors) take speculative positions based on price views and provide liquidity. They typically close out positions before physical delivery.

The interaction between physical market participants - who care about actual delivery - and financial participants - who trade paper contracts - creates the price discovery mechanism in commodity markets. The dominance of financial flows can at times decouple futures prices from physical fundamentals, a phenomenon sometimes called financialisation of commodities.

Spot Markets and Physical Delivery

A commodity spot transaction is an agreement to buy or sell a physical commodity for immediate delivery, where "immediate" is defined by the market convention of each commodity. In practice, spot transactions often settle a few days after trade, with the specific settlement window depending on the commodity and the terms of delivery.

Definition - Spot Price

The spot price $S_t$ is the current market price for immediate physical delivery of a standardised quantity and quality of a commodity at a specified location. Spot prices are determined by the intersection of current physical supply and demand and reflect immediate scarcity or surplus conditions.

Physical delivery specifications matter enormously. Crude oil contracts, for instance, specify grade (e.g., West Texas Intermediate, Brent Blend), sulphur content, API gravity, and delivery location (Cushing, Oklahoma for WTI; offshore North Sea for Brent). Deviations from the benchmark grade are reflected in quality differentials that trade in a separate market.

Exchange-Traded vs. OTC Markets

Definition - Exchange-Traded Contracts

Exchange-traded commodity contracts (futures and options) are standardised by the exchange in terms of quantity, quality, delivery location, and settlement dates. The exchange's central counterparty (CCP) eliminates bilateral credit risk through daily mark-to-market margining. Major commodity exchanges include the CME Group (NYMEX, COMEX), ICE Futures, and the London Metal Exchange (LME).

OTC commodity markets handle the non-standard transactions that exchange contracts cannot accommodate: bespoke quantities, off-benchmark delivery points, non-standard maturities, and complex structured products. OTC deals are negotiated bilaterally, though post-2008 regulatory reforms (Dodd-Frank, EMIR) have pushed standardised OTC commodity derivatives towards central clearing.

The Futures Curve and Market Term Structure

Perhaps the most distinctive feature of commodity markets is the term structure of futures prices - the futures curve $F(t, T)$ as a function of delivery date $T$. In financial markets, the forward curve is largely mechanically determined by carry. In commodity markets, the curve's shape conveys genuine economic information about expected supply and demand at different future dates.

Definition - Futures Curve

Let $F(t, T)$ denote the futures price at time $t$ for delivery at time $T$. The futures curve is the function $T \mapsto F(t, T)$ observed at a fixed time $t$. The prompt contract refers to the nearest expiry; the back months are the deferred contracts. The shape of the curve - whether it is upward-sloping (contango) or downward-sloping (backwardation) - is a key signal of current physical market conditions.

Example - WTI Crude Oil Futures Curve

During the COVID-19 demand shock of April 2020, WTI crude oil futures exhibited extreme contango: the May 2020 prompt contract briefly traded at $-37.63 per barrel as physical storage at Cushing, Oklahoma neared capacity and holders of paper contracts faced the prospect of taking physical delivery with nowhere to store it. The June 2020 contract traded near $20, reflecting the steep contango. This episode illustrates how physical constraints can drive prompt prices dramatically away from deferred prices.

In contrast, during supply-constrained periods (such as OPEC+ production cuts), crude markets often trade in steep backwardation with prompt prices well above deferred prices, reflecting current scarcity and the expectation of higher future supply.

Commodity Indices

Commodity indices provide exposure to a basket of commodity futures and are an important vehicle for financial investors seeking commodity beta. The major indices differ in their composition, weighting methodology, and roll conventions.

Remark - Major Commodity Indices

S&P GSCI (Goldman Sachs Commodity Index): production-weighted, heavily tilted toward energy (typically 60-70%). Designed to represent the global commodity universe by production volumes.

Bloomberg Commodity Index (BCOM): uses a hybrid weighting of production and liquidity, with caps to ensure diversification. More balanced across energy, metals, and agriculture than GSCI.

Roll methodology: index funds must periodically roll expiring futures contracts into the next contract. In contango markets this creates a negative roll yield - the fund sells a low-priced expiring contract and buys a higher-priced deferred contract, eroding returns. Roll yield is often the dominant source of return (positive or negative) in commodity index strategies.
