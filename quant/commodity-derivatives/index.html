Commodity Derivatives

These notes develop the quantitative foundations of commodity derivatives markets. Unlike financial assets, commodities are physical goods — crude oil, natural gas, gold, wheat — whose prices are shaped not only by supply and demand dynamics but by the physical constraints of production, storage, transportation, and consumption. This physical reality introduces concepts that have no analogue in pure financial markets: convenience yield, storage costs, seasonal price patterns, and the intricate relationship between spot and futures curves.

We begin with the structure of commodity markets and the concept of spot prices and convenience yield — the implicit benefit accruing to holders of physical inventory. The cost-of-carry model then provides the theoretical bridge between spot and futures prices, with contango and backwardation describing the two qualitatively different shapes the futures curve can take. Basis risk — the imperfect correlation between spot and futures prices — is a central risk management challenge for both producers and consumers of commodities.

Chapter 3 covers the Black-76 model, which prices options on futures contracts and is the workhorse of commodity options markets, along with the construction and interpretation of volatility surfaces which exhibit pronounced term structure and strike skew driven by supply disruption fears and storage constraints. The final chapter steps up to the portfolio level, covering commodity index investing — index construction, roll mechanics, and roll yield — and cross-commodity hedging, where exposures without a direct futures market are hedged using statistically-related instruments.

Throughout, the emphasis is on how physical constraints translate into mathematical structure — storage costs appear as negative dividend yields, convenience yield captures the option value of holding inventory, and mean-reversion in commodity prices reflects the supply response to high prices. Sector-specific applications of this framework — energy, power, metals, and agriculture — are developed in their own dedicated subjects.
