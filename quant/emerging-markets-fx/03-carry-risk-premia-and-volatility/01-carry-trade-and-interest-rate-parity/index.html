3.1 The Carry Trade and Interest Rate Parity

The carry trade - borrowing in a low-interest-rate currency and investing in a high-interest-rate currency - is among the oldest and most studied trades in FX markets. Its persistence and apparent profitability sit uneasily with one of the foundational no-arbitrage relationships in international finance, uncovered interest rate parity, and reconciling theory with the empirical record is a central theme of EM FX risk premia.

Covered Interest Rate Parity

Theorem - Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIP)

Let $S_0$ be the spot exchange rate (units of domestic/EM currency per unit of foreign/funding currency), $F_0(T)$ the $T$-maturity forward rate in the same quotation, $r_d$ the domestic (EM) interest rate, and $r_f$ the foreign (funding) interest rate, both for tenor $T$. Covered interest rate parity states: $$F_0(T) = S_0 \cdot \frac{1 + r_d T}{1 + r_f T}$$ CIP is a no-arbitrage condition enforced by the forward contract itself: an investor can lock in the forward rate today, eliminating exchange rate risk, so any deviation from this relationship would represent a riskless arbitrage (subject to transaction costs, credit risk, and - as discussed in Chapter 1 - capital controls that may prevent the arbitrage from being executed).

Uncovered Interest Rate Parity

Uncovered interest rate parity (UIP) makes a much stronger - and economically distinct - claim: that the expected future spot rate, not the forward rate, should equal the rate implied by the interest differential. Unlike CIP, UIP is not a no-arbitrage condition but a hypothesis about risk-neutral expectations.

Definition - Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP)

UIP holds if the expected exchange rate at $T$, under the physical (real-world) probability measure, satisfies: $$\mathbb{E}[S_T] = S_0 \cdot \frac{1 + r_d T}{1 + r_f T} \approx F_0(T)$$ i.e. the currency with the higher interest rate ($r_d > r_f$) is expected to depreciate (in the domestic-per-foreign quotation, $S$ rises) by approximately the interest differential, exactly offsetting the carry advantage in expectation. If UIP held exactly, the carry trade would have zero expected return - the higher interest income would be exactly offset, on average, by currency depreciation.

The Forward Premium Puzzle

A large body of empirical research - going back to seminal work in the 1980s - finds that UIP is systematically violated, and in a specific direction: high-interest-rate currencies tend, on average, to appreciate or remain flat, rather than depreciate by the interest differential as UIP predicts. This is the "forward premium puzzle" or "Fama puzzle."

Remark - The Forward Premium Puzzle and Carry Trade Returns

If $\mathbb{E}[S_T] = F_0(T)$ held, a regression of the realized change in the spot rate on the forward premium $(F_0(T)-S_0)/S_0$ should produce a slope coefficient of approximately $1$. Empirically, this slope coefficient is frequently found to be close to zero or even negative for major currency pairs - meaning high forward premia (high carry currencies) are associated with currency appreciation, not depreciation, on average. The practical consequence is that systematic carry strategies - going long high-yield currencies and short low-yield currencies - have historically generated positive average returns over long samples, i.e. a violation of UIP in the direction that rewards carry traders on average.

The leading explanations for the forward premium puzzle center on risk premia: high-yield (often EM) currencies carry a risk premium because they are more exposed to global risk factors - they tend to depreciate sharply precisely during "bad times" (global risk-off episodes, liquidity crunches, commodity price collapses for commodity-exporting EMs). Investors demand compensation - the carry - for bearing this crash risk, which on average is realized as positive carry P&L punctuated by occasional severe drawdowns exactly when the investor's broader portfolio is also under stress.

Proposition - Carry as Compensation for Crash Risk

Decompose the realized return of a carry trade over horizon $T$ as: $$R = \underbrace{(r_d - r_f)T}_{\text{interest differential ("carry")}} + \underbrace{\frac{S_T - S_0}{S_0}}_{\text{FX return}}$$ The forward premium puzzle implies $\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{S_T-S_0}{S_0}\right]$ is, on average, less negative than $-(r_d-r_f)T$ (often close to zero), so $\mathbb{E}[R] > 0$ on average. However, the distribution of $\frac{S_T-S_0}{S_0}$ for EM carry-target currencies is typically left-skewed: small positive or near-zero outcomes most of the time, with a fat left tail of large depreciations concentrated in episodes of global stress. The positive average return is therefore a premium earned for bearing a risk that is correlated with bad aggregate states - consistent with standard asset pricing theory, even though it appears as a violation of the simple UIP expectations hypothesis.

Worked Example: A Carry Trade P&L

Example - One-Year EM Carry Trade

An investor borrows USD 10,000,000 at $r_f = 4\%$ for one year, converts to local currency at spot $S_0 = 18.00$ (local currency per USD), and invests in a 1-year local-currency deposit at $r_d = 12\%$.

Currency flat18.00see below

Local currency proceeds at maturity: $10{,}000{,}000 \times 18.00 \times 1.12 = 201{,}600{,}000$ LC. USD owed: $10{,}000{,}000 \times 1.04 = \$10{,}400{,}000$.

Flat ($S_T=18.00$): Convert back: $201{,}600{,}000 / 18.00 = \$11{,}200{,}000$. Net P&L $= \$11{,}200{,}000 - \$10{,}400{,}000 = +\$800{,}000$ (the full 8% carry differential on $10mm).

Depreciation 5% ($S_T=18.90$): Convert back: $201{,}600{,}000/18.90 = \$10{,}666{,}667$. Net P&L $= \$10{,}666{,}667-\$10{,}400{,}000=+\$266{,}667$ - still positive, but the 5% depreciation has eaten roughly two-thirds of the carry.

Crisis depreciation 25% ($S_T=22.50$): Convert back: $201{,}600{,}000/22.50 = \$8{,}960{,}000$. Net P&L $= \$8{,}960{,}000-\$10{,}400{,}000=-\$1{,}440{,}000$ - a loss of more than one and a half years' worth of carry income, realized potentially within days during a currency crisis. This asymmetry - many periods of modest gains, occasional large losses concentrated in crisis episodes - is the empirical signature of the carry trade's risk premium and the reason it is often described as "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller."
