4.2 Historical Case Studies

The models of Section 4.1 are abstractions; real crises combine elements of all three generations, and play out through specific institutional and political details that no single model captures. This section reviews three of the most-studied EM currency crisis episodes, drawing out the mechanisms — and lessons — most relevant to FX risk management and EM investing.

The 1997 Asian Financial Crisis

In the years leading up to 1997, several East and Southeast Asian economies — Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, and the Philippines among them — ran exchange rates that were de facto pegged or tightly managed against the US dollar, while running current account deficits and experiencing large inflows of short-term foreign-currency capital. Domestic banks and corporates borrowed heavily in US dollars at lower rates than were available domestically, then lent or invested the proceeds in local currency, often into real estate and other assets whose returns depended on continued capital inflows — a textbook setup for the balance-sheet (third-generation) channel described in Section 4.1.

Remark — Thailand and the Regional Spread

The crisis is conventionally dated from Thailand's decision in July 1997 to float the baht after exhausting much of its foreign exchange reserves defending the currency peg against sustained speculative pressure. The baht depreciated sharply, and the crisis quickly spread to other regional currencies — the Indonesian rupiah, Korean won, Malaysian ringgit, and Philippine peso all came under severe pressure within months. This rapid spread is a classic example of contagion: investors and creditors, having been surprised by the Thai devaluation, reassessed the vulnerability of other economies with similar characteristics (pegged or quasi-pegged exchange rates, large foreign-currency-denominated short-term debt, current account deficits) and withdrew capital from those economies too, regardless of whether each country's individual fundamentals were as weak as Thailand's. The result was a near-simultaneous wave of devaluations, banking-sector distress, and sharp recessions across the region, with several countries — including Indonesia and South Korea — entering IMF-supported programs.

The Asian crisis is widely cited as the canonical illustration of the third-generation, balance-sheet/twin-crisis mechanism: the currency depreciations that followed the collapse of the pegs caused the local-currency cost of servicing dollar-denominated debt to surge, pushing already-fragile banking systems into insolvency, which in turn deepened the currency crisis as foreign lenders refused to roll over short-term credit — exactly the feedback loop described in the Remark on twin crises in Section 4.1.

Argentina: The 2001 Convertibility Crisis and the 2018 Crisis

Argentina's 1991 "Convertibility Plan" established a currency board fixing the peso to the US dollar at parity (1 peso = 1 USD), with the central bank required to hold dollar reserves backing the monetary base one-for-one. The arrangement initially succeeded in ending a period of hyperinflation, but over the following decade Argentina ran persistent fiscal deficits, accumulated substantial external (largely dollar-denominated) debt, and — crucially — the peso became significantly overvalued in real terms, particularly after Brazil, a major trading partner, devalued its own currency in 1999.

Remark — The 2001 Collapse of Convertibility

By 2001, the combination of an overvalued fixed exchange rate, unsustainable public debt levels, and a deepening recession led to a loss of confidence, large-scale capital flight, and a run on bank deposits. The government imposed restrictions on bank withdrawals (the "corralito"), defaulted on its sovereign debt — at the time the largest sovereign default in history — and in early 2002 abandoned the currency board, allowing the peso to float. The peso depreciated sharply against the dollar in the aftermath. The episode illustrates a recurring theme: a hard peg (here, a currency board, an even more rigid commitment device than an ordinary peg) can buy credibility and lower inflation for a time, but does not by itself resolve underlying fiscal and competitiveness problems, and when the peg eventually breaks under the weight of those problems, the adjustment is typically more abrupt — and the loss of policy credibility more severe — than under a more flexible regime.

Argentina experienced a further currency crisis in 2018, under a floating exchange rate regime adopted after 2015. A combination of persistent fiscal and current account deficits, high inflation, and reliance on foreign-currency borrowing left the peso vulnerable to shifts in investor sentiment; a broader emerging-market sell-off in 2018 (associated with rising US interest rates and a stronger dollar pressuring EM currencies generally) triggered a sharp depreciation of the peso and a return to an IMF-supported program. The 2018 episode is a useful contrast with 2001: even without a peg to defend, a currency can still depreciate abruptly when underlying fiscal and external imbalances are large and global financial conditions tighten — the absence of a fixed exchange rate removes one specific crisis mechanism (a discrete peg-break) but does not eliminate currency crisis risk where fundamentals are weak.

Turkish Lira Crises

The Turkish lira has experienced repeated episodes of sharp depreciation over the past several decades, most notably a severe banking and currency crisis in 2000-2001 and a major depreciation episode in 2018, with continued pressure in subsequent years. Common features across these episodes include persistent current account deficits (Turkey has long relied on capital inflows to finance a structural gap between domestic investment/consumption and savings), elevated inflation relative to trading partners, and significant foreign-currency-denominated borrowing by Turkish banks and corporates — again creating the balance-sheet vulnerability central to third-generation crisis models.

Remark — 2018 and the Interest Rate-Inflation-Exchange Rate Spiral

The 2018 lira crisis was characterized by a sharp depreciation against the dollar and euro amid concerns about the independence of monetary policy and the central bank's willingness to raise interest rates to defend the currency in the face of high and rising inflation. The episode highlighted a dynamic relevant well beyond Turkey: when a central bank is perceived as unwilling or unable to raise real interest rates sufficiently to compensate investors for currency and inflation risk, the currency can enter a self-reinforcing spiral — depreciation feeds through to higher import prices and inflation (a strong "pass-through" effect in economies with significant import dependence and foreign-currency pricing), higher inflation further erodes confidence in the currency and real returns on lira assets, prompting further capital outflows and depreciation. Breaking such a spiral typically requires a credible commitment to substantially higher real interest rates — precisely the costly defense that, per the second-generation logic of Section 4.1, governments are often reluctant to undertake, and whose absence can itself validate the depreciation that markets are pricing in.

Common Lessons for EM FX Risk

Remark — Recurring Themes Across Episodes

Despite very different institutional settings — a regional contagion event across pegged Asian currencies in 1997, a currency board collapse and later a floating-regime crisis in Argentina, and recurring inflation-linked depreciation episodes in Turkey — several themes recur, and connect directly back to the rest of this subject: (1) overvaluation under a peg or quasi-peg (Chapter 2) creates a one-way bet that speculators eventually take; (2) reliance on foreign-currency debt to finance current account deficits transforms a currency depreciation into a domestic credit and banking crisis via the balance-sheet channel; (3) contagion means that an attack on one country's currency can rapidly reprice the perceived risk of others with similar vulnerabilities, even absent new country-specific information — exactly the kind of correlated, "bad state" risk that the carry trade's risk premium (Chapter 3) is compensation for; and (4) central bank credibility and willingness to raise real rates is often the decisive factor in whether a crisis is contained or spirals — a theme that links the intervention and reserve-management tools of Chapter 2 directly to the crisis dynamics of this chapter. For an EM FX investor or risk manager, the practical implication is that the carry, the skew priced into options (Chapter 3), and the reserve and policy framework of a country (Chapter 2) are not independent considerations — they are different views of the same underlying vulnerability to the kinds of crisis dynamics described in this chapter.
