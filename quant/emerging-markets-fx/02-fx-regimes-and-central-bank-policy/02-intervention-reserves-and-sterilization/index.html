2.2 Central Bank Intervention, Reserves, and Sterilization

Central banks influence their exchange rates primarily by buying or selling foreign currency (typically USD) against the domestic currency in the spot or forward market - foreign exchange intervention. The scale and effectiveness of intervention is constrained by the central bank's stock of foreign exchange reserves, and the choice of how intervention is financed (sterilized or unsterilized) has consequences for domestic monetary conditions that extend well beyond the exchange rate itself.

FX Reserves

Definition - Foreign Exchange Reserves

Foreign exchange reserves are foreign-currency-denominated assets (predominantly highly liquid sovereign or quasi-sovereign securities such as US Treasuries, plus gold and reserve positions at the IMF) held by a central bank or monetary authority. Reserves serve multiple purposes: providing the foreign currency needed to defend a peg or smooth volatility via intervention, serving as a buffer against external shocks (e.g. sudden stops in capital inflows or commodity price shocks for commodity exporters), and supporting the country's external creditworthiness. A common rule-of-thumb adequacy metric is reserves relative to short-term external debt (the "Greenspan-Guidotti rule": reserves should cover at least one year of maturing short-term external debt) or relative to several months of imports.

Sterilized vs. Unsterilized Intervention

When a central bank intervenes by selling foreign currency and buying domestic currency (to support the domestic currency), it removes domestic currency from circulation - a contractionary effect on the domestic monetary base, all else equal. Conversely, buying foreign currency and selling domestic currency (to weaken or cap appreciation of the domestic currency) injects domestic currency into the banking system - an expansionary effect. Whether the central bank allows this monetary base effect to persist, or offsets it with an opposing domestic money-market operation, defines sterilized vs. unsterilized intervention.

Definition - Sterilized Intervention

Unsterilized intervention is FX intervention left to flow through to the domestic monetary base unaltered - buying FX (selling local currency) expands the monetary base; selling FX (buying local currency) contracts it. Sterilized intervention pairs the FX operation with an offsetting domestic operation - typically issuing (or buying back) central bank bills, reverse repos, or adjusting reserve requirements - so that the net effect on the domestic monetary base, and hence (in principle) on domestic short-term interest rates, is approximately zero. Most EM central bank intervention in normal times is sterilized, in order to pursue an exchange rate objective without compromising the separate inflation/interest rate objective of monetary policy.

Remark - The Sterilization Trilemma

Sterilized intervention is the practical resolution central banks attempt for the open-economy "trilemma": a country cannot simultaneously maintain (1) a fixed exchange rate, (2) free capital mobility, and (3) independent monetary policy. Sterilized intervention attempts to hold (1) and (2) while preserving some independence in (3), but this is not free - sterilization typically requires the central bank to issue domestic-currency liabilities (sterilization bills) paying the domestic policy rate while holding foreign reserves earning the (often lower) foreign risk-free rate, generating a "quasi-fiscal cost" of intervention that grows with the scale and duration of sterilized positions, particularly when the domestic-foreign rate differential is large and persistent - exactly the situation that motivates capital inflows and appreciation pressure in the first place.

Signaling and the Forward Market

Beyond its direct, mechanical effect on the spot exchange rate (via the supply/demand impact of the transaction itself), intervention carries a signaling component: it reveals information about the central bank's reaction function, its tolerance for currency moves, and potentially the scale of reserves it is willing to commit. Markets often react to intervention disproportionately to its size, because participants update their beliefs about the probability and scale of future intervention.

Remark - Intervention in the Forward Market vs. Spot Market

Central banks can intervene directly in the FX forward or NDF market (rather than, or in addition to, spot), which has the advantage of not requiring an immediate cash outflow of reserves - a forward sale of USD settles only at maturity. This is particularly relevant where spot reserves are limited but the central bank wishes to influence the forward curve (and hence the implied interest rate differential priced by NDFs) directly. Some EM central banks have, at times, built up very large forward USD-selling books as a substitute for spot reserve depletion - a strategy that defers, but does not eliminate, the eventual need to deliver or roll the position, and can itself become a source of stress if forwards come due during a period of reserve scarcity.

Worked Example: Intervention and Forward Points

Example - Sterilized Intervention's Effect on the Forward Curve

Consider a currency trading at spot $S_0 = 20.00$ (local currency per USD) under depreciation pressure. The 1-year forward, prior to intervention, trades at $F_0 = 21.00$, implying (via CIP with a 5% USD rate) a local 1-year rate of:

$$1 + r_{\text{local}} = (1+r_{\$})\frac{F_0}{S_0} = 1.05 \times \frac{21.00}{20.00} = 1.05 \times 1.05 = 1.1025 \implies r_{\text{local}} \approx 10.25\%$$

The central bank now intervenes by selling USD 2bn spot against local currency, pushing spot to $S_0' = 19.80$ (a modest appreciation). Selling USD and buying local currency mechanically drains local-currency liquidity from the banking system; a pure sterilization operation would offset this by injecting liquidity back, which would tend to push local rates back down - working against the intervention's goal of supporting the currency. To reinforce the spot intervention instead of merely sterilizing it, the central bank allows local short-term rates to rise to 11.25% (a 100bp hike, tightening liquidity beyond what pure sterilization would imply).

The new 1-year forward consistent with CIP is: $$F_0' = S_0' \times \frac{1+r_{\text{local}}'}{1+r_{\$}} = 19.80 \times \frac{1.1125}{1.05} \approx 19.80 \times 1.0595 \approx 20.98$$

Despite the spot appreciation from 20.00 to 19.80, the forward barely moved (21.00 to 20.98) because the rate hike widened the interest differential, which mechanically pushes forward points higher (more local currency per USD at maturity) even as spot strengthens. This illustrates why analyzing only the spot reaction to intervention can be misleading: the forward curve - and the NDF-implied rate differential - often carries the larger and more persistent signal of the policy package's net effect, and a hawkish rate response can substantially offset the forward-curve impact of a spot-supportive intervention.
