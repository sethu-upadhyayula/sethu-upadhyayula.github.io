2.1 Exchange Rate Regimes: Pegs, Bands, and Managed Floats

The IMF classifies exchange rate arrangements along a spectrum from "hard pegs" with no independent monetary policy, through various intermediate regimes involving explicit or implicit bands and crawling adjustments, to "floating" arrangements where the exchange rate is, in principle, market-determined. Where a currency sits on this spectrum has first-order implications for how its forwards and options should be priced and risk-managed.

A Taxonomy of Regimes

Definition - Exchange Rate Regime Classifications

Hard peg / currency board. The domestic currency is fixed at a specified rate to an anchor currency (often USD or EUR), with the central bank legally committed - and typically required to hold foreign reserves backing the entire monetary base - to exchange domestic currency for the anchor at that fixed rate on demand. Examples include Hong Kong's currency board (HKD pegged to USD) and historically Argentina's convertibility regime (1991-2001).

Conventional (fixed) peg. The currency is pegged to an anchor (or basket) at a fixed central rate, with the central bank intervening to defend the peg, but without the strict reserve-backing rules of a currency board. The peg can in principle be adjusted (devalued or revalued) by policy decision.

Crawling peg / crawling band. The central rate is adjusted at a pre-announced or de facto pace - e.g. depreciating by a fixed percentage per month - typically to accommodate an inflation differential with the anchor currency while preserving relative price competitiveness. A crawling band combines a crawling central rate with a tolerance band around it.

Managed float (with no pre-announced path). The exchange rate is nominally determined by the market, but the central bank intervenes - sometimes heavily and frequently - without committing to any specific level or path, in order to smooth volatility, resist disorderly moves, or pursue an undisclosed target.

Free float. The exchange rate is determined by market forces with no (or only occasional, exceptional) central bank intervention. Few EM currencies are true free floats in practice; most fall somewhere in the managed float category.

Pricing Implications: The Peg as a Truncated Distribution

A credible peg or band fundamentally changes the distribution of the exchange rate at any future horizon $T$ relative to a freely floating currency. Where a free float's distribution of $S_T$ might be reasonably modelled as roughly lognormal with a volatility reflecting macro and flow-driven uncertainty, a currency pegged within a band $[\underline{S}, \overline{S}]$ has a distribution that is - as long as the peg holds - truncated to that band, with probability mass piling up near the edges if a realignment is anticipated.

Remark - Implied Volatility Under a Credible Band

For a currency trading inside a credible band, at-the-money implied volatility for short tenors can be very low - the market assigns high probability to the spot rate remaining within the band - while skew can be steep and concentrated in one direction: option markets price a small probability of a discontinuous "jump" out of the band (a devaluation or band-widening), generating fat tails in one direction that show up as elevated implied volatility for out-of-the-money options struck beyond the band edge, even while at-the-money volatility remains compressed. This is the classic shape of a "peg risk" volatility smile.

Regime-Switching and the Forward Curve

A crawling peg generates a forward curve that is, mechanically, close to a straight-line extrapolation of the announced (or de facto) crawl rate - as long as the market believes the crawl will continue. Forward points under a crawling peg are therefore driven less by the conventional interest rate differential (covered interest parity) and more directly by the announced or inferred rate of currency depreciation, especially at shorter tenors where capital controls limit pure interest-arbitrage activity.

Proposition - Forward Points Under a Crawling Peg

If the central bank depreciates the currency at an annualized crawl rate $c$ (so $S_t \approx S_0 e^{ct}$ as long as the crawl is maintained), and the market assigns this crawl high credibility over horizon $T$, the forward rate will trade close to: $$F_0(T) \approx S_0\, e^{cT}$$ independent of the conventional CIP-implied differential $r_{\text{local}} - r_{\$}$. If the market instead believes the crawl rate may change - e.g. the central bank may accelerate depreciation in response to inflation or reserve pressure - the forward will incorporate a probability-weighted blend of crawl-rate scenarios, and the implied "interest rate differential" backed out via CIP will simply reflect the market's expectation of the future crawl path rather than any actual money-market rate differential.

Regime Changes

A central feature of EM FX risk is that the exchange rate regime itself is not fixed - pegs are abandoned, bands are widened or narrowed, and managed floats can become de facto pegs (or vice versa) as policy priorities shift. A regime change is typically a discontinuous event: the exchange rate can move by a large percentage in a single day when a peg is abandoned, dwarfing the typical daily move under either the old or new regime.

Remark - Regime Risk as a Distinct Risk Factor

For risk management purposes, it is useful to decompose total EM FX risk into (1) risk conditional on the current regime persisting - which may be small for a credible peg - and (2) the risk of a regime change itself, which is rare but can produce moves of 10-50% or more in a single session. Standard risk metrics calibrated to historical daily volatility under a persistent peg will badly understate tail risk if a regime change becomes plausible; this is precisely the gap that EM FX options markets attempt to price via skew, discussed further in Chapter 3.
