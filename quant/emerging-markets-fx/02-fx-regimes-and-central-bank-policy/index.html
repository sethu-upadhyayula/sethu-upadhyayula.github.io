2. EM FX Regimes and Central Bank Policy

Unlike most G10 currencies, which float relatively freely against each other, the majority of EM currencies operate under exchange rate arrangements where the central bank plays an active, ongoing role in determining or constraining the exchange rate's path. These arrangements range from hard pegs and currency boards, through crawling pegs and target bands, to "managed floats" that are nominally free-floating but subject to frequent discretionary intervention. Understanding where a currency sits on this spectrum — and how that classification can change, sometimes abruptly — is essential for pricing EM FX forwards, options, and for risk managing EM exposures.

This chapter first develops a classification of exchange rate regimes and works through the pricing implications of each — in particular how a peg, band, or crawling peg changes the shape of the implied volatility surface and the dynamics of forward points relative to a freely floating currency. The second section turns to the central bank's toolkit for managing its currency: how foreign exchange reserves are built and deployed, the distinction between sterilized and unsterilized intervention, and how intervention — and the credible threat of it — feeds through to forward points and the term structure of implied volatility.
