1. Emerging Markets Currency Markets

Emerging market (EM) currencies trade with distinctive features relative to the major developed-market (G10) currencies: wider bid-offer spreads, capital controls and convertibility restrictions in some jurisdictions, higher sensitivity to global risk sentiment, and — for many EM currencies — an offshore non-deliverable forward (NDF) market that trades alongside (or in place of) deliverable onshore markets.

Non-Deliverable Forwards

Definition — Non-Deliverable Forward (NDF)

An NDF is a forward contract on an exchange rate that is cash-settled in a convertible currency (typically USD) rather than by physical delivery of the EM currency. At maturity, the NDF is settled by reference to a published "fixing" rate $S_{\text{fix}}$ for the EM currency against USD:

$$\text{Payoff} = N \times \left(\frac{1}{K} - \frac{1}{S_{\text{fix}}}\right) \quad \text{(or the analogous expression depending on quotation convention)}$$

where $N$ is the notional (in USD) and $K$ is the contracted forward rate. NDFs allow offshore investors to take FX exposure to currencies subject to capital controls (historically including the Chinese yuan, Indian rupee, Korean won, Brazilian real, and others) without needing to hold or transact in the onshore currency itself.

Onshore-Offshore Basis

Where both an onshore deliverable forward market and an offshore NDF market exist for the same currency pair, their implied forward rates can diverge — the "onshore-offshore basis" — reflecting differences in liquidity, capital controls, and the cost of accessing each market.

Remark — CNY vs CNH

The Chinese renminbi trades onshore as CNY (subject to PBoC daily fixing bands and capital controls) and offshore (primarily in Hong Kong) as CNH, which trades more freely and is accessible to international investors without onshore account restrictions. CNY and CNH can trade at different spot levels and imply different forward points, with the spread between them widening during periods of capital flow pressure or policy intervention — a useful real-time gauge of market expectations for renminbi policy and capital account dynamics.

Carry and EM FX

EM currencies typically carry higher domestic interest rates than G10 currencies, so by covered interest rate parity (see FX Forwards and Interest Rate Parity), EM currencies trade at a forward discount to spot when quoted against USD or EUR — a long EM-currency, short-USD position earns positive carry from this interest rate differential.

Example — EM Carry Trade and Risk

An investor borrows USD at a low rate (say 2%) and invests in a local-currency EM bond yielding 10%, earning an 8% annualised carry before any change in the exchange rate. This trade profits as long as the EM currency does not depreciate by more than the carry differential over the holding period. However, EM currencies are prone to sharp, discontinuous depreciations during episodes of global risk aversion ("risk-off" events), capital flight, or domestic policy shocks — wiping out months or years of accumulated carry in a matter of days. This asymmetry (steady small gains, occasional large losses) is the classic risk profile of carry trades, sometimes summarised as "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller."

Capital Controls and Fixing Risk

Remark — Fixing Methodology Risk

Because NDFs settle against a published fixing rate rather than an actual currency exchange, the methodology and reliability of that fixing matters significantly. Some EM central banks have, at various points, restricted or suspended the fixings used for NDF settlement, or imposed restrictions on onshore banks' participation in the offshore NDF market, in an effort to reduce the influence of offshore trading on onshore exchange rates. Trading EM FX therefore carries not only market risk (the exchange rate itself) but also regulatory and convertibility risk — the risk that the rules governing how a position settles change during the life of the trade.
