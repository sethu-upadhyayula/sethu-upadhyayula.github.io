1.2 Onshore-Offshore Basis, Capital Controls, and Fixing Risk

When a currency trades in two segmented venues — an onshore market subject to domestic capital controls, and an offshore market open to international participants — the forward (and sometimes spot) rates in the two venues need not coincide. The gap between them, the onshore-offshore basis, is one of the most closely watched signals in EM FX: it is a direct, market-implied measure of how binding capital controls are at any given moment, and of the market's expectations for policy action.

The Canonical Example: CNY and CNH

The clearest illustration is the Chinese renminbi, which trades onshore as CNY — subject to a daily central parity (fixing) set by the People's Bank of China, a managed trading band around that parity, and restrictions on cross-border capital flows — and offshore, primarily in Hong Kong (and other offshore centres such as Singapore and London), as CNH, which trades with far fewer restrictions and is freely accessible to international investors.

Definition — Onshore-Offshore Basis

For a currency with onshore forward rate $F^{\text{on}}_0(T)$ and offshore (NDF or deliverable offshore) forward rate $F^{\text{off}}_0(T)$ for the same tenor $T$, the onshore-offshore basis is: $$\text{Basis}(T) = F^{\text{off}}_0(T) - F^{\text{on}}_0(T)$$ often expressed in forward points or as an annualized percentage of spot. The basis can also be defined on implied interest rate differentials: the gap between the offshore-implied local-currency rate $r^{\text{off}}_{\text{local}}$ (backed out of $F^{\text{off}}_0$ via CIP) and the onshore policy or money-market rate $r^{\text{on}}_{\text{local}}$.

Remark — CNY vs. CNH in Practice

CNY trades within a band (historically ±2%) around a daily reference rate set by the PBoC, with onshore banks required to operate within regulatory limits on their FX positions and cross-border flows tightly monitored. CNH, by contrast, is a genuinely market-determined exchange rate driven by offshore supply and demand for renminbi — which is itself influenced by trade settlement flows, offshore renminbi deposit levels in Hong Kong, and investor positioning. CNY and CNH spot rates can differ by tens to hundreds of pips, and their forward curves can imply quite different short-term interest rate differentials, especially around dates when capital flow pressures are acute (e.g. during episodes of expected renminbi depreciation, when offshore renminbi becomes scarce and offshore short-term rates spike, compressing CNH forward points relative to CNY).

How Capital Controls Drive the Basis

In a frictionless world, any divergence between onshore and offshore forward rates for the same currency would be arbitraged away: a bank with access to both markets could borrow in the cheap market, lend in the expensive one, and convert at spot, locking in a riskless profit. Capital controls break this arbitrage by restricting precisely the cross-border flows — currency conversion, lending, repatriation — that the arbitrage requires.

Proposition — Capital Controls as a Limit to Arbitrage

Let $A$ denote the maximum cross-border flow of the EM currency permitted by capital controls over horizon $T$ (a quota, licensing requirement, or outright prohibition for certain transaction types). When the onshore-offshore basis implies an arbitrage profit exceeding the cost of obtaining the necessary licenses, quota allocation, or circumventing controls (where legally possible at all), but the required flow exceeds $A$, the basis can persist in equilibrium without being arbitraged to zero. The tighter the controls (smaller $A$, or higher effective cost of accessing onshore markets), the larger the basis that can persist, and the more sensitive the basis becomes to shifts in offshore sentiment relative to onshore fundamentals.

In practice this means the onshore-offshore basis widens sharply during episodes when offshore investors expect depreciation (or capital flight) that onshore policy is trying to resist: offshore demand to short the currency forward pushes $F^{\text{off}}$ down (or the implied offshore funding rate for the local currency up) faster than the onshore market — where capital controls limit the same speculative activity — can adjust. The basis therefore functions as a real-time gauge of (a) the market's expectation for the currency's path and (b) the perceived credibility and binding-ness of the controls themselves.

Fixing Risk Revisited

Because NDF settlement depends on a published fixing, and the entity publishing that fixing is frequently the same central bank whose policy the market is trying to price, NDF positions carry a distinct form of risk: the fixing methodology itself can change, with direct and immediate P&L consequences for every open NDF position, independent of where the underlying currency "should" trade.

Remark — Historical Episodes of Fixing and NDF Market Intervention

At various points, EM central banks have taken direct action affecting NDF markets: restricting onshore banks' ability to transact in offshore NDFs (reducing the arbitrage link and widening the onshore-offshore basis further), changing the calculation methodology or publication time of the reference fixing, or using moral suasion and direct intervention to influence the fixing's level on a given day. From a risk management perspective, this means an NDF position is exposed not only to the economic fundamentals driving the exchange rate, but to the discretionary actions of an authority that may have an interest in the settlement outcome — a form of risk with no clean analogue in G10 deliverable forwards.

Worked Example: An Onshore-Offshore Basis Trade

Example — Trading the CNY/CNH Basis

Suppose the 3-month USD/CNY (onshore) forward points imply an annualized CNY funding rate of 2.5%, while the 3-month USD/CNH (offshore) NDF implies an annualized offshore CNH funding rate of 4.0%, with spot USD/CNY = USD/CNH = 7.00 (assume spot is aligned for simplicity).

QuantityOnshore (CNY)Offshore (CNH)
Spot7.007.00
Implied local-currency rate (3M, ann.)2.5%4.0%
USD rate (3M, ann.)5.0%5.0%

Using simple CIP, $F = S \cdot \frac{1 + r_{\text{local}} T}{1 + r_{\$}T}$ with $T = 0.25$:

Onshore forward: $F^{\text{on}} = 7.00 \times \frac{1 + 0.025 \times 0.25}{1 + 0.05 \times 0.25} = 7.00 \times \frac{1.00625}{1.0125} \approx 6.9568$

Offshore forward: $F^{\text{off}} = 7.00 \times \frac{1 + 0.040 \times 0.25}{1 + 0.05 \times 0.25} = 7.00 \times \frac{1.01000}{1.0125} \approx 6.9827$

The basis is $F^{\text{off}} - F^{\text{on}} \approx 6.9827 - 6.9568 = +0.0259$, i.e. the offshore (CNH) forward implies materially fewer forward points of CNY appreciation than the onshore market — offshore renminbi is "tighter" (offshore funding for CNH is more expensive), consistent with offshore depreciation pressure or constrained offshore CNH liquidity.

A bank with access to both markets would, in principle, want to receive the cheap onshore-implied rate and pay the expensive offshore-implied rate — e.g. borrow CNY onshore at 2.5%, swap into USD, lend USD, and separately borrow USD to lend CNH offshore at the equivalent of 4.0% — capturing the spread. In practice, capital controls prevent freely moving CNY raised onshore into the offshore CNH market, which is precisely why this 150 bp gap in implied rates can persist rather than being arbitraged away.
