1.1 Non-Deliverable Forwards and Offshore Markets

For many EM currencies, capital controls or convertibility restrictions prevent non-residents from freely holding, delivering, or transacting in the local currency outside the domestic banking system. To allow international investors and corporates to hedge or take exposure to these currencies anyway, the market developed the non-deliverable forward (NDF) - a cash-settled forward contract that replicates the economic exposure of a deliverable FX forward without requiring any exchange of the restricted currency.

Mechanics of an NDF

Definition - Non-Deliverable Forward

An NDF is an OTC contract between two counterparties to exchange, at a future settlement date $T$, the difference between a contracted forward rate $K$ and a reference spot rate $S_{\text{fix}}$ observed at $T$, applied to an agreed notional and settled in a convertible currency (almost always USD). No principal in the EM currency ever changes hands. For a USD-settled NDF on notional $N$ (in USD) with quote convention EM-currency-per-USD, the payoff to the party long the EM currency forward (i.e. long the NDF in the sense of being long the EM currency / short USD at rate $K$) is: $$\text{Payoff} = N \times \left(\frac{1}{K} - \frac{1}{S_{\text{fix}}}\right)$$ measured in USD. Equivalently, if the NDF notional is denominated in the EM currency and quoted USD-per-EM-currency, the payoff is $N\left(S_{\text{fix}} - K\right)$ in USD, discounted appropriately. The exact convention (which leg is fixed in USD vs. local currency terms) varies by currency pair and must be checked against the relevant ISDA template or broker convention.

Because settlement is purely a cash difference in USD, an NDF can be transacted, cleared, and settled entirely between two offshore counterparties - neither of whom needs an onshore account, a local custodian, or any regulatory approval to hold the EM currency. This is what makes the offshore NDF market accessible to the broad international investor base even where the onshore currency itself is tightly controlled.

Remark - NDFs as a Synthetic Forward

An NDF can be thought of as a bet on the level of the fixing rate, settled in hard currency. From the perspective of an offshore investor, a long NDF position in an EM currency behaves economically like a long position in a deliverable forward - gains and losses move one-for-one with the exchange rate (subject to the precise quotation convention) - but with counterparty credit risk to the NDF dealer substituting for the settlement and convertibility risk of actually holding the local currency.

The Fixing

The single most important design element of an NDF is the fixing - the reference spot rate $S_{\text{fix}}$ used to determine the cash settlement amount. Because the NDF's entire payoff depends on this one published number, the fixing source, methodology, and publication time are specified precisely in the trade confirmation and in industry-standard templates (e.g. EMTA templates for Latin American and Asian currencies).

Definition - Fixing Rate

The fixing rate $S_{\text{fix}}$ is a spot exchange rate published by a designated source - typically a central bank (e.g. the PBoC's USD/CNY central parity, or Banco Central do Brasil's PTAX rate), a polling agency that surveys a panel of local banks, or a recognized market data vendor - at a specified time on (or shortly before) the NDF's fixing date. The fixing date is usually one or two business days before the settlement/value date, mirroring the spot lag of the underlying currency.

Because the fixing is administratively determined - published by an authority rather than observed directly in a continuous market - it introduces a layer of operational and policy risk that does not exist in deliverable G10 forwards. A central bank that changes its fixing methodology, suspends publication, or manages the fixing toward a policy-preferred level directly changes the settlement value of every outstanding NDF referencing it, independent of where the "true" market-clearing spot rate might be.

Pricing an NDF: Covered Interest Rate Parity

In principle, an NDF should price like any other FX forward: via covered interest rate parity (CIP), using the interest rate differential between the two currencies over the forward's tenor. In practice, the relevant "interest rate" for the EM currency leg is not always the onshore policy or money-market rate, because offshore participants frequently cannot access onshore money markets directly - instead, an implied offshore interest rate is backed out from NDF prices themselves.

Theorem - Covered Interest Rate Parity for NDFs

Let $S_0$ be the current spot exchange rate (units of EM currency per USD), $F_0$ the NDF forward rate for tenor $T$, $r_{\$}$ the USD interest rate, and $r_{\text{EM}}^{\text{implied}}$ the implied EM-currency interest rate consistent with the quoted NDF. Covered interest rate parity gives: $$F_0 = S_0 \cdot \frac{1 + r_{\text{EM}}^{\text{implied}} \cdot T}{1 + r_{\$} \cdot T}$$ Rearranging, the NDF-implied interest rate differential (in forward points) is: $$F_0 - S_0 \approx S_0 \cdot T \cdot \left(r_{\text{EM}}^{\text{implied}} - r_{\$}\right)$$ When capital controls bind, $r_{\text{EM}}^{\text{implied}}$ can diverge materially from the onshore policy rate, reflecting the scarcity (or surplus) of the EM currency available to offshore counterparties relative to onshore conditions.

Offshore Market Access and Liquidity

NDF markets exist for most major EM currencies with restricted convertibility, historically including the Chinese renminbi (traded offshore as CNH/NDF before the CNH deliverable market matured), the Indian rupee (INR), the Korean won (KRW), the Brazilian real (BRL), the Malaysian ringgit (MYR), and others. Liquidity is concentrated in shorter tenors (1 week to 1 year) and tends to be deepest around the Asian and London trading sessions for Asian currencies, and the New York session for Latin American currencies.

Remark - Onshore Deliverable vs. Offshore Non-Deliverable

Even where a currency has a fully deliverable onshore forward market (e.g. for residents and licensed institutions), an offshore NDF market can persist and trade alongside it, serving participants who lack onshore market access. The two markets are linked only loosely - by arbitrage activity from banks with access to both - and during periods of capital flow restriction this arbitrage link can break down entirely, leaving the onshore and offshore markets to trade at materially different implied rates. This divergence, the onshore-offshore basis, is the subject of the next section.

Worked Example: Pricing and Settling an NDF

Example - 3-Month USD/BRL NDF

A US-based investor enters a 3-month NDF to sell USD 10,000,000 and buy Brazilian real at a contracted forward rate of $K = 5.20$ BRL per USD. The contract is quoted USD-per-notional with settlement based on the PTAX fixing.

ItemValue
Notional ($N$)USD 10,000,000
Contract rate ($K$)5.20 BRL/USD
PTAX fixing at maturity ($S_{\text{fix}}$)5.35 BRL/USD

The investor is long USD / short BRL forward at 5.20. Since BRL has depreciated (more BRL per USD at maturity than contracted), the investor's USD position has gained value. Using the BRL-per-USD payoff convention, the cash settlement to the long-USD party is: $$\text{Payoff} = N \times \left(\frac{S_{\text{fix}} - K}{S_{\text{fix}}}\right) = 10{,}000{,}000 \times \left(\frac{5.35 - 5.20}{5.35}\right) = 10{,}000{,}000 \times 0.02804 \approx \$280{,}400$$

No BRL changes hands at all - the entire economic exposure to the BRL/USD rate is captured in this single USD cash payment, computed purely from the difference between the contracted rate and the published fixing. Had the real instead appreciated relative to $K$ (e.g. fixing at 5.05), the same formula would produce a negative payoff to the long-USD party, i.e. a payment in the other direction.
