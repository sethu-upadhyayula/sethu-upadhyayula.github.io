Emerging Markets FX

Emerging market (EM) currencies trade with distinctive features relative to the major developed-market (G10) currencies: capital controls and convertibility restrictions, offshore non-deliverable forward (NDF) markets that trade alongside or in place of onshore markets, managed exchange rate regimes with active central bank intervention, structurally higher carry and volatility, and a history of sharp currency crises with global contagion effects. These notes build up the market structure, policy framework, risk premia, and crisis dynamics that distinguish EM FX trading and pricing from G10 FX.

The first chapter covers the institutional structure of EM currency markets: how NDFs work and settle, why offshore and onshore markets for the same currency can diverge, and the fixing and convertibility risks unique to EM. The second chapter turns to the policy side - the spectrum of exchange rate regimes from hard pegs to free floats, and how central banks manage reserves and intervene to defend or guide their currencies.

The third chapter develops the carry trade through the lens of covered and uncovered interest rate parity, and examines how EM FX option markets price skew and tail risk via risk reversals and butterflies. The fourth and final chapter studies how currency crises unfold mechanically - speculative attacks, reserve depletion, and self-fulfilling dynamics - and reviews several of the most consequential historical episodes, from the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis to Argentina's repeated currency crises and the Turkish lira's recent turmoil.
