2.2 Basis Swaps and Tenor Basis

Section 2.1 noted that forecast curves for different tenors of the same underlying index - e.g. 3-month and 6-month SOFR or LIBOR - are not generally identical, and that the spread between them is calibrated from tenor basis swaps. This section examines that instrument directly: what it is, why the basis exists, and how it is priced and used in curve construction.

Tenor Basis Swaps

Definition - Tenor Basis Swap

A tenor basis swap (or "basis swap") exchanges two floating legs referencing the same underlying index family but different tenors - most commonly 3-month vs. 6-month - on a common notional, with no fixed leg. One leg pays the shorter-tenor rate (e.g. 3M) plus a fixed spread $b$, the other pays the longer-tenor rate (e.g. 6M) flat: $$\text{Leg A: } N\big(L_{3M}(t_{i-1},t_i) + b\big)\delta_i^A \qquad \text{Leg B: } N\,L_{6M}(t_{j-1},t_j)\,\delta_j^B$$ The spread $b$ - quoted in basis points - is set so the swap has zero value at inception. By convention $b$ is usually added to the shorter-tenor leg, since shorter-tenor rates have historically traded below longer-tenor rates of the same credit family.

Why Tenor Basis Exists

In a frictionless market with no credit or liquidity differences, a strip of four consecutive 3-month rates should equal a single 6-month rate spanning the same period (compounding effects aside) - so the tenor basis spread should be roughly zero. In practice it is not, and the spread itself carries information about term funding markets.

Remark - Drivers of Tenor Basis

Credit and liquidity premium for longer commitments. A 6-month unsecured loan (historically, 6M LIBOR) embeds a credit and liquidity premium for the longer commitment period relative to a 3-month loan rolled twice - lenders demand more compensation for being exposed to a counterparty's credit for longer without the option to reprice. This premium is the classical explanation for positive 6M-vs-3M basis (6M trading above the rolled 3M strip).

Convexity and roll risk. A strip of 3M rates carries reinvestment/roll risk - the rate for each successive 3-month period is unknown until it resets - whereas a single 6M rate fixes the cost for the whole period upfront. This timing difference contributes a (typically small) convexity-related component to the basis.

Supply and demand for each tenor. Structural demand imbalances - e.g. floating-rate notes and loans that reference 3M, versus swap and bond markets that reference 6M - create supply/demand pressure on each tenor's forward curve independently of the credit-premium argument, and this pressure shows up directly in the basis swap market.

Pricing a Basis Swap and Calibrating Tenor Forecast Curves

Given the discount curve $P_d$ and the forecast curve for one tenor (say $P_{3M}$, already bootstrapped from vanilla 3M swaps as in Section 2.1), the basis swap market is the calibration instrument for $P_{6M}$.

Theorem - Pricing a Tenor Basis Swap

The value of the basis swap to the payer of the 3M-plus-spread leg is the difference of the two legs' present values: $$V = \underbrace{\sum_j \delta_j^B F_{6M,j}\,P_d(0,t_j)}_{PV\text{ of 6M leg}} - \underbrace{\sum_i \delta_i^A\big(F_{3M,i}+b\big)P_d(0,t_i)}_{PV\text{ of 3M+spread leg}}$$ where $F_{3M,i}$ are forward rates from the already-known $P_{3M}$ curve. Setting $V=0$ and solving for the par basis spread $b^*$ at a given maturity gives one calibration equation; bootstrapping $P_{6M}$ proceeds node-by-node exactly as in Section 2.1, but using basis swap quotes (and the known $P_{3M}$, $P_d$) rather than fixed-vs-floating swap quotes directly.

Equivalently, once $P_{6M}$ is calibrated, the basis swap is correctly repriced to zero using $P_d$ for discounting and $P_{3M}$, $P_{6M}$ for the respective forward rates - exactly analogous to how a vanilla swap reprices to zero using $P_d$ and a single forecast curve.

Worked Tenor Basis Example

Example - Implied 6M Forward from a 3M Curve and Basis Spread

Suppose for a particular 6-month period, the 3M forecast curve implies two consecutive 3M forward rates of $F_{3M,1}=4.80\%$ and $F_{3M,2}=4.85\%$ (each accruing over $\delta=0.25$), and the market 3M-vs-6M basis swap quotes a spread of $b=8\text{bp}=0.08\%$ added to the 3M leg over this period.

Ignoring discounting effects within the short period for simplicity (i.e. treating $P_d(0,t_i)\approx P_d(0,t_2)$ for both sub-periods), the par condition $\delta^B F_{6M} \approx \delta^A(F_{3M,1}+b) + \delta^A(F_{3M,2}+b)$ with $\delta^A=0.25$, $\delta^B=0.5$ gives: $$F_{6M} \approx \frac{0.25\times(0.0480+0.0008) + 0.25\times(0.0485+0.0008)}{0.5} = \frac{0.25\times 0.0488 + 0.25\times 0.0493}{0.5}$$ $$F_{6M} \approx \frac{0.01220+0.01233}{0.5} = \frac{0.02453}{0.5} \approx 4.905\%$$

The implied 6-month forward rate (4.905%) sits above the average of the two 3-month forwards (4.825%) by approximately the basis spread of 8bp - confirming that, in this simplified setting, the tenor basis spread is directly absorbed into the gap between the 3M and 6M forecast curves' implied forward rates over the same period.
