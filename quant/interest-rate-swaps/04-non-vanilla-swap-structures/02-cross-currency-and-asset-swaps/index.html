4.2 Cross-Currency Swaps and Asset Swaps

This final section covers two structures that connect the single-currency swap market developed in Chapters 1-3 to other markets: cross-currency swaps, which exchange cash flows in two different currencies and connect the rates market to the FX market; and asset swaps, which combine a swap with a bond to isolate and trade a bond's credit spread, connecting the rates market to the credit market.

Cross-Currency Swap Structure

Definition — Cross-Currency Basis Swap

A cross-currency (basis) swap exchanges floating-rate payments in two currencies, say USD and EUR, on notionals $N_{USD}$ and $N_{EUR}=N_{USD}/X_0$ (where $X_0$ is the spot exchange rate at inception). Unlike a single-currency swap, principal is exchanged: at inception, the two parties exchange $N_{USD}$ for $N_{EUR}$; at maturity, they re-exchange the same notional amounts back. During the life of the swap, each party pays the floating reference rate of the currency it received, typically with a spread (the cross-currency basis, quoted in bp) applied to one leg — usually the non-USD leg, by convention.

Mark-to-Market Resets

A plain cross-currency swap with fixed notionals exchanged only at inception and maturity leaves both counterparties exposed to FX-driven changes in the relative size of the two notionals over the swap's life — a USD payer's EUR notional, converted back to USD at the current spot rate, may now be worth far more or less than $N_{USD}$. Mark-to-market (MTM) cross-currency swaps address this.

Remark — MTM Notional Resets

In an MTM cross-currency swap, the non-USD notional is reset periodically (e.g. quarterly) to the then-current spot exchange rate, with the difference between the old and new notional exchanged as an additional cash flow at the reset date. This keeps the two legs' notionals continuously aligned with the current FX rate, eliminating the FX notional mismatch risk that a fixed-notional cross-currency swap would carry — at the cost of more frequent principal exchanges. MTM resets are now standard for most interdealer cross-currency swaps, particularly those referencing USD against other G10 currencies.

The Cross-Currency Basis

Remark — Why Cross-Currency Basis Exists

In theory, covered interest rate parity implies that borrowing in one currency and swapping into another via FX forwards/cross-currency swaps should cost the same as borrowing directly in the second currency — i.e. the cross-currency basis should be zero. In practice, persistent non-zero (often negative for non-USD currencies vs. USD) cross-currency bases have existed since 2008, reflecting structural USD funding demand from non-US banks, balance-sheet costs for the dealers who would otherwise arbitrage the basis away (post-crisis regulatory capital constraints), and supply/demand imbalances in cross-currency hedging flows (e.g. from foreign investors hedging USD bond holdings back to their home currency). The cross-currency basis is itself a curve — term structure across maturities — bootstrapped from market-quoted cross-currency swap spreads, analogous to the tenor basis curves of Section 2.2.

Asset Swaps

Definition — Asset Swap

An asset swap packages a fixed-rate bond together with an interest rate swap that converts the bond's fixed coupon into a floating rate plus (or minus) a spread — the asset swap spread. The investor buys the bond at its market price $P_{bond}$ (typically not par) and simultaneously enters a swap, paying the bond's fixed coupon $c_{bond}$ and receiving floating-plus-spread on a notional equal to the bond's face value. The asset swap spread $ASW$ is set so that the package has zero value at inception.

Theorem — Par Asset Swap Spread

For a "par" asset swap (notional equal to the bond's face value $F$, regardless of $P_{bond}$), the asset swap spread is approximately the value that reconciles the bond's price away from par with its coupon stream, expressed per period: $$ASW \approx \frac{(F - P_{bond}) + \big(PV_{fix}(c_{bond}) - PV_{fix}(c^*)\big)}{A}$$ where $c^*$ is the par swap rate (Section 1.1) for the bond's maturity, $A$ is the swap annuity, and $PV_{fix}(\cdot)$ denotes the fixed-leg PV at the given coupon. Intuitively, $ASW$ is the spread over the floating reference rate that compensates the investor for (a) the difference between the bond's price and par, amortized over the bond's life via the annuity, and (b) the difference between the bond's actual coupon and the prevailing par swap rate. A bond trading below par (reflecting wider credit spreads than the swap curve implies) will have a positive $ASW$.

Worked Asset Swap Spread Example

Example — Asset Swap Spread on a Discount Bond

Consider a 5-year bond with face value $F=100$, annual coupon $c_{bond}=3.50\%$, currently trading at a price $P_{bond}=96.50$ (i.e. below par, reflecting a credit spread above the swap curve). Suppose the 5-year par swap rate is $c^*=4.00\%$ and the swap annuity is $A=4.4518$ (using the discount factors from the Section 3.1 example).

The price-vs-par contribution is $F - P_{bond} = 100 - 96.50 = 3.50$. The coupon-vs-par-rate contribution is $PV_{fix}(c_{bond}) - PV_{fix}(c^*) = (0.0350-0.0400)\times 100 \times A = -0.0050\times 100\times 4.4518 = -2.2259$. Combining: $$ASW \approx \frac{3.50 + (-2.2259)}{4.4518} = \frac{1.2741}{4.4518} \approx 0.02862 = 286.2\text{bp}$$ The investor who buys this bond at 96.50 and enters the asset swap receives the floating reference rate plus approximately 286bp — a spread that compensates for the bond trading 3.50 points below par despite its coupon already being below the swap rate, with the price discount dominating. This spread is the standard market measure of the bond's credit risk relative to the swap (and hence SOFR/OIS) curve, stripped of the effects of the bond's specific coupon and price.
