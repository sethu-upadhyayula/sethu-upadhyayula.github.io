1. Interest Rate Swaps

The interest rate swap is the workhorse instrument of the rates market — by notional outstanding, the largest derivatives market in the world. A vanilla swap exchanges a stream of fixed payments for a stream of floating payments referencing a benchmark rate, allowing counterparties to transform their interest rate exposure without exchanging principal. Swaps are simultaneously the primary hedging instrument for fixed income risk, the main vehicle for expressing views on the level and shape of the yield curve, and — through their par rates — the principal source of market data used to build discount curves.

This chapter covers the mechanics and valuation of vanilla fixed-for-floating swaps, including the par swap rate formula and the swap's representation as a portfolio of forward rate agreements. It then turns to the post-2008 shift to overnight indexed swap (OIS) discounting and the LIBOR-to-SOFR transition, which together gave rise to the multi-curve framework that is now standard across the industry: one curve for discounting cash flows, and separate curves for projecting the floating rates that determine those cash flows.
