2.1 CSAs and Variation Margin

The Credit Support Annex (CSA) is the legal document — typically an annex to an ISDA Master Agreement — that governs the exchange of collateral between two counterparties to mitigate the counterparty credit risk identified in Chapter 1. Variation margin (VM) is the collateral that tracks the mark-to-market value of the netting set, exchanged periodically (usually daily) so that the secured exposure remains close to zero.

CSA Mechanics

Definition — Variation Margin Call

On each margin call date $t$, the collateral-taking party computes the net mark-to-market $V(t) = \sum_i V_i(t)$ of the netting set under the CSA. The required collateral balance is: $$C^{\star}(t) = \max\big(V(t) - H,\, 0\big) \quad \text{(from the perspective of the party owed money)}$$ where $H$ is the unsecured threshold. If the existing posted collateral balance $C(t^-)$ differs from $C^{\star}(t)$ by more than the Minimum Transfer Amount (MTA), a margin call is issued for the difference $\Delta C = C^{\star}(t) - C(t^-)$, rounded to the nearest collateral increment.

Threshold and Minimum Transfer Amount

Definition — Threshold (H) and MTA
  • Threshold $H$: the amount of exposure that may remain uncollateralised before a margin call is triggered. A "zero-threshold" CSA collateralises from the first dollar of exposure; a positive threshold leaves a permanent unsecured exposure band of size $H$ even with daily margining.
  • Minimum Transfer Amount (MTA): the smallest collateral movement that will actually be called, to avoid the operational burden of transferring trivial amounts every day. A typical MTA is \$100k-\$500k for an interdealer CSA.
  • Rounding: calls are usually rounded up to the nearest specified increment (e.g. \$10,000), which introduces a small additional residual exposure band.

Residual Exposure Under a CSA

Theorem — Collateralised Exposure

Under a one-way or two-way CSA with threshold $H$ and margin period of risk (MPOR) $\Delta$, the current exposure at the moment of counterparty default is approximately: $$CE_{\text{CSA}}(t) \approx \max\!\Big(V(t) - C(t-\Delta) ,\, 0\Big)$$ where $C(t-\Delta)$ is the collateral balance held $\Delta$ days before default — the lag during which the portfolio may move further against the collateral-taker before collateral can be liquidated. The MPOR (typically 10 business days for non-cleared bilateral trades, 5-7 for cleared) captures the operational delay: notification of default, dispute resolution, and close-out.

The residual exposure that survives collateralisation is therefore driven by three components: the threshold $H$, the MTA/rounding band, and the potential market move over the MPOR. CVA on a well-collateralised netting set is dominated by the latter — the "gap risk" over the margin period of risk.

Example — VM Call on a Swap Portfolio

Two dealers run a two-way CSA with $H = \$0$, MTA $= \$250{,}000$, daily margining, USD cash collateral. At close of business the net MTM of the netting set (from Dealer A's perspective) moves from $+\$4.10\text{m}$ to $+\$4.42\text{m}$.

$\Delta C = \$4.42\text{m} - \$4.10\text{m} = \$320{,}000 > \text{MTA} = \$250{,}000$, so Dealer A issues a call to Dealer B for $\$320{,}000$ of additional variation margin.

If instead the move had been $+\$180{,}000$, no call would be made — that amount remains uncollateralised until cumulative moves exceed the MTA, contributing a small residual exposure to CVA.

Eligible Collateral and Haircuts

Remark — Collateral Quality, FX and ColVA

CSAs specify eligible collateral types (cash in one or more currencies, government bonds) and apply haircuts to non-cash collateral to absorb potential value declines before the next margin call. When variation margin can be posted in a currency other than the trade's discounting currency, the embedded optionality to choose the cheapest-to-deliver collateral currency gives rise to a "cheapest-to-deliver" (CTD) discounting curve and a small additional valuation adjustment sometimes called ColVA. Because most major interdealer CSAs now mandate cash VM in the trade's settlement currency remunerated at the overnight index rate (e.g. SOFR, €STR), discounting collateralised derivatives at the relevant overnight rate — OIS discounting — has become the market standard, as introduced in the Interest Rate Swaps module.
