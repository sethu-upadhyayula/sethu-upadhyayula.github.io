1.2 CVA

The Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) is the market value of counterparty credit risk: the expected loss from the default of an OTC derivatives counterparty, expressed as a present-value deduction from the risk-free price of the trade. CVA is now a P&L item that must be booked at trade inception and marked to market daily, making it a major source of volatility on bank balance sheets.

Unilateral CVA Formula

Theorem - Unilateral CVA

Under the independence assumption (default time $\tau_C$ of the counterparty is independent of the trade's underlying risk factors), and assuming zero recovery $R_C = 0$, the unilateral CVA is: $$\text{CVA} = -\mathbb{E}^Q\!\left[D(0,\tau_C)\,V^+(\tau_C)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{\tau_C \leq T\}}\right]$$ where $D(0,t) = e^{-\int_0^t r(s)\,ds}$ is the stochastic discount factor and $V^+(t) = \max(V(t),0)$ is the positive part of the portfolio value. Discretising over time steps: $$\text{CVA} \approx (1-R_C)\sum_{k=1}^{n} EE(t_k)\cdot P(0,t_k)\cdot [Q_C(t_{k-1}) - Q_C(t_k)]$$ where $EE(t_k) = \mathbb{E}[\max(V(t_k),0)]$ is the expected exposure, $P(0,t_k)$ the risk-free discount factor, and $[Q_C(t_{k-1})-Q_C(t_k)]$ the marginal default probability of the counterparty in interval $(t_{k-1},t_k]$.

CVA as Integral over EE Profile

Definition - Continuous-Time CVA

In continuous time, using the hazard rate $\lambda_C(t)$ of the counterparty: $$\text{CVA} = (1-R_C)\int_0^T EE(t)\cdot P(0,t)\cdot \lambda_C(t)\cdot Q_C(t)\,dt$$ For a flat hazard rate ($\lambda_C(t) = \lambda_C$) and assuming $EE(t) \approx EPE$ (constant): $$\text{CVA} \approx (1-R_C)\cdot EPE \cdot \int_0^T P(0,t)\,\lambda_C\,e^{-\lambda_C t}\,dt \approx (1-R_C)\cdot EPE\cdot s_C\cdot \text{CDS Annuity}$$ where $s_C \approx \lambda_C(1-R_C)$ is the counterparty CDS spread. This gives the intuition: CVA $\approx$ EPE $\times$ LGD $\times$ PD, the familiar expected loss formula.

Example - CVA on an Interest Rate Swap

A bank has a 5-year receiver IRS with a corporate counterparty. The swap has EPE $\approx \$2.5\text{m}$ (approximately 2.5% of \$100m notional). The counterparty CDS spread is 150 bps, recovery 40%.

$\lambda_C = s_C / LGD = 0.015/0.6 = 2.5\%$ p.a. CDS Annuity $\approx 4.55$ years (5Y at 150 bps).

$$\text{CVA} \approx (1-0.4) \times \$2.5\text{m} \times 0.015 \times 4.55 \approx \$102,375$$

This \$102k is charged to the client at trade inception (or included in the swap rate as a spread), and the CVA desk hedges the counterparty credit exposure by buying CDS protection on the corporate.

Bilateral CVA

Definition - Bilateral CVA (BCVA)

Bilateral CVA recognises that both parties face default risk. Assuming independent default times $\tau_C$ (counterparty) and $\tau_B$ (bank): $$\text{BCVA} = \text{CVA} - \text{DVA}$$ where: $$\text{DVA} = (1-R_B)\sum_{k=1}^{n} ENE(t_k)\cdot P(0,t_k)\cdot [Q_B(t_{k-1}) - Q_B(t_k)]$$ and $ENE(t_k) = \mathbb{E}[|\min(V(t_k),0)|]$ is the expected negative exposure. DVA is a gain to the bank from its own default (controversial - see next section). The BCVA is the net valuation adjustment that accounts for both parties' default risks.

Remark - CVA Hedging and the CVA Desk

Most major banks operate a dedicated CVA desk that centrally manages counterparty credit risk. The desk buys CDS protection on counterparties to hedge CVA sensitivity. The P&L of the CVA desk is the difference between the CVA charged to the trading desk at inception and the actual hedging cost over the life of the trade. CVA is also a significant source of market risk: the "CVA VaR" or "CVA SVaR" capital charge under Basel III was one of the most contentious regulatory developments post-2008.
