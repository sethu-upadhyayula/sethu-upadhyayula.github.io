1.3 DVA and FVA

The XVA universe extends beyond CVA to include the Debit Valuation Adjustment (DVA) — the value of the bank's own default — and the Funding Valuation Adjustment (FVA) — the cost or benefit of funding uncollateralised derivatives positions. Both are economically significant, contentious, and the subject of ongoing debate between practitioners, accountants, and regulators.

DVA — Debit Valuation Adjustment

Definition — DVA

DVA is the value to the bank of the possibility that the bank itself defaults while the trade has negative value (i.e. the bank owes money to the counterparty). Symmetrically to CVA: $$\text{DVA} = (1-R_B)\sum_{k=1}^{n} ENE(t_k) \cdot P(0,t_k) \cdot [Q_B(t_{k-1}) - Q_B(t_k)]$$ where $ENE(t_k) = \mathbb{E}[|\min(V(t_k),0)|]$ is the expected negative exposure profile and $Q_B(t)$ is the bank's own survival probability. DVA is a gain: when the bank's credit spreads widen, DVA increases, partially offsetting CVA losses.

Remark — The DVA Controversy

DVA is required under IFRS 13 / ASC 820 fair value accounting, yet it is deeply counterintuitive: a bank books a profit when its own creditworthiness deteriorates. The economic interpretation is that the bank's liabilities are worth less when it is in distress, benefiting shareholders. However, DVA cannot be monetised by selling protection on oneself, and regulators (Basel III) exclude DVA benefits from regulatory capital. Hull and White (2012) argued that DVA should be regarded as a transfer to shareholders from bondholders, not a true economic gain. In practice, banks hedge DVA by buying back their own senior debt or through proxy hedges.

FVA — Funding Valuation Adjustment

FVA arises because a bank must fund the cash flows of uncollateralised derivatives from its treasury at its own (credit-risky) funding rate, not at the risk-free OIS rate. The difference is the funding cost or benefit.

Definition — FVA

Let $f_B(t) = r_B(t) - r_f(t)$ be the bank's funding spread above the risk-free rate. FVA is split into: $$\text{FCA} = \int_0^T f_B(t)\cdot \mathbb{E}^Q[\max(V(t),0)]\cdot P(0,t)\,dt \quad \text{(Funding Cost Adjustment)}$$ $$\text{FBA} = \int_0^T f_B(t)\cdot \mathbb{E}^Q[|\min(V(t),0)|]\cdot P(0,t)\,dt \quad \text{(Funding Benefit Adjustment)}$$ so that $\text{FVA} = \text{FBA} - \text{FCA}$. When the bank's position has positive value (it is owed money), it must fund the positive MTM on its hedges at the funding spread — a cost (FCA). When its position has negative value, it receives funding from the hedge counterparty — a benefit (FBA).

Example — FVA on an Uncollateralised Swap

A bank executes an uncollateralised 5-year IRS with a corporate client and hedges it with a collateralised back-to-back swap in the interbank market. The interbank hedge is fully collateralised (no funding cost), but the client-facing swap has positive EPE = \$2m. The bank's funding spread is 80 bps p.a.

$$\text{FCA} \approx 0.0080 \times \$2\text{m} \times 4.40 \text{ (annuity)} = \$70,400$$

This \$70,400 FCA is the cost of carrying the uncollateralised positive exposure — the bank must fund the collateral posted to the interbank market out of its own pocket, at its own funding spread, for the life of the trade.

The FVA Debate and Regulatory Treatment

Remark — FVA and Risk-Neutral Pricing

Hull and White (2014) argued that FVA should not be charged to derivatives because it double-counts DVA: a bank with a funding spread is already factoring in its own default risk. However, Burgard and Kjaer (2011), and most practitioners, argue that FVA is a real cash cost that shareholders (not counterparties) should not bear — it must be charged to trades.

Under Basel III, FVA is not recognised in regulatory capital (regulators view it as an internal transfer price), but it is a real P&L item. The full XVA suite — CVA, DVA, FVA, KVA (capital), MVA (initial margin), ColVA (collateral) — now constitutes a significant portion of derivatives pricing and is a major focus of quantitative finance research and bank technology investment.

Definition — XVA Summary

The adjusted derivatives value from the bank's perspective, including all valuation adjustments, is: $$V_{\text{adj}} = V_{\text{risk-free}} - \text{CVA} + \text{DVA} - \text{FVA} - \text{KVA} - \text{MVA} + \cdots$$

  • CVA: cost of counterparty default — always a debit.
  • DVA: benefit of own default — a credit (controversial).
  • FVA: net cost of funding the uncollateralised position.
  • KVA: cost of holding regulatory capital against the trade over its life.
  • MVA: cost of posting initial margin to CCPs or bilateral counterparties.
