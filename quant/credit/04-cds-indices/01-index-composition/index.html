4.1 Index Composition

CDS indices pool together equally-weighted single-name CDS contracts into a standardised, tradeable instrument. Their liquidity far exceeds the underlying single-name market, making them the preferred vehicle for macro credit hedging, expressing directional views, and relative value trading.

Major Index Families

Definition — CDX and iTraxx Index Families

CDX (North America):

  • CDX.NA.IG: 125 North American investment grade corporates, 5Y standard maturity, 100 bps fixed coupon.
  • CDX.NA.HY: 100 North American high yield names, 5Y, 500 bps fixed coupon.
  • CDX.EM: emerging market sovereigns and quasi-sovereigns.

iTraxx (Europe/Asia):

  • iTraxx Europe: 125 European investment grade corporates, 5Y, 100 bps fixed coupon.
  • iTraxx Crossover: 75 sub-investment grade European names, 5Y, 500 bps coupon — the most actively traded high-yield index in Europe.
  • iTraxx Asia ex-Japan, iTraxx Japan: regional Asian credit benchmarks.

Index Roll and Series

Definition — Series, On-the-Run, and Off-the-Run

Every six months (March 20 and September 20), a new series of each index is launched — the on-the-run (OTR) series. The composition is reconstituted: names that have defaulted, been acquired, or fallen below the liquidity eligibility criteria are removed and replaced. The dealer consortium (IHS Markit) determines the composition by dealer poll and credit criteria.

Previous series become off-the-run (OFR) and continue to trade until maturity, but at reduced liquidity. The roll trade — selling the old series and buying the new — is a standard desk activity at each roll date. Roll activity drives significant basis between series.

Intrinsic Spread vs Index Spread

Definition — Intrinsic (Fair) Spread

The intrinsic spread $s_{\text{intr}}$ of an index is the equal-weighted average of the par CDS spreads of its $n$ constituent names: $$s_{\text{intr}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i$$ The index spread $s_{\text{idx}}$ is the actual market quote for the index as a whole. The difference: $$\text{Skew} = s_{\text{idx}} - s_{\text{intr}}$$ is called the index skew. A positive skew means the index trades wider than its intrinsic value, often due to higher supply of index protection (macro hedging demand) or liquidity premium. A negative skew implies cheapness of the index versus single names.

Index Factor and Credit Events

Definition — Index Factor

The index factor tracks the fraction of the original $n$ names still active (not yet defaulted) in the index. After $k$ defaults: $$f = \frac{n - k}{n}$$ When a constituent defaults, an ISDA auction determines the recovery rate $R_j$ for that name. The protection seller on the index pays $(1-R_j)/n$ per unit notional at that time, and the notional of the index is reduced by $1/n$. The index factor governs all subsequent premium calculations: $$\text{Premium}_t = s_{\text{coupon}} \times N \times f_t \times \delta_t$$

Example — Credit Event in CDX.NA.IG

CDX.NA.IG has 125 names with equal weight $1/125 = 0.8\%$ each. Suppose one name defaults with ISDA auction recovery of 35%.

The protection seller pays $(1-0.35) \times 0.8\% \times N = 0.52\% \times N$ immediately.

Subsequently, the index factor drops to $f = 124/125 = 99.2\%$, and ongoing premiums are reduced accordingly. The index continues to trade on the remaining 124 names.

Remark — Index vs Single-Name Basis

The index skew creates relative value opportunities: when the skew is significantly positive, a dealer can sell the index and buy the constituent single-name CDS (a "skew trade"), monetising the basis at the cost of executing 125 single-name trades. Correlation between names, transaction costs, and balance-sheet constraints limit this arbitrage in practice.
