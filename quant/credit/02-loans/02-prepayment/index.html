2.2 Prepayment Modelling

A borrower typically has the right — but not the obligation — to repay a loan or mortgage before its scheduled maturity. This prepayment optionality creates cash flow uncertainty for the lender and is the primary source of risk in mortgage-backed securities (MBS). Accurate prepayment modelling is essential for pricing, hedging, and portfolio management of mortgage and consumer loan portfolios.

Single Monthly Mortality (SMM) and CPR

Definition — SMM and Conditional Prepayment Rate

The Single Monthly Mortality (SMM) is the fraction of the beginning-of-month outstanding balance (net of scheduled principal) that prepays voluntarily in a given month: $$\text{SMM}_t = \frac{\text{Unscheduled Principal}_t}{\text{Balance}_{t-1} - \text{Scheduled Principal}_t}$$ The annualised equivalent is the Conditional Prepayment Rate (CPR): $$\text{CPR} = 1 - (1 - \text{SMM})^{12}$$ Conversely, $\text{SMM} = 1 - (1 - \text{CPR})^{1/12}$. A CPR of 20% means that 20% of the remaining balance is expected to prepay over the next 12 months.

PSA Prepayment Benchmark

The Public Securities Association (PSA) standard benchmark models the prepayment rate as a CPR that ramps up linearly during the first 30 months of a loan's life (the seasoning ramp), then remains constant.

Definition — PSA Model

Under the 100% PSA standard: $$\text{CPR}(t) = \begin{cases} 6\% \times (t/30) & t \leq 30\text{ months} \\ 6\% & t > 30\text{ months} \end{cases}$$ where $t$ is the loan age in months. The "100 PSA" curve is the baseline benchmark. "$k$ PSA" means prepayments run at $k\%$ of the benchmark CPR at each age: $$\text{CPR}_{k\,\text{PSA}}(t) = k\% \times \text{CPR}_{100\,\text{PSA}}(t)$$ A pool running at 150 PSA prepays 50% faster than benchmark at every age.

Example — PSA Cash Flow Impact

For a \$100m pool of 30-year 6% mortgages at 100 PSA:

Month 10: CPR $= 6\% \times 10/30 = 2\%$, SMM $= 1-(1-0.02)^{1/12} \approx 0.168\%$.

Month 30+: CPR $= 6\%$, SMM $= 1-(0.94)^{1/12} \approx 0.514\%$ per month.

Higher PSA assumptions reduce the pool's weighted average life (WAL) and accelerate principal return to investors, compressing yield if the pool was purchased at a premium.

Prepayment Drivers

Remark — Economic Drivers of Prepayment

Prepayments are driven by three main forces:

  • Refinancing incentive: when prevailing rates fall below the loan coupon rate, borrowers refinance at lower rates. The key variable is the WAC (weighted average coupon) minus current market mortgage rates.
  • Housing turnover: home sales trigger full prepayment via due-on-sale clauses. This is relatively rate-insensitive and driven by housing market activity.
  • Defaults and curtailments: involuntary prepayment via foreclosure, and partial principal payments by borrowers.

Sophisticated prepayment models (e.g. Andrew Davidson, Bloomberg Prepayment Model) combine S-curve refinancing response, burnout (experienced refinancers leave the pool), seasonality, and seasoning adjustments.

Option-Adjusted Spread for MBS

Because prepayments depend on the interest rate path, MBS cannot be priced by Z-spread. The OAS framework uses Monte Carlo simulation over interest rate paths.

Definition — MBS OAS via Monte Carlo

Simulate $N$ interest rate paths $\{r^{(j)}_t\}$ under the risk-neutral measure. On each path, run the prepayment model to generate path-specific cash flows $\{CF^{(j)}_t\}$. The OAS is the spread such that: $$P_{\text{MBS}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N}\sum_{t} CF^{(j)}_t \cdot e^{-\int_0^t (r^{(j)}_s + \text{OAS})\,ds}$$ equals the observed market price. OAS measures pure credit and liquidity compensation after the prepayment option value is removed.

Remark — Negative Convexity

MBS exhibit negative convexity: when rates fall, prepayments accelerate (borrowers refinance), shortening duration and capping price appreciation. When rates rise, prepayments slow, extending duration and amplifying price losses. This asymmetric price behaviour — losing more than it gains symmetrically — is the key distinction from standard bonds, which have positive convexity.
