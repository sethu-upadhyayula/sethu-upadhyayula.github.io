2.1 Loan Structure and Pricing

A loan is a contractual arrangement in which a lender (or syndicate of lenders) provides capital to a borrower at agreed terms. The key structural features are: facility type, tenor, amortisation schedule, interest basis, and fee structure. Leveraged loans — extended to sub-investment-grade borrowers — are the raw material of CLO portfolios.

Facility Types

Definition — Term Loan vs Revolving Credit Facility

A term loan is a fully drawn facility where principal reduces on a fixed amortisation schedule until maturity. Term Loan A (TLA) amortises regularly over its life. Term Loan B (TLB), dominant in leveraged finance, has minimal scheduled amortisation (1% per annum) and a bullet repayment at maturity.

A revolving credit facility (RCF) is a committed line of credit: the borrower may draw, repay, and re-draw up to a maximum commitment at will during the availability period. The commitment fee accrues on the undrawn portion; the margin applies only to drawn amounts.

Interest Rate and Margin

Most loans price as a floating benchmark rate plus a fixed credit spread (margin). After the LIBOR transition, the dominant reference rates are SOFR (USD), SONIA (GBP), and EURIBOR (EUR).

Definition — Loan All-In Rate

The periodic interest payment on a drawn balance $L$ over a period of length $\delta$ (in years) is: $$\text{Interest} = L \times (\text{SOFR}_t + s) \times \delta$$ where $\text{SOFR}_t$ is the compounded overnight SOFR over the interest period (or term SOFR at reset) and $s$ is the contractual credit margin. For leveraged loans, the margin typically ranges from 300 to 600 bps over benchmark.

Remark — SOFR Floor

Many leveraged loan agreements include a SOFR floor — a minimum reference rate, typically 0% or 50 bps — so that even in a zero-rate environment the borrower pays at least the margin plus the floor. This floor has option value to the lender and is priced into the all-in margin.

Amortisation Schedule

Definition — Fully Amortising Loan Payment

For a fully amortising loan of principal $L_0$, $n$ equal payment periods, and periodic interest rate $r$ (coupon rate divided by periods per year), the constant periodic payment $A$ is: $$A = L_0 \cdot \frac{r}{1-(1+r)^{-n}}$$ The outstanding principal after $k$ payments is: $$L_k = L_0 \cdot \frac{(1+r)^n - (1+r)^k}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$ The interest component of payment $k$ is $I_k = L_{k-1}\cdot r$ and the principal component is $P_k = A - I_k$. Over time, the interest share declines and the principal share grows.

Example — Annual Amortisation Table

A \$1,000 loan at 5% p.a., 3-year fully amortising: $A = 1000 \times 0.05 / (1-1.05^{-3}) = \$367.21$.

Year 1: Interest = \$50.00, Principal = \$317.21, Balance = \$682.79.

Year 2: Interest = \$34.14, Principal = \$333.07, Balance = \$349.72.

Year 3: Interest = \$17.49, Principal = \$349.72, Balance = \$0.00. Total interest paid = \$101.63.

All-In Yield and OID

Definition — Original Issue Discount and All-In Spread

Leveraged loans are frequently issued at a discount to par — an Original Issue Discount (OID). A loan issued at 98 cents on the dollar has a 2% OID. The all-in spread approximately equals: $$\text{All-in spread} \approx s + \frac{\text{OID} + \text{Upfront Fee}}{\text{Tenor (years)}}$$ The exact all-in yield (IRR) solves: $$\text{Issue Price} = \sum_{t} \frac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t}$$ where $CF_t$ includes periodic interest payments, scheduled amortisation, and bullet principal at maturity. IRR is computed numerically.

Remark — Commitment Fee and Utilisation Fee

For revolving facilities, the commitment fee (typically 30–50% of the drawn margin) accrues daily on the undrawn commitment. A utilisation fee steps up the effective margin when the drawn utilisation percentage exceeds specified thresholds (commonly 33% and 66%). These fees compensate lenders for maintaining committed capital even when the facility is undrawn.
