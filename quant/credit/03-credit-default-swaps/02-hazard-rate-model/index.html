3.2 Hazard Rate Model

The hazard rate model is the industry-standard framework for CDS pricing. It belongs to the class of reduced-form (or intensity-based) credit models, where default is modelled as the first jump of a Poisson process with a stochastic or deterministic intensity $\lambda(t)$. This approach bypasses the need to observe firm asset values and fits directly to market CDS quotes.

Default Time and Hazard Rate

Definition — Hazard Rate

Let $\tau$ be the (random) default time of the reference entity. The hazard rate (default intensity) $\lambda(t) \geq 0$ is defined by: $$\lambda(t) = \lim_{dt \to 0} \frac{P(\tau \leq t + dt \mid \tau > t)}{dt}$$ It is the instantaneous conditional rate of default given survival to time $t$. Under the assumption that $\tau$ is a Cox process (doubly stochastic Poisson process), $\lambda(t)$ may itself be stochastic and driven by state variables.

Survival Probability

Theorem — Survival Probability

Given a deterministic hazard rate curve $\{\lambda(t)\}_{t \geq 0}$, the probability of surviving (no default) to time $T$ is: $$Q(T) \equiv P(\tau > T) = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^T \lambda(s)\,ds\right)$$ The unconditional probability of defaulting in the interval $(t_1, t_2]$ is: $$P(t_1 < \tau \leq t_2) = Q(t_1) - Q(t_2) = Q(t_1)\left(1 - e^{-\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\lambda(s)\,ds}\right)$$ For a piecewise-flat (step) hazard rate with $\lambda(t) = \lambda_i$ for $t \in (T_{i-1}, T_i]$: $$Q(T_n) = \exp\!\left(-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i (T_i - T_{i-1})\right)$$

Risky Discount Factor

The combined effect of time value of money and the possibility of default is captured in the risky discount factor.

Definition — Risky Discount Factor

Under the independence assumption (default time $\tau$ is independent of interest rates), the risky discount factor for a zero-recovery defaultable cashflow at time $T$ is: $$\tilde{P}(0,T) = P(0,T) \cdot Q(T) = e^{-r(T)\cdot T} \cdot e^{-\int_0^T \lambda(s)\,ds}$$ where $P(0,T) = e^{-r(T)T}$ is the risk-free discount factor. The hazard rate augments the discount rate: the entity is discounted at $r(t) + \lambda(t)$.

Bootstrapping the Hazard Rate Curve

In practice, a term structure of hazard rates is calibrated to market CDS quotes at multiple maturities (1Y, 2Y, 3Y, 5Y, 7Y, 10Y) by bootstrapping, exactly analogous to bootstrapping discount factors from swap rates.

Definition — Bootstrapping Procedure

Given market CDS spreads $\{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n\}$ at maturities $\{T_1 < T_2 < \cdots < T_n\}$ and assuming piecewise-flat hazard rates $\lambda_i$ on $(T_{i-1}, T_i]$:

  1. For $i=1$: solve for $\lambda_1$ such that the CDS with maturity $T_1$ is fair (zero NPV) given the market spread $s_1$.
  2. For $i=2$: given $\lambda_1$ already determined, solve for $\lambda_2$ such that the $T_2$-maturity CDS is fair given $s_2$.
  3. Continue iteratively. Each step is a one-dimensional root-finding problem (Newton–Raphson or bisection).
Example — Flat Hazard Rate

A reference entity trades at a flat CDS spread of 200 bps across all maturities, with assumed recovery $R = 40\%$. Under the approximation $s \approx \lambda(1-R)$: $$\lambda = \frac{0.020}{1 - 0.40} = \frac{0.020}{0.60} \approx 3.33\% \text{ per year}$$ Survival probabilities: $Q(1) = e^{-0.0333} \approx 96.7\%$, $Q(3) \approx 90.4\%$, $Q(5) \approx 84.4\%$.

These survival probabilities are the inputs to the CDS pricing formula derived in the next section.

Remark — Stochastic Hazard Rate Models

For exotic credit derivatives, a deterministic hazard rate curve is insufficient. Stochastic intensity models (e.g. Cox–Ingersoll–Ross intensity, affine models) allow the hazard rate to evolve randomly over time, introducing credit spread volatility and enabling pricing of credit options and swaptions. The Jarrow–Turnbull and Duffie–Singleton models are canonical examples where the intensity follows a diffusion process under the risk-neutral measure.
