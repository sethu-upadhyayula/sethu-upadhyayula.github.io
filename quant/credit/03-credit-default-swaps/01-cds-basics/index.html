3.1 CDS Structure

A Credit Default Swap (CDS) is a bilateral contract that transfers the credit risk of a specified reference entity from the protection buyer to the protection seller. It is the most liquid instrument for expressing views on or hedging single-name credit risk, and the benchmark against which bond spreads are compared.

Economic Mechanics

Definition — CDS Contract

A CDS on a reference entity has the following structure:

  • Protection buyer pays a periodic premium (the CDS spread $s$, quoted in bps p.a.) on the notional $N$ for the life of the contract, or until a credit event occurs.
  • Protection seller receives the premium and, upon a credit event, pays the buyer $(1 - R) \times N$, where $R$ is the recovery rate (settlement price of the cheapest deliverable obligation).
  • The contract terminates upon a credit event or at scheduled maturity, whichever is first.

The protection buyer is economically short credit (benefits if the reference entity deteriorates); the protection seller is long credit (benefits if the entity remains healthy).

ISDA Definitions and Credit Events

Definition — ISDA Credit Events

The 2003 (and updated 2014) ISDA Credit Derivatives Definitions govern CDS contracts. Standard credit events for corporate reference entities include:

  • Bankruptcy: insolvency proceedings, administration, or liquidation.
  • Failure to Pay: the reference entity fails to make a scheduled payment above the payment requirement threshold (typically \$1m) after any applicable grace period.
  • Restructuring: a change in terms of obligations that is materially adverse to holders — e.g. debt maturity extension, coupon reduction, or change in currency. The inclusion of restructuring is jurisdiction-specific (Modified-Modified Restructuring for European CDS; often excluded for North American corporates in SNAC/Standard North American Contract form).

Settlement

Definition — Physical vs Cash Settlement

Physical settlement: the protection buyer delivers a defaulted obligation (bond or loan) of the reference entity and receives par ($100) from the protection seller. The buyer has a cheapest-to-deliver (CTD) option: they deliver the lowest-priced eligible obligation, creating option value embedded in the CDS contract.

Cash settlement (now standard via auction): following an ISDA-administered auction, a single recovery rate $R$ is determined by competitive bidding in the defaulted debt. Protection sellers pay $(1-R) \times N$ to buyers. The ISDA auction mechanism, introduced post-2005, dramatically reduced settlement risk and basis risk across the market.

Standard CDS Conventions (SNAC)

Remark — Post-2009 Standardisation (Big Bang / Small Bang)

Following the 2009 CDS Big Bang (North America) and Small Bang (Europe), CDS adopted standardised conventions to facilitate central clearing:

  • Fixed coupons: North American investment grade trades at 100 bps or 500 bps fixed running spread. High yield trades at 500 bps. European investment grade at 25, 100, 500 bps.
  • Upfront payment: because the fixed coupon differs from the fair (par) spread, an upfront payment is exchanged at trade inception to compensate for the difference. This upfront is the primary traded quantity (alongside the fixed coupon).
  • IMM dates: CDS mature on standard IMM roll dates (20 March, 20 June, 20 September, 20 December).
  • Accrued interest on default: upon a credit event, the protection buyer pays the accrued premium from the last coupon date to the event date.
Example — CDS as a Credit Hedge

A bank holds \$10m of a corporate bond. To hedge credit risk, it buys \$10m protection via a 5-year CDS at a running spread of 120 bps (standardised to 100 bps coupon with upfront).

Annual premium: $\$10\text{m} \times 0.012 = \$120,000$ (or adjusted via upfront under SNAC). If the issuer defaults with a recovery rate of 40%, the bank receives $(1-0.40) \times \$10\text{m} = \$6\text{m}$ from the protection seller, offsetting the loss on the bond position.

The net cost of the hedge is the cumulative premium paid, reduced by any mark-to-market gain on the CDS if spreads had widened before default.
