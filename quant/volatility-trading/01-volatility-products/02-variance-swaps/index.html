1.2 Variance Swaps

A variance swap is an over-the-counter derivative whose payoff depends on the realized variance of an underlying's returns over a specified period. Unlike a vanilla option, whose value depends on a single Black-Scholes volatility input, a variance swap can be priced and hedged using a static portfolio of vanilla options, making it a remarkably "model-free" instrument for trading volatility.

Definition — Variance Swap

A variance swap with maturity $T$ and variance strike $K_{\text{var}}$ pays, at maturity, the difference between the annualized realized variance of the underlying's log-returns over $[0,T]$ and the fixed strike, times a notional: $$\text{Payoff} = N_{\text{var}} \times \left(\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2 - K_{\text{var}}\right)$$ where the annualized realized variance is computed from $n$ daily log-returns $r_i = \ln(S_{t_i}/S_{t_{i-1}})$ as $$\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2 = \frac{A}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i^2$$ with $A$ the annualization factor (e.g., $252$ for daily observations), and $N_{\text{var}}$ is the variance notional, often quoted via a "vega notional" $N_{\text{vega}} = N_{\text{var}} \times 2\sqrt{K_{\text{var}}}$ so that a 1-volatility-point change in realized vol near the strike corresponds to a P&L of approximately $N_{\text{vega}}$.

The buyer of a variance swap is long realized variance: they profit if realized variance over the life of the contract exceeds the strike $K_{\text{var}}$, and lose if it falls short. The strike $K_{\text{var}}$ is set at inception so that the swap has zero initial value, and represents the market's risk-neutral expectation of future realized variance — the "fair variance."

Replication via a Continuum of Options

The key theoretical result underpinning variance swaps is that the fair variance strike can be computed, model-independently, from the prices of a continuum of European out-of-the-money options across all strikes — without reference to any particular volatility model.

Theorem — Carr-Madan / Log-Contract Replication

Under the assumption that $S_t$ follows a continuous (no-jump) process with risk-neutral dynamics $dS_t/S_t = (r-q)\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t$, the total realized variance over $[0,T]$ can be replicated by a static position in a "log contract" — a portfolio with payoff $-\ln(S_T/F)$ where $F$ is the forward price — together with continuous dynamic trading in the underlying. The fair variance strike is given by $$K_{\text{var}} = \frac{2}{T}\left(\int_0^{F} \frac{1}{K^2} P(K)\,dK + \int_F^{\infty} \frac{1}{K^2} C(K)\,dK\right)$$ where $P(K)$ and $C(K)$ are prices of out-of-the-money European puts (for $K < F$) and calls (for $K > F$), and $F = S_0 e^{(r-q)T}$ is the forward price.

Proof Sketch

By Itô's lemma applied to $\ln S_T$, $$d(\ln S_t) = \left(\mu_t - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_t^2\right)dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t$$ Subtracting this from $dS_t/S_t = \mu_t\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t$ gives $$\frac{dS_t}{S_t} - d(\ln S_t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_t^2\,dt$$ Integrating over $[0,T]$ and taking risk-neutral expectations, $$\mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^T \sigma_t^2\,dt\right] = 2\,\mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^T \frac{dS_t}{S_t} - \ln\frac{S_T}{S_0}\right]$$ The first term on the right is replicated by continuously rebalancing a position of $1/S_t$ shares (a delta-hedging argument that costs nothing in expectation under the risk-neutral measure beyond the financing already embedded in the forward). The second term, $-\ln(S_T/S_0)$, is the payoff of the log contract. A standard result (the Carr-Madan spanning formula) shows that any sufficiently smooth payoff $f(S_T)$ can be written as a combination of a bond, a forward, and a continuum of out-of-the-money puts and calls weighted by $f''(K)$. Applying this to $f(S) = -\ln(S/F)$, for which $f''(K) = 1/K^2$, yields the integral formula for $K_{\text{var}}$ above.

Remark — Why "Model-Free"

The replication formula does not reference any particular volatility model (Black-Scholes, Heston, local vol, etc.) — it holds for any continuous diffusion process, since it relies only on Itô's lemma and the spanning of payoffs by options. This is why variance swaps are described as "model-free": their fair value can be read directly off the implied volatility surface (via the option prices $P(K)$, $C(K)$ at each strike), without needing to assume a dynamic model for how that surface evolves. The replication does, however, assume continuous trading and no jumps; in the presence of jumps, realized variance includes a jump contribution that the option-replication argument does not fully capture, creating a (typically small) model-dependent residual.

Volatility Swaps and the Convexity Adjustment

A closely related instrument is the volatility swap, which pays based on realized volatility (the square root of variance) rather than variance directly.

Definition — Volatility Swap

A volatility swap with strike $K_{\text{vol}}$ pays $N_{\text{vega}}\left(\sigma_{\text{realized}} - K_{\text{vol}}\right)$ at maturity, where $\sigma_{\text{realized}} = \sqrt{\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2}$ is the realized volatility (square root of realized variance).

Because the square root function is concave, Jensen's inequality implies $\mathbb{E}[\sqrt{X}] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X]}$ for a random variable $X \geq 0$. Applying this to realized variance:

Example — The Convexity Adjustment

Let $\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2$ be the realized variance, with risk-neutral expectation $K_{\text{var}} = \mathbb{E}[\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2]$. The fair volatility strike satisfies $$K_{\text{vol}} = \mathbb{E}\left[\sqrt{\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2}\right] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2]} = \sqrt{K_{\text{var}}}$$ so $K_{\text{vol}} < \sqrt{K_{\text{var}}}$ whenever realized variance is itself random (i.e., volatility is stochastic). The gap $\sqrt{K_{\text{var}}} - K_{\text{vol}} > 0$ is the convexity adjustment (or "Jensen's gap"), and grows with the volatility of realized variance — i.e., with vol-of-vol. A common second-order approximation is $$K_{\text{vol}} \approx \sqrt{K_{\text{var}}}\left(1 - \frac{\text{Var}(\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2)}{8\,K_{\text{var}}^2}\right)$$

Because variance swaps are exactly replicable from vanilla options while volatility swaps are not (the square root introduces model dependence), variance swaps are far more commonly traded; volatility swaps and "vol futures" such as VIX futures are typically priced and hedged with reference to a model for the dynamics of variance.

Practical Considerations

Remark — Discrete Monitoring and Other Practicalities

Discrete vs. continuous monitoring: the replication argument assumes continuous-time trading, but real variance swaps define realized variance using a finite set of discrete observations (typically daily closing prices). The discretization introduces a small bias relative to the continuous-time integral $\int_0^T \sigma_t^2\,dt$, which is usually negligible for daily monitoring but can matter for weekly or less frequent observation schedules.

Replication in practice: the integral over a continuum of strikes must be approximated using the finite set of strikes actually traded in the market, with extrapolation beyond the most extreme listed strikes. Errors in the tails (where $1/K^2$ weighting is small but option prices can be mismeasured) are usually a minor source of pricing error.

Caps on realized variance: to limit the seller's exposure to extreme jump events (e.g., a market crash causing a huge single-day return that dominates $\sum r_i^2$), variance swaps are often traded with a cap on the realized variance used in the payoff calculation, typically around $2.5\times$ the strike.
