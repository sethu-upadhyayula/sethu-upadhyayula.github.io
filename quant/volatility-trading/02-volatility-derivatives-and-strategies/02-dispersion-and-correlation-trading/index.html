2.2 Dispersion and Correlation Trading

The variance of a stock index is not simply the average of the variances of its constituents - it also depends on the correlations between them. This relationship is the basis of dispersion trading, a strategy that takes a view on the difference between index-level and single-stock-level implied volatility, which is fundamentally a view on implied correlation.

The Variance-Correlation Identity

Theorem - Index Variance as a Function of Constituent Variances and Correlation

Let an index $I = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i S_i$ be a weighted sum of $n$ constituents with weights $w_i$. To a good approximation (treating returns rather than levels, with $w_i$ now denoting the weight of stock $i$'s return contribution to the index return), the variance of the index return is $$\sigma_I^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} w_i w_j \rho_{ij} \sigma_i \sigma_j$$ where $\sigma_i$ is the volatility of constituent $i$ and $\rho_{ij}$ is the pairwise correlation between constituents $i$ and $j$. If all pairwise correlations are assumed equal to a single average correlation $\bar\rho$, this simplifies to $$\sigma_I^2 \approx \bar\rho \sum_{i\neq j} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j + \sum_i w_i^2\sigma_i^2$$

The key implication is that, holding constituent volatilities fixed, index variance is increasing in average correlation $\bar\rho$. When $\bar\rho = 1$ (constituents move in lockstep), the index behaves like a weighted average of perfectly correlated assets and $\sigma_I = \sum_i w_i \sigma_i$ (volatilities add linearly). When $\bar\rho < 1$, diversification reduces index variance below this level - the index is "less volatile than its parts," with the gap widening as correlation falls.

Implied Correlation

Definition - Implied Correlation

Given the implied (variance-swap) volatilities of the index, $\sigma_I$, and of each constituent, $\sigma_i$, the implied correlation $\rho_{\text{impl}}$ is the single average pairwise correlation that reconciles the variance identity: $$\rho_{\text{impl}} = \frac{\sigma_I^2 - \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i \neq j} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j}$$ This is a market-implied quantity, extracted purely from option (or variance swap) prices on the index and its constituents, analogous to how implied volatility is extracted from an option price given a pricing model.

Empirically, implied correlation tends to be higher than subsequently realized correlation - analogous to the volatility risk premium for implied vs. realized volatility (Section 1.3) - and tends to spike during market stress, when constituent stocks become more correlated (a "risk-off" environment in which idiosyncratic factors are dominated by a common macro factor).

The Dispersion Trade

Definition - Dispersion Trade

A (long) dispersion trade consists of selling a variance swap on the index and buying variance swaps on the index's constituents, in proportions weighted to be vega-neutral at inception: $$\text{Sell } \sigma_I^2 \text{ variance swap}, \qquad \text{Buy } \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 \text{ (or similar weighting) of constituent variance swaps}$$ The trade profits if realized correlation comes in lower than the correlation implied by the relative pricing of index vs. single-stock variance at inception - i.e., it is a short-correlation position. A "short dispersion" trade does the opposite (long index variance, short constituent variance) and is a long-correlation position.

Example - Worked Dispersion P&L

Consider a simplified index of two equally-weighted stocks ($w_1 = w_2 = 0.5$), each with implied variance-swap volatility $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = 30\%$, and suppose the implied correlation priced into the index variance swap is $\rho_{\text{impl}} = 0.5$. Using the identity above with $\sigma_1=\sigma_2=\sigma$, $$\sigma_I^2 = w_1^2\sigma^2 + w_2^2\sigma^2 + 2w_1w_2\rho\,\sigma^2 = \sigma^2(w_1^2+w_2^2 + 2w_1w_2\rho)$$ With $w_1=w_2=0.5$ and $\rho=0.5$: $\sigma_I^2 = 0.09 \times (0.25+0.25+2\times0.25\times0.5) = 0.09 \times 0.75 = 0.0675$, so $\sigma_I \approx 26.0\%$.

Suppose a trader sells the index variance swap at $K_{\text{var}}^I = 26.0\%^2$ and buys each single-stock variance swap at $K_{\text{var}}^i = 30\%^2$, sized so the position is vega-neutral. If realized correlation over the life of the trade turns out to be only $\rho_{\text{real}}=0.2$, while realized single-stock volatilities come in exactly at their strikes (30% each), then realized index variance would be $$\sigma_I^{2,\text{real}} = 0.09 \times (0.25+0.25+2\times0.25\times0.2) = 0.09 \times 0.6 = 0.054 \implies \sigma_I^{\text{real}} \approx 23.2\%$$

The index variance swap (sold at $26.0\%^2$) settles below its strike - a gain to the seller - while the single-stock variance swaps (bought at $30\%$ each) realize exactly at their strikes, roughly breakeven. The net P&L is therefore positive, driven entirely by the gap between implied correlation ($0.5$) and realized correlation ($0.2$), illustrating how a dispersion trade isolates correlation as the primary P&L driver even when single-name volatility forecasts are accurate.

Practical Considerations

Remark - Why Dispersion Trades Are Hard to Execute Perfectly

In practice, dispersion trades face several frictions. First, trading variance swaps (or their option-replication portfolios) on every constituent of a broad index is operationally heavy and incurs significant transaction costs and bid-ask spreads, especially for less liquid single names - so dispersion books are often implemented on a subset of the largest or most liquid constituents, introducing tracking error against the true index variance.

Second, the variance-correlation identity assumes a single average pairwise correlation, but realized correlations across pairs can diverge substantially, especially during idiosyncratic events (e.g., an earnings surprise at a single constituent). Third, dispersion is structurally a short-correlation position on average (since implied correlation tends to exceed realized), which means it shares some of the "short volatility risk premium" character of other short-vol strategies discussed in Chapter 4 - generating steady gains in normal markets but exposed to sharp losses when correlations spike during systemic events, exactly when single-stock variance and index variance move together most strongly.
