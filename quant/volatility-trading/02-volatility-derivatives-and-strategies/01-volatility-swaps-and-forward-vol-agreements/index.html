2.1 Volatility Swaps and Forward Vol Agreements

Section 1.2 introduced the variance swap and its model-free replication via a static portfolio of options weighted by $1/K^2$. This section extends that framework in two directions: the volatility swap, whose payoff is linear in realized volatility rather than variance, and the forward volatility agreement, whose exposure begins not at trade inception but at some future date.

Variance Swap Replication Revisited

Remark - The Replicating Portfolio

Recall that the fair variance strike is $$K_{\text{var}} = \frac{2}{T}\left(\int_0^{F} \frac{1}{K^2} P(K)\,dK + \int_F^{\infty} \frac{1}{K^2} C(K)\,dK\right)$$ A trading desk that has sold a variance swap can hedge its exposure by purchasing this static "log-strip" of out-of-the-money puts and calls weighted by $1/K^2\,dK$, plus a dynamically rebalanced position of $1/S_t$ shares of the underlying. The static option portfolio is purchased once at inception and held to maturity - no rebalancing of the option positions is required, which is the source of the variance swap's "model-free" character. Only the delta-hedge of the log contract (the $1/S_t$ share position) requires continuous rebalancing.

Volatility Swaps

A volatility swap pays based on realized volatility (the square root of realized variance) rather than variance itself. As discussed in Section 1.2, this introduces a convexity (Jensen's) gap between the volatility strike $K_{\text{vol}}$ and $\sqrt{K_{\text{var}}}$, which depends on the volatility of realized variance.

Example - Approximate Replication of a Volatility Swap

Because volatility swaps cannot be replicated exactly from vanilla options (the square-root payoff is not spanned by the $1/K^2$-weighted strip), dealers typically price and hedge them as variance swaps adjusted for the convexity gap, using a model for the variance of realized variance (vol-of-vol) to estimate $$K_{\text{vol}} \approx \sqrt{K_{\text{var}}}\left(1 - \frac{\text{Var}(\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2)}{8\,K_{\text{var}}^2}\right)$$

In practice, a desk that sells a volatility swap will buy a variance swap of approximately the right vega notional and then manage the residual convexity exposure - the difference between the volatility swap's linear-in-$\sigma$ payoff and the variance swap's linear-in-$\sigma^2$ payoff - dynamically, since this residual is itself a position whose value depends on the realized path of variance (a "gamma" exposure to variance itself).

Forward Volatility Agreements

A forward volatility agreement (FVA), sometimes called a forward-starting variance swap, is a contract whose variance accrual period begins at a future date $T_1 > 0$ and ends at $T_2 > T_1$, rather than starting immediately. It allows a trader to take a pure view on the level of forward volatility - the volatility expected to prevail between $T_1$ and $T_2$ - without exposure to volatility realized between today and $T_1$.

Definition - Forward Variance

The forward variance for the period $[T_1, T_2]$ is defined by the consistency relation $$K_{\text{var}}(0,T_2)\,T_2 = K_{\text{var}}(0,T_1)\,T_1 + K_{\text{var}}(T_1,T_2)\,(T_2-T_1)$$ where $K_{\text{var}}(0,T)$ is the fair variance strike for a swap starting today and maturing at $T$, and $K_{\text{var}}(T_1,T_2)$ is the forward variance strike for the period $[T_1,T_2]$. Rearranging, $$K_{\text{var}}(T_1,T_2) = \frac{K_{\text{var}}(0,T_2)\,T_2 - K_{\text{var}}(0,T_1)\,T_1}{T_2-T_1}$$ This is exactly analogous to extracting a forward interest rate from two spot zero rates: total variance is additive across non-overlapping time intervals, just as total (continuously compounded) interest is additive.

Example - Replicating a Forward Variance Swap

A forward variance swap on $[T_1,T_2]$ can be statically replicated as a portfolio that is long a variance swap maturing at $T_2$ with notional proportional to $T_2$, and short a variance swap maturing at $T_1$ with notional proportional to $T_1$ - combined so that the variance accrued over $[0,T_1]$ cancels, leaving exposure only to $[T_1,T_2]$. Concretely, if $N$ is the desired vega notional for the forward leg, the position is long $N \cdot T_2/(T_2-T_1)$ of variance notional in the $T_2$ swap and short $N \cdot T_1/(T_2-T_1)$ of variance notional in the $T_1$ swap.

A key risk in this replication is that it requires unwinding the $T_1$-maturity leg at $T_1$, at which point its mark-to-market depends on the realized variance over $[0,T_1]$ - so while the strike $K_{\text{var}}(T_1,T_2)$ is locked in today, the cash flows are not perfectly static, unlike a standard (spot-starting) variance swap. This makes forward variance swaps somewhat less "model-free" in practice than spot variance swaps, though the static replication argument still anchors their fair value.

The Forward Volatility Smile

Just as the spot implied volatility surface has a term structure and skew, the market also has implicit views about the forward smile - the smile that will prevail at $T_1$ for options expiring at $T_2$. This is a critical input for pricing forward-starting and cliquet-style structures (introduced in the exotic equity products chapters).

Remark - Model Dependence of the Forward Smile

Unlike forward variance (which is pinned down by the additivity relation above, independent of any model), the forward smile - i.e., the skew and curvature of $\sigma(K,T_1,T_2)$ as a function of strike - is highly model-dependent. Local volatility models tend to produce a forward smile that flattens quickly as $T_1$ increases, while stochastic volatility models (e.g., Heston) produce a forward smile that retains more skew. This divergence is one of the main reasons forward-starting products are sensitive to model choice even when the underlying spot volatility surface is matched identically by both models - and is a key consideration in choosing between local, stochastic, and local-stochastic volatility models for pricing such products.
