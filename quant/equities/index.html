Equities

These notes develop the quantitative theory of equity markets and equity derivatives. Equities are claims on the residual cash flows of a business — after all liabilities are met, what remains belongs to shareholders. This simple description conceals a rich mathematical structure: stock prices follow stochastic processes, dividends create discontinuities in forward curves, and the volatility surface encodes the market's entire risk-neutral distribution of future returns.

We begin with the mechanics of equity markets: how stocks and indices are constructed, how dividends affect pricing, and the institutional structure of exchanges and market microstructure. Equity forwards and futures are then developed through the cost-of-carry relationship, with careful treatment of discrete dividends and the distinction between dividend-adjusted and unadjusted prices. Vanilla options — calls and puts — are introduced both through put-call parity (a model-independent result) and through the Black-Scholes framework.

The Black-Scholes chapter is treated in depth: the derivation via the replicating portfolio and the risk-neutral measure, the formula itself, the Greeks and their interpretation as sensitivities for dynamic hedging, and the concept of implied volatility which inverts the formula to extract the market's forward-looking volatility estimate. The empirical failure of constant implied volatility — the volatility smile and skew — motivates the volatility products chapter, which covers the construction of the implied volatility surface, the replication of variance swaps through a continuum of options, and the VIX methodology.

The final chapter addresses exotic equity products. Barrier options — knockins and knockouts — are path-dependent and require dynamic hedging strategies that must contend with discontinuous payoffs near the barrier. Autocallables are structured products widely distributed in retail markets across Europe and Asia; their complex path-dependent features mean that hedging desk risk is dominated by short-dated volatility and forward skew exposures.
