4.1 Volatility Surface

The Black-Scholes model assumes a single constant volatility $\sigma$ for an underlying asset. In practice, however, options on the same underlying with different strikes $K$ and maturities $T$ trade at different implied volatilities. Plotting the implied volatility $\sigma_{\text{imp}}(K,T)$ that equates the Black-Scholes price to the observed market price, as a function of strike and maturity, produces the implied volatility surface.

Definition — Implied Volatility Surface

The implied volatility surface is the function $$\sigma(K,T) = \sigma_{\text{imp}} \text{ such that } C_{\text{BS}}(S_0, K, T, r, q, \sigma_{\text{imp}}) = C_{\text{market}}(K,T)$$ For a fixed maturity $T$, the cross-section $K \mapsto \sigma(K,T)$ is the volatility smile (or skew): for equity indices it is typically downward-sloping, with out-of-the-money puts trading at higher implied volatility than out-of-the-money calls. For a fixed strike (or moneyness), the cross-section $T \mapsto \sigma(K,T)$ is the term structure of implied volatility.

The existence of a non-flat surface reflects the market's view that returns are not lognormally distributed: the equity skew prices in the empirical observation that large downward moves are more likely (and more feared) than large upward moves of the same magnitude — a phenomenon often linked to the leverage effect and the demand for downside protection.

Smile Dynamics: Stickiness Rules

A central practical question for a derivatives desk is how the volatility surface is expected to move when the underlying moves. The answer determines the P&L of a delta-hedged option position and is captured by "stickiness rules" — empirical descriptions of how $\sigma(K,T)$ shifts as $S_0$ changes.

Definition — Sticky Strike, Sticky Delta, Sticky Moneyness

Sticky strike: the implied volatility for a given absolute strike $K$ remains unchanged as the spot moves: $\sigma(K,T)$ is constant in $S_0$. The smile is "pinned" to fixed strike levels.

Sticky delta (sticky moneyness in $K/S$): the implied volatility for a given moneyness $K/S_0$ (or equivalently, a given option delta) remains unchanged as spot moves. The entire smile shifts with the spot, so that $\sigma(K,T)$ becomes a function of $K/S_0$ rather than $K$ alone.

Sticky moneyness: often used interchangeably with sticky delta in equity markets; the smile is parametrized in terms of log-moneyness $\ln(K/S_0)$ and that parametrization is invariant to spot moves.

The choice of stickiness regime has a direct impact on the P&L of a delta-hedged book, because it determines how much of the change in option value, when spot moves, is attributable to a change in implied volatility (vega P&L) versus a change in the underlying alone (delta/gamma P&L).

Example — Hedging P&L Under Different Stickiness Regimes

Consider a trader who is long a delta-hedged out-of-the-money put with negative skew (typical for equity index options, where $\partial \sigma / \partial K < 0$). Suppose the spot price falls.

Under sticky strike: $\sigma(K,T)$ for the put's strike is unchanged. The position's value changes only through the option's gamma (the position is long gamma, so a delta-hedged long-gamma position profits from the move, partially offset by theta decay).

Under sticky delta: as spot falls, the put becomes more in-the-money in moneyness terms; sticky delta implies the implied volatility smile shifts so that the volatility at the put's now-higher relative moneyness reflects the (higher) volatility that used to apply further out-of-the-money. Because of the negative skew, this typically means the implied volatility for that strike rises as spot falls — generating an additional positive vega P&L on top of the gamma P&L. This is why, empirically, equity index implied volatility tends to rise when the index falls (a "sticky delta"-like dynamic combined with negative skew amplifies the relationship between spot moves and implied vol moves).

Local Volatility Models

A local volatility model is a one-factor diffusion model in which the volatility of the underlying is a deterministic function of the current spot level and time, $\sigma_{\text{loc}}(S,t)$, chosen so that the model exactly reproduces the entire observed implied volatility surface.

Theorem — Dupire's Formula

Suppose European call prices $C(K,T)$ are known for all strikes $K > 0$ and maturities $T > 0$, and are arbitrage-free, twice differentiable in $K$ and once in $T$. Then there exists a unique local volatility function $\sigma_{\text{loc}}(K,T)$ such that the risk-neutral process $$dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sigma_{\text{loc}}(S_t,t)\,S_t\,dW_t$$ reproduces all call prices, given by Dupire's formula: $$\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(K,T) = \frac{\dfrac{\partial C}{\partial T} + (r-q)K\dfrac{\partial C}{\partial K} + qC}{\frac{1}{2}K^2 \dfrac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2}}$$

Local volatility models have the appealing property of being complete (one-factor) models that fit the entire observed smile exactly by construction. However, their predicted dynamics for the smile are typically "sticky strike"-like and tend to flatten the forward smile too quickly relative to what is observed empirically — a known limitation when pricing forward-starting and barrier-type exotics.

Stochastic Volatility Models

Stochastic volatility models treat volatility itself as a second random factor, driven by its own Brownian motion (typically correlated with the underlying's Brownian motion). This allows the model to generate realistic smile dynamics — including a "sticky delta"-like behaviour and a more persistent forward smile — that local volatility models struggle to replicate.

Remark — The Heston Model

The most widely used stochastic volatility model is Heston's, in which the variance $v_t$ follows a mean-reverting square-root (CIR) process: $$dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^S, \qquad dv_t = \kappa(\theta - v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v$$ with $\text{Corr}(dW_t^S, dW_t^v) = \rho$. A negative correlation $\rho < 0$ — falling prices accompanied by rising volatility — generates a negative skew consistent with equity markets. The model admits a semi-closed-form (characteristic function) solution for European option prices via Fourier methods, making it computationally tractable while capturing smile dynamics that local volatility cannot.

In practice, many trading desks use hybrid "local-stochastic volatility" (LSV) models that combine a local volatility component with a stochastic volatility factor, to match both the static smile (via the local component) and realistic dynamics (via the stochastic component).

Arbitrage Constraints on the Surface

Not every surface $\sigma(K,T)$ corresponds to a set of arbitrage-free option prices. Two key static no-arbitrage conditions constrain the shape of the surface.

Theorem — Calendar Spread and Butterfly Arbitrage

Calendar spread arbitrage: for a fixed strike $K$, the total variance $w(K,T) = \sigma^2(K,T)\,T$ must be non-decreasing in $T$: $$T_2 > T_1 \implies w(K,T_2) \geq w(K,T_1)$$ Violation implies that a calendar spread (long the longer-dated option, short the shorter-dated option, same strike) can be constructed for a guaranteed profit, since the longer-dated option must always be worth at least as much as the shorter-dated option of the same strike (its payoff dominates).

Butterfly arbitrage: for a fixed maturity $T$, the call price $C(K,T)$ must be a convex, decreasing function of $K$ (equivalently, the risk-neutral density $\partial^2 C/\partial K^2 \geq 0$ must be non-negative everywhere). A smile that is "too steep" or has a kink can imply a negative implied density at some strike, which is a butterfly arbitrage: a portfolio long one option at $K-\Delta K$, long one at $K+\Delta K$, and short two at $K$ would have a negative cost but a non-negative payoff.

Remark — Practical Surface Construction

In practice, volatility surfaces are constructed by interpolating and extrapolating a finite set of liquidly-traded option quotes (typically a handful of strikes per maturity) using a parametric form — such as the SVI (stochastic volatility inspired) parametrization — chosen so that the resulting surface is, by construction, free of calendar spread and butterfly arbitrage across the full range of strikes and maturities used for pricing and risk management.
