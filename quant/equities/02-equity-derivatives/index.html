2. Equity Derivatives

Equity derivatives are contracts whose value derives from the price (and, for some instruments, the dividends or volatility) of an underlying stock or equity index. This chapter covers the building blocks of the equity derivatives market: forwards and futures, which provide linear exposure to the underlying with payoffs that depend on the cost of carry — financing rates and dividends — and vanilla options, which provide non-linear, optionality-based exposure.

Forwards and futures are priced by no-arbitrage replication: a long forward position can be synthesised by borrowing cash to buy the stock today, so its fair price is pinned down by the financing rate and any dividends the holder forgoes. Options, by contrast, embed a choice — the right but not the obligation to transact — and require a model of the underlying's future price distribution to value, which is the subject of the Black-Scholes chapter that follows. Together, these instruments form the foundation on which more complex structured and exotic products are built.
