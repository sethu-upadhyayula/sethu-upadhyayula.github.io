1.2 Hazard Rate Model

The reduced-form (intensity-based) approach to credit risk models the default time $\tau$ of the reference entity directly as a random time, governed by a "hazard rate" or "default intensity" process, without modelling the underlying causes of default (e.g., the firm's asset value falling below its liabilities, as in structural models). This approach is the workhorse of CDS pricing because it produces tractable, closed-form expressions for survival probabilities directly calibrated to market CDS spreads.

The Hazard Rate

Definition - Hazard Rate and Survival Probability

Let $\tau$ denote the random default time. The hazard rate (or default intensity) $\lambda(t)$ is defined such that, conditional on survival to time $t$, the probability of default in the next instant $dt$ is $\lambda(t)\,dt$:

$$\mathbb{P}(\tau \in [t, t+dt) \mid \tau > t) = \lambda(t)\,dt$$

The survival probability to time $T$ is then given by:

$$Q(T) = \mathbb{P}(\tau > T) = \exp\left(-\int_0^T \lambda(u)\,du\right)$$

In the simplest case where $\lambda(t) = \lambda$ is constant ("flat hazard rate"), this reduces to $Q(T) = e^{-\lambda T}$, so $\tau$ is exponentially distributed with rate $\lambda$.

Relating Hazard Rate to CDS Spread

Under a flat hazard rate and a flat risk-free rate $r$, a simple approximation relates the CDS spread $s$, the hazard rate $\lambda$, and the recovery rate $R$:

Theorem - Credit Triangle

In the continuous-time limit (continuous premium accrual, immediate protection payment on default), the fair CDS spread satisfies:

$$s \approx \lambda (1 - R)$$

This relationship - often called the "credit triangle" - links the three quantities: spread, hazard rate, and loss given default $(1-R)$. It allows a quick back-of-envelope conversion: a 5-year CDS trading at $s = 100$bp with an assumed recovery of $R = 40\%$ implies a hazard rate of $\lambda = 0.0100 / 0.60 \approx 1.67\%$ per annum, i.e., roughly a 1.67% annual probability of default.

Bootstrapping the Hazard Rate Curve

In practice, CDS spreads are quoted at multiple maturities (1Y, 3Y, 5Y, 7Y, 10Y), and a term structure of hazard rates $\lambda(t)$ - typically piecewise constant between these tenors - is bootstrapped so that each quoted spread is exactly reproduced by the corresponding CDS pricing formula (see CDS Pricing).

Example - Piecewise-Constant Bootstrap

Suppose 1Y and 3Y CDS spreads are observed. The hazard rate $\lambda_1$ on $[0,1]$ is first chosen so that the 1Y CDS prices to par (zero upfront) given the 1Y spread. Then, holding $\lambda_1$ fixed, the hazard rate $\lambda_2$ on $(1,3]$ is chosen so that the 3Y CDS prices to par given the 3Y spread, with survival probabilities on $[0,1]$ already pinned down by $\lambda_1$. This sequential procedure is repeated outward across all quoted maturities, exactly analogous to bootstrapping a discount curve from swap rates.

Default Correlation and Copulas

For single-name CDS, only the marginal distribution of $\tau$ matters. When pricing instruments referencing multiple names - such as CDS index tranches - the joint distribution of default times across names becomes critical, and is typically modelled via a copula function applied to the marginal hazard-rate-implied survival distributions; see Structured Credit for the Gaussian copula approach.
