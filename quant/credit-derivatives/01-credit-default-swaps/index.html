1. Credit Default Swaps

A credit default swap (CDS) is a bilateral derivative contract that transfers the credit risk of a reference entity (a corporate or sovereign borrower) from one party (the protection buyer) to another (the protection seller). The protection buyer makes regular premium payments - the CDS spread - to the protection seller, who in exchange agrees to compensate the buyer for the loss incurred if the reference entity experiences a "credit event," most commonly a default, bankruptcy, or restructuring.

This chapter introduces the mechanics of single-name CDS contracts, develops the hazard rate (reduced-form) framework used to model the timing of default, and shows how this framework is used to price the CDS spread itself.

Remark - Why CDS Matter

CDS spreads are widely used as a market-implied measure of credit risk: a wider spread implies the market assigns a higher probability to default (or a lower expected recovery in default) over the life of the contract. Because CDS are unfunded - no upfront principal changes hands beyond the running premium - they allow investors to take a pure view on credit risk, going short credit (buying protection) without needing to borrow and short the underlying bond.
