1.1 CDS Structure

A single-name CDS is a contract between a protection buyer and a protection seller, referencing a specific entity (the "reference entity") and a specific class of its debt obligations. The buyer pays a periodic premium; the seller pays out if a contractually defined credit event occurs.

Contract Mechanics

Definition — CDS Contract Terms

A standard single-name CDS is specified by:

  • Reference entity — the corporate or sovereign whose default risk is being transferred.
  • Notional $N$ — the face value of debt the protection covers.
  • Maturity $T$ — typically standardised maturities of 1, 3, 5, 7, and 10 years, with 5-year being the most liquid.
  • CDS spread (or "running spread") $s$ — the annualised premium, in basis points of notional, paid by the protection buyer.
  • Premium leg — quarterly payments of $s \cdot N \cdot \Delta_i$ on standardised IMM dates (20 March, June, September, December), where $\Delta_i$ is the accrual fraction for period $i$, paid until maturity or until a credit event, whichever comes first.
  • Protection leg — a single contingent payment made by the protection seller if a credit event occurs before $T$, equal to $N(1-R)$, where $R$ is the recovery rate of the reference obligation.

Credit Events

The events that trigger the protection leg are defined by standardised documentation (ISDA Credit Derivatives Definitions). The most common are:

  • Bankruptcy — the reference entity becomes insolvent or is unable to pay its debts.
  • Failure to pay — the reference entity fails to make a payment on one of its obligations after any applicable grace period.
  • Restructuring — the terms of a debt obligation are altered in a way adverse to creditors (e.g., maturity extension, coupon reduction). Restructuring is often excluded or modified in standard CDS contracts ("CR" vs "MR" vs "XR" clauses) because of the ambiguity it introduces around which obligations are deliverable.

Settlement

Remark — Cash vs Physical Settlement

Historically, CDS were physically settled: the protection buyer delivered a defaulted bond (any bond ranking pari passu with the reference obligation, from a list of "deliverable obligations") to the protection seller in exchange for par, $N$. Since the introduction of the "Big Bang Protocol" in 2009, most CDS now settle via a cash settlement process administered through ISDA-run credit event auctions: dealers submit bids and offers for the defaulted bonds, an auction final price $P$ is determined, and the protection seller pays the buyer $N(1-P)$ in cash. This auction price $P$ serves as the market estimate of the recovery rate $R$.

The Upfront and Standardised Coupons

Since the Big Bang Protocol, most single-name CDS trade with a fixed standardised running coupon (e.g., 100bp or 500bp per annum, depending on whether the reference entity is investment-grade or high-yield), with the difference between this fixed coupon and the "fair" market spread $s$ exchanged as an upfront payment at trade inception:

Definition — Upfront Payment

If the fair market spread is $s$ and the standardised running coupon is $\bar{s}$, the protection buyer pays the protection seller an upfront amount (as a percentage of notional) approximately equal to the present value of the spread differential over the life of the trade:

$$\text{Upfront} \approx (s - \bar{s}) \times \text{RPV01}$$

where RPV01 (risky PV01) is the present value of a 1bp annuity on the premium leg, accounting for the probability of survival at each payment date — see Hazard Rate Model for its derivation. If $s > \bar{s}$ (the credit is riskier than the standard coupon implies), the buyer pays the seller upfront; if $s < \bar{s}$, the seller pays the buyer.
