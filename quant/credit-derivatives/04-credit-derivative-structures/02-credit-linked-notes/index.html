4.2 Credit-Linked Notes

A credit-linked note (CLN) is a "funded" credit derivative: a debt security issued by an entity (typically a special-purpose vehicle, SPV, or a bank) whose principal repayment and/or coupon are linked to the credit performance of a separate reference entity (or basket of entities). Unlike a CDS, where no principal changes hands upfront, the CLN investor pays the full notional at issuance - exactly like buying a bond.

Structure: Embedding a CDS in a Note

Definition - Credit-Linked Note Structure

A standard SPV-issued CLN is structured as follows:

  • The investor pays notional $N$ to the SPV at issuance, which the SPV invests in high-quality collateral (e.g. government bonds) earning the risk-free rate $r$.
  • The SPV sells CDS protection on the reference entity to a dealer, receiving the CDS spread $s$ on notional $N$.
  • The SPV pays the investor a coupon of $r + s$ (the collateral yield plus the CDS premium income) for the life of the note, assuming no credit event.
  • If no credit event occurs, at maturity the SPV returns the full notional $N$ to the investor (the collateral is liquidated to fund this).
  • If a credit event occurs, the SPV must pay the CDS protection buyer (the dealer) the default loss $N(1-R)$. This is funded by liquidating the collateral, and the investor receives only the residual: $N \times R$ (the recovery amount) at maturity, instead of full principal.
Remark - The Investor's Position

Economically, the CLN investor has sold CDS protection on the reference entity (earning the spread $s$ as enhanced coupon) while simultaneously lending money to the note issuer/collateral (earning $r$). The combination - collateralised bond + short CDS protection - is what allows the CDS exposure to be "funded": the investor's maximum loss is capped at their initial investment $N$, just as if they had simply purchased a risky bond.

Investor Motivations

Remark - Why Investors Use CLNs
  • Access for unfunded-derivative-restricted investors. Many institutional investors (insurance companies, certain funds, pension plans) face regulatory or mandate restrictions on entering derivative contracts (such as selling CDS protection directly) but can freely buy notes/bonds. A CLN lets such investors gain CDS-like credit exposure through an instrument that is, legally and operationally, simply a bond.
  • Enhanced yield. The coupon $r+s$ is higher than the risk-free rate $r$ alone, compensating the investor for taking on the reference entity's credit risk - attractive in low-rate environments where investors search for yield pickup.
  • No counterparty risk to a derivatives dealer (for the investor). Because the CLN is a funded note backed by collateral held in the SPV, the investor's exposure is to the SPV/collateral structure and the reference entity, not to a derivative counterparty's solvency in the way a direct CDS seller would be exposed to the protection buyer's payment obligations (though the investor does take collateral and structural risk in the SPV).
  • Customisation. CLNs can be structured on baskets (first-to-default notes), with leverage, or referencing maturities and currencies not directly available in the CDS market, packaged into a single security.

Worked Payoff Example

Example - CLN Payoff Under Both Scenarios

An investor buys a 5-year CLN with notional $N = \$10\text{mm}$, referencing a single corporate entity. The risk-free rate is $r = 4\%$ and the 5Y CDS spread on the reference entity is $s = 200\text{bp}$, so the note pays an annual coupon of $r+s = 6\%$, i.e. \$600,000 per year.

Scenario 1 - No credit event. Over the 5-year life, the investor receives $\$600{,}000 \times 5 = \$3{,}000{,}000$ in coupons, plus the full \$10mm principal at maturity. Total cash received: \$13mm against an initial \$10mm investment - an annualised return of approximately 6%, consistent with the enhanced coupon.

Scenario 2 - Credit event in year 3, recovery $R = 30\%$. The investor has received coupons of $\$600{,}000 \times 3 = \$1{,}800{,}000$ over the first three years (assuming coupons up to the credit event date are paid in full, a common but not universal convention - some structures prorate the final coupon). At the credit event, the note is terminated early and the SPV pays the investor the recovery amount, $N \times R = \$10\text{mm} \times 0.30 = \$3{,}000{,}000$, instead of the full \$10mm principal. Total cash received: $\$1{,}800{,}000 + \$3{,}000{,}000 = \$4{,}800{,}000$ against the \$10mm investment - a loss of \$5.2mm, reflecting that the investor bore the full default loss on the notional just as a direct seller of CDS protection would have, but with their loss capped at the amount invested.

Remark - CLNs and the Broader Credit Toolkit

The CLN closes the loop on this subject's progression: Chapter 1 built the pricing machinery for a single CDS; Chapter 2 showed how CDS are packaged into liquid indices; Chapter 3 showed how a term structure of CDS spreads is turned into a usable curve and related back to the cash bond market via the basis; and this chapter has shown two of the principal ways - TRS and CLN - that the same underlying credit risk is repackaged for investors with different funding profiles, regulatory constraints, and risk appetites. All of these instruments are, at their core, different wrappers around the same fundamental object: the market's assessment of a reference entity's survival probability curve.
