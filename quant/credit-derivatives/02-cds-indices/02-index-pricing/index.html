2.2 Index Pricing

A CDS index can in principle be priced as a portfolio of its constituent single-name CDS, each priced using the hazard-rate framework of the previous chapter. In practice, however, the index trades as its own liquid instrument with its own quoted spread, which need not exactly equal the notional-weighted average of constituent spreads.

Theoretical (Intrinsic) Spread

Definition — Intrinsic Index Spread

The intrinsic (or "theoretical") spread of an index with $n$ equally-weighted constituents, each with individual fair spread $s_i$ and risky annuity $\text{RPV01}_i$, is the notional- and risk-weighted average:

$$s_{\text{intrinsic}} = \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n s_i \cdot \text{RPV01}_i}{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \text{RPV01}_i}$$

This is the spread at which the index would trade if it were valued purely as a portfolio of its constituents, each priced off its own single-name CDS curve.

The Index Basis

Definition — CDS Index Basis

The index basis is the difference between the quoted (market) index spread $s_{\text{index}}$ and the intrinsic spread:

$$\text{Basis} = s_{\text{index}} - s_{\text{intrinsic}}$$

The basis is typically small but persistently non-zero, driven by several factors:

  • Technical demand — the index is the primary instrument for expressing macro credit views and hedging broad portfolios, so its spread is influenced by supply and demand for portfolio-level protection independent of the constituents' individual curves.
  • Liquidity premium — the index trades with much tighter bid-offer spreads than assembling the equivalent single-name portfolio, so the index can trade "rich" or "cheap" relative to intrinsics.
  • Counterparty and funding considerations — index trades often face central clearing, while not all single names are cleared, affecting relative funding costs.
Remark — Basis Trading

A "basis trade" involves buying protection on the index and selling protection on each constituent (or vice versa) to capture a non-zero basis, profiting if the basis converges toward zero. In practice this requires trading all $n$ constituent CDS individually, incurring transaction costs and funding/margin requirements that limit how tightly the basis is arbitraged away — which is why persistent index bases are observed in practice, particularly during periods of stress when liquidity in single names dries up faster than in the index.

Quoting Conventions

Like single-name CDS, index contracts trade with a fixed coupon and an upfront payment. The upfront (as a percentage of remaining notional) is computed analogously, using the index's own risky annuity computed off a flat hazard rate calibrated to the quoted index spread:

Example — Index Upfront

$$\text{Upfront} \approx (s_{\text{index}} - \bar{s}) \times \text{RPV01}_{\text{index}}$$

where $\bar{s}$ is the index's fixed coupon (e.g., 100bp for CDX.NA.IG) and $\text{RPV01}_{\text{index}}$ is computed from a flat hazard rate that reproduces $s_{\text{index}}$ under the index's own maturity and discount curve. This mirrors exactly the single-name upfront calculation in CDS Structure, applied at the index level.
