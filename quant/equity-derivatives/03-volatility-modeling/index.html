3. Volatility Modeling

The previous chapter showed that the Black-Scholes assumption of constant volatility is contradicted by the market's own prices: implied volatility varies systematically across strike and maturity, tracing out the smile, skew, and term structure of the implied volatility surface. This chapter develops the two main families of models that practitioners use to price and risk-manage options consistently across that entire surface, rather than relying on a single Black-Scholes volatility per option.

We first study local volatility, the Dupire model, which reinterprets the volatility surface as a deterministic function of spot and time, $\sigma_{loc}(S,t)$, calibrated so the model exactly reproduces every observed European option price. We then turn to stochastic volatility models, exemplified by Heston, in which volatility itself follows a random process — a richer and more realistic description of how the surface evolves dynamically, at the cost of a more involved calibration and pricing machinery.
