3.1 Local Volatility

The local volatility model, introduced by Bruno Dupire (and independently by Derman and Kani), takes a pragmatic approach to the smile: rather than assuming a more complex stochastic process for volatility, it asks what deterministic function $\sigma_{loc}(S,t)$ — a volatility that depends only on the current spot level and time, with no extra source of randomness — would cause a Black-Scholes-type model to reproduce every observed European option price exactly.

Definition — Local Volatility Model

Under the local volatility model, the underlying follows $$dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sigma_{loc}(S_t,t)\,S_t\,dW_t$$ where $\sigma_{loc}(S,t)$ is a deterministic function of the spot price and time (not a separate stochastic process). The model has a single source of randomness, exactly like Black-Scholes, but the instantaneous volatility now depends on where the spot happens to be.

Dupire's key insight was that, given a complete, arbitrage-free surface of European call prices $C(K,T)$ for all strikes and maturities, there is a unique local volatility function that reproduces it exactly — and it can be extracted in closed form by differentiating the price surface.

Dupire's Formula

Theorem — Dupire's Equation

Let $C(K,T)$ denote the price of a European call with strike $K$ and maturity $T$, as a function of $K$ and $T$, observed (or interpolated) from the market. Then the local volatility consistent with this surface is $$\sigma_{loc}^2(K,T) = \frac{\dfrac{\partial C}{\partial T} + (r-q)K\dfrac{\partial C}{\partial K} + qC}{\tfrac{1}{2}K^2 \dfrac{\partial^2 C}{\partial K^2}}$$

The numerator measures how the call price surface evolves through time and across strikes (a calendar-spread and a risk-neutral drift term), while the denominator $\tfrac{1}{2}K^2 C_{KK}$ is proportional to the risk-neutral probability density of $S_T$ at $K$ (by the Breeden-Litzenberger result). Local volatility at a point $(K,T)$ is therefore the variance rate required to make the surface's local curvature in $K$ consistent with its evolution in $T$.

Remark — Practical Calibration

Smoothness matters. Dupire's formula involves a second derivative with respect to strike, $\partial^2 C/\partial K^2$. Real option price quotes are discrete and noisy, so directly differencing market prices produces a wildly unstable local volatility surface. In practice, a smooth parametric or semi-parametric form is first fitted to the implied volatility surface (e.g. an SVI parametrisation per maturity), and Dupire's formula is applied to the smoothed surface.

Equivalent formulation in implied volatility. Dupire's formula is often re-expressed directly in terms of the implied volatility surface $\sigma_{imp}(K,T)$ and its partial derivatives with respect to strike and maturity, avoiding the need to convert back and forth between prices and volatilities — this is the form most commonly implemented in practice.

Example — Reading the Formula

Suppose at a given $(K,T)$ the call price surface is locally flat in $T$ (so $\partial C/\partial T \approx 0$, no calendar effect) and $r=q=0$. Dupire's formula reduces to $$\sigma_{loc}^2(K,T) \approx \frac{0}{\tfrac{1}{2}K^2 C_{KK}} = 0$$

which would be degenerate — in practice $\partial C/\partial T$ is always strictly positive away from expiry (option value increases with more time), so the numerator is positive and the formula yields a well-defined, positive local variance whenever the surface is arbitrage-free (which guarantees $C_{KK} > 0$, i.e. a positive risk-neutral density).

Properties and Limitations

Remark — What Local Volatility Gets Right and Wrong

Exact fit, by construction. A local volatility model calibrated via Dupire's formula reproduces every European option price in the input surface exactly — this makes it the natural choice for pricing path-dependent or exotic payoffs consistently with the observed vanilla market.

Unrealistic dynamics. The local volatility surface implied by Dupire's formula tends to flatten and "wash out" over time in a way that does not match how implied volatility surfaces actually evolve in practice — in particular, local volatility models predict that the volatility skew flattens as spot moves, whereas empirically the skew tends to move with the spot (sticky-strike vs. sticky-delta behaviour). This leads to mispriced forward-starting and barrier-type exotics whose value depends on the future shape of the smile.

A single Brownian driver. Because local volatility models retain a single source of randomness (like Black-Scholes), they cannot capture the empirically observed fact that volatility itself moves somewhat independently of spot — motivating the stochastic volatility models of the next section.
