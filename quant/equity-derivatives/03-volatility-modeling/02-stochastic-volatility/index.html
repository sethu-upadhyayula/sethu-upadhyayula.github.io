3.2 Stochastic Volatility Models

Stochastic volatility models add a second source of randomness: the instantaneous variance of the underlying is itself a stochastic process, typically correlated with the underlying's own returns. This extra degree of freedom allows these models to capture both the static shape of the implied volatility smile and realistic dynamics — how the smile evolves as spot and time move — which local volatility models cannot do.

The Heston Model

Definition — Heston (1993) Model

The Heston model specifies the joint dynamics of the spot price $S_t$ and its instantaneous variance $v_t$ as $$dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^S$$ $$dv_t = \kappa(\theta - v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v$$ with $\text{Corr}(dW_t^S, dW_t^v) = \rho\,dt$. The variance follows a CIR (Cox-Ingersoll-Ross) mean-reverting square-root process with: $\theta$ the long-run variance, $\kappa$ the speed of mean reversion, $\xi$ the volatility-of-volatility ("vol of vol"), and $\rho$ the correlation between spot and variance shocks (typically negative for equities — the leverage effect).

Remark — The Feller Condition

The CIR variance process remains strictly positive (almost surely never touches zero) if and only if $2\kappa\theta \ge \xi^2$, known as the Feller condition. In practice, calibrated Heston parameters frequently violate this condition (the variance process can reach zero, though it is instantaneously reflected back to positive values), which has implications for the choice of numerical scheme used to simulate the process — naive Euler discretisation can produce negative variances and requires a fix such as full truncation or reflection.

How the Parameters Shape the Smile

Remark — Parameter Intuition

Correlation $\rho$ controls the skew: a negative $\rho$ means that downward moves in spot tend to coincide with upward moves in variance, which raises the price (and hence implied volatility) of out-of-the-money puts relative to calls — producing the downward-sloping skew typical of equity index options.

Vol of vol $\xi$ controls the convexity (curvature) of the smile: a higher $\xi$ means variance itself is more volatile, fattening the tails of the terminal distribution of $S_T$ and steepening the smile away from at-the-money.

Mean reversion $\kappa$ and long-run variance $\theta$ primarily govern the term structure: they determine how quickly the smile's shape at short maturities (dominated by today's instantaneous variance $v_0$) relaxes toward a long-run shape governed by $\theta$ as maturity increases.

Pricing in Heston

Unlike Black-Scholes, the Heston model does not admit a simple closed-form analogue of the Black-Scholes formula in terms of elementary functions. However, it remains semi-analytically tractable: European option prices can be computed via the characteristic function of $\log S_T$, which is known in closed form.

Theorem — Heston via the Characteristic Function

The characteristic function $\phi(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iu\log S_T}]$ of the Heston model is known in closed form (involving exponentials of $\kappa$, $\theta$, $\xi$, $\rho$, $v_0$, and complex square roots). Given $\phi(u)$, European option prices follow from Fourier-inversion methods such as the Carr-Madan FFT approach or direct numerical integration of the Gil-Pelaez/Heston inversion formula. This makes calibration of the five Heston parameters $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho,v_0)$ to an observed implied volatility surface computationally feasible — each candidate parameter set can be priced across the whole surface via fast Fourier methods rather than Monte Carlo simulation.

Example — A Typical Equity Index Calibration

A representative set of Heston parameters calibrated to S&P 500 options might look like $\kappa \approx 1.5$, $\theta \approx 0.04$ (corresponding to a long-run volatility of $\sqrt{0.04}=20\%$), $\xi \approx 0.5$, $\rho \approx -0.7$, and $v_0 \approx 0.035$. The strongly negative $\rho$ reproduces the steep downward skew observed in index options, while $2\kappa\theta = 0.12 < \xi^2 = 0.25$ shows the Feller condition is typically violated for such fits — the variance process can and does reach zero under these parameters.

Beyond Heston: Stochastic-Local Volatility

Remark — Stochastic-Local Volatility (SLV) Models

Pure stochastic volatility models like Heston, even when calibrated well, generally cannot match an observed implied volatility surface exactly at every strike and maturity — there are only a handful of free parameters versus a continuum of market quotes. Local volatility models, by contrast, fit the surface exactly but have unrealistic dynamics.

Stochastic-local volatility (SLV) models combine both: the dynamics are $$dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + L(S_t,t)\sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^S, \qquad dv_t = \kappa(\theta-v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v$$ where $v_t$ follows a Heston-like stochastic process for realistic dynamics, and the leverage function $L(S,t)$ is calibrated (via a particle method or PDE-based approach, akin to Dupire but applied to the joint density) so the combined model exactly reproduces the input volatility surface. SLV models are widely used at major derivatives desks as the standard pricing engine for exotic equity products, precisely because they combine an exact fit to vanillas with realistic smile dynamics for path-dependent payoffs.
