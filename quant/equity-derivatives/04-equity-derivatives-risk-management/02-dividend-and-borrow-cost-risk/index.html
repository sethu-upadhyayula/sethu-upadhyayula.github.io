4.2 Dividend and Borrow Cost Risk

The Black-Scholes formula and its extensions typically assume a continuous dividend yield $q$ and a frictionless ability to borrow stock to sell short. Real single-stock options desks face neither: dividends are paid as discrete cash amounts on specific (and sometimes uncertain) dates, and borrowing stock to hedge a short position can be costly or, for some names, nearly impossible at reasonable rates. Both effects feed directly into the forward price of the underlying and therefore into option values — getting them wrong is a quiet but persistent source of P&L leakage.

Dividend Risk in Option Pricing

Definition — Continuous vs. Discrete Dividend Models

Continuous dividend yield. The standard Black-Scholes-Merton extension assumes the stock pays dividends continuously at a constant rate $q$, so the forward price is $F = S_0 e^{(r-q)T}$ and the stock's risk-neutral drift is reduced uniformly by $q$. This is a reasonable approximation for broad equity indices, where dividends from hundreds of constituents are paid throughout the year and aggregate into something close to a smooth flow.

Discrete (escrowed) dividends. For single stocks, dividends are paid as known or estimated cash amounts $D_i$ on specific ex-dividend dates $t_i$. The standard adjustment is the escrowed dividend model: the present value of all dividends to be paid before option expiry is subtracted from the spot price before applying Black-Scholes, $$S_0^{adj} = S_0 - \sum_{t_i \le T} D_i e^{-r t_i}, \qquad F = S_0^{adj}\,e^{rT}$$ so that the option is priced on the "dividend-adjusted" forward, which only reflects the price appreciation of the stock net of cash distributed to shareholders.

Remark — Why the Choice of Model Matters

Forward level. The continuous-yield and escrowed-dividend models can imply meaningfully different forward prices, especially for stocks with large discrete dividends relative to spot, or for short-dated options that straddle an ex-dividend date. Since the forward price is the anchor around which the entire smile is built, a mismatch here shifts the effective moneyness of every strike.

Dividend uncertainty. Even within the escrowed model, the dividend amounts $D_i$ for dates beyond the next one or two quarters are estimates, not known quantities — companies can cut, suspend, raise, or accelerate dividends (e.g. ahead of a tax law change or corporate action). A position with significant exposure to a far-dated dividend assumption carries dividend risk: the position's value changes if the market's consensus dividend forecast changes, even with spot, rates, and implied volatility all unchanged.

Special dividends and corporate actions. Large, unscheduled "special" dividends are a particular hazard: if a stock announces a large one-time cash dividend, the forward price (and hence the relevant strike grid for existing options) drops by approximately the dividend amount, which can move deep out-of-the-money options sharply into or out of the money. Exchanges typically adjust strikes for listed options around such events, but OTC and exotic positions must be manually re-struck according to the contract's adjustment provisions.

Stock Borrow Cost

Definition — Borrow Cost and the Repo Rate

To sell a stock short, a market participant must borrow shares from a lender (typically via a securities lending desk), posting collateral and paying a borrow fee, often quoted as an annualized rate $b$. The economic effect on the forward price is analogous to an additional dividend-like drag: a market participant who is short the stock (e.g. a dealer hedging a long call by selling stock short, or directly replicating a synthetic short) earns the risk-free rate on collateral but pays away the borrow fee, so the effective financing rate for a short position is $r - b$ rather than $r$. The forward price for a stock with continuous dividend yield $q$ and borrow cost $b$ becomes $$F = S_0\,e^{(r - q - b)T}$$

Remark — Hard-to-Borrow Names

General collateral vs. hard-to-borrow. Most large-cap, liquid stocks trade "general collateral" (GC), with borrow fees of a few basis points per year — effectively negligible. A "hard-to-borrow" (HTB) name — often a heavily shorted stock, one with limited float, or one undergoing a corporate event such as a merger or spin-off — can have a borrow fee of several percent, occasionally tens of percent, per year.

Effect on option prices. A high borrow cost $b$ lowers the forward price $F$, which lowers call prices and raises put prices relative to a zero-borrow assumption, all else equal — the same directional effect as an increase in the dividend yield $q$. For a heavily shorted, hard-to-borrow stock, put-call parity computed assuming $b=0$ will appear systematically violated unless the correct (positive) borrow rate is included in the discounting of the forward.

Borrow rate risk. Unlike the risk-free rate, borrow rates for individual names can be volatile and can change sharply — a stock can become hard to borrow overnight following news, a short squeeze, or a change in lendable supply (e.g. if a large holder recalls shares). A market maker who is short stock to hedge a position is directly exposed to this risk: if the borrow rate spikes, the cost of maintaining the hedge rises, and the position may need to be unwound or re-hedged via other instruments (e.g. options or total return swaps) that do not require borrowing the physical stock.

Worked Example — Impact on Option Value

Example — Dividend and Borrow Assumptions Side by Side

Consider a stock trading at $S_0 = 100$, with a risk-free rate $r = 4\%$, a one-year European call with strike $K=100$, and Black-Scholes implied volatility $\sigma = 25\%$. The stock is expected to pay a single dividend of $D=2$ in six months ($t_1 = 0.5$).

Case A — continuous dividend yield, no borrow cost. Approximating the dividend as a continuous yield $q \approx D/S_0 = 2\%$ and assuming $b=0$, the forward is $$F_A = 100\,e^{(0.04 - 0.02)(1)} = 100\,e^{0.02} \approx 102.02$$

Case B — escrowed discrete dividend, no borrow cost. Subtracting the present value of the discrete dividend from spot, $S_0^{adj} = 100 - 2e^{-0.04 \times 0.5} \approx 100 - 1.96 = 98.04$, and growing at the full risk-free rate, $$F_B = 98.04\,e^{0.04} \approx 102.05$$ For this single mid-year dividend the two forwards are close, but for stocks with multiple large dividends or for short-dated options that bracket an ex-date, the two approaches can diverge by a percent or more — enough to shift an at-the-money option meaningfully off-the-money in forward terms.

Case C — adding a 3% borrow cost. Suppose the stock has also become hard to borrow, with $b=3\%$. Using the escrowed-dividend spot $S_0^{adj}\approx 98.04$ and the effective financing rate $r-b=1\%$, $$F_C = 98.04\,e^{(0.04-0.03)(1)} = 98.04\,e^{0.01} \approx 99.02$$ Relative to Case B, the forward drops by roughly $3.0$, almost the entire borrow cost — and because a one-year at-the-money call's value is highly sensitive to the forward (its delta with respect to forward is close to $0.5$ for an ATM option), this alone moves the call's value down by an amount comparable to several tenths of a vega's worth of implied volatility. A desk that priced this option using Case A's assumptions (continuous dividend yield, zero borrow) while the true economics resemble Case C would be carrying a position mispriced by an amount that has nothing to do with the volatility surface at all — purely a forward-level (dividend and financing) error.
