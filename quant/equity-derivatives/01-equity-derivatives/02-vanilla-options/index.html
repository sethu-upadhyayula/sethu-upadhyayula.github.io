1.2 Vanilla Options

A vanilla option grants its holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying at a fixed strike price $K$ on or before a maturity date $T$. This optionality — the asymmetry between the right to act and the obligation to do so — is what distinguishes options from forwards and gives rise to the rich mathematical theory of derivatives pricing.

Definition — Call and Put Payoffs

A European call with strike $K$ and maturity $T$ pays at expiry $$C_T = \max(S_T - K, 0) = (S_T - K)^+$$ A European put with the same strike and maturity pays $$P_T = \max(K - S_T, 0) = (K - S_T)^+$$ A European option may only be exercised at maturity $T$; an American option may be exercised at any time up to and including $T$.

Payoff and Profit Diagrams

The payoff diagram plots the option's value at expiry, $C_T$ or $P_T$, against the terminal stock price $S_T$. A long call payoff is zero for $S_T \le K$ and rises linearly with slope $1$ for $S_T > K$ — a "hockey stick" shape kinked at $K$. A long put payoff is the mirror image: it falls linearly with slope $-1$ from $S_T=0$ to $S_T=K$, then is zero thereafter.

The profit diagram (as opposed to payoff) subtracts the premium paid: profit $= C_T - C_0 e^{rT}$ for a call bought at price $C_0$ (future-valuing the premium to maturity for a fair comparison). The breakeven stock price for a long call is $K + C_0$ (ignoring discounting), and for a long put is $K - P_0$.

Moneyness

Definition — Moneyness

An option is classified by the relationship between the spot price $S_0$ (or forward price $F$) and the strike $K$:

In-the-money (ITM): a call with $S_0 > K$, or a put with $S_0 < K$ — the option has positive intrinsic value.

At-the-money (ATM): $S_0 \approx K$ (or, in derivatives markets, often defined as $F \approx K$, the forward price equals the strike).

Out-of-the-money (OTM): a call with $S_0 < K$, or a put with $S_0 > K$ — the option has zero intrinsic value and would expire worthless if exercised today.

Definition — Intrinsic Value and Time Value

The premium of an option decomposes as $$\text{Premium} = \text{Intrinsic Value} + \text{Time Value}$$ where the intrinsic value is the payoff if exercised immediately, $\max(S_0-K,0)$ for a call or $\max(K-S_0,0)$ for a put, and the time value is the remaining premium — the market's assessment of the additional value from the possibility that the option moves further into the money before expiry. Time value is always non-negative for European options and decays to zero as $T \to 0$, a phenomenon known as time decay, captured by the Greek $\Theta$ (theta).

Put-Call Parity

Put-call parity is a model-independent, no-arbitrage relationship linking the prices of European calls and puts with the same strike and maturity to the price of the underlying.

Theorem — Put-Call Parity

For European options on a stock paying a continuous dividend yield $q$, with risk-free rate $r$, strike $K$, and maturity $T$: $$C - P = S_0 e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$$ where $C$ and $P$ are the call and put premia. Equivalently, in terms of the forward price $F = S_0 e^{(r-q)T}$: $C - P = (F-K)e^{-rT}$.

Proof — Static Replication

Consider two portfolios constructed at $t=0$:

Portfolio A: one long call (price $C$) plus cash of $Ke^{-rT}$ invested at the risk-free rate.

Portfolio B: one long put (price $P$) plus $e^{-qT}$ shares of stock (cost $S_0 e^{-qT}$), with all dividends reinvested so the holding grows to exactly one share by $T$.

At maturity $T$, Portfolio A is worth $\max(S_T-K,0) + K = \max(S_T, K)$. Portfolio B is worth $\max(K-S_T,0) + S_T = \max(S_T, K)$.

Both portfolios have identical payoffs in every state of the world at $T$, so by the law of one price they must have the same value at $t=0$: $$C + Ke^{-rT} = P + S_0 e^{-qT} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad C - P = S_0 e^{-qT} - Ke^{-rT} \qquad \blacksquare$$ If this relationship is violated, a riskless arbitrage profit can be locked in by buying the cheaper portfolio and selling the more expensive one — this is the conversion/reversal trade well known to options market makers.

Example — Applying Put-Call Parity

A non-dividend-paying stock trades at $S_0 = \$50$. A 1-year European call with strike $K=\$50$ trades at $C = \$4.50$. The risk-free rate is $r=3\%$. With $q=0$, put-call parity gives $$P = C - S_0 + Ke^{-rT} = 4.50 - 50 + 50e^{-0.03} = 4.50 - 50 + 48.522 = \$2.92$$

If the put were instead quoted at $\$3.50$, an arbitrageur could sell the put, buy the call, short the stock, and invest $Ke^{-rT}$ at the risk-free rate, locking in a riskless profit of $3.50 - 2.92 = \$0.58$ per share at inception.

American Options and Early Exercise

An American option's value is at least that of the otherwise-identical European option, since it grants all the same rights plus the additional flexibility to exercise early: $C^{Am} \ge C^{Eu}$ and $P^{Am} \ge P^{Eu}$. Whether this extra flexibility has positive value — i.e. whether early exercise is ever optimal — depends critically on dividends.

Theorem — Early Exercise of American Calls

It is never optimal to exercise an American call on a non-dividend-paying stock before expiry; hence $C^{Am} = C^{Eu}$ in this case.

Intuition: exercising early surrenders the option's remaining time value and forfeits the time value of money on the strike (paid earlier than necessary), in exchange only for the intrinsic value $S_t - K$. Holding the option (or, if a sale is desired, selling it in the market) always dominates, because $C_t \ge S_t e^{-q(T-t)} - Ke^{-r(T-t)} > S_t - K$ for $S_t > 0$ when $q=0$.

Remark — Dividends Change the Picture

American calls on dividend-paying stocks may be optimally exercised immediately before an ex-dividend date, if the dividend is large enough that the post-dividend price drop would erode more value than is given up by losing the remaining time value and prepaying the strike. This is the standard justification for the "exercise just before the last ex-dividend date before expiry" heuristic used in approximate American option pricing methods (e.g. Black's approximation).

American puts may be optimally exercised early even on a non-dividend-paying stock, if the stock price falls low enough (deep ITM) that the time value of money on the strike $K$ received upon exercise outweighs the remaining optionality. Formally, $P^{Am} > P^{Eu}$ whenever the early-exercise boundary is reached, which can occur for sufficiently small $S_t$ regardless of dividends.
