1.1 Equity Forwards and Futures

A forward contract on a stock obligates the holder to buy (long) or sell (short) the stock at a fixed price $F$ on a future date $T$, with no cash changing hands at inception. Futures are exchange-traded, standardised, and marked to market daily, but for the purposes of pricing under deterministic interest rates, forward and futures prices on the same underlying coincide; we use the terms interchangeably here.

Forward Price under Continuous Dividend Yield

Definition - Forward Price (Continuous Yield)

If the underlying stock or index pays dividends continuously at rate $q$ and the risk-free rate for maturity $T$ is $r$ (both continuously compounded), the no-arbitrage forward price is $$F = S_0 \, e^{(r-q)T}$$ where $S_0$ is the spot price. The quantity $r-q$ is the cost of carry: the net cost of holding the underlying asset to maturity, financing the purchase at $r$ while earning dividend yield $q$.

Proof - Replication Argument

Consider two strategies, both initiated at $t=0$ with zero net cost, that deliver one share at time $T$:

Strategy A: Enter a long forward at price $F$. At $T$, pay $F$ and receive one share.

Strategy B: Borrow $S_0 e^{-qT}$ today and buy $e^{-qT}$ shares, reinvesting the continuous dividend stream back into the position so that the holding grows to exactly one share by $T$ (this is the standard "buy fractional shares, reinvest dividends" construction). At $T$, repay the loan with interest, owing $S_0 e^{-qT} \cdot e^{rT} = S_0 e^{(r-q)T}$, and own one share.

Both strategies deliver one share at $T$ for zero initial cost; by no-arbitrage their time-$T$ cash outflows must be equal, so $F = S_0 e^{(r-q)T}$. If $F$ were higher, an arbitrageur would sell the forward and execute Strategy B (cash-and-carry); if lower, they would buy the forward and short the stock, investing the proceeds (reverse cash-and-carry). $\blacksquare$

Forward Price with Discrete Dividends

Individual stocks pay discrete cash dividends on known (or estimated) dates rather than a smooth continuous yield. The forward price formula must be adjusted to remove the present value of these cash flows from the spot price before compounding forward.

Definition - Forward Price (Discrete Dividends)

If the stock pays known dividends $D_1, D_2, \dots, D_n$ at times $t_1 < t_2 < \dots < t_n \le T$, the forward price for maturity $T$ is $$F = \left(S_0 - \sum_{i=1}^{n} D_i \, e^{-r t_i}\right) e^{rT}$$ The intuition: $S_0 - \sum_i D_i e^{-rt_i}$ is the value of the stock net of the dividends the forward buyer will not receive (since the current owner collects them). This "ex-dividend" value is then grown forward at the risk-free rate, since the forward buyer earns no dividend income but also commits no capital until $T$.

Example - Forward Price with a Dividend

A stock trades at $S_0 = \$100$. It will pay a dividend of $D = \$1$ in $t_1 = 0.25$ years, and the forward matures in $T = 1$ year. The risk-free rate is $r = 4\%$ continuously compounded.

The present value of the dividend is $D e^{-r t_1} = 1 \times e^{-0.04 \times 0.25} = 0.9900$.

The forward price is $$F = (100 - 0.9900) \, e^{0.04 \times 1} = 99.0100 \times 1.0408 = \$103.05$$ Compare this to the naive (no-dividend) forward $100 \, e^{0.04} = \$104.08$ - the dividend lowers the forward price by roughly the future value of the dividend, $\$1.03$.

Single Stock Futures and Index Futures

Single stock futures (SSFs) are exchange-traded futures on individual equities, priced by the discrete-dividend formula above using the issuer's announced or estimated dividend schedule. They are less liquid than index futures and are used mainly for financing trades, dividend arbitrage, and short positions that avoid stock-borrow costs.

Index futures (e.g. E-mini S&P 500, Euro Stoxx 50) are priced using the continuous-yield formula, with $q$ representing the blended dividend yield of the index basket. Index futures are extraordinarily liquid and are the primary vehicle for expressing macro equity views, hedging portfolios, and executing index arbitrage.

Definition - Basis

The basis is the difference between the futures price and the spot price: $$\text{Basis} = F - S_0 = S_0\left(e^{(r-q)T} - 1\right) \approx S_0(r-q)T$$ When $r > q$ the futures trade above spot (contango); when $q > r$, as is common for high-dividend equity indices when rates are low, the futures trade below spot (backwardation). As $T \to 0$, the basis converges to zero - a phenomenon known as convergence. The basis is the central object of index arbitrage: when the futures price deviates from fair value by more than transaction costs, arbitrageurs trade the futures against a basket of the underlying constituents.

Dividend Futures and Dividend Swaps

The forward price formulas above show that uncertainty about future dividends $D_i$ (or the dividend yield $q$) is a source of risk in equity forward and futures pricing. Dividend derivatives allow market participants to isolate and trade this risk separately from the price risk of the underlying.

Definition - Dividend Swap

A dividend swap on an index (or single stock) for period $[0,T]$ exchanges, at maturity, a fixed amount $K$ (the dividend strike, agreed at inception) for the realised sum of ordinary cash dividends paid by the index constituents over $[0,T]$, often expressed in index points: $$\text{Payoff (to receiver of floating)} = \left(\sum_{i} D_i^{\text{realised}}\right) - K$$ The swap is initiated at zero cost, so $K$ is set equal to the market's expectation of realised dividends - effectively a forward price on the dividend stream itself.

Remark - Why Trade Dividends Separately?

Dividend futures (exchange-listed, e.g. on Eurex for Euro Stoxx 50 dividends) provide standardised, centrally-cleared exposure to a single calendar year's dividend payments from an index, settling to the realised dividend total.

These instruments matter because an equity derivatives desk that is long stock and short calls (a typical hedged book) has exposure not just to the stock price but to the dividends embedded in the forward used for hedging. If realised dividends fall short of the market-implied $q$ used at trade inception (a common occurrence during economic stress, when companies cut dividends), the desk's hedge will have been mis-calibrated. Dividend swaps and futures let desks hedge this risk directly, decomposing total equity risk into a "pure price" component and a "dividend" component.
