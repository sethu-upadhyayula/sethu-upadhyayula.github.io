2. The Black-Scholes Framework

The Black-Scholes-Merton model is the foundational result of modern derivatives pricing. Building on the geometric Brownian motion model of stock prices introduced in the previous chapters, it shows that a European option's payoff can be perfectly replicated by continuously trading the underlying stock and a risk-free bond. This replication argument leads to a partial differential equation whose solution is the celebrated closed-form pricing formula for calls and puts - and, more importantly, establishes the principle of risk-neutral valuation that underlies essentially all of derivatives pricing theory.

This chapter develops the model in three stages. We first derive the Black-Scholes PDE and its closed-form solution, the Black-Scholes formula, together with the put-call parity relationship that links call and put prices. We then study the Greeks - the partial derivatives of the option price with respect to its inputs - which quantify risk exposures and underpin dynamic hedging strategies used by options market makers. Finally, we examine implied volatility: the practice of inverting the Black-Scholes formula to back out the volatility input from observed market prices, and the rich empirical structure (smiles, skews, term structure) that reveals the limitations of the model's constant-volatility assumption.
