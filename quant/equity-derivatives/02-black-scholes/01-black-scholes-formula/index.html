2.1 The Black-Scholes Formula

The Black-Scholes-Merton model (1973) provides a closed-form formula for the price of a European option, derived from a small set of assumptions about the dynamics of the underlying asset and the existence of a frictionless market in which to trade it. Despite its simplifying assumptions, the model remains the lingua franca of options markets: prices are quoted in terms of "implied volatility" - the volatility that, when input into the Black-Scholes formula, reproduces the observed market price.

Assumptions and the GBM Model

Definition - Black-Scholes Assumptions

The Black-Scholes model assumes:

1. The stock price follows geometric Brownian motion with constant volatility $\sigma$: $dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$.

2. The risk-free rate $r$ is constant and borrowing/lending occurs at this rate.

3. The stock pays a continuous dividend yield $q$ (possibly zero).

4. Markets are frictionless: no transaction costs, no taxes, infinitely divisible assets, continuous trading, and short-selling is permitted.

5. There is no arbitrage.

The crucial insight is that under these assumptions, an option's payoff can be replicated exactly by a self-financing trading strategy in the stock and a risk-free bond. Because the replicating portfolio's value must equal the option's value at all times (otherwise an arbitrage exists), the option's price is fully determined - it does not depend on the stock's real-world drift $\mu$, investor risk preferences, or expectations about future returns.

The Black-Scholes PDE

Let $V(S,t)$ denote the value of a derivative on a stock with price $S$ at time $t$. Consider a portfolio $\Pi$ that is long the derivative and short $\Delta = \partial V/\partial S$ shares of stock - the delta-hedged portfolio: $$\Pi = V - \Delta S$$

Derivation Sketch - The Black-Scholes PDE

By Itô's lemma, the change in the derivative's value over $dt$ is: $$dV = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}dt + \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}dS + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}dt$$ The change in the hedged portfolio is therefore: $$d\Pi = dV - \Delta\,dS = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}\right)dt$$ Notice that the $dS$ (Brownian) terms cancel exactly because $\Delta = \partial V/\partial S$ - the portfolio is instantaneously riskless. By no-arbitrage, a riskless portfolio must earn the risk-free rate: $d\Pi = r\Pi\,dt = r(V - S\,\partial V/\partial S)\,dt$. Equating the two expressions for $d\Pi$ and including a continuous dividend yield $q$ (which reduces the drift of $S$ available to the hedger) yields the Black-Scholes PDE: $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + (r-q)S\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - rV = 0$$

This PDE, together with the terminal (payoff) condition $V(S,T) = \max(S-K, 0)$ for a call (or $\max(K-S,0)$ for a put), and appropriate boundary conditions as $S \to 0$ and $S \to \infty$, has a unique solution. Solving it - either directly or, equivalently, by computing the discounted expected payoff under the risk-neutral measure - gives the Black-Scholes formula.

Risk-Neutral Valuation

Theorem - Risk-Neutral Pricing Formula

The Black-Scholes PDE is equivalent to the statement that, under a probability measure $\mathbb{Q}$ (the risk-neutral measure) in which the discounted stock price $e^{-(r-q)t}S_t$ is a martingale, the value of any European derivative with payoff $V(S_T,T)$ is the discounted expectation of the payoff: $$V(S_0,0) = e^{-rT}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\big[V(S_T,T)\big]$$ Under $\mathbb{Q}$, the stock price evolves as $dS_t = (r-q)S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}$ - the real-world drift $\mu$ is replaced by the risk-free carry rate $r-q$. This is the "risk-neutral drift": in a risk-neutral world, every asset earns the risk-free rate, and risk preferences play no role in pricing.

The Black-Scholes Formula

Solving the PDE (or computing the risk-neutral expectation, which reduces to integrating the lognormal density of $S_T$ against the payoff) gives the closed-form prices for European calls and puts.

Theorem - Black-Scholes Formula

For a stock with spot price $S_0$, paying a continuous dividend yield $q$, with constant volatility $\sigma$ and risk-free rate $r$, the price of a European call with strike $K$ and maturity $T$ is: $$C = S_0 e^{-qT}N(d_1) - K e^{-rT}N(d_2)$$ and the price of the corresponding European put is: $$P = K e^{-rT}N(-d_2) - S_0 e^{-qT}N(-d_1)$$ where $N(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF and: $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + (r - q + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$

Remark - Interpreting $d_1$ and $d_2$

$N(d_2)$ is the risk-neutral probability that the option expires in the money, i.e. $\mathbb{Q}(S_T > K)$ for a call.

$N(d_1)$ is the delta of the call (in the case $q=0$): the hedge ratio. It also arises as the probability of exercise under the "stock numeraire" measure - equivalently, $S_0 e^{-qT}N(d_1)$ is the present value of receiving the stock conditional on the option finishing in the money, and $Ke^{-rT}N(d_2)$ is the present value of paying the strike conditional on exercise. The call value $C$ is the difference of these two conditional present values.

Example - Pricing a Call

Consider a stock with $S_0 = 100$, $K = 105$, $r = 4\%$, $q = 1\%$, $\sigma = 25\%$, $T = 0.5$ years. Then: $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(100/105) + (0.04 - 0.01 + 0.25^2/2)(0.5)}{0.25\sqrt{0.5}} \approx \frac{-0.0488 + 0.0231}{0.1768} \approx -0.145$$ $$d_2 = d_1 - 0.25\sqrt{0.5} \approx -0.145 - 0.1768 \approx -0.322$$ Using $N(-0.145) \approx 0.4424$ and $N(-0.322) \approx 0.3738$: $$C = 100\,e^{-0.005}(0.4424) - 105\,e^{-0.02}(0.3738) \approx 44.02 - 38.45 \approx 5.57$$ The call is worth approximately $5.57 despite being out of the money, reflecting the time value embedded in the option from five months of remaining volatility.

Put-Call Parity

Put-call parity is a model-independent, no-arbitrage relationship between European call and put prices with the same strike and maturity. It follows purely from static replication and holds regardless of the dynamics assumed for $S$.

Theorem - Put-Call Parity

For European options on a stock paying continuous dividend yield $q$: $$C - P = S_0 e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$$ Equivalently, $C + Ke^{-rT} = P + S_0e^{-qT}$: a call plus a cash bond that pays $K$ at $T$ has the same payoff as a put plus the (dividend-reinvested) stock, since at maturity both portfolios are worth $\max(S_T, K)$.

It is straightforward to verify that the Black-Scholes call and put formulas satisfy put-call parity exactly, using the identity $N(x) + N(-x) = 1$. Put-call parity is useful in practice for inferring one option price from the other, for extracting the implied forward price and dividend yield from option markets, and as a consistency check on any pricing model - any model that fails to satisfy it admits an arbitrage.
