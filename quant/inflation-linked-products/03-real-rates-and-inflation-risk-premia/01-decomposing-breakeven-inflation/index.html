3.1 Decomposing Breakevens: Expected Inflation vs Risk Premia

Section 1.2 introduced the idea that observed breakeven inflation is not a pure measure of expected inflation, writing $\pi_{\text{BE}} = \mathbb{E}[\pi] + \text{IRP} - \text{Convexity Adj.} - \text{Liquidity Premium}$. This section develops each term in that decomposition, discusses how it can be estimated empirically, and explains why the decomposition matters for both policymakers reading breakevens as a signal and traders positioning around them.

Survey-Based vs Market-Based Inflation Expectations

Definition - Two Sources of Inflation Expectations

Market-based expectations are extracted from traded instruments - breakeven inflation from the bond market, or ZC inflation swap rates from the derivatives market (Section 2.1). They are observable in real time, at high frequency, and across the full maturity spectrum, but are entangled with risk premia and liquidity effects as discussed below.

Survey-based expectations come from periodic surveys of professional forecasters, consumers, or businesses (e.g. the Survey of Professional Forecasters, the University of Michigan consumer survey, or central bank inflation expectation surveys). These are direct point estimates of expected inflation, free of risk premia by construction, but are available only at discrete (often quarterly) intervals, for a limited set of horizons, and may be subject to behavioural biases that make them imperfect proxies for the "market's" expectation as priced by sophisticated investors.

Estimating the Inflation Risk Premium

If survey-based measures of $\mathbb{E}[\pi]$ are taken as a reasonable proxy for the market's true central expectation, the residual between the market-based breakeven and the survey measure provides a rough estimate of the combined risk-premium and liquidity terms:

Remark - A Simple Residual Decomposition

$$\underbrace{\pi_{\text{BE}}(T) - \mathbb{E}_{\text{survey}}[\pi(T)]}_{\text{observable residual}} \approx \underbrace{\text{IRP}(T)}_{\text{inflation risk premium}} - \underbrace{\text{Convexity Adj.}(T)}_{\text{small, Section 1.2}} - \underbrace{\text{Liquidity Premium}(T)}_{\text{TIPS-specific}}$$ The residual is typically positive on average over long samples (consistent with a positive inflation risk premium dominating the smaller convexity and liquidity terms), but can swing sharply negative during liquidity crises - most dramatically in March 2020, when 10-year TIPS breakevens fell to roughly $0.5\%$ even though survey-based long-run inflation expectations remained anchored near $2\%$, a gap of $150$bp or more driven almost entirely by a collapse in TIPS liquidity rather than any change in inflation expectations.

Term Structure of the Risk Premium

The inflation risk premium is generally believed to be small or even slightly negative at very short horizons (where near-term inflation outcomes are relatively predictable and dominated by already-known data) and to increase with maturity, reflecting the greater cumulative uncertainty about inflation over longer horizons.

Remark - Why the Premium Should Be Positive at Long Horizons

A nominal bondholder's real return is $\frac{1+y_n}{\text{CPI}(T)/\text{CPI}(0)} - 1$ - a decreasing, convex function of realised cumulative inflation. Higher cumulative inflation tends to coincide with "bad" macroeconomic states for a typical investor (eroded real wealth, often associated with economic stress), so nominal bonds provide a poor hedge against - indeed amplify exposure to - high-inflation states. Investors demand compensation for bearing this exposure, which manifests as a positive inflation risk premium embedded in nominal yields, pushing $y_n$ (and hence $\pi_{\text{BE}}$) above pure expected inflation. The magnitude of this effect compounds with horizon, consistent with an upward-sloping term structure of inflation risk premia.

Worked Example - Decomposing a 10-Year Breakeven

Example - Splitting Breakeven into Components

Suppose the 10-year breakeven inflation rate, computed from nominal and real yields as in Section 1.2, is $\pi_{\text{BE}}(10) = 2.40\%$. A survey of professional forecasters' median 10-year-ahead average CPI inflation expectation is $\mathbb{E}_{\text{survey}}[\pi(10)] = 2.10\%$. The convexity adjustment at this maturity is estimated to be small, around $-3$bp (i.e. it reduces the breakeven by $3$bp relative to a no-convexity world).

The residual is $2.40\% - 2.10\% = 0.30\%$, or $30$bp. Attributing the $-3$bp convexity effect, the implied combined inflation risk premium and (negative, i.e. breakeven-depressing) TIPS liquidity premium is: $$\text{IRP}(10) - \text{Liquidity Premium}(10) \approx 0.30\% - (-0.03\%) = 0.33\%, \text{ i.e. } 33\text{bp}$$

Without additional information, this $33$bp cannot be split further between the risk premium and liquidity premium components - doing so requires either a structural term-structure model estimated jointly on nominal and real yield data, or direct evidence from inflation swap markets (which embed the risk premium but not the TIPS-specific liquidity premium), comparing the swap-implied breakeven to the bond-implied breakeven to isolate the liquidity component.
