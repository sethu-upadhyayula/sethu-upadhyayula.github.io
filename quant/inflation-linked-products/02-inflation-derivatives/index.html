2. Inflation Derivatives

While inflation-linked bonds remain the largest source of inflation exposure outstanding, the inflation derivatives market - dominated by zero-coupon and year-on-year inflation swaps, with a smaller but liquid market in inflation caps and floors - provides a more direct, unfunded way to trade and hedge inflation. Inflation swaps in particular are the workhorse instrument for pension funds and insurers hedging inflation-linked liabilities, and their quoted rates provide a cleaner market-based measure of inflation expectations than bond-derived breakevens, since they are not distorted by the relative liquidity of the cash bond market.

The first section of this chapter develops the payoff and pricing of zero-coupon inflation swaps - the simplest and most liquid inflation derivative - and contrasts them with year-on-year inflation swaps, whose annually-resetting structure introduces an inflation convexity effect not present in the zero-coupon contract. The second section covers inflation caps, floors, and options, including the distinction between year-on-year and cumulative (zero-coupon-style) caps, and a basic Black-style framework for pricing them. Together these instruments complete the toolkit - alongside the cash bond and real-yield-curve framework of Chapter 1 - needed to analyse the inflation risk premium decomposition of Chapter 3 and the portfolio applications of Chapter 4.
