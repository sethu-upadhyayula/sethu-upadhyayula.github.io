2.2 Inflation Caps, Floors, and Options

Inflation caps and floors are option contracts on realised inflation, used to hedge against inflation rising above (cap) or falling below (floor) a specified strike level. They are widely used to hedge pension liabilities with explicit inflation caps and floors written into their indexation rules — for example, UK defined-benefit pensions frequently apply a "0% floor, 5% cap" (an "LPI 0-5") to annual benefit increases. This section defines the payoffs of YoY and cumulative inflation caps and floors, and outlines a Black-style approach to pricing them.

Year-on-Year Inflation Caps and Floors

Definition — Year-on-Year Inflation Caplet

A YoY inflation caplet for the period $[t_{k-1}, t_k]$, with strike $K$, pays at $t_k$: $$\max\left(\frac{\text{CPI}(t_k)}{\text{CPI}(t_{k-1})} - 1 - K,\; 0\right)$$ A YoY inflation cap is a strip of such caplets across consecutive annual periods. The corresponding YoY floorlet pays $\max\left(K - \left(\dfrac{\text{CPI}(t_k)}{\text{CPI}(t_{k-1})}-1\right),\,0\right)$, and a YoY floor is a strip of floorlets. Each caplet/floorlet is, structurally, a call/put option on the YoY inflation rate underlying the corresponding leg of a YoY inflation swap (Section 2.1).

Cumulative (Zero-Coupon-Style) Caps and Floors

A second family of inflation options pays based on cumulative inflation from the trade's inception to each payment date, analogous to the ZC inflation swap's payoff rather than the YoY swap's.

Definition — Cumulative Inflation Cap/Floor

A cumulative inflation caplet maturing at $T$ with strike rate $K$ pays: $$\max\left(\frac{\text{CPI}(T)}{\text{CPI}(0)} - (1+K)^T,\; 0\right)$$ i.e., a call option on the cumulative CPI ratio struck at the cumulative growth implied by the fixed rate $K$. The corresponding floor pays $\max\left((1+K)^T - \dfrac{\text{CPI}(T)}{\text{CPI}(0)},\,0\right)$. This structure is the natural option analogue of the ZC inflation swap (Section 2.1), just as the YoY cap/floor is the option analogue of the YoY swap.

Remark — Which Structure Matches a Given Liability

The choice between YoY and cumulative structures should match the liability being hedged. Pension benefits that apply an annual cap/floor to each year's increase (e.g. "increases capped at 5% per annum, floored at 0% per annum") are naturally hedged with YoY caps/floors, because the option triggers (or doesn't) independently each year regardless of the path of inflation in other years. A liability that caps/floors only the cumulative increase over the full term — much rarer in practice — would instead be hedged with a cumulative structure. Because annual floors compound (a 0% floor every year for 10 years floors cumulative inflation at 0% only if every single year individually floors, which is far more valuable than a single 0% floor on 10-year cumulative inflation), YoY floor strips are typically significantly more expensive than the corresponding cumulative floor of the same strike and maturity — a frequent source of confusion when comparing quotes.

Black-Style Pricing

A common practical approach prices each caplet/floorlet by treating the relevant forward inflation rate as a lognormally (or normally) distributed underlying and applying the Black (or Bachelier) option pricing formula, with an implied volatility taken from the inflation options market (itself often quoted, broker-sourced, and relatively illiquid compared to nominal rate option markets).

Definition — Black Formula for a YoY Inflation Caplet

Let $F_k$ denote the forward YoY inflation rate for $[t_{k-1},t_k]$ (from the YoY swap curve, including the convexity adjustment of Section 2.1), $\sigma_k$ the Black implied volatility for that caplet, $K$ the strike, and $P(0,t_k)$ the relevant discount factor. The Black price of the caplet is: $$\text{Caplet} = P(0,t_k)\left[(1+F_k)\,N(d_1) - (1+K)\,N(d_2)\right]$$ $$d_{1,2} = \frac{\ln\left(\frac{1+F_k}{1+K}\right) \pm \frac{1}{2}\sigma_k^2 t_k}{\sigma_k\sqrt{t_k}}$$ where $N(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF. This is the standard Black formula applied to the "gross" inflation ratio $1 + \pi_{\text{YoY}}$ rather than to $\pi_{\text{YoY}}$ directly, which avoids issues with the lognormal assumption when inflation rates can be negative — though for very low or negative strikes, a normal (Bachelier) model applied directly to $\pi_{\text{YoY}}$ is often preferred in practice.

Remark — Limitations of the Black Approach

The Black approach treats each caplet/floorlet independently with its own implied volatility, ignoring the term structure correlations between consecutive YoY periods that matter for path-dependent structures (e.g. cumulative caps/floors, or caps/floors on multi-year compounded inflation). More sophisticated approaches use a full term-structure model of the price index jointly with nominal and real rates (again, typically in a Jarrow-Yildirim-style framework), calibrated to both the ZC/YoY swap curves and the available cap/floor volatility surface. For a first-pass valuation or risk estimate, however, the Black approach with a YoY volatility surface remains the market standard quoting convention.

Worked Example — Pricing a YoY Floorlet

Example — 1-Year Forward-Starting YoY Inflation Floorlet at 0% Strike

Consider a 1-year YoY inflation floorlet, forward-starting in 4 years (so it pays at $t_k = 5$ based on inflation realised over $[4,5]$), struck at $K=0\%$ (a "zero floor"). Suppose the forward YoY inflation rate for that period is $F_k = 2.50\%$, the Black implied volatility is $\sigma_k = 90\%$ (YoY inflation volatilities are often quoted very high in relative terms because the underlying rate itself is small), and the relevant discount factor is $P(0,5) = 0.85$.

Using $1+F_k = 1.0250$ and $1+K=1.0000$: $$d_{1} = \frac{\ln(1.0250/1.0000) + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.90)^2 (5)}{0.90\sqrt{5}} = \frac{0.02469 + 2.025}{2.0125} \approx 1.018$$ $$d_2 = d_1 - \sigma_k\sqrt{t_k} \approx 1.018 - 2.0125 \approx -0.995$$

With $N(d_1)\approx 0.846$ and $N(d_2)\approx 0.160$, the corresponding caplet value is $P(0,5)\left[1.0250 \times 0.846 - 1.0000\times 0.160\right] \approx 0.85 \times (0.867 - 0.160) \approx 0.601$. By put-call parity for this gross-ratio formulation, $\text{Floorlet} - \text{Caplet} = P(0,5)\left[(1+K) - (1+F_k)\right] = 0.85 \times (1.0000-1.0250) = -0.02125$, so: $$\text{Floorlet} \approx \text{Caplet} - 0.02125 \approx 0.601 - 0.021 = 0.580$$

In annualised running-spread terms this would be converted to a small fraction of a percent of notional — but the key takeaway is qualitative: with a positive forward inflation rate and very high implied volatility, a 0%-strike YoY floorlet carries substantial value, since there is meaningful probability that a single year's inflation print comes in below zero even when the long-run forward is comfortably positive.
