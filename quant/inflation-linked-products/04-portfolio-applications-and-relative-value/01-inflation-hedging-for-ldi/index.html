4.1 Inflation Hedging for Liability-Driven Investors

Pension funds and insurers with inflation-linked liabilities - final-salary pension benefits that escalate with a price index, or annuity and insurance contracts with explicit CPI-linked payouts - face a direct economic exposure to realised inflation that mirrors, in reverse, the exposure of an investor holding inflation-linked bonds. This section develops the liability-driven investment (LDI) framework for hedging that exposure, focusing on how hedge ratios are constructed using linkers and inflation swaps, and works through a worked example of a typical LDI inflation hedge.

The Inflation-Linked Liability

A defined-benefit pension scheme's liabilities are, in present-value terms, a stream of future benefit cash flows, many of which are contractually linked to a price index (commonly CPI or RPI, depending on jurisdiction and scheme rules). The present value of these liabilities therefore depends not only on the discount curve used to value them, but also on the path of future inflation embedded in that valuation.

Definition - Liability Inflation Sensitivity

Write the present value of the liabilities as $L = \sum_{i} N_i \cdot \text{IR}(T_i) \cdot P(0,T_i)$, where $N_i$ is the nominal (base-year) cash flow due at time $T_i$, $\text{IR}(T_i)$ is the cumulative index ratio applied to that cash flow (Section 1.1), and $P(0,T_i)$ is the discount factor. The liability's sensitivity to a parallel shift in the inflation curve - its inflation duration - is $$D_{\pi}^{L} = -\frac{1}{L}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \pi} = \frac{1}{L}\sum_i N_i \cdot \text{IR}(T_i) \cdot P(0,T_i) \cdot T_i$$ a cash-flow-weighted average of the times $T_i$, exactly analogous to the Macaulay duration of a bond - except that here it measures sensitivity to the inflation curve rather than the nominal or real discount curve.

Hedging Instruments: Linkers vs Inflation Swaps

Two broad instrument classes are available to hedge this exposure, and most LDI portfolios use a combination of both, chosen according to funding constraints, collateral requirements, and the shape of the liability cash flow profile.

Remark - Linkers vs Swaps in an LDI Context

Inflation-linked bonds (linkers). Holding a linker requires funding its full notional, which competes with the scheme's return-seeking assets for capital. Linkers also provide real-rate duration alongside inflation exposure - a linker hedges both the real-rate and inflation-linkage components of the liability simultaneously, which can be a feature (if the liability also has real-rate sensitivity through its discount factors) or a complication (if the scheme wants to isolate the inflation exposure only).

Inflation swaps (ZC and YoY, Section 2.1). A receive-inflation ZC swap is unfunded (aside from collateral posting under a CSA), allowing the scheme to add inflation exposure without displacing capital from return-seeking assets. Swaps can also be tailored to arbitrary maturities and notionals to match the liability cash flow profile more precisely than the available linker universe, which is constrained to whatever maturities and coupons the relevant sovereign or supranational issuers have in issue.

Constructing the Hedge Ratio

The basic objective is to choose a notional of hedging instrument such that the inflation-driven change in the value of the hedge offsets the inflation-driven change in the value of the liability, for a given shift in the inflation curve.

Definition - Inflation Hedge Ratio

For a hedging instrument $H$ (a linker or inflation swap) with inflation-curve sensitivity $\partial H/\partial \pi$, the notional $N_H$ required to hedge a liability $L$ with sensitivity $\partial L/\partial \pi$ against a parallel shift in the inflation curve solves $$N_H \cdot \frac{\partial H}{\partial \pi}\bigg|_{\text{per unit notional}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \pi}$$ The hedge ratio is then expressed as $N_H / L$ - the notional of hedging instrument per unit of liability present value - and a hedge ratio of 100% (often termed "full hedging") means the inflation sensitivities are matched one-for-one at the portfolio level. In practice, schemes often run hedge ratios below 100% (e.g. 60-80%) reflecting funding constraints, a desire to retain some upside if realised inflation undershoots curve-implied expectations, or because only a subset of liability cash flows (typically the longest-dated, least certain ones) are deemed appropriate to hedge with available instruments.

Matching the Curve Shape, Not Just the Level

A single parallel-shift hedge ratio is a useful summary statistic, but liabilities typically have cash flows spread across many decades, while the available hedging instruments are concentrated at specific maturities (linker issuance points, or liquid swap tenors). A robust hedge therefore requires matching sensitivity bucket-by-bucket across the curve, not just in aggregate.

Remark - Key Rate Inflation Durations

Analogous to key rate durations for nominal curve risk (Section 3.1 of Rates Fundamentals), the liability's inflation sensitivity can be decomposed into a vector of sensitivities to shifts in specific points on the inflation curve (e.g. 5y, 10y, 20y, 30y breakevens or ZC swap rates). The hedge portfolio - a combination of linkers and swaps at various maturities - is then constructed to match this sensitivity vector as closely as possible, subject to the constraint that the available instruments are discrete points rather than a continuum. Liabilities with very long-dated cash flows (40-50 years) often cannot be fully matched even with the longest-dated available linkers or swaps, leaving residual "tail risk" that the scheme must either accept or hedge with proxy instruments at shorter maturities, extrapolated using assumptions about the long end of the curve.

Worked Example - Constructing an LDI Inflation Hedge

Example - Sizing a Receive-Inflation Swap Overlay

Suppose a pension scheme has CPI-linked liabilities with a present value of $L = \$2.0\text{bn}$ and an inflation duration of $D_\pi^L = 18$ years, so $\partial L/\partial \pi \approx L \times D_\pi^L = \$2.0\text{bn} \times 18 = \$36\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr}$ (in present-value terms, per unit of parallel inflation curve shift, e.g. per $1\%$ shift this is a $\$360\text{m}$ change in liability value).

The scheme currently holds $\$0.6\text{bn}$ notional of long-dated linkers with an average inflation duration of $20$ years, contributing $\partial H_{\text{linker}}/\partial \pi \approx \$0.6\text{bn} \times 20 = \$12\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr}$. The scheme targets a hedge ratio of $80\%$, i.e. it wants total hedge sensitivity of $0.80 \times \$36\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr} = \$28.8\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr}$.

The remaining sensitivity to be hedged via an inflation swap overlay is $\$28.8\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr} - \$12\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr} = \$16.8\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr}$. If the scheme uses 30-year ZC inflation swaps (inflation duration $\approx 30$ years per unit notional), the required notional is: $$N_{\text{swap}} = \frac{\$16.8\text{bn}\cdot\text{yr}}{30\text{ yr}} = \$0.56\text{bn}$$

The scheme therefore enters a receive-fixed (pay-CPI, i.e. receive the realised inflation leg) 30-year ZC inflation swap with $\$0.56\text{bn}$ notional, alongside its existing $\$0.6\text{bn}$ linker holding, to bring the portfolio's aggregate inflation sensitivity to roughly $80\%$ of the liability's sensitivity. This is, of course, only a parallel-shift approximation - in practice the scheme would also check the key-rate inflation duration profile of the combined linker-plus-swap hedge against the liability's profile (per the remark above) to ensure the hedge is not concentrated at the wrong point on the curve relative to where the liability cash flows actually fall.
