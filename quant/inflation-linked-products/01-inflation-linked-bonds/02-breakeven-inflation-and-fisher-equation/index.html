1.2 Breakeven Inflation and the Fisher Equation

The real yield curve developed in Section 1.1 becomes most informative when compared against the nominal yield curve for government bonds of the same currency and maturity. The spread between the two - the breakeven inflation rate - is one of the most widely watched market-implied measures of inflation expectations. This section defines breakeven inflation precisely, develops the Fisher equation that relates nominal, real, and inflation rates, and examines why the simple Fisher relationship is only an approximation in practice.

Defining Breakeven Inflation

Definition - Breakeven Inflation Rate

For a given maturity $T$, let $y_n(T)$ denote the nominal zero-coupon yield (from the conventional government bond curve) and $y_r(T)$ the real zero-coupon yield (from the inflation-linked bond curve of Section 1.1). The breakeven inflation rate to maturity $T$ is defined as the spread between the two: $$\pi_{\text{BE}}(T) \approx y_n(T) - y_r(T)$$ Plotted across maturities, $\pi_{\text{BE}}(T)$ traces out the breakeven inflation curve. It represents the average annual inflation rate over $[0,T]$ at which an investor would be indifferent, on a purely mechanical basis, between holding a nominal bond and an inflation-linked bond of the same maturity.

Remark - Breakeven as a Relative-Value Signal

If realised average inflation over $[0,T]$ turns out to exceed $\pi_{\text{BE}}(T)$, the inflation-linked bond outperforms the nominal bond (in total-return terms, holding the real yield curve constant); if realised inflation falls short, the nominal bond outperforms. This makes breakeven inflation simultaneously (i) a market-implied inflation forecast, and (ii) the carry-and-roll-adjusted relative value signal between the nominal and real curves - two interpretations that are explored further in Sections 3.1 and 4.2.

The Fisher Equation

The relationship between nominal rates, real rates, and inflation has a long history in economics, going back to Irving Fisher. In its exact, compounding-consistent form, the Fisher equation states that one plus the nominal rate equals the product of one plus the real rate and one plus the expected inflation rate:

Theorem - The Fisher Equation

For a single period, let $i$ denote the nominal interest rate, $r$ the real interest rate, and $\pi^e$ the expected inflation rate over the period. The exact Fisher relation is: $$(1+i) = (1+r)(1+\pi^e)$$ Expanding the product gives $1 + i = 1 + r + \pi^e + r\pi^e$, so that: $$i = r + \pi^e + r\pi^e$$ The familiar approximation $i \approx r + \pi^e$ drops the cross term $r\pi^e$, which is small (second-order) whenever $r$ and $\pi^e$ are individually small - e.g. for $r = 2\%$ and $\pi^e = 3\%$, the cross term is $0.0006$, or $6$bp, versus a sum of $500$bp.

Example - Exact vs Approximate Fisher Decomposition

Suppose the 10-year nominal yield is $y_n = 4.50\%$ and the 10-year real yield is $y_r = 2.00\%$.

Approximate breakeven: $\pi_{\text{BE}} \approx y_n - y_r = 4.50\% - 2.00\% = 2.50\%$.

Exact Fisher-implied breakeven solves $(1+y_n) = (1+y_r)(1+\pi_{\text{BE}})$: $$\pi_{\text{BE}} = \frac{1+y_n}{1+y_r} - 1 = \frac{1.0450}{1.0200} - 1 = 1.02451 - 1 = 2.451\%$$

The approximate and exact breakevens differ by about $5$bp here - small but not negligible at the level of precision used in relative-value trading, and the discrepancy grows for higher rate levels or longer horizons.

Limitations: Risk Premia, Convexity, and Jensen's Inequality

Even the exact Fisher equation describes a relationship that would hold under certainty - if $\pi^e$ were the inflation rate that would actually be realised with probability one. In reality, future inflation is uncertain, and this uncertainty introduces two further effects that drive a wedge between market-implied breakeven inflation and the market's true expectation of average inflation.

Remark - Jensen's Inequality and the Inflation Risk Premium

Because the relationship between bond prices and yields is convex, and the inflation-linked bond's payoff is a convex function of the realised price index (it scales multiplicatively with $\text{IR}(T) = \text{CPI}(T)/\text{CPI}(0)$), Jensen's inequality implies that the expected payoff of the inflation-linked bond, evaluated under the true distribution of future CPI, is strictly greater than its payoff evaluated at the expected CPI path. A risk-neutral investor would therefore be willing to accept a slightly lower real yield (pay a slightly higher price) for the inflation-linked bond than the simple Fisher relation would suggest - this convexity effect is sometimes called the inflation convexity or Jensen's inequality adjustment, and tends to push observed breakevens slightly below pure expected inflation, all else equal.

Remark - The Inflation Risk Premium

Separately, holders of nominal bonds bear inflation risk: an unexpected rise in inflation erodes the real value of fixed nominal coupons, whereas inflation-linked bondholders are insulated from this risk. Risk-averse investors therefore demand compensation - an inflation risk premium ($\text{IRP}$) - for holding nominal rather than real exposure. This premium is embedded in nominal yields and pushes the observed breakeven above pure expected inflation. The two effects above (Jensen's inequality and the inflation risk premium) work in opposite directions and need not cancel, so their net effect on breakevens varies over time and across maturities.

Definition - Decomposing the Observed Breakeven

Putting the pieces together, the observed market breakeven can be written, to a first approximation, as: $$\pi_{\text{BE}}(T) = \mathbb{E}[\pi(T)] + \text{IRP}(T) - \text{Convexity Adj.}(T) - \text{TIPS Liquidity Premium}(T)$$ where $\mathbb{E}[\pi(T)]$ is the market's true expectation of average inflation to $T$, $\text{IRP}(T)$ is the inflation risk premium embedded in nominal yields, the convexity adjustment reflects the Jensen's inequality effect above, and the liquidity premium reflects any additional yield investors demand for holding the less liquid inflation-linked bond relative to the nominal bond. Chapter 3 develops this decomposition further and discusses how each component can be estimated in practice.

Why This Matters

The gap between "breakeven inflation" and "expected inflation" is not a theoretical nicety - it has direct practical consequences. Central banks and policymakers who read breakeven inflation as a real-time market survey of inflation expectations must account for the risk-premium and liquidity components, especially during periods of market stress when liquidity premia can move sharply and temporarily dominate the breakeven, as happened to TIPS breakevens in March 2020. Traders who take positions based on breakevens - whether outright or as part of a relative-value strategy (Section 4.2) - are implicitly taking views not just on inflation but on these risk-premium and liquidity dynamics as well.
