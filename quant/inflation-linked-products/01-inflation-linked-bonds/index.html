1. Inflation-Linked Bonds

Inflation-linked bonds (ILBs) — such as U.S. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) and UK Index-Linked Gilts — pay coupons and principal that are adjusted for changes in a reference price index (typically a national CPI), protecting investors from the erosion of purchasing power that affects fixed-coupon nominal bonds.

Indexation Mechanics

Definition — Index Ratio

An inflation-linked bond's principal is adjusted by an index ratio that tracks the cumulative change in a reference CPI since the bond's issuance:

$$\text{Index Ratio}(t) = \frac{\text{CPI}(t)}{\text{CPI}(0)}$$

The inflation-adjusted principal at time $t$ is $F \times \text{Index Ratio}(t)$, where $F$ is the original face value, and each coupon payment is computed as the fixed real coupon rate applied to this adjusted principal: $c \times F \times \text{Index Ratio}(t)$. At maturity, the investor receives the inflation-adjusted principal (subject, for TIPS, to a floor at the original par value in the event of cumulative deflation).

Because the reference CPI is published with a lag, ILBs use an "indexation lag" (commonly three months for TIPS and UK gilts) — the index ratio applicable on a given date is computed using the CPI level from three months prior, interpolated daily between monthly CPI releases.

Real Yields and Breakeven Inflation

Definition — Breakeven Inflation Rate

The yield on an inflation-linked bond is a real yield $y_r$ — the return an investor earns above inflation. The yield on a comparable-maturity nominal (fixed-coupon) government bond is a nominal yield $y_n$. The breakeven inflation rate is approximately the difference between the two:

$$\pi_{\text{BE}} \approx y_n - y_r$$

The breakeven rate represents the average inflation rate over the bond's maturity at which an investor would be indifferent between holding the nominal bond and the inflation-linked bond. If realised inflation exceeds the breakeven rate, the inflation-linked bond outperforms; if realised inflation is below the breakeven rate, the nominal bond outperforms.

Example — Reading Breakevens

If the 10-year nominal Treasury yield is $y_n = 4.5\%$ and the 10-year TIPS real yield is $y_r = 2.0\%$, the 10-year breakeven inflation rate is approximately $2.5\%$ — the bond market's implied average annual CPI inflation expectation over the next decade. Breakeven rates are widely watched as a real-time, market-based gauge of inflation expectations, complementing survey-based measures.

The Fisher Equation and Its Limitations

The relationship $y_n \approx y_r + \pi_{\text{BE}}$ is an application of the Fisher equation, which in its exact form is $(1+y_n) = (1+y_r)(1+\pi_{\text{BE}})$. In practice, the breakeven rate extracted from bond yields is not a pure measure of expected inflation — it also embeds an inflation risk premium (compensation nominal bondholders demand for bearing inflation uncertainty) and a liquidity premium (TIPS and index-linked gilts are generally less liquid than nominal government bonds, so their yields can be distorted by liquidity effects independent of inflation expectations).

Remark — Decomposing the Breakeven

$$\pi_{\text{BE}} = \mathbb{E}[\pi] + \text{Inflation Risk Premium} - \text{TIPS Liquidity Premium}$$

During periods of market stress (e.g., the early stages of the 2008 and 2020 crises), TIPS liquidity can deteriorate sharply, causing breakeven rates to fall well below any reasonable estimate of expected inflation — not because the market expects deflation, but because TIPS prices fall (real yields rise) due to forced selling and reduced market-making capacity, independent of the inflation outlook.

Inflation Swaps

Inflation swaps provide a more direct way to trade inflation expectations without the funding and liquidity considerations of holding physical bonds. In a zero-coupon inflation swap, one party pays a fixed rate (the breakeven rate agreed at trade inception) and receives the realised cumulative inflation over the swap's term, with a single net cash flow exchanged at maturity:

Definition — Zero-Coupon Inflation Swap Payoff

At maturity $T$, the floating-rate payer pays the fixed-rate payer (per unit notional):

$$\left(\frac{\text{CPI}(T)}{\text{CPI}(0)} - 1\right) - \left((1+\pi_{\text{fixed}})^T - 1\right)$$

i.e., the difference between realised cumulative inflation and the cumulative fixed rate agreed at inception. Inflation swaps are widely used by pension funds and insurers to hedge inflation-linked liabilities, and their quoted fixed rates provide another market-based measure of inflation expectations, generally considered cleaner than bond-derived breakevens since they do not embed the TIPS liquidity premium.
