2.1 Contract Specifications and Basis Risk

An agricultural futures contract is a standardized promise to deliver (or accept delivery of) a specific grade and quantity of a commodity at a specific location during a specific delivery period. The precision of these specifications is what makes the contract fungible and tradeable on an exchange - but it also means that the futures price is, strictly speaking, the price of grain of a particular grade at a particular delivery point, which may differ systematically from the price a given producer or end user actually faces. That difference is the basis, and managing it is a central activity of grain merchandising.

Deliverable Grades and Delivery Points

Definition - Deliverable Grade and Delivery Points

Each agricultural futures contract specifies a par (deliverable) grade - for CBOT corn, for example, No. 2 Yellow Corn - along with a schedule of premiums and discounts ("grade differentials") for delivering grain of a different grade (e.g., No. 1 or No. 3) that meets minimum quality standards. The contract also specifies a set of approved delivery points, typically licensed warehouses or shipping stations along major transportation corridors (e.g., the Illinois River and other points near Chicago for CBOT grains). A trader who holds a short futures position to expiration without offsetting it may be required to deliver grain of the par grade (or an allowable substitute, adjusted by the grade differential) at one of these delivery points; a trader holding a long position may be required to accept it.

Remark - Why Delivery Rarely Happens, But Still Matters

In practice, the overwhelming majority of futures positions are offset before expiration rather than settled by physical delivery - delivery is operationally cumbersome and most market participants (speculators, index funds, and even many commercial hedgers) have no interest in actually moving grain through an exchange-approved warehouse. Nevertheless, the possibility of delivery is what anchors the futures price to the cash price at the delivery points: if the futures price diverged too far from the cash price of deliverable-grade grain at an approved location, an arbitrageur could profit by buying (or selling) cash grain, taking the offsetting futures position, and making (or taking) delivery. This convergence-by-arbitrage is why futures and local cash prices, while not identical, are tightly linked.

Defining Basis

For a producer or merchandiser at a specific physical location, the relevant price is not the futures price quoted on the exchange but the local cash price - what an elevator will actually pay for grain delivered to its facility, or what a processor will pay for grain delivered to its plant. The relationship between these two prices is basis.

Definition - Basis

The basis at a given location and time is defined as: $$\text{Basis} = \text{Local Cash Price} - \text{Futures Price}$$ using the futures contract conventionally referenced for that location and time period (often, though not always, the nearby contract month). Basis is typically quoted as a number of cents per bushel, and is negative ("under") when the local cash price is below the futures price - the more common case for locations far from delivery points or export terminals, reflecting the cost of transporting grain to a delivery point or end market. Basis can be positive ("over") in locations with strong local demand, such as near a processing plant or export terminal during periods of tight local supply.

Remark - What Drives Basis

Basis reflects, primarily, the cost and availability of transportation from the local market to the relevant delivery point or end-use market (freight rates, rail car availability, river barge conditions), and secondarily, local supply and demand conditions - a nearby ethanol plant or feedlot bidding aggressively for grain will tend to strengthen (make less negative, or more positive) the local basis, while a large local harvest with limited storage capacity will tend to weaken it.

Basis also has its own seasonal pattern, generally distinct from the seasonal pattern of outright futures prices: basis tends to be weakest (most negative) at harvest, when local supply is most abundant relative to elevator storage capacity and farmers are selling into the cash market, and tends to strengthen through the marketing year as local supplies are drawn down.

Basis Risk

A producer who sells futures to hedge an anticipated harvest, or a processor who buys futures to hedge anticipated grain purchases, has hedged their exposure to the futures price - but their actual economic exposure is to the local cash price. The difference between these two exposures is basis risk.

Definition - Basis Risk

Basis risk is the risk that the basis at the time a hedge is lifted (and the corresponding cash transaction occurs) differs from the basis assumed when the hedge was placed. A producer who hedges by selling futures locks in the futures price component of their eventual sale price, but the cash price they actually receive equals (futures price at the time of sale) + (basis at the time of sale) - and if basis weakens between when the hedge was placed and when the cash sale occurs, the producer's effective sale price is lower than anticipated, even though the futures price itself may be unchanged or favourable.

Worked Example - Basis and a Producer's Effective Price

Example - Hedging a Corn Harvest

In June, a corn farmer in central Illinois expects to harvest 50,000 bushels in October. The December corn futures contract is trading at $4.60 per bushel. Based on historical patterns, the farmer expects the local harvest-time basis (cash minus December futures, at the farmer's local elevator) to be approximately $-$0.35 per bushel - i.e., the local cash price at harvest is typically about 35 cents under the December futures price.

To hedge, the farmer sells 10 December corn futures contracts (10 $\times$ 5,000 bushels = 50,000 bushels) at $4.60. The farmer's expected effective sale price, before any basis surprise, is: $$\text{Expected price} = F_{Dec} + \text{Basis} = 4.60 + (-0.35) = \$4.25 \text{ per bushel}$$

Suppose that by October, December futures have fallen to $4.20 (a $0.40 decline), but because the local harvest was unusually large and elevator space is tight, the actual local basis weakens to $-$0.50 (10 cents weaker than expected).

The farmer sells cash corn locally at $4.20 - 0.50 = \$3.70$ per bushel, and offsets (buys back) the futures position, gaining $4.60 - 4.20 = \$0.40$ per bushel on the hedge. The combined effective price is: $$\text{Effective price} = 3.70 + 0.40 = \$4.10 \text{ per bushel}$$

The futures hedge successfully offset the $0.40 decline in the outright price level - without it, the farmer would have realized only $3.70. But the effective price of $4.10 is 15 cents below the originally expected $4.25, entirely attributable to the 15-cent basis surprise ($-$0.50 actual vs. $-$0.35 expected). This illustrates the central fact about futures hedging: it eliminates outright price risk but leaves the hedger fully exposed to basis risk.
