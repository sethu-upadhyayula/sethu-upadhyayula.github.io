2.2 Calendar Spreads and the New-Crop/Old-Crop Transition

A calendar spread (also called an inter-month or time spread) is a position consisting of offsetting long and short positions in futures contracts of the same commodity but different delivery months. Calendar spreads are traded both as a way to express a view on the relative supply/demand balance between two periods, and as the natural instrument for "rolling" a hedge or speculative position from one contract month to the next as expiration approaches.

Calendar Spread Mechanics

Definition — Calendar Spread

A calendar spread is conventionally quoted as the price difference between a nearby contract and a deferred contract: $$\text{Spread} = F(t,T_1) - F(t,T_2), \qquad T_1 < T_2$$ A trader who is "long the spread" buys the nearby contract and sells the deferred contract, profiting if the nearby contract's price rises relative to the deferred contract's price (the spread widens in the nearby contract's favour, or equivalently the deferred contract's discount to the nearby contract grows, or its premium shrinks). A trader who is "short the spread" takes the opposite position.

Remark — Spreads and the Cost-of-Carry Relationship

Recall from Section 1.2 that $F(t,T) \approx S_t e^{(r+u-y)(T-t)}$. For two contracts on the same underlying with maturities $T_1 < T_2$: $$F(t,T_2) - F(t,T_1) \approx F(t,T_1)\left[(r+u-y)(T_2-T_1)\right]$$ so the calendar spread between two contracts is approximately the cost of carrying the commodity from $T_1$ to $T_2$ — financing and storage costs less convenience yield, applied to the intervening period. A widening of the deferred contract's premium over the nearby (the spread moving toward "full carry") signals that the market is willing to pay to store the commodity for that additional period — i.e., $y$ is low relative to $r+u$. A move toward backwardation (the nearby trading at a premium to the deferred, or the deferred's premium shrinking) signals rising convenience yield — the market places increasing value on having supply available now rather than later.

The New-Crop / Old-Crop Spread

The most economically significant calendar spread in grain markets is the spread between the last contract month before a new harvest becomes deliverable (the "old-crop" contract) and the first contract month after the new harvest (the "new-crop" contract). For corn, this is typically the July/December spread; for soybeans, the July/November spread.

Definition — New-Crop / Old-Crop Spread

The old-crop contract represents supply available from the current (already-harvested) crop, while the new-crop contract represents supply that will become available only after the upcoming harvest. The old-crop/new-crop spread therefore reflects the market's assessment of how tight the remaining old-crop supply is relative to the size and timing of the next harvest. A large positive old-crop premium (old-crop trading well above new-crop) signals that the market expects old-crop stocks to be drawn down to critically low levels before the new harvest arrives — an "inversion" that rations remaining supply by making it expensive to hold old-crop grain rather than wait for the new crop.

Remark — Drivers of the New-Crop/Old-Crop Spread

The new-crop/old-crop spread is driven by two largely independent sets of information: (1) the current marketing year's supply/demand balance, which determines how tight old-crop stocks are likely to be as the next harvest approaches, and (2) expectations for the size of the upcoming harvest, which determine how much relief the new crop is expected to provide. A worsening old-crop balance (e.g., stronger-than-expected export demand drawing down old-crop stocks, as might be revealed in a WASDE report) tends to push the spread toward old-crop premium (inversion). Conversely, an improving outlook for the new crop (e.g., favourable planting weather, larger-than-expected planted acreage from the Acreage report) tends to push the spread toward new-crop premium, or at least dampen any old-crop inversion, since the market anticipates that the supply squeeze will be relieved relatively soon.

Because these two information sets are updated on different schedules — old-crop balance information arrives via monthly WASDE reports and weekly export sales data throughout the marketing year, while new-crop information arrives via Prospective Plantings (March), Acreage (June), weekly Crop Progress reports (through the growing season), and ultimately the harvest itself — the new-crop/old-crop spread evolves continuously as each new piece of information shifts the relative balance of the two stories.

Worked Example — Pricing and P&L on a Calendar Spread

Example — July/December Corn Spread

In April, July corn futures (old-crop) trade at $4.80 per bushel and December corn futures (new-crop) trade at $4.55 per bushel. The spread (July $-$ December) is: $$\text{Spread}_{\text{Apr}} = 4.80 - 4.55 = +\$0.25 \text{ (July at a 25-cent premium)}$$

A trader believes that old-crop stocks will tighten further over the coming months (perhaps due to strong export demand reported in successive WASDE updates), while the new crop is shaping up to be average-sized. The trader puts on a spread position: long 1 July contract, short 1 December contract (5,000 bushels each) — "long the spread."

Over the following two months, July rises to $5.10 while December rises only to $4.65 (the broader corn market rallied on a weather scare, but the rally was concentrated in the old-crop contract due to tightening old-crop stocks). The new spread is: $$\text{Spread}_{\text{June}} = 5.10 - 4.65 = +\$0.45$$

The spread widened by $0.45 - 0.25 = \$0.20$ per bushel. On a 5,000-bushel position, the spread trade's P&L is: $$\text{P\&L} = 0.20 \times 5{,}000 = \$1{,}000$$

Note that this gain was realized even though both legs of the spread moved in the same direction (both rose) — the spread trader's P&L depends only on the relative move between the two contracts, not on the direction of the broader market. This is the defining characteristic of spread trading: it isolates a view on relative value (here, the tightness of old-crop relative to new-crop supply) while substantially reducing exposure to the outright direction of the overall price level, which is instead driven by macro factors, energy market linkages (for corn, via ethanol), and broad risk sentiment that affect both contract months similarly.
