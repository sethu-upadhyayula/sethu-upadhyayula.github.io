3.1 Options on Agricultural Futures

Options on agricultural futures are exchange-traded American-style options whose underlying is a futures contract rather than the physical commodity itself. They are widely used by producers and end users to construct price floors and ceilings, by merchandisers to manage option-embedded features of forward contracts (such as "min-price" or "max-price" contracts offered to farmers), and by speculators to express views on both the direction and the volatility of grain prices — particularly around the scheduled USDA report dates discussed in Section 1.1.

American-Style Exercise on a Futures Underlying

Definition — Option on a Futures Contract

A call (put) option on an agricultural futures contract gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to enter a long (short) position in the underlying futures contract at the strike price $K$, at any time up to and including the option's expiration. Agricultural futures options are American-style: they may be exercised on any business day prior to expiration, not only at expiration. Upon exercise, the holder receives a futures position at price $K$ together with a cash variation margin payment equal to the difference between $K$ and the prevailing futures price — economically equivalent to immediately receiving the intrinsic value of the option in cash while simultaneously establishing a futures position at the current market price.

Remark — Early Exercise and Assignment

Because the underlying is a futures contract — which itself has zero cost to enter (aside from margin) and pays no dividend or carry to the holder — the standard arguments against early exercise of American options on non-dividend-paying stocks do not straightforwardly apply. In particular, for a deep in-the-money American put on a futures contract, early exercise can be optimal: exercising immediately converts the option's intrinsic value into a cash variation margin payment today, which can then earn interest, whereas holding the option forgoes that interest. The same logic can favour early exercise of deep in-the-money calls when interest rates are positive. As a result, American options on futures can trade at a premium to their European counterparts that is more pronounced than is typical for equity options.

From the perspective of an option writer, assignment — being on the other side of an exercised option — results in receiving the opposite futures position (a short futures position if a long call you wrote is exercised, for example) plus the corresponding cash variation margin payment. Because exercise can occur on any day, option writers carry ongoing assignment risk for the life of the position, in contrast to European-style options where this risk exists only at expiration.

Volatility Patterns Around USDA Report Dates

The scheduled-information structure of grain markets, discussed in Section 1.1, produces a distinctive and well-documented pattern in implied volatility term structure: implied volatility for options expiring shortly after a major USDA report (particularly WASDE, Acreage, and Prospective Plantings) tends to be elevated relative to options with similar time-to-expiration but no intervening report, and tends to decline sharply — to "crush" — once the report is released and its information is absorbed.

Remark — Term Structure Effects and Trading Implications

This pattern creates a kink in the implied volatility term structure: an option expiring the day after a major report will typically show meaningfully higher implied volatility than an otherwise-similar option expiring the day before. Traders sometimes characterize this as the market "pricing in" the report — the elevated implied volatility reflects the market's assessment of how large a price move the report could plausibly trigger.

A common speculative structure designed to harvest this pattern is a short strangle or short straddle established just before a report release and closed immediately after, profiting if the realized price move is smaller than what the elevated pre-report implied volatility had priced in — though this strategy carries substantial tail risk, since WASDE and similar reports occasionally produce moves that are large even relative to elevated pre-report volatility expectations (a "fat tail" event). Conversely, a long strangle or straddle into the report is a way to position for an outsized move without taking a directional view, at the cost of needing the realized move to exceed what was already priced into the elevated pre-report implied volatility.

Worked Example — Pricing a Pre-Report Straddle

Example — December Corn Options Ahead of a WASDE Release

December corn futures are trading at $4.60 two trading days before a WASDE release. At-the-money (strike = $4.60) call and put options expiring shortly after the report are quoted with the following premiums:

Premium (cents/bu)Value per contract (5,000 bu)
$4.60 Call14.0$700
$4.60 Put13.5$675
Straddle total27.5$1,375

The straddle premium of 27.5 cents represents the option market's break-even estimate of the magnitude of the move the futures price needs to make (in either direction) by expiration for the straddle buyer to profit. As a rough rule of thumb, this is consistent with the market pricing in roughly a $\pm 6\%$ move in the futures price ($27.5 / 460 \approx 6.0\%$) — substantially larger than a "normal" two-day move, reflecting the elevated implied volatility ahead of the report.

Suppose the WASDE report is released and turns out to be close to consensus expectations — no major surprise to ending stocks. December futures move only modestly, to $4.65. Immediately after the report, implied volatility collapses back toward its normal level, and the same straddle (now with futures at $4.65, so the original $4.60 strike has 5 cents of intrinsic value in the call) might be quoted at, say, 16 cents total — a loss of 11.5 cents (27.5 $-$ 16.0) for a straddle buyer, or a gain of 11.5 cents for a straddle seller, despite the underlying having moved. This illustrates that pre-report option strategies are bets on the magnitude of the move relative to what was priced in, not simply on whether the underlying moves at all.
