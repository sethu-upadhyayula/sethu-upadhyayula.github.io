1.2 Seasonality, Storage Economics, and Soft Commodities

Unlike oil or industrial metals, where production can in principle be adjusted relatively continuously in response to price signals, grain and oilseed production occurs in discrete annual (or, accounting for Southern Hemisphere crops, semi-annual) cycles dictated by the calendar of planting and harvest. This produces a highly characteristic seasonal pattern in price levels, volatility, and the shape of futures curves - and the storage economics that connect spot and futures prices within a crop year are central to understanding it.

The Annual Crop Cycle

Remark - Planting, Growing, and Harvest

Planting season (spring for U.S. row crops): prices are sensitive to planting intentions and early-season weather. A wet, cold spring that delays planting can push farmers toward shorter-season varieties or, in extreme cases, reduce total planted acreage - both of which affect the eventual harvest.

Growing season (summer): typically the period of highest price volatility, since the size of the eventual harvest remains uncertain and is acutely sensitive to weather during critical reproductive windows - drought or extreme heat during corn pollination (silking) or soybean pod-set can sharply reduce yields with no opportunity to recover later in the season.

Harvest (autumn for U.S. row crops): as the crop is harvested and supply uncertainty resolves, the "harvest low" often appears - a seasonal price decline as the new supply becomes available and farmers sell into the cash market to generate revenue and free up storage. Futures curves frequently exhibit a pattern where new-crop contracts (for delivery after harvest) trade at a discount to old-crop contracts (for delivery before the new harvest is available), reflecting the step-change in available supply at harvest time.

Storage Costs and Convenience Yield

The cost-of-carry framework used for general commodities applies with particular force to grains, where the shape of the futures curve within a single crop year directly reflects the economics of carrying inventory from one harvest to the next.

Definition - Cost-of-Carry with Convenience Yield

For a storable commodity with spot price $S_t$, the futures price for delivery at $T$ satisfies: $$F(t,T) \approx S_t\, e^{(r + u - y)(T-t)}$$ where $r$ is the risk-free financing rate, $u$ is the proportional physical storage cost (warehousing, elevator fees, insurance), and $y$ is the convenience yield - the non-monetary benefit accruing to holders of the physical commodity (e.g., the ability to keep a mill or feedlot running without interruption regardless of how tight futures-implied supply appears). When $y < r+u$, the curve is in contango (full or partial carry); when $y > r+u$, the curve is in backwardation, and the commodity is said to be in "tight" or "scarce" supply.

Example - Carry Within a Crop Year

Immediately after a large harvest, grain elevators are full and the convenience yield on holding additional inventory is low - there is little marginal benefit to holding extra physical grain when supply is abundant and readily available. The futures curve for contracts maturing later in the same crop year typically reflects close to full carry, i.e. $y \approx 0$ and $F(t,T) \approx S_t e^{(r+u)(T-t)}$.

As the crop year progresses and stocks are drawn down toward the next harvest, the convenience yield $y$ rises - holders of physical grain place increasing value on having supply on hand to meet processing or feeding needs without relying on the (increasingly uncertain) availability of new-crop supply. If stocks become tight enough late in the crop year - an "old-crop/new-crop" supply squeeze - the nearby (old-crop) contract can move into backwardation relative to the new-crop contract, reflecting $y > r+u$ and signalling that the market is rationing scarce remaining old-crop supply by bidding up its price relative to the futures price for grain that won't be available until after the next harvest.

Remark - Seasonal Pattern Across, Not Just Within, Crop Years

Because each crop year begins with a harvest and ends just before the next one, the convenience yield - and hence the shape of the futures curve - has a structural seasonal component superimposed on top of year-to-year variation driven by the overall supply/demand balance. In a year with a large harvest and ample carryover stocks, the curve tends to stay in carry (contango) throughout the crop year. In a year with a small harvest or strong demand drawing down stocks rapidly, the curve can move into backwardation well before the next harvest, and the magnitude of that backwardation is itself informative about how tight the market perceives supply to be.

Weather as the Dominant Supply Shock

Remark - Why Weather Dominates Agricultural Volatility

Because there is essentially one opportunity per year (per hemisphere) to produce a given crop, weather events during critical growth windows - drought during pollination, excess rain delaying planting or harvest, an early frost - can have outsized and immediate effects on price. Unlike oil, where OPEC+ can adjust output, or shale producers can drill additional wells in response to price signals within months, there is no possibility of a rapid supply response to a weather-driven shortfall mid-season: the crop that is damaged is the crop that will be harvested. This makes agricultural futures and options markets highly responsive to weather forecasts during the growing season, and explains why weather-related news (e.g., a forecast shift toward or away from rain in the U.S. Corn Belt, or a frost warning for Brazilian coffee regions) is among the most reliable drivers of short-term price volatility in these markets.

Soft Commodities: Coffee, Sugar, and Cotton

The "softs" - coffee, sugar, cocoa, and cotton - share the seasonality and weather sensitivity of grains, but their production is concentrated in tropical and subtropical regions, and several have important cross-linkages to other markets that grains generally lack.

Remark - Characteristics of the Softs

Coffee (arabica traded on ICE in New York, robusta on ICE Europe in London) is produced primarily in Brazil, Vietnam, and Colombia. Arabica coffee trees are particularly sensitive to frost events in Brazil's growing regions (especially Minas Gerais); because coffee trees take several years to mature and a damaging frost can reduce productive capacity for multiple subsequent harvests, frost scares in Brazil's winter (June-August) are a recurring source of sharp price spikes.

Sugar markets are heavily influenced by Brazilian milling decisions: Brazilian sugar mills can flexibly allocate harvested sugarcane between producing raw sugar for export and producing ethanol for the domestic fuel market, depending on the relative prices of sugar and ethanol (which is itself linked to gasoline prices). This flexibility links global sugar prices to both food and energy market dynamics in a way that has no direct analogue in the grain complex.

Cotton demand is tied to global textile manufacturing and apparel consumption, with China, India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Vietnam representing major sources of mill demand, while production is concentrated among a smaller set of major growing countries (the U.S., India, China, Brazil, and Pakistan). Cotton therefore sits at the intersection of agricultural supply dynamics and global consumer demand cycles, making it sensitive to both weather in growing regions and macroeconomic conditions affecting textile demand.
