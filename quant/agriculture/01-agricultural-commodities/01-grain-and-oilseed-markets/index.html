1.1 Grain and Oilseed Markets

The grain and oilseed complex - corn, wheat, soybeans, and the soybean crush products - forms the largest and most liquid segment of agricultural derivatives markets. Each crop has a distinct demand profile, growing geography, and futures contract structure, but all three share a common institutional feature: their prices are punctuated by a regular calendar of government reports that resolve supply and demand uncertainty in discrete jumps.

The Major Row Crops

Definition - Corn, Wheat, and Soybeans

Corn is used primarily as livestock feed and, in the United States, as the principal feedstock for ethanol production - roughly a third of the U.S. corn crop is processed into ethanol. This creates a direct link between corn prices and energy markets via biofuel blending mandates (the Renewable Fuel Standard) and the relative economics of ethanol versus gasoline.

Wheat is grown in several distinct classes (hard red winter, hard red spring, soft red winter, white wheat, durum) across many growing regions globally - the U.S., Russia, Ukraine, the EU, Australia, Argentina, and Canada all contribute meaningfully to global supply. Because no single region dominates production, global wheat prices are sensitive to weather conditions across multiple geographically dispersed growing areas simultaneously, and to export policy actions (e.g., export bans) by major producing countries.

Soybeans are crushed into soybean meal (a high-protein animal feed) and soybean oil (used in cooking and, increasingly, as a biodiesel and renewable diesel feedstock). The relationship between the price of soybeans and the combined value of the meal and oil it yields is captured by the crush spread, conceptually analogous to the crack spread that relates crude oil to refined products.

Futures Contract Structure

Corn, soybeans, and wheat (Chicago SRW wheat, Kansas City HRW wheat, and Minneapolis spring wheat) all trade as standardized futures contracts on the CME Group (CBOT). While exact specifications vary by contract, several features are common across the complex.

Remark - Common Contract Features

Contract size: the standard CBOT grain contract represents 5,000 bushels. Prices are quoted in cents and quarter-cents per bushel, so a price move of one cent per bushel changes the value of one contract by $50.

Contract months: grain futures trade in a fixed cycle of delivery months tied to the crop calendar. For corn and soybeans, the principal months are March, May, July, September, and December (corn) or January, March, May, July, August, September, and November (soybeans), with the November contract for soybeans and the December contract for corn representing the first "new-crop" months after the U.S. harvest.

Crush spread quoting: the soybean crush is conventionally expressed as the dollar value of the products (meal and oil) from one bushel of soybeans, less the price of that bushel of soybeans - i.e., $\text{Crush} = (0.022 \times P_{meal} + 11 \times P_{oil}) - P_{soybeans}$, where the constants reflect the approximate yield of meal (in tons) and oil (in pounds) per bushel crushed, with each term expressed in consistent dollar units.

USDA Reports as Scheduled Market-Moving Events

Unlike most financial markets, where information arrives continuously, grain markets receive large discrete information shocks on a published schedule from the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA). Trading strategies, options positioning, and risk management around these dates are a central feature of the grain markets.

Definition - WASDE (World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates)

The WASDE is a monthly report, typically released around the 10th-12th of each month, providing the USDA's current estimates of production, consumption, exports, and ending stocks for major U.S. and global agricultural commodities. The report updates these "supply and demand balance sheets" in light of the latest data, and the resulting changes - particularly to projected ending stocks (the carryover inventory expected at the end of the marketing year) - are closely watched. A larger-than-expected cut to ending stocks is generally bullish (tighter supply relative to demand), while an unexpected increase is bearish. WASDE releases are among the most reliably volatility-inducing scheduled events in agricultural markets.

Definition - Acreage and Prospective Plantings Reports

The USDA's Prospective Plantings report, released at the end of March, surveys farmers about their planting intentions for the coming season - the first concrete, nationwide estimate of how many acres will be devoted to each crop. The Acreage report, released at the end of June after planting is largely complete, updates this with actual planted and harvested acreage. Because the eventual size of the harvest is, to a first approximation, planted acres times yield per acre, these reports directly anchor the market's expectations for total supply months before harvest begins.

Definition - Crop Progress Reports

Released weekly during the growing season (typically April through November for U.S. row crops), the Crop Progress report estimates the percentage of the crop in each state that has reached specific developmental milestones - percent planted, percent emerged, percent in specific reproductive stages (e.g., silking and denting for corn), and percent harvested - along with an overall crop condition rating (percent rated "good" or "excellent"). Comparing these figures to the prior year and to the five-year average lets the market assess in real time whether the season is running ahead of or behind a normal pace, and whether crop conditions are deteriorating or improving.

Remark - Implied Volatility Around Report Dates

Because WASDE and other major USDA releases are scheduled and resolve material supply/demand uncertainty, options on grain futures typically exhibit elevated implied volatility in the trading session immediately preceding a release, with volatility "crushing" - falling sharply - once the report is published and its information is absorbed into prices. This pattern is directly analogous to implied volatility behaviour around scheduled earnings announcements or central bank meetings in other asset classes, and option strategies (e.g., short straddles into the report, or calendar spreads that isolate the report-date volatility) are built around this predictable volatility term structure.

Worked Example - Reading a WASDE Surprise

Example - Ending Stocks Surprise

Suppose that ahead of a WASDE release, the average analyst estimate for U.S. corn ending stocks for the current marketing year is 1.45 billion bushels, and the prior month's WASDE estimate was also 1.45 billion bushels (i.e., the market expects no change). The new WASDE report instead shows ending stocks of 1.30 billion bushels - a reduction driven by an upward revision to export demand.

This 150-million-bushel downward surprise to ending stocks represents a meaningful tightening of the stocks-to-use ratio (ending stocks as a percentage of total annual usage). All else equal, a tighter stocks-to-use ratio is associated with higher and more volatile prices, since there is less of a buffer to absorb any further negative supply shock (e.g., poor weather in South America during their growing season) before stocks become critically low. The futures market would typically react to this surprise with an immediate upward price move, with the magnitude depending on how far the actual figure deviated from the pre-report range of analyst estimates rather than simply the direction of the change.
