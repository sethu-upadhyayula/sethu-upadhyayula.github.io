4.1 Producer and End-User Hedging Strategies

Commercial participants in agricultural markets — farmers who produce grain, elevators and merchandisers who store and transport it, and processors and feedlots who consume it — face price exposures that arise naturally from their physical business operations. Hedging programs use futures and options to offset these exposures, converting an otherwise volatile revenue or cost stream into one that is more predictable, without requiring the participant to take a view on the direction of prices.

The Producer's Hedge: Selling Forward an Anticipated Crop

Definition — Short Hedge

A producer who will have grain to sell in the future faces the risk that prices fall before the sale occurs. A short hedge — selling futures contracts (or buying put options) against the anticipated production — offsets this risk: if cash prices fall, the loss on the eventual cash sale is offset by a gain on the short futures position (or by exercising the put). The producer thereby locks in (with a futures hedge) or sets a floor on (with a put option) the price to be received, subject to the basis risk discussed in Section 2.1.

The End User's Hedge: Buying Forward an Anticipated Need

Definition — Long Hedge

A processor or feedlot operator who will need to purchase grain in the future faces the opposite risk — that prices rise before the purchase occurs. A long hedge — buying futures contracts (or buying call options) against anticipated purchase needs — offsets this risk: if cash prices rise, the higher cost of the eventual cash purchase is offset by a gain on the long futures position (or by exercising the call), locking in or setting a ceiling on the price to be paid.

Hedge Ratios

In the simplest case — where the hedger's cash exposure is denominated in the same units as the futures contract and basis risk is ignored — a "1:1" hedge (hedging each bushel of cash exposure with futures covering one bushel) is a natural starting point. In practice, hedge ratios are often adjusted to reflect the statistical relationship between cash and futures price changes, or to reflect a deliberate choice to hedge only a portion of the exposure.

Definition — Minimum-Variance Hedge Ratio

If $\Delta S$ denotes the change in the cash price and $\Delta F$ the change in the futures price over the hedging horizon, the variance-minimizing hedge ratio $h^*$ — the number of futures units held per unit of cash exposure — is: $$h^* = \rho_{S,F}\,\frac{\sigma_S}{\sigma_F}$$ where $\sigma_S$ and $\sigma_F$ are the standard deviations of $\Delta S$ and $\Delta F$, and $\rho_{S,F}$ is their correlation. When cash and futures prices move in lockstep (the basis is constant, so $\rho_{S,F}=1$ and $\sigma_S = \sigma_F$), $h^* = 1$ — a full 1:1 hedge. When the cash and futures markets are less than perfectly correlated (e.g., hedging a commodity with a related but not identical futures contract, such as hedging milo with corn futures, or hedging a specialty crop with the futures of a related commodity), $h^*$ will generally differ from 1, and the hedge will be imperfect even setting aside ordinary basis risk.

Worked Example — An Elevator's Hedging Program

Example — Hedging Purchased Grain Pending Resale

A grain elevator buys 100,000 bushels of corn from local farmers at a price reflecting the local cash market (futures plus basis), intending to hold the grain in storage and resell it to a processor one month later. Between the purchase and the resale, the elevator is "long" 100,000 bushels of cash corn and is exposed to a price decline.

At the time of purchase, December futures are at $4.50 and the local basis is $-$0.20, so the elevator's purchase price is $4.50 - 0.20 = \$4.30$ per bushel, for a total cost of $430,000.

To hedge this inventory position, the elevator sells 20 December futures contracts (20 $\times$ 5,000 = 100,000 bushels) at $4.50 — a short hedge against its long cash position.

One month later, the elevator sells the physical corn to a processor. Suppose December futures have fallen to $4.30, and the local basis has strengthened slightly to $-$0.15 (perhaps due to the processor's demand). The elevator sells the cash corn at $4.30 - 0.15 = \$4.15$ per bushel, for total proceeds of $415,000 — a loss of $15,000 on the cash position relative to its purchase cost.

On the hedge, the elevator bought back its short futures position at $4.30 versus the $4.50 sale price, for a gain of $0.20 per bushel $\times$ 100,000 bushels = $20,000. The combined result is a net gain of $20,000 - 15,000 = \$5,000$ — the elevator not only protected its inventory value but actually profited slightly, because the local basis strengthened by 5 cents (from $-$0.20 to $-$0.15) over the holding period, a gain that is unrelated to the outright price decline and that the futures hedge does nothing to offset or create. This illustrates that even a well-executed futures hedge leaves the hedger with a residual exposure to basis — sometimes favourable, sometimes unfavourable — that is the essence of basis risk introduced in Section 2.1.

Combining Futures and Options: The Hedge with a Floor

Remark — Producer Hedging with Put Options

A pure futures short hedge locks in a price — eliminating both downside risk and upside potential. Many producers instead prefer to buy put options, which set a price floor while preserving the ability to benefit if prices rise: if prices fall below the put's strike, the put's payoff offsets the cash market loss (subject to the put premium paid); if prices rise, the producer lets the put expire worthless and sells the physical crop at the higher prevailing cash price, less only the premium already paid. The cost of this flexibility is the option premium, which is paid regardless of outcome — effectively the cost of insurance against the downside scenario. Many commercial hedging programs use a mix of futures (for the portion of expected production the producer wants to lock in unconditionally) and options (for additional production where the producer wants to retain upside participation), adjusted as the season progresses and production estimates firm up.
