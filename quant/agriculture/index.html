Agriculture

Agricultural commodities - grains, oilseeds, and "softs" such as coffee, sugar, and cotton - are produced through biological processes governed by planting and harvest cycles, and are therefore subject to a degree of seasonality and weather sensitivity unmatched by metals or even most energy commodities. These notes develop the institutional, pricing, and risk-management framework needed to understand agricultural futures, options, and the markets that connect farmers, elevators, processors, and financial participants.

We begin with the fundamentals of grain, oilseed, and soft commodity markets: contract specifications, the USDA reports that drive price discovery, seasonal planting-growing-harvest cycles, and the storage economics (convenience yield) that shape the shape of agricultural futures curves.

The second chapter covers futures contract specifications, basis, and calendar spread trading - the practical tools that connect cash and futures markets and that drive the economics of grain merchandising. The third chapter turns to options on agricultural futures and the weather derivatives market used to transfer crop risk. The fourth and final chapter addresses producer and end-user hedging strategies, position limits, commodity index investment, and the broader market structure of agricultural derivatives. Together these four chapters provide a comprehensive foundation for understanding how price risk is created, transferred, and managed in agricultural markets.
