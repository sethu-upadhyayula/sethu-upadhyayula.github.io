1.1 Tranche Structure

Tranching is the financial engineering technique at the heart of all structured credit products. By ordering who absorbs losses first, a single portfolio of risky assets can be restructured into liabilities spanning the full spectrum from highly-leveraged equity to near risk-free super-senior notes.

Attachment and Detachment Points

Definition — Tranche, Attachment, and Detachment

A tranche is characterised by its attachment point $A$ and detachment point $D$ (with $0 \leq A < D \leq 1$), expressed as fractions of the total portfolio notional $N$. The tranche absorbs losses $L(t)$ from the portfolio as follows: $$\text{Tranche Loss}(t) = \min(L(t), D \cdot N) - \min(L(t), A \cdot N) = [\min(L(t), D) - \min(L(t), A)] \cdot N$$ The tranche notional is $(D - A) \cdot N$. The tranche is written down as portfolio losses exceed $A$ and is completely wiped out once losses reach $D$.

Tranche Hierarchy

Definition — Standard iTraxx / CDX Tranche Structure

For the iTraxx Europe index (125 names, recovery 40%), the standard synthetic CDO tranches are:

  • Equity (0-3%): first-loss piece; highly leveraged, absorbs the first 3% of portfolio losses. Typically retained by the structurer or sold to hedge funds.
  • Mezzanine (3-6%, 6-9%): intermediate tranches with moderate credit risk. Investment grade if correlation is high enough.
  • Senior (9-12%, 12-22%): low probability of loss; rated AA/AAA.
  • Super-Senior (22-100%): last-loss; protected by all other tranches. Near risk-free in benign scenarios but devastated in systemic crises.

Expected Tranche Loss

Definition — Expected Tranche Loss

The expected loss of a tranche $[A, D]$ over horizon $T$ is: $$EL_{[A,D]}(T) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Tranche Loss}(T)] = \mathbb{E}[\min(L(T),D)] - \mathbb{E}[\min(L(T),A)]$$ where $L(T)$ is the portfolio fractional loss at time $T$. Defining the expected shortfall function: $$e(K) \equiv \mathbb{E}[\min(L,K)] = K - \int_0^K F_L(x)\,dx = \int_0^K (1 - F_L(x))\,dx$$ where $F_L$ is the CDF of $L$, the tranche expected loss is simply $e(D) - e(A)$.

Tranche Delta and CS01

Definition — Tranche Delta

The tranche delta $\Delta_{[A,D]}$ measures the sensitivity of the tranche spread to a 1 bp parallel shift in all constituent CDS spreads: $$\Delta_{[A,D]} = \frac{\partial \text{Tranche Spread}}{\partial s_{\text{index}}}$$ Equivalently, in terms of notionals, delta is the index notional hedge required to delta-hedge one unit of tranche notional. The equity tranche has a very high delta (close to 1); the super-senior tranche has near-zero delta in normal market conditions but can spike dramatically during stress.

Example — Tranche Loss Payoff

A \$100m CDO portfolio (100 names, \$1m each, recovery 40%, so LGD = \$600k each). The equity tranche covers $[0\%, 3\%]$, notional \$3m.

If 3 names default (total loss = $3 \times \$600k = \$1.8m$): equity tranche absorbs \$1.8m fully; mezzanine unaffected.

If 6 names default (total loss = \$3.6m): equity tranche wiped out (\$3m loss), mezzanine absorbs \$0.6m.

If 20 names default (total loss = \$12m): equity wiped out (\$3m), mezzanine $[3\%,9\%]$ wiped out (\$6m), senior $[9\%,12\%]$ absorbs \$3m.

Remark — Correlation Sensitivity

Tranche values are extremely sensitive to default correlation. High correlation concentrates losses — making equity tranches safer (fewer names default together) but senior tranches more dangerous (if defaults are correlated, they can breach the senior attachment point). The equity tranche spread decreases with correlation; senior tranches widen with correlation. This is the central insight driving the base correlation framework and the correlation trading market.
