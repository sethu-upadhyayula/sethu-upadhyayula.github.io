1.3 CLO Modelling

A Collateralised Loan Obligation (CLO) is a cash flow CDO backed by a managed portfolio of leveraged loans. Unlike synthetic CDOs, CLOs involve actual loan purchases, active portfolio management, and a detailed cash flow waterfall that distributes interest and principal proceeds to liability holders in strict priority order.

CLO Structure Overview

Definition - CLO Liability Stack

A typical CLO issues the following tranches (in descending seniority):

  • AAA notes (Class A): 60-65% of capital structure; pays SOFR + ~130-160 bps; benefits from full subordination below.
  • AA notes (Class B): ~10%; pays SOFR + ~200-230 bps.
  • A notes (Class C): ~6%; pays SOFR + ~280-320 bps.
  • BBB notes (Class D): ~5%; pays SOFR + ~400-500 bps.
  • BB notes (Class E): ~4%; rated below investment grade; highest spread.
  • Equity (subordinated notes): ~8-10% of capital; unrated; receives residual cash flows; the CLO manager typically retains a portion.

Reinvestment Period and Portfolio Dynamics

Definition - Reinvestment Period

A CLO has two phases:

Reinvestment period (typically 4-5 years from closing): the CLO manager actively manages the portfolio. Principal proceeds from loan repayments, sales, and defaults (after recovery) are reinvested in new loans subject to eligibility criteria (minimum rating, diversification, industry concentration limits). The portfolio target par amount is maintained.

Amortisation period (post-reinvestment): principal proceeds are sequentially paid to noteholders in priority order (Class A first, then B, C, D, E, equity). No new purchases are made. The average life of the notes depends on the speed of loan amortisation and prepayment.

Cash Flow Waterfall

Definition - Interest Waterfall

Each payment period, interest proceeds are distributed in the following priority (simplified):

  1. Senior expenses (trustee, admin, rating agency fees).
  2. Class A interest.
  3. Class A/B Overcollateralisation (OC) Test: if failing, divert cash to repay Class A principal until the test passes.
  4. Class B interest.
  5. Class B/C OC Test (and IC test).
  6. Class C, D, E interest (each with their OC/IC tests).
  7. CLO manager subordinated management fee.
  8. Equity residual distributions.

OC and IC Coverage Tests

Definition - Overcollateralisation and Interest Coverage Tests

The Overcollateralisation (OC) ratio for tranche $k$ is: $$\text{OC}_k = \frac{\text{Collateral Par (adjusted for defaulted assets)}}{\text{Notional of Class } k \text{ and above}}$$ If $\text{OC}_k < \text{OC trigger}_k$, the waterfall diverts interest to pay down the most senior tranche until the test is cured or the notes are paid off.

The Interest Coverage (IC) ratio is: $$\text{IC}_k = \frac{\text{Interest Income from Collateral}}{\text{Interest Due on Class } k \text{ and above}}$$ IC tests protect note holders from collateral income shortfalls. Failing tests result in accelerated senior note amortisation, protecting senior noteholders at the expense of equity IRR.

CLO Equity IRR Analysis

Definition - Static CLO Equity IRR

In a static (no-reinvestment) CLO model, the equity IRR is the rate $r_E$ that equates the equity investment $E_0$ to the present value of equity cash flows: $$E_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{CF^{eq}_t}{(1+r_E)^t}$$ where $CF^{eq}_t$ = (total interest income - senior costs - note interest - manager fees) during the reinvestment period, plus residual principal at maturity after all notes are repaid. The equity IRR is sensitive to: default rate assumptions, recovery rates, prepayment speed (WAL), and the SOFR curve (since liabilities are floating).

Remark - Dynamic CLO Modelling

A full dynamic CLO model simulates the portfolio and waterfall over time: loan defaults are drawn from a correlated default model (often a one-factor model similar to the Gaussian copula), prepayments from an assumed CPR, and the manager's trading activity from reinvestment rules. Monte Carlo simulation across thousands of scenarios generates distributions of tranche losses, OC test failures, and equity IRRs, enabling proper credit analysis of CLO notes. Rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) each use proprietary versions of such simulation models.
