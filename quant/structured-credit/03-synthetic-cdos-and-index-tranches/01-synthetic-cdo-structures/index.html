3.1 Synthetic CDO Structures

A synthetic CDO achieves the same tranched risk transfer as a cash CDO without buying or selling the underlying bonds or loans. Instead, credit risk is transferred via a portfolio of credit default swaps (CDS), and tranches reference slices of the resulting portfolio loss distribution. This unfunded structure was the foundation of the index tranche market that grew rapidly through the mid-2000s.

CDS Portfolio Plus Tranching

Definition - Synthetic CDO via CDS Reference Portfolio

A synthetic CDO references a portfolio of $n$ single-name CDS (or a standardised CDS index), each with notional $N_i$, recovery rate $R_i$, and survival probability curve $Q_i(t)$. The portfolio loss process is: $$L(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{N_i(1-R_i)}{\sum_j N_j} \, \mathbb{1}\{\tau_i \leq t\}$$ where $\tau_i$ is the default time of name $i$. A tranche $[A,D]$ pays and receives cash flows based on $\text{Tranche Loss}(t) = [\min(L(t),D) - \min(L(t),A)] \times \text{Portfolio Notional}$, exactly as in Section 1.1, but with no exchange of the underlying notional - only premium and protection legs referencing the tranche notional change hands.

Premium and Protection Legs

Definition - Tranche Premium and Protection Legs

The protection buyer pays a periodic premium (the tranche spread $s_{[A,D]}$, plus possibly an upfront payment for equity tranches) on the outstanding tranche notional, which amortises as the tranche absorbs losses. The protection seller pays the protection buyer the incremental tranche loss as it occurs. The premium leg value is: $$\text{PV}_{\text{premium}} = s_{[A,D]} \sum_{k=1}^{m} \Delta_k \, Z(0,t_k) \, \mathbb{E}\big[(D-A) - \text{Tranche Loss}(t_k)\big]$$ and the protection leg value is: $$\text{PV}_{\text{protection}} = \sum_{k=1}^{m} Z(0,t_k) \, \mathbb{E}\big[\text{Tranche Loss}(t_k) - \text{Tranche Loss}(t_{k-1})\big]$$ where $Z(0,t)$ is the discount factor and $\Delta_k$ the accrual period. The fair spread $s_{[A,D]}$ equates the two legs, and the expectations are computed from the loss distribution implied by the chosen copula and correlation, as in Sections 1.2 and 2.1.

Attachment, Detachment, and Subordination

Remark - Subordination as Credit Enhancement

The attachment point $A$ represents the subordination available to a tranche - the fraction of portfolio losses that must occur before the tranche is affected at all. A protection seller on the $[6\%,9\%]$ tranche is exposed only once cumulative portfolio losses exceed 6%, and is wiped out once losses reach 9%. From the protection buyer's perspective, buying protection on a mezzanine or senior tranche is a capital-efficient way to hedge tail credit risk in a portfolio, since the premium reflects only the risk of losses within that specific loss layer rather than the full notional.

Single-Tranche CDOs (STCDOs)

Definition - Single-Tranche CDO

A single-tranche CDO (STCDO) is a bilateral trade in which a dealer sells protection on (or buys protection from) a client on a single tranche $[A,D]$ of a reference portfolio, without issuing or placing any other tranche of the capital structure. The dealer does not need to find investors for the rest of the stack - it manages the resulting exposure on its own book using the correlation-sensitive hedging techniques covered in Chapter 4.

Remark - Why STCDOs Drove the Index Tranche Market

Before STCDOs, a dealer wishing to sell a mezzanine tranche to a client had to place every other tranche of the same deal to complete the structure - a slow, capital-intensive process. STCDOs let dealers warehouse single-tranche exposures, hedge them dynamically with the underlying index CDS (delta hedging, Section 4.1), and quote tranches on demand at any attachment/detachment combination, including "bespoke" portfolios tailored to a client's holdings. This flexibility, combined with standardised index tranches on CDX and iTraxx (covered in Section 3.2), made the single-tranche format the dominant vehicle for correlation trading until the 2008 crisis sharply reduced liquidity in the bespoke market.
