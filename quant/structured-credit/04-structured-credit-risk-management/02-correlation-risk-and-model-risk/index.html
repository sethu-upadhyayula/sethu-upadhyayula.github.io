4.2 Correlation Risk and Model Risk

Once a tranche position is delta-hedged against the underlying index, its remaining risk is driven primarily by the correlation assumptions used to price it. Because correlation is not directly observable - it is implied from a small set of standard tranche quotes and then extrapolated or mapped to other attachment points and portfolios - tranche books carry significant correlation risk and model risk on top of their hedged spread exposure.

Correlation Sensitivity by Tranche Seniority

Definition - Tranche Correlation Sensitivity ("Rega" / Correlation Vega)

The sensitivity of a tranche value to a parallel shift $\Delta\rho$ in the base correlation curve, holding the index spread fixed, is: $$\text{Rega}_{[A,D]} = \frac{\partial V_{[A,D]}}{\partial \rho} \approx \frac{V_{[A,D]}(\rho+\Delta\rho) - V_{[A,D]}(\rho-\Delta\rho)}{2\Delta\rho}$$ Unlike delta, the sign and magnitude of correlation sensitivity vary sharply by seniority. An increase in correlation widens the loss distribution - more probability mass moves into the tails (very low and very high losses) and away from the middle. Equity tranches and senior tranches both gain value from higher correlation (more scenarios of very low loss for equity's attachment-zero floor, and fewer scenarios of catastrophic loss reaching senior attachment points reduce senior's expected loss less than one might think - but the net effect typically benefits equity and senior at the expense of mezzanine), while mezzanine tranches typically lose value, since higher correlation reduces the probability of losses landing precisely within the mezzanine's narrow loss layer.

Why Correlation Risk Cannot Be Hedged with the Index Alone

Remark - No Liquid Instrument for Pure Correlation Exposure

Delta hedging with the index CDS neutralises spread risk but does nothing to offset correlation risk, because the index itself has no tranching and therefore no correlation sensitivity. The only way to hedge correlation exposure on one tranche is to take an offsetting position in another tranche (e.g. a mezzanine-long, equity-short "correlation trade"), but this introduces basis risk: the two tranches may not move in lockstep if the base correlation curve shifts non-parallel (a change in the skew of the correlation curve rather than its level). A desk running a tranche book therefore typically monitors a "correlation skew" risk in addition to the parallel rega above - sensitivity to the slope and curvature of $\rho_{\text{base}}(D)$ across detachment points.

Model Risk in Structured Credit

Definition - Sources of Model Risk

Model risk in structured credit arises from several layers of assumption stacked on top of each other:

  1. Copula choice - the Gaussian copula (Section 1.2) and alternative factor copulas (Section 2.2) make different assumptions about tail dependence between defaults; the choice materially affects tranche prices, especially for senior tranches sensitive to joint extreme events.
  2. Correlation mapping for bespoke portfolios - mapping a bespoke portfolio's correlation from the standard index curve (Section 3.2) relies on assumptions about how differences in average spread or expected loss translate into correlation differences; errors compound for portfolios that differ significantly from the index composition.
  3. Recovery rate assumptions - tranche valuations assume fixed recovery rates per name, but realised recoveries are stochastic and correlated with the broader default cycle, an effect the standard model does not capture.
  4. Static vs. dynamic correlation - the base correlation framework recalibrates a static correlation parameter at each valuation date rather than modelling correlation as a stochastic process, so it can misprice instruments whose payoff depends on the path of correlation rather than just its terminal level.

Lessons from the 2008 Crisis

Example - Correlation Products in the 2008 Crisis

The 2008 financial crisis was a severe stress test for every assumption underlying the structured credit pricing framework, and the failures were instructive:

Correlation skew dislocation. As default correlations across the financial system rose sharply during the crisis, the base correlation curve itself shifted dramatically and non-parallel - senior tranche correlations spiked as the market began pricing systemic, all-or-nothing default scenarios that the pre-crisis curve had assigned negligible probability. Desks holding "correlation trades" (long mezzanine, short equity or senior, or vice versa) that looked delta-hedged and correlation-neutral under the old curve suffered large losses as the skew moved in ways the static base correlation framework had never been calibrated to anticipate.

Liquidity evaporation and mark-to-market spirals. As dealers attempted to de-risk simultaneously, bid-ask spreads on tranches widened enormously and bespoke tranches became essentially unmarkable, forcing desks to rely on extrapolated correlation mappings (Section 3.2) that had never been tested in such stressed regimes - compounding mark-to-market losses with valuation uncertainty.

Wrong-way risk and recovery assumptions. Recovery rates on defaulted names came in far lower than the standard 40% assumption used throughout the pricing framework, and defaults were correlated with the macro deterioration that was simultaneously widening spreads - a "wrong-way" interaction between recovery, correlation, and spread that the Gaussian copula with static recovery had no mechanism to represent.

The overarching lesson was that correlation and model risk are not small residual exposures left over after delta hedging - in a systemic crisis they can dominate P&L, and risk frameworks that treat the correlation surface as a slowly-varying, parallel-shifting curve will badly underestimate losses when the entire shape of that surface changes at once.
