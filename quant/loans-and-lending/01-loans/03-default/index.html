1.3 Default Modelling

Credit risk has three fundamental components: the Probability of Default (PD), the Loss Given Default (LGD), and the Exposure at Default (EAD). These combine to give Expected Loss — the central quantity in credit portfolio management, loan loss provisioning, and regulatory capital under Basel III/IV.

Expected Loss Framework

Definition — Expected Loss

$$EL = PD \times LGD \times EAD$$ where:

  • $PD$: probability the borrower defaults within a given horizon (typically 1 year for regulatory capital, lifetime for IFRS 9 provisioning)
  • $LGD = 1 - \text{Recovery Rate}$: fraction of EAD lost upon default, net of recoveries and workout costs
  • $EAD$: the drawn balance plus an estimate of undrawn commitments that will be drawn before default, via the Credit Conversion Factor (CCF): $EAD = \text{Drawn} + \text{CCF} \times \text{Undrawn}$

Merton Structural Model

Merton (1974) treats the firm's equity as a call option on its assets. Default occurs when the asset value falls below the face value of debt at maturity, establishing a rigorous link between capital structure and credit risk.

Theorem — Merton Model (1974)

Let firm asset value $V_t$ follow geometric Brownian motion under the risk-neutral measure: $$dV_t = r V_t\,dt + \sigma_V V_t\,dW_t$$ Debt has face value $F$ due at time $T$. By put-call parity on the assets, equity value is: $$E_0 = V_0\,\Phi(d_1) - F\,e^{-rT}\,\Phi(d_2)$$ where: $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(V_0/F)+(r+\sigma_V^2/2)T}{\sigma_V\sqrt{T}}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma_V\sqrt{T}$$ The risk-neutral probability of default is $PD^{RN} = \Phi(-d_2)$. The credit spread implied by the model is: $$s = -\frac{1}{T}\ln\!\left[\Phi(d_2) + \frac{V_0}{F\,e^{-rT}}\Phi(-d_1)\right]$$

Remark — Distance to Default

The quantity $d_2$ is called the distance to default: it measures how many standard deviations of asset returns separate the current asset level from the default barrier. Moody's KMV extended this concept to a commercial PD model by mapping $d_2$ to empirical default frequencies using a large historical database of firm defaults.

Reduced-Form Hazard Rate Model

Structural models require asset value observation, which is not directly available for private borrowers. Reduced-form models instead specify a stochastic default intensity (hazard rate) directly in terms of observable quantities.

Definition — Hazard Rate and Survival Probability

Let $\tau$ be the default time. The hazard rate $\lambda(t)$ is the instantaneous conditional default intensity: $$P(\tau \in [t, t+dt]\,|\,\tau > t) = \lambda(t)\,dt$$ The survival probability (probability of no default up to time $T$) is: $$Q(T) = P(\tau > T) = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^T \lambda(s)\,ds\right)$$ For a flat (constant) hazard rate $\lambda$: $Q(T) = e^{-\lambda T}$, and $PD(T) = 1 - e^{-\lambda T} \approx \lambda T$ for small $\lambda T$.

Definition — Risky Bond Pricing and Spread-Hazard Relationship

A defaultable zero-coupon bond paying 1 at maturity (with zero recovery) has risk-neutral price: $$P_{\text{risky}}(0,T) = e^{-(r+\lambda)T}$$ The hazard rate $\lambda$ acts as an additional discount rate for credit risk. With fractional recovery $R$ of face value at default: $$P_{\text{risky}}(0,T) \approx e^{-(r+\lambda(1-R))T}$$ The implied credit spread is $s \approx \lambda(1-R)$, giving the key market intuition: $$\lambda \approx \frac{s}{1-R} = \frac{\text{Spread}}{\text{LGD}}$$

Example — Bootstrapping a Hazard Rate

A 1-year corporate bond yields 5.5% against a 1-year OIS rate of 3.5%. Assumed recovery $R = 40\%$. $$s = 5.5\% - 3.5\% = 2.0\%$$ $$\lambda = \frac{s}{1-R} = \frac{0.02}{0.60} = 3.33\% \text{ per year}$$ Survival probability: $Q(1) = e^{-0.0333} \approx 96.7\%$. Annualised PD $\approx 3.3\%$.

For a multi-year bond, hazard rates are bootstrapped from successive maturity points on the credit curve, exactly as discount factors are bootstrapped from swap rates.

Remark — Basel IRB Capital Formula

Under the Basel Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) approach, regulatory capital is based on the Vasicek single-factor model. Asset returns are $A_i = \sqrt{\rho}\,M + \sqrt{1-\rho}\,\epsilon_i$ where $M$ is a systematic factor and $\epsilon_i$ is idiosyncratic. At the 99.9% confidence level the conditional default rate is $\Phi\!\left(\frac{\Phi^{-1}(PD)-\sqrt{\rho}\,\Phi^{-1}(0.999)}{\sqrt{1-\rho}}\right)$, and capital $= LGD \times (CDR_{99.9\%} - PD) \times EAD$, capturing unexpected loss above expected loss.
