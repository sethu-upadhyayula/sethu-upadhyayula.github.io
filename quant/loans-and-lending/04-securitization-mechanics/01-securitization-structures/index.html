4.1 Securitization Structures

Securitization is the process of pooling a portfolio of loans - mortgages, auto loans, credit card receivables, or corporate loans - and using that pool's cash flows to back securities sold to investors. Chapter 2 examined the cash-flow mechanics of mortgage-backed securities; this section steps back to the legal and structural building blocks that make any securitization possible: how the assets are transferred away from the originator, the vehicle that holds them, and how a single pool of collateral is carved into tranches of differing seniority and risk.

True Sale vs Synthetic Securitization

Definition - True Sale Securitization

In a true sale, the originator (e.g. a bank) sells a pool of loans outright to a special purpose vehicle (SPV). The assets are legally and economically removed from the originator's balance sheet: the SPV becomes the owner of the receivables, and investors in the SPV's notes have a direct claim on the cash flows generated by the underlying loans. True sale is the dominant structure for residential mortgage-backed securities (RMBS), auto loan ABS, and credit card ABS, and is the structure assumed implicitly in Chapter 2.

Definition - Synthetic Securitization

In a synthetic securitization, the underlying loans remain on the originator's balance sheet. Instead of selling the assets, the originator transfers only the credit risk of the pool to investors, typically via credit default swaps (CDS) or financial guarantees referencing the pool, with the SPV (if one is used) issuing credit-linked notes whose principal is reduced by losses on the reference pool. Synthetic structures are common for corporate loan portfolios and regulatory capital relief trades, where the originator wants to retain the loans (and the customer relationship) for funding or commercial reasons but offload the tail credit risk to reduce regulatory capital requirements.

Remark - Why the Distinction Matters

The choice between true sale and synthetic structures is driven largely by accounting and regulatory capital treatment. A true sale that meets "sale accounting" criteria (e.g. under US GAAP ASC 860 or IFRS 9 derecognition rules) removes the assets - and the associated regulatory capital requirement - from the originator's balance sheet entirely. A synthetic transaction keeps the assets on balance sheet (so funding is unaffected) but, if structured to transfer a "significant" portion of the credit risk under Basel rules, allows the originator to hold capital against a much smaller residual exposure. Both structures achieve risk transfer; they differ in whether funding risk is transferred as well.

The Special Purpose Vehicle

Definition - Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV)

A special purpose vehicle (also called a special purpose entity, SPE) is a legal entity - typically a trust or limited liability company - created solely to hold the securitized pool of assets and issue notes against it. The SPV has no employees, no other business activities, and minimal discretion: its activities are governed entirely by the transaction documents (the trust deed, indenture, and servicing agreement). Its defining legal feature is bankruptcy remoteness: the SPV is structured so that it cannot be consolidated into the originator's bankruptcy estate, and so that the SPV itself is extremely unlikely to become insolvent independent of the performance of its assets.

Remark - Achieving Bankruptcy Remoteness

Bankruptcy remoteness from the originator is achieved through several mechanisms working together: (1) the transfer of assets to the SPV is structured as a "true sale" in the legal sense, so the assets are not part of the originator's estate if the originator becomes insolvent; (2) the SPV's governing documents include restrictions on its activities ("separateness covenants") and often require an independent director whose consent is needed for any voluntary bankruptcy filing; (3) the SPV is typically thinly capitalized and prohibited from incurring additional debt beyond the securitization notes, minimizing its own insolvency risk. Legal opinions ("true sale" and "non-consolidation" opinions) are obtained from counsel to confirm these features hold under the relevant jurisdiction's law - this is a central part of the due diligence in any securitization.

Tranching and Subordination

Once the SPV owns the pool, it issues multiple classes ("tranches") of notes against the same pool of collateral. Tranches differ in seniority - the order in which they have a claim on cash flows and absorb losses - which is the central risk-transformation mechanism of securitization: a pool of individually risky loans can be repackaged into a senior tranche that is very safe and junior tranches that concentrate the risk.

Definition - Tranche Hierarchy

A typical securitization capital structure, from most to least senior, consists of:

  • Senior tranche - the largest tranche by face value, typically rated AAA, with first claim on collateral cash flows and last exposure to losses.
  • Mezzanine tranche(s) - one or more intermediate tranches (often rated AA through BB), absorbing losses after the equity tranche is exhausted but before the senior tranche is affected.
  • Equity (or "first-loss" / residual) tranche - the most junior tranche, often unrated and retained by the originator, which absorbs the first losses on the pool and receives any residual cash flow after all other tranches are paid.
Definition - Subordination and Credit Enhancement

Subordination refers to the ordering of loss allocation: losses are absorbed by the most junior outstanding tranche first, and only "write down" or impair a more senior tranche once all tranches junior to it have been completely written off. The subordination level (or "attachment point") of a tranche is the fraction of the pool's face value that lies below it in the capital structure - equivalently, the cumulative loss rate the pool must experience before that tranche begins to take losses. If a pool's total face value is $N$ and a tranche has attachment point $a$ and detachment point $d$ (with $0 \le a < d \le 1$, expressed as fractions of $N$), the tranche's loss given a pool loss rate $\ell$ is: $$L_{\text{tranche}} = \frac{\min(\max(\ell - a, 0), d-a)}{d - a} \times (d-a)N \Big/ (d-a)N = \frac{\min(\max(\ell-a,0),\,d-a)}{d-a}$$ i.e. the tranche loses nothing while $\ell \le a$, loses proportionally while $a < \ell < d$, and is wiped out once $\ell \ge d$. Subordination is the primary form of credit enhancement: by sizing the equity and mezzanine tranches to absorb a multiple of the pool's expected loss, the senior tranche can achieve a rating far higher than the average credit quality of the underlying loans.

Example - Tranche Loss Allocation

Consider a $\$500\text{m}$ pool securitized into three tranches: a senior tranche of $\$420\text{m}$ (84% of the pool, attachment point $a=0.16$, detachment $d=1.00$), a mezzanine tranche of $\$50\text{m}$ (10%, $a=0.06$, $d=0.16$), and an equity tranche of $\$30\text{m}$ (6%, $a=0$, $d=0.06$).

Suppose the pool experiences a cumulative loss rate of $\ell = 9\%$ (i.e. $\$45\text{m}$ of losses on the $\$500\text{m}$ pool).

  • Equity tranche ($a=0$, $d=0.06$): $\ell=9\% > d=6\%$, so the tranche is fully written off - a 100% loss of $\$30\text{m}$.
  • Mezzanine tranche ($a=0.06$, $d=0.16$): the loss fraction is $\frac{0.09-0.06}{0.16-0.06} = \frac{0.03}{0.10} = 30\%$, i.e. a $\$15\text{m}$ loss out of $\$50\text{m}$.
  • Senior tranche ($a=0.16$, $d=1.00$): $\ell=9\% < a=16\%$, so the senior tranche takes no loss - its $\$420\text{m}$ remains fully intact.

A pool loss rate of 9% - which would be a severe outcome for the underlying loans - translates into zero loss for the senior tranche, because the $16\%$ of subordination beneath it (equity plus mezzanine) absorbs the first $\$80\text{m}$ of losses entirely.

Other Forms of Credit Enhancement

Remark - Excess Spread and Overcollateralization

Subordination is usually supplemented by other enhancement mechanisms. Excess spread is the difference between the weighted-average interest rate earned on the pool and the weighted-average coupon paid to noteholders plus servicing fees; this surplus can be trapped and used to pay down notes or build a reserve fund if pool performance deteriorates. Overcollateralization exists when the face value of the collateral pool exceeds the face value of the notes issued against it, providing an additional cushion before any tranche is impaired. Many structures also include a reserve fund - cash held by the SPV, funded at closing or built up from excess spread - that can be drawn to cover shortfalls. Together with subordination, these mechanisms determine the rating agencies' assessment of how much loss each tranche can withstand, and hence the credit rating assigned to it.
