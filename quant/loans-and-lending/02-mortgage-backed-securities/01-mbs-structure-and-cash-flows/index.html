2.1 MBS Structure and Cash Flows

A pass-through mortgage-backed security is the simplest and most widely issued MBS structure. A trustee pools a large number of individual mortgage loans with similar characteristics (coupon, term, origination vintage) and issues certificates representing pro-rata claims on the pool's cash flows. Agency pass-throughs issued by Ginnie Mae, Fannie Mae, and Freddie Mac carry an explicit or implicit guarantee of timely payment of principal and interest, isolating investors from the credit risk discussed in Chapter 1 and leaving prepayment risk as the dominant driver of value.

Pool Formation and Pass-Through Mechanics

Definition — Pass-Through Security

A pass-through security entitles the holder to a pro-rata share of all cash flows generated by the underlying pool of mortgages: scheduled interest, scheduled principal amortisation, and unscheduled (prepaid) principal. If an investor holds a fraction $f$ of the pool's outstanding certificates, their cash flow in period $t$ is simply $f \times CF_t^{\text{pool}}$. No cash flow is redirected or reshaped — this is the key distinction from the tranched structures of Chapter 4.

Pool Cash Flow Mechanics

Each loan in the pool amortises according to the schedule developed in Section 1.1. At the pool level, the gross cash flow each period is the sum of scheduled interest, scheduled principal, and any unscheduled (prepaid) principal across all loans in the pool. The pass-through rate — the coupon paid to investors — is typically below the weighted average coupon (WAC) of the underlying loans, with the difference (the servicing strip and guarantee fee) retained by the servicer and guarantor.

Definition — Pool-Level Cash Flow Decomposition

For a pool with beginning-of-period balance $B_{t-1}$, weighted average coupon $c$, pass-through rate $c_p < c$, scheduled amortisation $SP_t$ (from the amortisation formula of Section 1.1 applied at the pool level), and prepayment rate $\text{SMM}_t$: $$\text{Interest to investors}_t = B_{t-1} \times c_p \times \frac{1}{12}$$ $$\text{Unscheduled Principal}_t = \text{SMM}_t \times (B_{t-1} - SP_t)$$ $$\text{Total Principal}_t = SP_t + \text{Unscheduled Principal}_t$$ $$B_t = B_{t-1} - \text{Total Principal}_t$$ Investors receive $\text{Interest}_t + \text{Total Principal}_t$ each period; both components flow through dollar-for-dollar (net of the servicing strip on interest).

Worked Example — Single Period Cash Flow

Example — Pool Cash Flow Projection

Consider a \$200m pool of 30-year mortgages with WAC of 6.0%, pass-through rate of 5.5% (50 bps servicing/guarantee strip), at month 24 of seasoning, prepaying at 150 PSA.

From Section 1.2, at month 24 (within the 30-month ramp), $\text{CPR}(24) = 6\% \times (24/30) \times 1.5 = 7.2\%$, so $\text{SMM} = 1-(1-0.072)^{1/12} \approx 0.622\%$.

Suppose scheduled principal for the month is $SP_t = \$350{,}000$ on the \$200m balance.

Unscheduled principal $= 0.622\% \times (\$200{,}000{,}000 - \$350{,}000) \approx \$1{,}241{,}300$.

Total principal $\approx \$1{,}591{,}300$. Interest to investors $= \$200{,}000{,}000 \times 5.5\% / 12 \approx \$916{,}667$.

Total cash flow to investors this month $\approx \$2{,}507{,}967$. The new pool balance is $\$200{,}000{,}000 - \$1{,}591{,}300 \approx \$198{,}408{,}700$, which becomes $B_t$ for the next period's calculation.

Weighted Average Life

Definition — Weighted Average Life (WAL)

The weighted average life of a pass-through is the average time to receipt of a dollar of principal, weighted by the size of each principal payment: $$\text{WAL} = \sum_{t} t \times \frac{\text{Total Principal}_t}{B_0}$$ where $t$ is measured in years and $B_0$ is the original pool balance. WAL is the single most important summary statistic for an MBS: it shortens as the prepayment speed (PSA) increases, since faster prepayment returns principal earlier.

Remark — Stripped MBS

A pass-through's interest and principal cash flows can be separated and sold to different investors as IO (interest-only) and PO (principal-only) strips. PO holders benefit from faster prepayment (principal returns sooner, raising the IRR on a security purchased at a discount), while IO holders are hurt by faster prepayment (the interest-bearing balance shrinks faster than expected). IO and PO strips therefore have opposite sensitivities to prepayment speed and are widely used to hedge MBS portfolios.
