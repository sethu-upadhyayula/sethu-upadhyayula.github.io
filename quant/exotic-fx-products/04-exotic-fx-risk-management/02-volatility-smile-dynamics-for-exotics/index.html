4.2 Volatility Smile Dynamics for Exotics

A vanilla option's value at a point in time depends on the current volatility smile - the implied volatilities across strikes for its expiry - but its hedge ratios (delta, vega) depend on how the smile is assumed to move as spot moves. For vanilla options this "smile dynamics" assumption has a second-order effect on hedging performance. For exotics, particularly barrier and digital-like products, the assumed smile dynamics can change the valuation itself by a significant amount, even holding the current smile fixed - making smile dynamics a first-order source of model risk for the products covered throughout this subject.

Sticky Strike vs. Sticky Delta

Definition - Sticky Strike and Sticky Delta

Two polar conventions describe how the implied volatility smile is assumed to evolve as spot moves from $S_0$ to $S_1$:

  • Sticky strike: the implied volatility for a given absolute strike $K$ remains unchanged - $\sigma(K, S_1) = \sigma(K, S_0)$. The smile, plotted against strike, does not move when spot moves.
  • Sticky delta (equivalently, sticky moneyness): the implied volatility for a given delta (or equivalently, a given ratio $K/S$) remains unchanged - the smile shifts with spot, so that the volatility applicable to "the 25-delta put" is the same before and after the spot move, even though the absolute strike corresponding to "25-delta put" has changed.
Remark - Why This Matters for Vanilla Delta Hedging

Under sticky strike, a vanilla option's implied volatility (for its fixed strike) does not change as spot moves, so its Black-Scholes delta computed today is the "right" delta in the sense that no additional vega-driven repricing occurs purely from the spot move. Under sticky delta, as spot moves, the implied volatility applicable to the option's fixed strike does change (because the option's moneyness/delta has changed, and the smile has shifted to keep volatility-by-delta constant) - this induces an additional contribution to the option's delta beyond the pure Black-Scholes delta, often called the "smile-adjusted delta" or "vanna-adjusted delta," reflecting the correlation between spot moves and volatility moves that the smile shape encodes (the FX market convention of quoting risk reversals, introduced in earlier chapters, is itself evidence that spot and volatility are correlated - typically a falling spot is associated with rising volatility for the corresponding side of the smile in many currency pairs).

Impact on Barrier and Digital Valuation

Remark - Barriers Are Especially Sensitive to Smile Dynamics

A barrier option's value depends on the probability of spot reaching the barrier $H$, which may be far from the current spot $S_0$ - i.e., it depends on the volatility applicable to a strike/level that is currently far out-of-the-money. Under sticky strike, this volatility (for the fixed level $H$) is assumed not to change as spot drifts towards $H$. Under sticky delta, as spot drifts towards $H$, the level $H$ becomes progressively closer to at-the-money (in moneyness terms), and the volatility "attached" to $H$ under the sticky-delta convention is whatever the at-the-money-ish volatility is at that time - which, given a typical smile shape, may be quite different from the volatility originally attached to $H$ when it was far out-of-the-money. Two models that perfectly agree on the current smile, but differ in their assumed dynamics (sticky strike vs. sticky delta vs. something in between, such as a local volatility or stochastic volatility model with its own implied dynamics), can therefore produce materially different prices and - more importantly for a hedger - materially different hedge ratios (delta, vega) for the same barrier option.

The Vanna-Volga method introduced in earlier sections implicitly embeds an assumption about smile dynamics through its replication of vanna and volga exposures; local volatility models imply a smile dynamic close to (but not exactly) sticky strike for short-dated moves; and stochastic volatility models (e.g., SABR, Heston) imply dynamics that interpolate between sticky strike and sticky delta depending on parameters, typically calibrated to match observed historical relationships between spot moves and smile shifts.

Model Risk for Barrier and Digital Products

Remark - Model Risk as a Recurring Theme

Across this subject - barrier options and TARFs (Chapter 1), accumulators and decumulators (Chapter 2), and basket, quanto, and best-of/worst-of options (Chapter 3) - a consistent pattern emerges: these products' valuations depend on aspects of the joint distribution of underlying rates (tail probabilities, correlations, smile dynamics) that are far less directly observable from liquid market prices than the at-the-money volatility and forward rate that anchor vanilla pricing. Two dealers, both calibrated to the identical set of liquid vanilla quotes, can produce meaningfully different prices and hedge ratios for the same exotic structure, with the difference attributable entirely to model choice - smile dynamics assumption, correlation assumption, or monitoring convention.

This is model risk: the risk that a position's marked value, and the hedging strategy derived from that mark, depends on modelling choices that cannot be fully validated against observable prices. For an exotic FX trading desk, managing model risk means not just having a model that fits the current smile, but understanding - via the kind of analysis in this section - how sensitive a given position's value and hedge ratios are to plausible alternative assumptions about smile dynamics, discrete monitoring, and correlation, and ensuring that reserves, limits, and hedging practice are robust to that uncertainty rather than to a single point estimate from a single model.

This concludes the exotic FX products notes. Together, the four chapters have covered the two foundational path-dependent single-pair exotics (barriers and TARFs), the structured-product family built on repeated barrier-like fixings (accumulators and decumulators), the multi-currency extensions that introduce correlation as a pricing input (baskets, quantos, best-of/worst-of options), and the cross-cutting risk management themes - pin risk, discrete monitoring, and smile dynamics - that determine how robustly any of these products can actually be priced and hedged in practice.
