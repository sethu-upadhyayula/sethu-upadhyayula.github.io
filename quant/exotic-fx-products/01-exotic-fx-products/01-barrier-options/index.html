1.1 Barrier Options

A barrier option is a vanilla call or put whose existence is conditional on the underlying spot rate touching (or failing to touch) a specified level - the barrier - at any time during the option's life. Barrier options are among the most heavily traded FX exotics, prized for offering customised, conditional payoffs at a lower premium than the corresponding vanilla option.

Types of Barrier Options

Definition - Knock-In and Knock-Out

Let $H$ denote the barrier level and $S_t$ the spot rate. A barrier option is classified by:

  • Knock-out: the option is a standard vanilla call/put unless and until $S_t$ touches $H$, at which point it is extinguished (becomes worthless), possibly subject to payment of a fixed rebate.
  • Knock-in: the option has no payoff unless $S_t$ touches $H$ at some point during its life, at which point it becomes a standard vanilla call/put.
  • Up vs. Down: the barrier is approached from below ($H > S_0$, "up") or from above ($H < S_0$, "down").
Remark - In-Out Parity

For the same barrier $H$, the same strike $K$, and the same option type, a knock-in and a knock-out with no rebate are exactly complementary: holding both is equivalent to holding the vanilla option, since exactly one of the two will be "active" at maturity depending on whether $H$ was touched. Hence:

$$\text{Knock-In} + \text{Knock-Out} = \text{Vanilla}$$

Combining the up/down and in/out distinctions with call/put gives eight basic single-barrier types: down-and-out call, down-and-in call, up-and-out call, up-and-in call, and the corresponding four for puts. Double-barrier options, with both an upper and lower barrier, are also traded, typically as double knock-out structures (the option is extinguished if either barrier is touched).

Example - A Down-and-Out USD Call / JPY Put

A Japanese exporter expecting USD receivables in three months wants to hedge against USD/JPY weakening (JPY strengthening) below its budget rate of 145, but believes USD/JPY is unlikely to fall all the way to 138 over the period. It buys a USD put / JPY call struck at 145 with a down-and-out barrier at 138. If spot never trades at or below 138, the exporter has standard protection below 145; if spot does trade at 138 at any point, the option knocks out and the exporter is unprotected - but pays a significantly lower premium than for the vanilla put, reflecting the (in this exporter's view) low-probability scenario it has chosen to leave unhedged.

Why Barriers Are Popular in FX

Remark - Corporate Hedging Rationale

Corporates hedging transactional FX exposure (e.g., an exporter receiving foreign currency revenue) often have a view not just on the direction of the currency but on the range of plausible outcomes. A vanilla option provides protection across the entire range below (or above) the strike, and this comprehensive protection is priced accordingly.

A knock-out option removes protection in a tail scenario the corporate considers unlikely (or is willing to bear), substantially reducing premium - often making the difference between a hedge the corporate is willing to pay for in cash versus a zero-cost collar that would otherwise cap the corporate's upside. Knock-in options, conversely, are used to obtain favourable rates that only "activate" if the market moves to a level the buyer expects, often embedded in structured forwards.

Pricing Considerations

Closed-form pricing formulas for single barrier options under Garman-Kohlhagen (the FX analogue of the Black-Scholes barrier formulas, due to Merton and later generalised by Reiner and Rubinstein) exist for continuously monitored barriers under constant volatility. In practice, several complications arise:

Remark - Discrete vs. Continuous Monitoring

The closed-form formulas assume the barrier is monitored continuously - i.e., the option knocks in/out the instant spot trades at $H$, at any time, day or night. Many traded contracts instead specify discrete monitoring, where the barrier is checked only at specified times (e.g., daily at a fixed fixing time, such as the WM/Reuters 4pm London fix, or only during certain trading hours). A discretely monitored barrier is "harder to hit" than a continuously monitored one, so its price differs from the continuous formula. A standard correction (Broadie-Glasserman-Kou) adjusts the effective barrier level by a factor of $e^{\pm 0.5826\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}}$ (sign depending on barrier direction) to approximate the discrete-monitoring price using the continuous formula.

Remark - Smile-Consistent Pricing

Because a barrier option's value depends on the probability of spot reaching a level that may be far from the current spot - often deep in the wings of the distribution - its price is highly sensitive to the volatility smile, not just the ATM volatility. The Vanna-Volga method (introduced in the previous section) is widely used in FX precisely because it adjusts a flat-volatility barrier price for the smile by replicating the barrier's vega, vanna, and volga exposures using the ATM, risk reversal, and butterfly instruments.

Hedging Near the Barrier and Barrier Shifting

The Greeks of a barrier option - particularly delta and gamma - can become extremely large and discontinuous as spot approaches the barrier, especially close to expiry. A knock-out option's value drops to zero (or the rebate) discontinuously at the barrier, so its delta can spike dramatically just before the barrier is touched, creating severe hedging difficulties (a dealer may need to trade a very large, rapidly changing spot position in a thin market right at the barrier level).

Remark - Barrier Shifting

A common risk management technique is barrier shifting: for risk management purposes (though not for the contractual payoff), the dealer treats the option as if its barrier were at a level $H'$ slightly inside the true barrier $H$ (e.g., a few pips closer to spot for a knock-out, conservatively assuming the barrier triggers earlier). This builds in a buffer that accounts for the discontinuous risk near $H$, the bid-offer cost of unwinding the hedge at the barrier, and discrete monitoring effects, at the cost of a more conservative (higher) reserve against the position.
