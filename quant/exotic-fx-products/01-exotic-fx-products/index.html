1. Exotic FX Products

This first chapter covers two of the most widely traded exotic FX structures, both of which build directly on the vanilla pricing and volatility surface machinery developed elsewhere, but introduce path-dependence that requires additional modelling care.

Barrier options — knock-in and knock-out structures — are extremely popular in FX because they allow corporates and investors to obtain conditional protection or participation at a lower premium than a vanilla option, in exchange for the risk that the option ceases to exist (or only comes into existence) if the spot rate trades through a specified level. Their pricing and, especially, their hedging near the barrier are sensitive to the shape of the volatility smile and to market conventions around how the barrier is monitored.

Target Redemption Forwards (TARFs) are structured products consisting of a strip of forward-like fixings with a leveraged payoff that terminates once a cumulative target gain is reached. They were heavily marketed to corporates — particularly in emerging markets — as a way to achieve an enhanced forward rate, but carry substantial tail risk if the market moves persistently against the corporate's hedged direction, as dramatically illustrated by losses linked to the Swiss franc in 2015.
