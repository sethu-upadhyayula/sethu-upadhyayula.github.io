3.2 Best-of/Worst-of Options

Best-of and worst-of options are multi-asset options whose payoff depends on the maximum or minimum performance among a set of underlyings - in the FX context, typically a basket of exchange rates relative to a common reference currency. They are popular in structured notes (e.g., a note that pays a high coupon if the worst-performing of three currency pairs stays within a range) because they allow the issuer to offer an enhanced coupon or strike in exchange for the buyer taking on exposure to whichever underlying performs worst - a form of correlation-dependent risk that is often poorly priced into the headline terms.

Payoff Structures

Definition - Best-of and Worst-of Call Options

For underlyings $S^{(1)}, \dots, S^{(n)}$ (e.g., $n$ exchange rates, each normalised to its return relative to $t=0$) and strike $K$, the maturity payoffs are:

$$\text{Best-of call: } \left(\max_i S^{(i)}_T - K\right)^+ \qquad \text{Worst-of call: } \left(\min_i S^{(i)}_T - K\right)^+$$

and analogously for puts. A common FX application is a "worst-of digital" embedded in a yield-enhancement note: the note pays an enhanced coupon unless any of $n$ specified currency pairs moves outside a pre-defined range, in which case the coupon is reduced or the note's redemption is adjusted - economically, the investor has sold the issuer a worst-of digital option across all $n$ pairs.

Correlation Sensitivity

Remark - Best-of/Worst-of Options Are Short/Long Correlation

Consider a worst-of call as $n \to$ many underlyings, or even just $n=2$. Holding the individual marginal distributions of $S^{(1)}, \dots, S^{(n)}$ fixed:

  • If the underlyings are highly correlated ($\rho \to 1$), they tend to move together, so $\min_i S^{(i)}_T$ behaves similarly to any single $S^{(i)}_T$ - the worst-of payoff is close to a single-asset option.
  • If the underlyings are weakly or negatively correlated ($\rho \to 0$ or below), it becomes more likely that at least one underlying performs poorly even if others perform well - the minimum of several largely-independent draws tends to be lower than any single draw, dragging down $\min_i S^{(i)}_T$ on average.

A worst-of call therefore increases in value as correlation decreases (lower correlation pushes the distribution of the minimum down, which is bad news for a worst-of call... but the issuer of a worst-of-conditioned note, who is short this optionality, benefits from lower correlation - equivalently, the note's investor, who has implicitly sold worst-of downside protection, is exposed to a "short correlation" position: they lose more, in expectation, the less correlated the underlyings are). Symmetrically, a best-of call increases in value as correlation decreases, since a lower correlation makes it more likely that at least one underlying performs very well, pushing up $\max_i S^{(i)}_T$.

Pricing Intuition via Correlation Skew

Remark - There Is No Single Market "Correlation"

Unlike single-pair implied volatility, which is directly observable from vanilla option prices across a continuum of strikes, pairwise correlations between exchange rates are not directly traded as a liquid instrument across a range of "correlation strikes." Dealers infer an implied correlation from the relatively small set of multi-asset products that do trade (basket options, worst-of notes, and similar structures), but this implied correlation typically exhibits its own "skew": the correlation level that reprices an out-of-the-money worst-of put (sensitive to joint tail co-movement, i.e. all currencies weakening together - a classic "risk-off" scenario) is often higher than the correlation level that reprices an at-the-money best-of/worst-of structure, reflecting the market's view that correlations between currency pairs tend to rise sharply in stressed, risk-off scenarios even if they are more modest in calm markets.

Remark - Practical Calibration Challenges

Because liquid correlation-sensitive instruments are scarce, dealers often calibrate the correlation matrix used for best-of/worst-of pricing from a combination of: (i) historical realised correlations between the relevant exchange rate returns, typically over several different lookback windows to gauge stability; (ii) any observable prices of similar multi-asset structures, inverted to back out an implied correlation; and (iii) a stressed correlation overlay applied to tail scenarios, reflecting the empirical tendency (documented across many crisis episodes) for cross-asset and cross-currency correlations to spike towards 1 (for "risk-off" co-movements) during market stress, even when calmer-period historical correlations are much lower. The gap between calm-period and stressed correlation assumptions can be a significant source of model risk and of day-one P&L volatility if a position is re-marked using a different correlation regime than the one used at inception.

Best-of and worst-of structures thus require not just a volatility surface for each underlying exchange rate (as for the single-pair exotics of earlier chapters) but a full correlation matrix - itself effectively a surface with its own skew and term structure - making these among the most model-dependent products in the exotic FX universe, and a natural bridge to the broader risk management themes of model risk and smile dynamics taken up in the final chapter.
