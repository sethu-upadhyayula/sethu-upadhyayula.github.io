3.1 Basket and Quanto Options

Basket and quanto options are the simplest multi-currency exotics in that their payoffs are linear (or close to linear) functions of the underlying exchange rates, but they introduce a genuinely new modelling ingredient relative to the single-pair products of Chapters 1 and 2: the correlation between exchange rate movements, which enters both the payoff distribution directly (for baskets) and the risk-neutral drift of the payoff currency (for quantos).

Basket FX Options

Definition - FX Basket Option

A basket option on $n$ exchange rates $S^{(1)}, \dots, S^{(n)}$ with weights $w_1, \dots, w_n$ has a call payoff at maturity $T$ of:

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i S^{(i)}_T - K\right)^+$$

for strike $K$. A common use case is a basket on a corporate's actual exposure currencies - e.g., a US exporter receiving revenue in EUR, GBP, and JPY might buy a single put option on a weighted basket of EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and USD/JPY (appropriately signed) reflecting the relative size of each currency's contribution to revenue, rather than buying three separate vanilla options.

Correlation Across Currency Pairs

Remark - Why Baskets Are Cheaper Than the Sum of Vanillas

The sum of $n$ correlated lognormal random variables is not itself lognormal, so a basket option cannot be priced with a simple Garman-Kohlhagen formula even given each pair's individual volatility - the basket's variance depends on the full covariance matrix:

$$\text{Var}\left(\sum_i w_i S^{(i)}_T\right) = \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 (S_0^{(i)})^2 T + \sum_{i \neq j} w_i w_j \rho_{ij} \sigma_i \sigma_j S_0^{(i)} S_0^{(j)} T$$

where $\rho_{ij}$ is the correlation between the (log-)returns of $S^{(i)}$ and $S^{(j)}$. If the pairwise correlations $\rho_{ij}$ are positive, the basket's volatility is lower than the weighted sum of the individual volatilities - diversification reduces the basket's effective volatility, and hence a basket option is cheaper than the corresponding sum of individually-struck vanilla options on each pair. This is precisely why baskets are attractive to corporates with multi-currency exposure: the hedge cost reflects the (typically imperfect) correlation between the corporate's various currency exposures, rather than treating each one as fully independent.

In practice, basket options are usually priced via Monte Carlo simulation of the joint dynamics of $S^{(1)}, \dots, S^{(n)}$ under a correlated multivariate lognormal (or more sophisticated) model, since no closed-form formula exists for the sum-of-lognormals distribution. A common approximation, moment-matching, fits a single lognormal (or shifted lognormal) distribution to the basket by matching its first two moments (computed from the formula above), then applies the Black-Scholes/Garman-Kohlhagen formula to that approximating distribution - fast, but it can be inaccurate for baskets with very different individual volatilities or extreme correlations.

Quanto Options

Definition - Quanto Option

A quanto (quantity-adjusting) option pays off based on an underlying denominated in one currency, but the payoff itself is settled in a different currency at a pre-fixed exchange rate (often 1:1, regardless of the actual exchange rate at maturity). For example, a USD-based investor might buy a "quanto call" on the Nikkei 225 index (denominated in JPY) that pays $(\text{Nikkei}_T - K)^+$ in USD - i.e., the JPY-denominated payoff is converted to USD at a fixed rate of 1, eliminating the investor's JPY/USD exchange rate exposure on the payoff itself.

The Quanto Adjustment

Remark - Why Quantos Need a Drift Adjustment

Pricing the quanto payoff "as if" it were a domestic-currency option on the foreign underlying - i.e., simply plugging the foreign underlying's volatility into a domestic Black-Scholes formula - is not correct, because removing the FX conversion from the payoff changes the risk-neutral measure under which the underlying must be simulated. Specifically, under the domestic risk-neutral measure, the foreign underlying $U_t$ (e.g., the Nikkei) acquires an additional drift term proportional to $-\rho \sigma_U \sigma_{FX}$, where $\sigma_U$ is the underlying's volatility, $\sigma_{FX}$ is the volatility of the relevant exchange rate, and $\rho$ is the correlation between the underlying's returns and the exchange rate's returns. This is the quanto adjustment:

$$\mu_U^{\text{quanto}} = \mu_U - \rho\,\sigma_U \sigma_{FX}$$

Intuitively, the quanto contract has removed the investor's exposure to the exchange rate, but the underlying and the exchange rate are correlated in the real world - so by fixing the conversion rate, the bank selling the quanto has taken on a residual correlation exposure, which must be compensated for via this drift shift in the pricing measure.

Worked Example

Example - Quanto Adjustment for a USD-Settled Nikkei Call

A USD-based investor buys a 1-year quanto call on the Nikkei 225, struck at $K = 33{,}000$, with the JPY payoff converted to USD at a fixed rate of $1{:}1$ (i.e., $(\text{Nikkei}_T - 33{,}000)^+$ paid in USD). The Nikkei has volatility $\sigma_U = 18\%$, the USD/JPY exchange rate has volatility $\sigma_{FX} = 9\%$, and the correlation between Nikkei returns and USD/JPY returns is $\rho = -0.30$ (historically, JPY tends to strengthen - USD/JPY falls - when the Nikkei falls, so Nikkei returns and JPY strength are positively related, giving Nikkei returns and USD/JPY a negative correlation).

The quanto adjustment to the Nikkei's drift is:

$$-\rho\,\sigma_U \sigma_{FX} = -(-0.30)(0.18)(0.09) = +0.00486 \approx +48.6\text{bp per year}$$

Because $\rho < 0$, the quanto adjustment is positive: the Nikkei's effective drift under the pricing measure for this quanto is higher than its drift under the standard JPY risk-neutral measure, by about 49bp per year. This raises the value of the quanto call relative to what a naive (no-adjustment) pricing would suggest. The intuition is that scenarios where the Nikkei rallies tend to coincide with JPY weakening (USD/JPY rising) - in an un-quanto'd JPY payoff, this JPY weakness would partially offset the USD value of the gain to a USD investor, but the quanto structure removes that offset, making the high-Nikkei scenarios relatively more valuable to the USD investor than they would be without the quanto feature - captured by the upward drift adjustment.
