2.2 Risk Management of Accumulators

An accumulator can be decomposed into a strip of forwards plus a strip of digital (knockout) options plus a strip of leveraged vanilla options, one set per fixing date, with the crucial complication that the knockout barrier is shared across all fixings: once it is touched on any date, every remaining fixing is cancelled simultaneously. This shared, path-dependent barrier is what makes risk-managing an accumulator book materially harder than running a book of independent vanilla or single-barrier positions.

Path-Dependent Greeks

Remark - Delta as a Function of Remaining Life

The delta of an accumulator at any point in its life is the sum of the deltas of all remaining (not-yet-fixed, not-yet-knocked-out) legs. Early in the structure's life, with many fixings remaining and spot well away from the barrier $H$, the delta is relatively smooth and dominated by the leveraged exposure on the unfavourable side (since the dealer is short the leverage to the client). As spot approaches $H$, the delta of the knockout digital component grows rapidly and can dominate the position: the dealer's overall delta can swing sharply because a touch of $H$ would instantaneously cancel a large number of future leveraged legs, each of which currently contributes delta. This is structurally the same phenomenon as the delta discontinuity of a single barrier option (Section 1.1), but compounded across every remaining fixing rather than a single expiry.

Remark - Gamma and Vega Concentrate Near the Barrier

As with single barrier options, gamma and vega become large and sign-indeterminate as spot approaches $H$, but for an accumulator this effect is amplified by the number of remaining fixings: a small move in spot near the barrier can change the expected number of future "live" fixings discontinuously, and therefore change the aggregate notional the dealer expects to transact with the client by a large multiple. Vega is further complicated because the probability of touching $H$ before each future fixing date depends on the volatility term structure across all remaining maturities, not a single expiry - so the position has vega exposure to the shape of the term structure, not just its level.

Gap Risk at the Knockout Barrier

Definition - Gap Risk

Gap risk refers to the risk that spot moves discontinuously through a barrier level - for example overnight, over a weekend, or around a major economic data release or central bank announcement - such that the dealer's hedge, which was calibrated to the pre-gap delta, is no longer appropriate for the post-gap (knocked-out, in this case) position. Because the accumulator's delta can change very sharply across the barrier (Section 2.2 above), a gap through $H$ can leave the dealer with a large, suddenly "orphaned" hedge that no longer corresponds to any live exposure in the structured product.

Example - Weekend Gap Through the Knockout

Continuing the EUR/USD accumulator example from Section 2.1 (strike $K=1.0800$, knockout $H=1.1200$, leverage $L=2$, 70 fixings remaining), suppose that on Friday's close spot is at $1.1150$ - close to, but below, the barrier - and the dealer's hedge book holds a substantial short EUR position reflecting the expected leveraged delivery of EUR to the client across the remaining fixings (the dealer is short the leverage, so is positioned to need to source EUR).

Over the weekend, a surprise central bank announcement causes EUR/USD to gap to $1.1280$ at Monday's open - through the barrier without ever trading at intermediate levels. The accumulator knocks out instantly: all 70 remaining fixings are cancelled. The dealer's short EUR hedge, sized for a position that expected substantial further EUR purchases by the client at the favourable rate $K$, is now hedging a position that no longer exists. The dealer must unwind this now-orphaned hedge at Monday's gapped-open price, which - because the move has been entirely one-directional and the dealer's hedge was on the "wrong side" relative to the new spot level - is likely to result in a realised loss on the unwind, in addition to whatever mark-to-market the accumulator itself experienced at the knockout.

Dealer Hedging Challenges

Remark - Why Accumulators Are Harder to Hedge Than They Look

From a static, day-one perspective, an accumulator can be priced reasonably well as a sum of forwards and digital/vanilla strips using a model calibrated to the volatility smile across the relevant tenors (much as a TARF is, per Section 1.2). The hedging difficulty is dynamic: as time passes and spot moves, the relative weights of the "remaining live fixings" change continuously, and the position's sensitivity to a barrier touch becomes increasingly concentrated as fewer fixings remain (the same "pin risk near expiry" phenomenon discussed for single options in Section 4.1, but recurring at every fixing date for the life of the structure).

In practice, dealers running accumulator books manage this with a combination of: (i) frequent re-hedging of delta as spot moves and fixings occur, accepting the transaction costs this entails; (ii) holding a static "overlay" of short-dated barrier or digital options near $H$ to absorb some of the gap risk, effectively buying back some of the optionality sold to the client at a cost that is reflected in the pricing; and (iii) limiting the aggregate notional of accumulators with barriers clustered near similar levels across the book, since a single large gap move can crystallise correlated losses across many client positions simultaneously - a concentration risk that proved costly for several dealers during the sharp FX moves of 2008 and 2015.
