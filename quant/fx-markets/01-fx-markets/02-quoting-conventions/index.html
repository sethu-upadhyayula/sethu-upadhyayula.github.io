1.2 Quoting Conventions

Every FX rate is a relationship between two currencies, and the convention for which currency is "first" and which is "second" determines how the number is interpreted, how P&L is computed, and how hedge ratios transform. This section formalises base/quote currency conventions, surveys the market-standard quoting conventions for major pairs, and introduces the machinery of pips, cross rates, and bid-ask spreads.

Base and Quote Currencies

Definition - Base and Quote (Terms) Currency

For a currency pair written CCY1/CCY2, CCY1 is the base currency and CCY2 is the quote currency (also called the terms currency or price currency). The quoted rate $S$ is the number of units of the quote currency required to buy one unit of the base currency:

$$1 \text{ unit of CCY1} = S \text{ units of CCY2}$$

A quote is described as direct from the perspective of a country if its own currency is the quote currency (i.e., the rate expresses "how much of my currency per unit of foreign currency"). It is indirect if the domestic currency is the base currency (i.e., "how many units of foreign currency per unit of my currency"). For example, from a US perspective, USD/JPY = 149.50 is a direct quote (149.50 yen per dollar), while EUR/USD = 1.0850 is an indirect quote from the US perspective (1.0850 dollars buy one euro) but a direct quote from a eurozone perspective.

Remark - Why Conventions Matter

The convention determines the sign and scale of sensitivities. If a trader is "long EUR/USD," they profit when EUR appreciates against USD, i.e., when $S_{\text{EUR/USD}}$ rises. A delta hedge against this position is expressed in units of the base currency (EUR notional). Confusing which currency is base versus quote is one of the most common sources of error when translating between desks, systems, or risk reports that may use different conventions for the same currency pair.

Market Convention Pairs

While in principle any pair could be quoted with either currency as base, the market has settled on standard conventions for the major currencies, largely following a hierarchy: EUR > GBP > AUD > NZD > USD > CAD, CHF, JPY and other currencies. A currency higher in this hierarchy is conventionally the base currency against one lower.

Example - Standard Market Conventions
  • EUR/USD, GBP/USD, AUD/USD, NZD/USD: quoted as "USD per 1 unit of base currency" - e.g., EUR/USD = 1.0850 means \$1.0850 per euro.
  • USD/JPY, USD/CHF, USD/CAD: quoted as "units of CCY2 per 1 USD" - e.g., USD/JPY = 149.50 means ¥149.50 per dollar.
  • EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY: cross pairs follow the hierarchy, with EUR and GBP as base currencies against most others.

These conventions are not universal across all market participants - some retail platforms and some local markets quote pairs "the other way round" - but the conventions above are the interbank standard and are what is assumed throughout this text unless stated otherwise.

Pips and Big Figures

Definition - Pip and Big Figure

A pip (percentage in point) is the smallest standard increment in which a quote moves, conventionally:

  • $0.0001$ for most pairs (the fourth decimal place), e.g., EUR/USD moving from 1.0850 to 1.0851 is a one-pip move.
  • $0.01$ for JPY pairs (the second decimal place), e.g., USD/JPY moving from 149.50 to 149.51 is a one-pip move.
  • The big figure is the leading digits of the rate that are typically held fixed during a quoting session - e.g., for EUR/USD trading near 1.0850, the big figure is "1.08" and dealers quote only "50/52" for the last two decimal digits (in pips).

Electronic platforms often display a fifth (or third, for JPY pairs) decimal digit known as a pipette or fractional pip, allowing finer price discrimination than the traditional pip grid.

Cross Rates and Triangulation

A cross rate between two non-USD currencies is computed by triangulating through USD (or another common currency). The arithmetic depends on whether USD is the base or quote currency in each leg.

Example - Computing EUR/JPY from EUR/USD and USD/JPY

Suppose EUR/USD = 1.0850 (USD per EUR) and USD/JPY = 149.50 (JPY per USD). To find EUR/JPY (JPY per EUR), multiply directly because USD cancels as quote-then-base:

$$S_{\text{EUR/JPY}} = S_{\text{EUR/USD}} \times S_{\text{USD/JPY}} = 1.0850 \times 149.50 = 162.21$$

Had the second pair instead been quoted as JPY/USD (USD per JPY), one would divide rather than multiply. The general rule: align the units so that the common currency (USD) appears once in a numerator and once in a denominator, and the units cancel.

Bid-Ask Spreads in FX

As in spot, every FX quote - spot, forward, or cross - is two-sided: a bid (where the market maker buys the base currency) and an offer/ask (where the market maker sells it). When triangulating cross rates from two-sided quotes, the resulting cross-rate spread is wider than either underlying spread, because the client effectively crosses two bid-ask spreads.

Example - Cross-Rate Spread Widening

Suppose EUR/USD = 1.08470 / 1.08480 and USD/JPY = 149.495 / 149.510. To compute the EUR/JPY bid (sell EUR for JPY), a market maker would sell EUR for USD at the EUR/USD bid and sell USD for JPY at the USD/JPY bid:

$$\text{EUR/JPY bid} = 1.08470 \times 149.495 = 162.158$$

Similarly, the EUR/JPY offer is $1.08480 \times 149.510 = 162.205$. The resulting cross spread (about 4.7 pips) is wider than either constituent spread (about 1 pip and 1.5 pips respectively), reflecting the additional risk of warehousing two legs.

The BIS Triennial Survey

Remark - BIS Triennial Central Bank Survey

The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) conducts a Triennial Central Bank Survey of foreign exchange and OTC derivatives markets, the most authoritative source on global FX market structure, turnover, and instrument composition. The survey reports daily average turnover (over $7 trillion as of the most recent surveys), the relative shares of spot, outright forwards, FX swaps, currency swaps, and options, and the geographic distribution of trading activity.

Consistently, the survey shows USD on one side of roughly 88% of all trades (reflecting its role as the dominant vehicle currency), EUR/USD as the most actively traded pair, and FX swaps as the largest single instrument category by turnover - a fact directly relevant to the forwards and swaps material in the next chapter.
