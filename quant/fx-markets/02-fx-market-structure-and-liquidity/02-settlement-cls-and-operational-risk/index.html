2.2 Settlement, CLS, and Operational Risk

Every FX transaction culminates in the exchange of two payments, in two different currencies, typically across two different national payment systems operating in different time zones. This apparently mundane fact gives rise to one of the most consequential risks in the FX market: settlement risk, the possibility that one party pays away the currency it owes before confirming receipt of the currency it is owed.

Settlement Risk and Herstatt Risk

Definition - FX Settlement Risk

FX settlement risk (also called principal risk or Herstatt risk) is the risk that a party to an FX transaction pays the currency it sold but does not receive the currency it bought, because its counterparty defaults or fails between the two payment legs. The exposure is equal to the full principal value of the transaction, not merely a fraction such as a mark-to-market gain.

Remark - The Herstatt Episode

The name derives from Bankhaus Herstatt, a German bank that was closed by German regulators on 26 June 1974, after the close of the German payments system but before the corresponding US dollar payments had been made in New York (due to the time-zone gap between Frankfurt and New York). Counterparties that had paid Deutsche Mark to Herstatt earlier in the day never received the US dollars they were owed. The episode became the textbook illustration of settlement risk and motivated decades of subsequent regulatory and infrastructure work - culminating in the creation of CLS.

Continuous Linked Settlement (CLS)

Definition - CLS and Payment-versus-Payment

Continuous Linked Settlement (CLS) is a specialised financial market infrastructure, operated by CLS Bank International, that settles FX transactions on a payment-versus-payment (PvP) basis: both legs of a trade are settled simultaneously, or neither leg settles at all. CLS holds accounts at the relevant central banks for each settlement currency and acts as the common settlement agent for its member institutions, eliminating the principal risk that arises from the time-zone gap between two separately settled legs.

CLS currently settles a large share of global FX turnover across the major settlement currencies (including USD, EUR, GBP, JPY, CHF, and a number of other currencies). For trades in CLS-eligible currency pairs between CLS members, settlement risk in the Herstatt sense is essentially eliminated: CLS will only release the payment instructions for both legs if it can confirm that funding for both currencies has been received from the respective parties (or their settlement members) within the settlement window.

Remark - The PvP Mechanism

CLS operates a single settlement cycle per business day during a window when the relevant real-time gross settlement (RTGS) systems of the major central banks are simultaneously open (a window that exists despite the time-zone differences because of how the major RTGS operating hours overlap around the early morning in Central Europe).

Each CLS member submits matched trade instructions; CLS nets each member's obligations across all its trades in each currency for the day, calculates a single net pay-in or pay-out per currency per member, and only proceeds with settlement once it has verified, currency by currency, that all required pay-ins have been received. This combination of multilateral netting and PvP dramatically reduces both the gross settlement value at risk and the principal risk on each individual trade.

Residual Operational Risk

CLS substantially mitigates, but does not entirely eliminate, risk in FX settlement. Several categories of operational and settlement risk remain relevant even for participants that settle through CLS.

Remark - Sources of Residual Risk

Non-CLS currencies and counterparties. Many emerging market currencies are not CLS-eligible, and some smaller counterparties are not direct or indirect CLS members. Trades involving these currencies or counterparties settle on a traditional correspondent-banking basis and retain full Herstatt-style principal risk.

Timing and cut-off risk. Even within CLS, trades must be submitted and matched before specific cut-off times to be included in that day's settlement cycle. Operational errors - a trade booked late, a mismatch between the two parties' instructions, or a funding shortfall - can cause a trade to fail settlement, requiring it to be resubmitted for the next cycle and potentially generating compensation claims for the delay.

Intraday liquidity risk. CLS members must fund their net pay-ins in each settlement currency by the start of the settlement window, which can create significant intraday liquidity demands, particularly for members with large, directional net positions in a given currency on a given day.

Worked Example: Settlement Timing Across Time Zones

To make the time-zone mechanics concrete, consider a EUR/JPY trade between a European bank and a Japanese bank, settled bilaterally outside of CLS.

Example - Bilateral Settlement of EUR/JPY Without PvP

The Japanese bank sells EUR and buys JPY. The two payment legs are:

  • JPY leg: the Japanese bank's correspondent must pay JPY to the European bank's correspondent via Japan's domestic payment system (Zengin/BOJ-NET), which operates during Tokyo business hours.
  • EUR leg: the European bank's correspondent must pay EUR to the Japanese bank's correspondent via the eurozone payment system (TARGET2), which operates during European business hours.

Tokyo is several hours ahead of the eurozone. By the time TARGET2 opens in Europe, the Tokyo business day - and the JPY payment window - has typically already closed. In practice this means:

  1. The Japanese bank pays JPY during its morning (Tokyo time), well before the European bank's business day has begun.
  2. The European bank does not pay EUR until TARGET2 opens, several hours later (Tokyo time, this is already the following calendar day in Japan).
  3. For this entire gap - potentially many hours - the Japanese bank has paid away JPY and is a pure unsecured creditor of the European bank for the EUR amount, with full principal exposure should the European bank fail in the interim.

This is precisely the exposure window that CLS's PvP mechanism is designed to close: by settling both legs through accounts at the relevant central banks within a single coordinated cycle, CLS ensures that the JPY pay-in and the EUR pay-out (and vice versa for the counterparty) are conditioned on each other, regardless of the underlying time-zone gap between Tokyo and the eurozone.
