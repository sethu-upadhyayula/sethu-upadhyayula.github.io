2.1 Market Participants and Trading Venues

The FX market has no central exchange. Instead it is a network of bilateral and platform-based relationships connecting a heterogeneous set of participants, each accessing liquidity through different channels and at different cost levels. Understanding this structure is essential for interpreting quoted prices, anticipating market impact, and understanding why the "same" currency pair can trade at meaningfully different effective prices depending on who is asking.

The Interbank Market

Definition - Interbank Market

The interbank market is the wholesale tier of FX trading in which major commercial and investment banks deal directly with one another, either bilaterally (voice or electronic single-dealer platforms) or through inter-dealer broker venues. This tier sets the reference rates from which most other prices in the market are derived, and historically operated through dedicated inter-dealer platforms such as EBS (Electronic Broking Services) and Refinitiv (Reuters) Matching.

Banks in the interbank market act primarily as market makers, continuously quoting two-way prices and managing the resulting inventory risk by laying off positions with other banks. The interbank tier is characterised by the tightest spreads in the market - often a fraction of a pip for the most liquid pairs - and large minimum trade sizes, reflecting the scale at which these institutions operate.

ECNs and Multi-Dealer Platforms

Over the past two decades, electronic communication networks (ECNs) and multi-dealer platforms have come to dominate FX execution, displacing much of the voice-brokered and bilateral single-dealer flow.

Remark - ECNs vs. Multi-Dealer Platforms

ECNs (e.g., EBS Market, Refinitiv Matching) operate as central limit order books: market makers stream firm, executable prices into a shared book, and any participant with access can trade against the best available price on a first-come, first-served basis. Anonymity is typically preserved pre-trade.

Multi-dealer platforms (e.g., FXall, 360T, Bloomberg FXGO) operate primarily on a request-for-quote (RFQ) basis: a client requests a price for a specific size and pair, and a panel of liquidity providers responds with competing quotes. The client then selects the best price. This model gives the client more control over which counterparties see their flow, at the cost of revealing trading intent to a wider set of dealers.

The choice between streaming ECN liquidity and RFQ-based multi-dealer platforms is itself a microstructure decision: ECNs offer speed and anonymity but expose the order to the full depth (and toxicity) of the central book, while RFQ allows a client to curate its liquidity providers but signals trading interest to each dealer in the panel.

Prime Brokerage

Definition - FX Prime Brokerage

A prime brokerage (PB) arrangement allows a client - typically a hedge fund or other non-bank financial institution - to trade FX with multiple executing dealers under the credit and settlement umbrella of a single prime broker. Trades executed with any approved executing dealer are given up to the prime broker, which becomes the counterparty of record to the client, while the prime broker faces the executing dealer on a back-to-back trade.

Prime brokerage effectively decouples credit relationships from execution relationships: a hedge fund can access competitive pricing from dozens of executing banks without needing a direct credit line (and the associated documentation, such as ISDA agreements) with each one. The prime broker charges a "give-up" fee for this service. PB access is one of the primary mechanisms by which non-bank participants achieve execution quality close to that of the interbank tier.

Market Participant Types

Participants in the FX market differ systematically in their objectives, time horizons, and the information content of their order flow - all of which affect how dealers price and manage the resulting exposures.

Remark - Categories of FX Market Participant

Dealers (market makers): banks and non-bank market makers that continuously quote two-sided prices, earn the bid-offer spread, and manage net inventory through internalisation and interbank hedging. Their flow is, by construction, largely uninformed in aggregate - though individual client segments within their flow may be highly informed.

Hedge funds: typically short- to medium-horizon, often systematic or discretionary macro participants. Hedge fund flow tends to be relatively information-rich and is frequently associated with momentum or carry-trade strategies; dealers often price hedge fund flow with wider effective spreads to compensate for adverse selection risk.

Real money: asset managers, pension funds, and insurers executing FX primarily to hedge or implement allocation decisions in underlying equity and bond portfolios. Real money flow is generally larger in size, less frequent, and considered less toxic - it is often driven by portfolio rebalancing rather than a view on the currency itself.

Corporates: non-financial companies transacting FX to convert revenues, pay suppliers, or hedge balance-sheet exposures arising from international operations. Corporate flow is typically the least informationally sensitive and is highly valued by dealers, who often price it at the tightest end of their client spread schedule.

Liquidity Tiering

The combination of venue access and participant type produces a tiered liquidity structure, in which the effective spread available to a given participant depends on its position in the network.

Example - Liquidity Tiering for EUR/USD

Consider EUR/USD trading at a top-of-book interbank spread of 0.1 pip. A large bank's electronic pricing engine might offer:

  • Tier 1 (other interbank dealers, PB clients with top-tier access): spreads of 0.1-0.3 pips for sizes up to several hundred million.
  • Tier 2 (hedge funds via standard PB, large corporates): spreads of 0.5-1 pip, with size-dependent widening for larger tickets.
  • Tier 3 (smaller corporates, retail aggregators): spreads of 1-3 pips or more, often with a markup that reflects both credit risk and the value of the relationship to the dealer.

This tiering is not static: a dealer's pricing to a given client depends dynamically on the client's historical flow toxicity, the size and direction of the request relative to the dealer's current inventory, and prevailing market volatility - themes that are developed further in the discussion of order flow and price discovery in Chapter 3.
