4.1 Interest Rate Differentials and Flows

Interest rate differentials between two currencies are among the most persistent and economically grounded drivers of exchange rates. This section examines the mechanisms - both theoretical and flow-based - through which differentials in interest rates between countries translate into exchange rate movements, and situates these mechanisms within the broader balance of payments framework that accounts for all cross-border transactions in a currency.

Interest Rate Differentials and the Carry Trade

Definition - Interest Rate Differential and Carry

For a currency pair with domestic currency interest rate $r_d$ and foreign currency interest rate $r_f$, the interest rate differential is $r_f - r_d$ (or its negative, depending on convention). A carry trade involves borrowing in the lower-yielding currency and investing in the higher-yielding currency, earning the differential $r_f - r_d$ as long as the exchange rate does not depreciate by more than that differential over the holding period.

Uncovered interest rate parity (UIP) is the theoretical proposition that, in expectation, exchange rate movements should exactly offset interest rate differentials, leaving the expected return on a carry trade equal to zero. Empirically, UIP is strongly and persistently violated over short-to-medium horizons - high-interest-rate currencies have historically tended to appreciate, or at least not depreciate enough to offset the rate differential, generating the well-documented "carry trade" excess return. This is sometimes called the "forward premium puzzle," since the forward rate (which embeds the interest differential via covered interest parity) is, on average, a biased predictor of the future spot rate in the direction opposite to what UIP would imply.

Remark - Why Differentials Still Matter Despite UIP Violations

Even though UIP fails empirically over short horizons, interest rate differentials remain a central driver of FX flows for two reasons. First, the carry trade itself generates large, persistent capital flows: when a wide differential opens up, investors borrow in the low-yielder and buy the high-yielder, creating sustained demand for the high-yield currency that tends to support (and can even reinforce) its level - until the trade unwinds, often abruptly, in a "carry unwind" that can produce sharp depreciations in high-yield currencies during episodes of risk aversion. Second, over very long horizons, something closer to UIP (or at least purchasing power parity-adjusted versions of it) tends to reassert itself, anchoring the relationship between rates and currency levels even if it is a poor short-horizon predictor.

Trade Flows and Capital Flows

Beyond carry-related positioning, two broad categories of cross-border flow drive the underlying supply and demand for a currency.

Remark - Trade Flows vs. Capital Flows

Trade flows arise from the exchange of goods and services: an exporter who receives payment in a foreign currency typically converts it into domestic currency, creating demand for the domestic currency (supply of the foreign currency), and vice versa for importers. A persistent trade surplus tends to generate persistent net demand for the surplus country's currency, all else equal.

Capital flows arise from cross-border investment: purchases of foreign bonds, equities, direct investment in foreign businesses, or interbank lending. Capital flows are typically far larger in magnitude than trade flows for major currencies and far more sensitive to relative interest rates, growth expectations, and risk appetite - which is why short-to-medium-term exchange rate movements are often dominated by capital flow considerations (including carry trades) even when trade flows determine the longer-run "fair value" anchor.

The Balance of Payments Framework

Definition - Balance of Payments

The balance of payments (BoP) is a systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world over a period, divided into:

  • The current account, which records trade in goods and services, net income from cross-border investments (interest, dividends), and net transfers.
  • The capital and financial account, which records cross-border investment flows - direct investment, portfolio investment (bonds, equities), and other financial flows including official reserve transactions.

By construction (in the absence of measurement error, captured in a statistical discrepancy term), the current account and the capital/financial account must sum to zero: any current account deficit (the country is a net importer of goods/services and/or pays out more investment income than it receives) must be financed by a corresponding net capital inflow (foreigners net-purchasing the country's assets, or the country running down reserves), and vice versa for a surplus.

Example - A Country Running a Current Account Deficit

Suppose Country A imports more goods than it exports, running a current account deficit of $50 billion per year. For the balance of payments to balance, Country A must attract a net capital inflow of $50 billion - for example, foreign investors purchasing $50 billion of Country A's government bonds, equities, or other assets.

If foreign appetite for Country A's assets is strong (e.g., because Country A offers attractive interest rates or growth prospects), this capital inflow can occur easily, and Country A's currency need not depreciate to "pay for" the deficit. If foreign appetite weakens, however, financing the deficit requires either higher interest rates (to attract capital) or a weaker currency (to make the deficit smaller by reducing imports and boosting exports) - illustrating how trade flows, capital flows, and interest rate differentials are jointly determined within the balance of payments framework, rather than being independent drivers.

This framework provides the bridge to the next section: central banks, through their interest rate decisions and balance sheet policies, directly influence the interest rate differentials and capital flow incentives discussed here - making monetary policy one of the most direct levers through which a country can affect its balance of payments and, in turn, its exchange rate.
