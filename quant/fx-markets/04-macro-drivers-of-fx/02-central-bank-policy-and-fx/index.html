4.2 Central Bank Policy and FX

Central banks influence currencies through their ordinary conduct of monetary policy — setting the policy rate that anchors the interest rate differentials discussed in Section 4.1 — but also through channels that operate independently of, and sometimes in tandem with, those rate decisions: forward guidance about the future path of policy, large-scale asset purchase or sale programmes, and, in some cases, direct intervention in the FX market itself.

Transmission via Policy Rates and Forward Guidance

The most direct channel runs through the policy rate itself: a central bank that raises rates relative to its peers widens the interest rate differential in favour of its currency, which — per Section 4.1 — tends to attract carry-motivated capital inflows and support the currency, at least until the differential is fully priced in.

Definition — Forward Guidance

Forward guidance is communication by a central bank about the likely future path of monetary policy, intended to shape market expectations of future short rates even before any actual change in the current policy rate occurs. Because FX prices are forward-looking — reflecting the entire expected path of future interest rate differentials, not just the current differential — forward guidance can move exchange rates immediately and substantially, even with the current policy rate unchanged.

Example — A Hawkish Guidance Surprise

Suppose a central bank leaves its policy rate unchanged at a scheduled meeting, as universally expected, but its accompanying statement and updated economic projections signal that two further rate hikes are now likely over the coming year, where previously the market had priced in none. Even though no rate has actually changed, the market immediately re-prices the expected future path of short rates higher, widening the expected future interest rate differential against other currencies. The currency typically appreciates sharply on the announcement — the FX market is pricing the entire revised expected path, not merely the unchanged current rate.

Quantitative Easing and Tightening

Definition — QE and QT

Quantitative easing (QE) refers to large-scale purchases of government bonds and other securities by a central bank, typically used when policy rates are at or near their effective lower bound and conventional rate cuts are no longer available. Quantitative tightening (QT) is the reverse — allowing the central bank's balance sheet to shrink by letting holdings mature without reinvestment, or through outright sales.

QE affects exchange rates through several overlapping channels: it compresses domestic long-term yields (reducing the attractiveness of domestic assets to foreign investors and so tending to weaken the currency), it expands the domestic money supply (a traditional channel for currency depreciation), and it is often associated with — and partly intended to achieve — a general loosening of financial conditions that can encourage capital outflows in search of higher returns elsewhere ("portfolio rebalancing" effects). QT operates through the same channels in reverse, though empirically its effects have often been less pronounced than QE's, partly because QT is typically conducted gradually and well telegraphed in advance.

Remark — Relative QE and Currency Wars

Because exchange rates are inherently relative prices, what matters for FX is not the absolute stance of one central bank's QE programme but its stance relative to other major central banks. A period in which one major central bank is actively expanding its balance sheet while others are holding steady or tightening tends to be associated with depreciation of the QE-conducting central bank's currency relative to the others — a dynamic sometimes (often pejoratively) labelled "currency wars" when multiple central banks appear to be using monetary easing partly as a means of weakening their currencies to support exports.

Direct FX Intervention by Major Central Banks

Definition — FX Intervention

FX intervention is the direct buying or selling of foreign currency by a central bank (or finance ministry, acting through the central bank) with the explicit aim of influencing the exchange rate, as distinct from the indirect influence exerted through interest rate or balance sheet policy.

Among major developed-market central banks, outright FX intervention is comparatively rare and typically reserved for episodes of disorderly market conditions — extremely rapid, one-directional moves that are judged to be unwarranted by fundamentals and disruptive to financial stability — rather than as a routine tool for managing the exchange rate level. This stands in contrast to the more frequent and often less disclosed intervention practices of many emerging market central banks, covered separately in the emerging markets FX material.

Remark — Channels of Major Central Bank Intervention

Unilateral intervention: a single central bank buys or sells its own currency in the open market. Because the FX market is so large relative to any single central bank's resources, unilateral intervention by a major central bank is generally believed to be effective mainly as a signal of policy intent — altering market expectations about future policy — rather than through the direct effect of the transaction on supply and demand.

Coordinated (concerted) intervention: multiple major central banks intervene simultaneously in the same direction, historically used in episodes judged severe enough to warrant a strong, internationally coordinated signal. Concerted intervention is rarer than unilateral intervention precisely because it requires international agreement, which makes it a particularly strong signal when it does occur.

Verbal intervention ("jawboning"): public statements by officials expressing concern about the level or pace of movement of a currency, without any accompanying transaction. Verbal intervention can move markets on its own by raising the perceived probability of subsequent actual intervention or policy action, and is often used as a lower-cost first step before — or instead of — actual market operations.
