1. FX Forwards and Swaps

This chapter moves beyond the spot market to the instruments that allow market participants to fix exchange rates for future dates and to fund currency positions across time. The FX forward is the building block: an agreement to exchange two currencies at a rate fixed today for delivery on a future date. Its price is governed by covered interest rate parity, the no-arbitrage relationship linking spot rates, forward rates, and the interest rate differential between the two currencies.

The FX swap - a simultaneous spot and forward transaction in opposite directions - is, by traded volume, the largest FX instrument in the world, central to short-term funding, hedge rolling, and liquidity management at banks and corporates alike. Finally, the cross-currency basis swap extends this idea to longer tenors with periodic floating-rate exchanges, and its non-zero basis spread is one of the most closely watched indicators of dollar funding stress and post-crisis market segmentation.

Together, these three instruments - forwards, FX swaps, and cross-currency basis swaps - form the term structure of currency funding, and a working understanding of covered interest rate parity (and its real-world breakdown) is essential for anything from corporate hedging to multi-currency derivatives discounting.
