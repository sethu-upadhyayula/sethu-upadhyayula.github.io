2.2 Funding, Collateral, and the Post-2008 Basis

The previous section quantified the cross-currency basis as a CIP deviation and attributed it broadly to regulatory balance sheet costs. This section looks underneath that headline number at the three concrete channels through which the basis manifests in day-to-day markets: differences in unsecured funding costs across currencies, the effect of collateral and CSA currency choice on derivatives valuation, and the money market spreads that traders watch as leading indicators of basis moves.

Funding Cost Differentials Across Currencies

Remark — Bank-Specific Funding Curves

In a frictionless world, every bank would borrow at the same risk-free rate in every currency, and CIP would hold automatically. In reality, each bank funds itself at a spread over the risk-free (OIS) rate that depends on its own credit, the currency, and the tenor — its funding curve. A USD-centric bank with abundant USD deposits but limited EUR deposits, for example, may fund EUR at a wider spread to €STR than it funds USD over SOFR.

When many market participants face an asymmetric funding picture — structurally short USD funding relative to their home currency, a common situation for non-US banks and corporates with USD asset exposure — the aggregate effect is persistent one-directional demand to convert home-currency funding into USD via the FX swap and cross-currency markets. This directional demand is what keeps the basis persistently negative for USD against most other G10 currencies rather than fluctuating randomly around zero.

Collateral, CSA Currency, and Valuation

For collateralized derivatives, the choice of collateral currency specified in the Credit Support Annex (CSA) directly affects the discount rate used to value future cash flows — and therefore the present value of any multi-currency trade.

Definition — Collateral (CSA) Discounting

Under a CSA, variation margin is posted in a specified collateral currency and earns interest at that currency's overnight rate (e.g., SOFR for USD CSAs, €STR for EUR CSAs). The correct discount rate for a cash flow is the rate paid on the collateral that backs it — not necessarily the risk-free rate of the currency in which the cash flow itself is denominated. A EUR-denominated cash flow under a USD CSA must be discounted using a EUR/USD cross-currency basis-adjusted curve, reflecting the cost of holding USD collateral against a EUR-denominated exposure.

Remark — Why This Matters for the Basis

Consider a USD-denominated interest rate swap collateralized in EUR (a "EUR CSA on a USD swap" — common for European counterparties trading with US dealers). The present value of the USD cash flows must be computed using a discount curve that reflects the cost of funding USD collateral requirements via EUR — i.e., a curve built using the EUR/USD cross-currency basis.

If the basis is $-30\text{bp}$, discounting USD cash flows off a EUR-collateral curve versus a USD-collateral (OIS) curve produces a materially different present value for a long-dated swap — often tens of basis points of notional for a 10-30 year trade. Since the 2008 crisis, dealers have had to maintain separate discount curves for every (cash flow currency, collateral currency) pair they trade, each built using the relevant cross-currency basis — a direct, mechanical consequence of the basis being persistently non-zero.

Money Market Spreads as a Basis Driver

The cross-currency basis does not move in isolation — it is closely linked to spreads observable in each currency's own money markets, particularly the spread between unsecured interbank rates (where still quoted) and secured/overnight rates, and the spread between term funding and overnight-indexed swap (OIS) rates.

Remark — Linking Money Market Spreads to the Basis

FRA-OIS / Libor-OIS-style spreads: a widening of the spread between term unsecured funding rates and overnight rates in one currency (signalling stress in that currency's interbank funding market) typically coincides with a widening cross-currency basis against that currency, as banks scramble to secure term funding through whatever channel — including the FX swap market — is cheapest.

Repo and secured funding spreads: in currencies where unsecured interbank lending has largely disappeared (as in much of the post-LIBOR world), repo spreads and the cost of secured term funding play an analogous role — a dealer's marginal cost of funding USD via repo versus via an FX swap directly determines where it is willing to quote the cross-currency basis.

Quarter-end and year-end effects: just as repo and OIS rates spike around regulatory reporting dates (the "turn" effect discussed in the rates fundamentals notes), the cross-currency basis widens sharply around the same dates, as the same balance-sheet constraints that drive the turn in repo markets also constrain banks' willingness to run matched FX swap books over those dates.

Remark — Looking Ahead

With CIP, the basis, and their funding-market drivers established, Chapter 3 turns to the practical task of building a full FX forward curve from spot, deposit rates, and FX swap points — a curve that, in the presence of a non-zero basis, embeds exactly the funding effects described in this section.
