2.1 Affine Models and the CIR Model

Vasicek and Hull-White produced bond prices of the exponential-affine form $P(t,T) = A(t,T)e^{-B(t,T)r(t)}$ as a consequence of having a Gaussian short rate with affine drift and constant diffusion. This section makes that structure explicit and general, then studies the Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) model - the canonical affine model whose diffusion term depends on the level of the short rate, ruling out negative rates.

The Affine Term Structure Framework

Definition - Affine Term Structure Model (ATSM)

A short-rate model $dr(t) = \mu(r(t),t)\,dt + \sigma(r(t),t)\,dW_t$ is an affine term structure model if both the drift $\mu$ and the squared diffusion $\sigma^2$ are affine (linear plus constant) functions of $r(t)$: $$\mu(r,t) = \alpha(t) + \beta(t)\,r, \qquad \sigma^2(r,t) = \gamma(t) + \delta(t)\,r$$ Under this condition, zero-coupon bond prices admit the exponential-affine form $P(t,T) = A(t,T)\,e^{-B(t,T)\,r(t)}$, where $A$ and $B$ solve a system of ordinary differential equations (Riccati equations) derived from the bond-pricing PDE, rather than requiring a full PDE solution in $r$ and $t$.

Remark - Why Affine Structure Matters

The practical payoff of the affine property is enormous: $A(t,T)$ and $B(t,T)$ depend only on $t$ and $T$ (through ODEs with at most a handful of parameters), not on the current state $r(t)$. This means bond prices, and often option prices, can be computed in closed form or via fast numerical integration of low-dimensional ODEs, rather than by solving a PDE or running Monte Carlo for every valuation. Vasicek and Hull-White are the Gaussian special case ($\delta(t)=0$); CIR, introduced next, is the simplest non-Gaussian affine model.

The Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model

The Vasicek model's Gaussian short rate can become negative. The CIR model (1985) modifies the diffusion term so that volatility shrinks to zero as $r(t) \to 0$, which - combined with a sufficiently strong upward drift near zero - keeps the short rate non-negative.

Definition - CIR Short Rate Dynamics

Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the CIR short rate follows a mean-reverting square-root diffusion: $$dr(t) = a(b-r(t))\,dt + \sigma\sqrt{r(t)}\,dW_t$$ where $a>0$ is the speed of mean reversion, $b>0$ is the long-run mean, and $\sigma>0$ governs volatility. The $\sqrt{r(t)}$ term means that as $r(t)\to0^+$, the diffusion vanishes while the drift $a(b-r(t)) \to ab > 0$ remains strictly positive, pushing the rate back up - preventing $r(t)$ from going negative.

Remark - The Feller Condition

Whether $r(t)=0$ is actually attainable depends on the parameters. The Feller condition $$2ab \geq \sigma^2$$ guarantees that $r(t)$ remains strictly positive for all $t>0$ if $r(0)>0$. If $2ab < \sigma^2$, the process can touch zero (and is instantaneously reflected back into positive territory), but never goes negative. Either way, CIR rules out negative rates entirely - in contrast to Vasicek/Hull-White, where $r(t)$ is normally distributed and can be negative with positive probability.

Bond Pricing Under CIR

Despite the more complicated square-root diffusion, CIR is affine in the sense of the definition above ($\gamma(t)=0$, $\delta(t)=\sigma^2$), and the bond-pricing PDE can still be solved in closed form via the affine ansatz.

Theorem - CIR Bond Price Formula

The price at time $t$ of a zero-coupon bond maturing at $T$ under CIR is $P(t,T) = A(t,T)\,e^{-B(t,T)\,r(t)}$, where, writing $\tau=T-t$ and $\gamma=\sqrt{a^2+2\sigma^2}$: $$B(t,T) = \frac{2(e^{\gamma\tau}-1)}{(\gamma+a)(e^{\gamma\tau}-1)+2\gamma}$$ $$A(t,T) = \left[\frac{2\gamma\,e^{(a+\gamma)\tau/2}}{(\gamma+a)(e^{\gamma\tau}-1)+2\gamma}\right]^{2ab/\sigma^2}$$

As in Vasicek, $B(t,T)$ and $A(t,T)$ depend only on $(t,T)$ and the model parameters $(a,b,\sigma)$, not on $r(t)$ itself - the hallmark of the affine structure. The resulting yield curve $r(t,T) = \big(B(t,T)r(t)-\ln A(t,T)\big)/\tau$ is again affine in $r(t)$.

Remark - The Distribution of $r(t)$ Under CIR

Unlike the Gaussian Vasicek short rate, $r(t)$ under CIR follows a (scaled) non-central chi-squared distribution conditional on $r(0)$, which is supported on $[0,\infty)$ - consistent with the non-negativity property above. This non-Gaussianity is what allows CIR to avoid negative rates, but it also means the short rate's marginal distribution is skewed, with a heavier right tail than the Gaussian case for the same mean and variance.

CIR in Practice: Trade-offs

Remark - Why CIR Is Less Common Than Hull-White Today

CIR's non-negativity was historically viewed as its key advantage over Vasicek. However, it comes at a cost: CIR with constant parameters $(a,b,\sigma)$ cannot, in general, be exactly fit to an arbitrary initial yield curve the way Hull-White's time-dependent $\theta(t)$ can - extending CIR to match an arbitrary curve (the "CIR++" or shifted-CIR approach) requires adding a deterministic shift function, which can reintroduce the possibility of negative rates that the original model was designed to avoid. Combined with the post-2012 era of negative policy rates (where Hull-White's Gaussian negative-rate tolerance became a practical advantage rather than a flaw, as discussed in Section 1.1), CIR's strict non-negativity became less essential, and Hull-White remains more widely used for single-factor short-rate modelling. CIR's lasting importance is instead as the building block for multi-factor affine models - particularly for modelling stochastic volatility and variance processes (e.g. in the Heston model for equity options) - and as the prototype for the general affine framework developed further in the next section.
