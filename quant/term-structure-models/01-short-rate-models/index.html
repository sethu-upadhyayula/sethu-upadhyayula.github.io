1. Short Rate Models

The earlier chapters treated the discount curve and forward rates as exogenously given, calibrated daily from market quotes. To price exotic and path-dependent interest rate derivatives — Bermudan swaptions, callable bonds, structured notes — we need a dynamic model for how the entire yield curve evolves through time, consistent with no-arbitrage and (ideally) consistent with today's observed curve and option volatilities.

This chapter develops the major families of term-structure models used in practice. We begin with short-rate models (Vasicek and Hull-White), in which the entire curve is driven by a single state variable, the instantaneous short rate. We then generalise to the Heath-Jarrow-Morton (HJM) framework, which models the full forward curve directly and shows how short-rate models arise as special cases. Finally, we cover the LIBOR Market Model (LMM/BGM), the market-standard framework for simulating discrete forward rates consistently with Black's caplet and swaption formulas, and the workhorse for Monte Carlo pricing of Bermudan and other path-dependent rates products.
