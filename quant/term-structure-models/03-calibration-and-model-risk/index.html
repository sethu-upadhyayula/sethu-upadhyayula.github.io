3. Calibration and Model Risk

A term-structure model is only as useful as its calibration: the process of choosing parameter values so that model prices match observed market prices for a chosen set of liquid instruments. Chapters 1 and 2 developed the theoretical machinery - Vasicek, Hull-White, CIR, multi-factor affine models - but said relatively little about how $(a,\sigma)$, $\theta(t)$, or the multi-factor parameters are actually pinned down in practice, or what happens when the chosen calibration instruments do not uniquely determine those parameters.

This chapter addresses both questions. We first walk through the calibration of short-rate models to two distinct types of market data - the initial discount curve, and the prices of caps and swaptions - and discuss how time-dependent parameters such as Hull-White's $\theta(t)$ are fitted. We then turn to model risk: the risk that a model, even when perfectly calibrated to today's market, produces materially wrong prices or hedges for instruments outside its calibration set, due to model misspecification, calibration instability, or extrapolation beyond the range of observed data.
