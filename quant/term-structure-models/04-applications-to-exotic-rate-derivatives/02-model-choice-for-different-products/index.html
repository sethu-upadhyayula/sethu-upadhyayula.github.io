4.2 Model Choice for Different Products

The preceding sections developed a toolkit of term-structure models - Vasicek and Hull-White, HJM, LMM, and the affine family including CIR and multi-factor extensions - along with the calibration and model-risk considerations that govern how each is fitted to the market. No single model is best for every product: the right choice depends on the dimensionality of the risk being priced, the payoff's sensitivity to correlation and the shape of the volatility surface, and the computational budget available on the desk. This closing section sets out a practical framework for matching models to products.

The Core Trade-Off: Tractability vs. Realism

Remark - Three Axes of Model Choice

Every term-structure model represents a compromise along three axes:

  • Dimensionality. A 1-factor short-rate model (Vasicek, Hull-White, CIR) has a single source of randomness driving the entire curve, so all rates are perfectly (or near-perfectly) correlated. LMM and multi-factor affine models allow richer, partially-decorrelated curve dynamics at the cost of a much larger state space.
  • Distributional fit. The model must be able to reproduce the market's smile/skew for the instruments that drive the product's value - Hull-White and CIR each imply a specific (and limited) shape for the implied volatility smile, whereas LMM with SABR-extended dynamics, discussed alongside Section 2's affine extensions, can be made consistent with an observed skew across strikes.
  • Computational cost. Closed-form or quasi-closed-form pricing (Vasicek, Hull-White, CIR bond and option formulas from Sections 1.1 and 2.1) is essentially free. PDE/lattice methods for 1-2 factor models are fast. Monte Carlo on LMM, especially with Longstaff-Schwartz for early exercise as in Section 4.1, is by far the most expensive - but sometimes unavoidable.

A Product-by-Product Guide

Definition - Vanilla and Quasi-Vanilla Products: Short-Rate Models

For products whose payoff depends on the curve at a single date, or on a single rate observed at a single date - caps/floors, European swaptions, and (to good approximation) callable bonds with a single call date - a 1-factor Hull-White model, calibrated as in Section 3.1 to match today's curve exactly and to a relevant slice of the cap or swaption volatility surface, is usually sufficient. The model's closed-form or near-closed-form pricing makes it fast to calibrate and fast to risk-manage (Greeks via finite differences or analytic formulas), and the single-factor assumption is a reasonable approximation when the product's value depends primarily on the level of rates rather than on the relative behaviour of different points on the curve.

Definition - Bermudan Swaptions and Other Multi-Exercise-Date Products: LMM

As established in Section 4.1, products with multiple exercise dates whose value depends on the joint distribution of swap rates across those dates - Bermudan swaptions, callable range accruals, and target-redemption note structures with multiple observation dates - require LMM. The model's ability to be calibrated simultaneously to the full diagonal of co-terminal European swaptions (Section 3.1) and to a realistic forward-rate correlation structure (Section 3.2) is essential, and Monte Carlo with Longstaff-Schwartz regression is the standard pricing engine despite its cost.

Definition - CMS Products and Convexity-Sensitive Payoffs: HJM or Multi-Factor Affine

Constant-maturity-swap (CMS) caps/floors, CMS spread options, and other payoffs whose value depends materially on the shape of the curve - not just its level - benefit from the extra factors of the HJM framework (Section 1.2) or a two- or three-factor affine model (Section 2.2). These products are sensitive to the correlation between rates at different points on the curve (e.g. the spread between a 2-year and a 10-year swap rate), which a 1-factor model cannot represent: in a 1-factor model, all points on the curve move together, so a spread option would have essentially zero optionality. A second factor governing the slope of the curve is the minimum needed to give such payoffs realistic value and risk.

Definition - Credit-Sensitive and Default-Linked Rate Products: CIR-Type Affine Models

Where the underlying state variable must remain non-negative - modelling a hazard rate or a short rate in a near-zero-rate environment - the CIR model and its multi-factor affine extensions (Section 2.1) are preferred over Vasicek/Hull-White, whose normally-distributed short rate can go negative. The same affine machinery that gives CIR its closed-form bond prices extends naturally to default intensity models, making it a natural choice when the product (e.g. a credit-linked note with rate-dependent coupons) couples interest-rate and credit risk.

Practical Considerations for Desk Model Selection

Remark - Beyond the Payoff: Operational Constraints

In practice, the choice of model on a trading desk is rarely made purely on theoretical grounds. Several operational factors weigh heavily:

  • Consistency across the book. A desk that risk-manages a large book of vanilla and exotic products typically prefers a single model family (often Hull-White for the vanilla book and LMM for the exotics book) so that hedges computed in one model are meaningful against positions priced in the other, and so that aggregate Greeks can be sensibly combined.
  • Speed of recalibration. Models that must be recalibrated intraday - to reprice a book against a moving market - favour the fast, often closed-form, calibration routines of Section 3.1. LMM's larger parameter set and Monte Carlo pricing make frequent full recalibration impractical; desks often recalibrate LMM less frequently and interpolate or perturb between full calibrations.
  • Model risk reserves. As discussed in Section 3.2, every model choice carries model risk that must be reserved against. A simpler model with well-understood, well-documented limitations (e.g. Hull-White's single-factor assumption) may carry a smaller, more confidently-estimated reserve than a more "realistic" but less transparent model whose mis-calibration risk is harder to bound.
  • Auditability and regulatory scrutiny. Simpler models are easier to explain to risk committees, auditors, and regulators. The incremental pricing accuracy of a more complex model must be weighed against the cost of justifying and maintaining it through model validation processes.
Remark - A Summary Heuristic

As a rule of thumb: use the simplest model that can be calibrated to the instruments that actually drive the product's risk. Single-date, single-rate payoffs call for 1-factor Hull-White or CIR. Payoffs sensitive to the relative behaviour of different curve points - CMS spreads, steepener/flattener structures - need at least two factors (HJM or multi-factor affine). Payoffs with multiple exercise dates whose value depends on the joint, correlated evolution of many forward rates - Bermudans and similar callable structures - need the full machinery of LMM with Monte Carlo and regression-based exercise. Matching the model to the product in this way keeps pricing, hedging, calibration, and model-risk management tractable across the desk's entire book.
