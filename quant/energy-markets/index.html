Energy Markets

Energy commodities — crude oil, refined products, natural gas, and the derivatives written on them — form one of the largest and most economically consequential corners of the commodity universe. Unlike financial assets, energy commodities are physical goods with storage costs, transportation constraints, seasonal demand patterns, and supply controlled in part by a small number of large producers. These notes develop the pricing conventions, market structure, and derivative instruments that together describe how energy prices are formed and risk is transferred.

We begin with crude oil markets: the global benchmarks (WTI and Brent), the quality differentials between crude grades, and the refining economics — crack spreads, product markets, and storage dynamics — that link the price of crude to the prices of the gasoline and distillates refined from it. The second chapter turns to natural gas, covering regional pricing hubs, seasonal demand, LNG and pipeline economics, and the underground storage cycle that drives the gas forward curve.

The third chapter develops the energy derivatives that trade on top of these physical markets — futures, swaps, and the term structure of the forward curve, including the Samuelson effect and the crack and spark spreads used to hedge refining and power generation margins. The fourth chapter addresses energy market structure and risk: OPEC+ supply management and geopolitical shocks, and the emerging carbon and renewable energy markets that are reshaping the economics of the energy transition. Together these four chapters provide a working foundation for understanding how energy prices are set, hedged, and stressed.
