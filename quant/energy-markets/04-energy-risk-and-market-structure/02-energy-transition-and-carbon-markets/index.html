4.2 Energy Transition, Carbon Markets, and Renewable Risk

The decarbonisation of the global energy system has given rise to its own family of markets - for the right to emit carbon, for the environmental attributes of clean generation, and for the future value (or stranded-asset risk) of fossil-fuel infrastructure. These markets are increasingly interconnected with the conventional oil, gas, and power markets covered in earlier chapters, and an understanding of energy market structure is incomplete without them.

Carbon Pricing and the EU ETS

Definition - Cap-and-Trade and Carbon Allowances

A cap-and-trade system sets an overall limit ("cap") on the total quantity of greenhouse gas emissions permitted from covered installations (e.g., power plants, industrial facilities, aviation) over a compliance period, and issues a corresponding number of tradable emissions allowances - each allowance conferring the right to emit one tonne of CO₂-equivalent. Covered installations must surrender allowances equal to their actual emissions at the end of each compliance period; an installation that emits less than its allocation can sell its surplus allowances, while one that emits more must buy additional allowances on the market. The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the largest and most established such system, covering power generation, industry, and (increasingly) other sectors across the European Union.

Remark - The Cap as the Source of Price

The price of an EU ETS allowance is fundamentally determined by the interaction between the cap - which is set by policy and typically declines over time according to a pre-announced trajectory - and the marginal cost of abatement across covered installations. If the cap is tight relative to "business as usual" emissions, installations must either reduce emissions (through fuel switching, efficiency improvements, or curtailment) or buy allowances from others who have done so, and the allowance price rises to the level at which the marginal installation is indifferent between abating and paying. Because the cap trajectory is known in advance, allowance prices also embed expectations about future scarcity - a market that expects the cap to tighten sharply in future years will tend to price allowances today at a level consistent with that future scarcity, discounted back, an intertemporal arbitrage relationship sometimes summarised by the idea that allowance prices across years should grow at roughly the rate of interest (since a firm can choose to bank an allowance for later use rather than surrender it now).

Example - Allowance Cost Passing Through to Power Prices

Consider a coal-fired power plant with an emissions intensity of $1.0$ tonnes CO₂ per MWh generated, operating in a market where the EU ETS allowance price is $\text{€}80$/tonne. The carbon cost of generating one MWh from this plant is $1.0 \times 80 = \text{€}80$/MWh, which adds directly to the plant's variable cost alongside its fuel cost. If this coal plant is the marginal unit setting the power price in a given hour, the allowance price effectively becomes a component of the power price itself - a rise in the EU ETS price of $\text{€}10$/tonne raises the marginal cost (and hence, potentially, the power price) by $\text{€}10$/MWh for that plant. A gas-fired plant with a lower emissions intensity (e.g., $0.4$ tonnes CO₂/MWh) sees a smaller carbon cost addition ($\text{€}32$/MWh at the same allowance price) - so a rising carbon price tends to erode the relative cost advantage of higher-carbon-intensity generation, shifting the merit order toward lower-carbon sources over time.

Renewable Energy Certificates

Separately from carbon allowances, many jurisdictions operate markets for certificates that represent the environmental attributes of electricity generated from renewable sources, unbundled from the physical electricity itself.

Definition - Renewable Energy Certificate (REC)

A Renewable Energy Certificate (REC, or in Europe a Guarantee of Origin) represents the bundle of environmental, social, and other non-power attributes associated with one MWh of electricity generated from a qualifying renewable source. RECs are typically issued to the generator at the point of production and can be sold separately from the underlying electricity, which is sold into the wholesale power market like any other generation. A consumer (or utility, subject to a renewable portfolio standard or similar regulatory obligation) that purchases RECs can claim the corresponding renewable attribute - for example, "100% renewable electricity" - even if the physical electrons it consumes are drawn from the general grid and are indistinguishable from electricity generated by any other source.

Remark - RECs as a Separate Revenue Stream and Price Signal

Because RECs can be sold independently of the underlying power, they represent an additional revenue stream for renewable generators on top of whatever they receive for the electricity itself - a wind or solar project's total revenue per MWh is the sum of the wholesale power price it receives and the REC price. The REC price is determined by the supply of qualifying renewable generation relative to demand for certificates, which in many markets is driven by regulatory mandates (renewable portfolio standards requiring utilities to source a minimum percentage of supply from renewables, satisfied either by owning/contracting renewable generation directly or by purchasing RECs) and by voluntary corporate procurement. A REC price therefore reflects the marginal cost of satisfying the binding renewable requirement - if the mandated renewable share is comfortably exceeded by existing qualifying generation, REC prices tend to be low (reflecting abundant supply relative to the requirement), while a mandate that is binding and difficult to meet from existing supply tends to support higher REC prices.

Transition Risk for Fossil-Fuel Curves

The prospect of an accelerating energy transition introduces a distinct risk dimension for participants holding long-dated exposure to fossil-fuel prices or assets: the possibility that future demand for oil, gas, or coal will decline more rapidly than priced into current long-dated forward curves, leaving long-dated forward prices - and the value of physical assets whose economics depend on them - lower than currently anticipated.

Remark - Transition Risk and the Long End of the Curve

Unlike near-dated prices, which are governed primarily by current physical balances (Chapters 1-3), far-dated forward prices for fossil fuels embed the market's aggregate expectation of supply and demand many years into the future - including expectations about the pace of electrification, the deployment of renewable generation and storage, and the tightening of climate policy. A repricing of these expectations - for example, following a policy announcement that accelerates a country's planned phase-out of coal generation, or evidence that electric vehicle adoption is proceeding faster than previously assumed - can shift the far end of the relevant fossil-fuel forward curve without any corresponding change in current physical fundamentals, because the change is entirely about the future balance rather than the current one.

For holders of long-lived physical assets (e.g., an oil field with decades of remaining reserves, or a coal-fired power plant with a long remaining operating life), this transition risk is sometimes referred to as stranded asset risk: the possibility that an asset's expected future cash flows - which depend on both the volumes it can sell and the prices at which it can sell them - are reduced by a faster-than-expected transition, such that the asset's economic value is "stranded" below the level implied by its physical reserves or capacity alone. This is conceptually similar to the long-dated convexity and term-structure considerations discussed for conventional forward curves (Section 3.1), but the source of the long-dated uncertainty is structural and policy-driven rather than purely a function of storage and production-response dynamics.

Carbon Credit Derivatives

As carbon markets have matured, a derivatives layer analogous to that in conventional commodity markets has developed on top of the underlying allowances and credits, allowing participants to hedge and take positions on carbon prices without transacting in the underlying instruments directly.

Remark - Futures, Forwards, and Options on Carbon

Exchange-traded futures on EU ETS allowances (and on similar instruments in other cap-and-trade systems) trade in broadly the same way as the energy futures discussed in Section 3.1 - standardised contracts for delivery (transfer of allowances) at specified future dates, cleared through a central counterparty. A compliance entity that wishes to lock in the cost of allowances it will need to surrender at the end of a future compliance period can buy carbon futures today, fixing that cost in the same way a fuel consumer might hedge fuel costs with energy futures. Options on carbon allowances - both exchange-traded and OTC - allow participants to express views on the direction or volatility of carbon prices, or to hedge asymmetric exposures (for example, a generator that wants protection against a sharp rise in carbon costs but does not want to give up the benefit of carbon prices falling might buy a cap, structured as a call option on the allowance price).

Remark - Voluntary Carbon Credits versus Compliance Allowances

A separate but related market exists for voluntary carbon credits - credits generated by projects (e.g., reforestation, methane capture, renewable energy projects in jurisdictions without their own cap-and-trade system) that represent a claimed reduction or removal of emissions, purchased voluntarily by organisations seeking to offset their own emissions footprint outside of any regulatory mandate. Unlike EU ETS allowances, which derive their value from a binding, government-enforced cap with legal consequences for non-compliance, voluntary credits derive their value from the credibility of the underlying project's claimed emissions impact and from voluntary corporate demand - factors that are considerably harder to standardise and verify, and which have historically led to much greater price dispersion and quality differentiation across voluntary credits than is observed across compliance allowances within a single cap-and-trade scheme.
