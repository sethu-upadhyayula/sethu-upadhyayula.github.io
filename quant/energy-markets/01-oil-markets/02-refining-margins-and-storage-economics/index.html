1.2 Refining Margins, Product Markets, and Storage Economics

Crude oil itself has little direct end-use value - it must be refined into the products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, heating oil, residual fuel) that are actually consumed. The economics of this conversion process, and the markets for the resulting products, are a primary driver of crude demand and of the term structure of crude prices.

Refining Margins and the Crack Spread

A refiner's profitability depends on the spread between the prices it receives for refined products and the price it pays for crude - the refining margin. Because actual refinery economics depend on a complex slate of dozens of products and a specific refinery's configuration, the industry uses simplified proxies called crack spreads to approximate refining profitability using liquid, exchange-traded benchmarks.

Definition - 3-2-1 Crack Spread

The 3-2-1 crack spread approximates the margin from refining 3 barrels of crude oil into 2 barrels of gasoline and 1 barrel of distillate (heating oil/diesel): $$\text{3-2-1 Crack} = \frac{2 \times P_{\text{gasoline}} + 1 \times P_{\text{distillate}} - 3 \times P_{\text{crude}}}{3}$$ where all prices are expressed per barrel. RBOB gasoline and ULSD (heating oil) futures are quoted per gallon and must be converted to per-barrel terms using 42 gallons per barrel. The result is an approximate gross margin per barrel of crude processed, before accounting for other operating costs (energy, labour, catalysts), yield differences, and byproducts not captured by the simplified 2:1 product mix.

Remark - Other Crack Spread Ratios

The 3-2-1 ratio approximates a "balanced" refinery output. Other ratios are used to approximate different refinery configurations or to isolate a single product: the 1-1-0 crack (gasoline only, $P_{\text{gasoline}} - P_{\text{crude}}$) and the 1-0-1 crack (distillate only, $P_{\text{distillate}} - P_{\text{crude}}$) are simple single-product margins. The choice of ratio should roughly match the yield slate of the refinery or trading book being hedged.

Example - Computing a 3-2-1 Crack Spread

Suppose WTI crude trades at $\$75.00$/bbl, RBOB gasoline futures trade at $\$2.30$/gallon, and ULSD futures trade at $\$2.50$/gallon. Converting product prices to per-barrel terms ($\times 42$):

$P_{\text{gasoline}} = 2.30 \times 42 = \$96.60$/bbl, $\quad P_{\text{distillate}} = 2.50 \times 42 = \$105.00$/bbl.

$$\text{3-2-1 Crack} = \frac{2(96.60) + 1(105.00) - 3(75.00)}{3} = \frac{298.20 - 225.00}{3} \approx \$24.40 \text{ per barrel}$$ A widening crack spread signals improving refining economics and tends to encourage refiners to run at higher utilisation rates, increasing crude demand (supporting crude prices) and increasing product supply (capping product prices) - a feedback mechanism that links crude and product markets and tends to mean-revert crack spreads over time.

Product Markets: Gasoline and Distillates

Gasoline (RBOB futures in the U.S.) and distillates (ULSD/heating oil futures, used as a proxy for on-road diesel, off-road diesel, and to a lesser extent jet fuel) each have their own term structure, driven by seasonal demand patterns that are often independent of - and sometimes counter to - the crude curve.

Remark - Seasonal Demand Patterns

Gasoline demand in the U.S. peaks during the summer driving season (roughly Memorial Day to Labor Day). Refiners must switch to summer-grade gasoline - blends with lower Reid Vapor Pressure (RVP) to reduce evaporative emissions in warm weather - in the spring, a transition that temporarily reduces effective supply and tends to widen the gasoline crack ahead of the driving season ("spring crack rally").

Distillates (heating oil, diesel) see winter demand from space heating in colder climates (heating oil remains common in the U.S. Northeast), plus year-round demand from diesel-powered freight, agriculture, construction, and rail. Because diesel demand tracks broad industrial and freight activity, the diesel crack is often watched as a leading indicator of economic activity - a widening diesel crack alongside falling gasoline demand can signal a shift in the economy toward goods movement and away from consumer travel, or vice versa.

OPEC+ and Supply Dynamics

Unlike most commodity markets, a substantial share of global crude oil production is coordinated by a producer alliance. The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries and its allies (OPEC+, which includes Russia, Kazakhstan, and other major non-OPEC producers alongside the original OPEC membership) periodically agree on production targets intended to manage the balance between global supply and demand and, by extension, the price of crude.

Remark - OPEC+ Mechanics and Market Impact

Spare capacity is the difference between a producer's maximum sustainable output and its current production - effectively, the ability to bring on additional supply within a short timeframe (typically 30-90 days). OPEC+ spare capacity, concentrated almost entirely in a handful of Gulf producers (principally Saudi Arabia and the UAE), acts as the market's buffer against supply disruptions elsewhere; when spare capacity is thin, the market becomes acutely sensitive to outages, sanctions, or geopolitical events anywhere in the world.

Compliance refers to the degree to which member countries actually implement announced production targets. Compliance is monitored via independent secondary-source production estimates, tanker tracking, and satellite data, since self-reported figures can diverge from actual output (whether due to capacity constraints, sanctions evasion, or strategic over/under-production).

Non-OPEC supply response - particularly U.S. shale - is comparatively short-cycle: horizontal wells with hydraulic fracturing can be drilled, completed, and brought online within months, in contrast to the multi-year lead times of conventional offshore or oil-sands projects. This makes shale production more responsive to price signals and acts as a partial counterweight to OPEC+ supply management - high prices induced by OPEC+ cuts tend to elicit a shale supply response that can erode the effectiveness of those cuts over time.

Storage Economics and Contango/Backwardation

The relationship between the spot price of crude and the prices of futures contracts for future delivery - the shape of the futures curve - is governed by the economics of storing the physical commodity. This is the cost-of-carry relationship, modified for commodities by the concept of convenience yield.

Definition - Cost of Carry and Convenience Yield

For a storable commodity with spot price $S_t$, the no-arbitrage futures price $F_{t,T}$ for delivery at $T$ satisfies: $$F_{t,T} = S_t\, e^{(r + u - y)(T-t)}$$ where $r$ is the risk-free rate, $u$ is the storage cost (as a rate), and $y$ is the convenience yield - the non-monetary benefit of holding the physical commodity (e.g., the ability to avoid disruptions to a refiner's input supply, or to meet unexpected demand). When $y$ is large relative to $r+u$, $F_{t,T} < S_t$: the curve is in backwardation. When $r+u$ dominates $y$, $F_{t,T} > S_t$: the curve is in contango.

Example - Storage and the Curve at Cushing, April 2020

When Cushing inventories approach the working capacity of the storage hub, the marginal cost of storing an additional barrel rises sharply - at the limit, there is simply nowhere to put it, and the effective storage cost $u$ becomes very large (or infinite). By the cost-of-carry relationship, this pushes $F_{t,T}$ far above $S_t$ for any prompt delivery - an extremely steep contango. In the extreme case of the May 2020 WTI contract expiry on April 20, 2020, demand destruction from COVID-19 lockdowns combined with near-full storage meant holders of expiring futures who could not arrange physical storage or transport had to pay counterparties to take delivery, resulting in a settlement price of $-\$37.63$/bbl.

Conversely, when Cushing inventories are low and the prompt market is tight, the convenience yield $y$ of holding physical barrels rises (immediate access to crude becomes valuable), pushing the curve toward backwardation. The WTI front-month/second-month calendar spread is therefore one of the most direct, continuously observable signals of physical tightness or oversupply at Cushing.

Remark - The Storage Arbitrage Bound

In a contango market, a trader can in principle buy spot crude, pay to store it, and simultaneously sell a futures contract for delivery at $T$, locking in the difference. This "cash-and-carry" trade is only profitable if $F_{t,T} - S_t$ exceeds the actual cost of physical storage and financing over $[t,T]$; otherwise the trade loses money even though the curve is in contango. This places a practical (rather than purely theoretical) upper bound on how steep contango can become before storage capacity is bid up and filled by traders executing this arbitrage - which is itself part of why storage tanks filled so rapidly in April 2020.
