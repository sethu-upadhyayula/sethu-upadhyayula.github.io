1.1 Crude Benchmarks, Grades, and Quality Differentials

Crude oil is not a single homogeneous commodity. Hundreds of distinct grades are produced around the world, varying in density, sulphur content, and the mix of refined products they yield. To make this heterogeneous market tradable, the industry has converged on a small number of benchmark crudes whose futures prices serve as reference points, with every other grade trading at a differential to one of these benchmarks.

WTI and Brent

Definition - WTI and Brent

West Texas Intermediate (WTI) is a light, sweet (low-sulphur) crude oil that serves as the pricing benchmark for North American crude. The NYMEX WTI futures contract specifies physical delivery at Cushing, Oklahoma, a major pipeline and storage hub at the crossroads of U.S. crude pipeline infrastructure.

Brent is a light, sweet crude blend produced from North Sea fields, used as the benchmark for roughly two-thirds of internationally traded crude, including most of the crude priced out of the Middle East, Africa, and Europe. ICE Brent futures settle financially against the ICE Brent Index, which is itself derived from the underlying physical Dated Brent market - a complex of cash (Dated), forward (BFOE - Brent, Forties, Oseberg, Ekofisk), and contract-for-difference (CFD) instruments.

The WTI-Brent spread is one of the most closely watched relationships in the oil market. It reflects relative supply/demand balances between the landlocked U.S. mid-continent and the seaborne Atlantic Basin/global market, the available pipeline and export capacity connecting U.S. production to the coast, and U.S. crude export policy (the export ban that was in place until late 2015 historically forced WTI to trade at a structural discount to Brent). Since the shale boom, WTI has typically traded at a discount of a few dollars to Brent, though the spread can swing sharply - including periods where WTI trades at a premium - depending on relative inventory builds and pipeline bottlenecks.

Crude Quality: API Gravity and Sulphur Content

Every crude grade is characterised primarily along two physical dimensions that determine both its refining value and the cost of processing it.

Definition - API Gravity and Sweet/Sour

API gravity, defined by the American Petroleum Institute, measures the density of crude oil relative to water: $$\text{API}^\circ = \frac{141.5}{\text{SG}} - 131.5$$ where $\text{SG}$ is specific gravity at $60^\circ\text{F}$. Higher API gravity corresponds to a lighter, less dense crude. "Light" crudes (API $> 31^\circ$, roughly) yield a greater proportion of high-value light products (gasoline, naphtha, jet fuel, diesel) on distillation; "heavy" crudes (API $< 22^\circ$) yield more residual fuel oil and require additional upgrading (coking, hydrocracking) to produce light products.

Sulphur content, expressed as a percentage of weight, determines whether a crude is "sweet" (typically $< 0.5\%$ sulphur) or "sour" ($> 0.5\%$, often $1$-$3\%$ or more for grades like Saudi Heavy or Mexican Maya). Sour crudes require additional hydrotreating/desulphurisation capacity to produce fuels meeting environmental sulphur specifications, raising processing cost and reducing the price refiners are willing to pay relative to sweet crude.

Remark - Why Quality Matters for Price

A refinery is a fixed configuration of distillation, conversion (cracking, coking), and treating units, each sized to process crude within a certain range of API gravity and sulphur content. A refinery configured for light sweet crude can process heavy sour crude only at reduced yield and higher cost (or not at all without modification). Consequently, the price a given crude commands depends not just on its intrinsic quality but on the configuration of refineries that can economically run it - a heavy sour grade is worth more in a market with abundant coking capacity than in one without.

Quality (Grade) Differentials

Each traded crude grade - Dubai/Oman, Urals, WCS (Western Canadian Select), Maya, ESPO, Murban, and dozens of others - trades at a differential to the relevant regional benchmark (Brent for most of the Eastern Hemisphere and Atlantic Basin, WTI for North America, Dubai/Oman for the Middle East/Asia complex). This differential compresses and widens with several drivers.

Remark - Drivers of Grade Differentials

Light-heavy spread: when light-heavy product spreads (e.g., gasoline minus fuel oil) are wide, refiners value light sweet crude more highly relative to heavy sour crude, and heavy/sour differentials to the light/sweet benchmark widen (heavy crude becomes cheaper, relatively).

Refining capacity utilisation and configuration: a region with abundant coking/hydrocracking capacity can absorb more heavy sour crude, narrowing its discount; outages at complex refineries that process heavy crude widen the discount for heavy grades.

Freight and logistics: the cost and availability of tankers or pipeline capacity to move a given grade to its natural market affects the netback value at the wellhead, and therefore the differential at which it is sold.

Example - Computing a Grade Differential

Suppose Dated Brent is trading at $\$82.00$/bbl, and a cargo of Urals (a medium-sour Russian export grade priced off Dated Brent) is offered at a differential of $-\$4.50$/bbl. The outright price of the Urals cargo is: $$P_{\text{Urals}} = P_{\text{Brent}} + \text{differential} = 82.00 + (-4.50) = \$77.50/\text{bbl}$$

If sour-crude refining margins subsequently widen - say because heavy/sour processing capacity comes back online after maintenance - demand for Urals-type grades increases, and the differential might narrow to $-\$3.00$/bbl, raising the outright Urals price to $\$79.00$/bbl even with Brent unchanged. Grade differentials are therefore an independent source of price variation layered on top of the benchmark price itself, and are actively traded in their own right (e.g., via the Platts/Argus assessed differential market).

The Cushing Hub and WTI Delivery Mechanics

Cushing, Oklahoma - the delivery point specified in the NYMEX WTI futures contract - sits at the intersection of a dense network of pipelines connecting it to the Permian Basin, the Gulf Coast, and the U.S. mid-continent. Because the futures contract requires physical delivery (or an equivalent financial settlement against the spot price) at this single location, the balance of crude flowing into and out of Cushing, and the level of inventory held in storage tanks there, directly affects the deliverable supply underlying the contract and therefore the WTI price itself.

Remark - Cushing Inventories and the Curve

U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) weekly inventory reports for Cushing are closely watched by the market. Rising inventories - particularly as they approach the working storage capacity of the hub - signal an oversupplied local market and tend to push the front-month WTI contract down relative to deferred months (toward contango), since holders of physical barrels with nowhere to store them must discount the prompt price to find a buyer. Falling inventories signal tightness and push the curve toward backwardation. The relationship between Cushing stock levels and the front-month/second-month WTI calendar spread is one of the most direct physical-to-financial linkages in any commodity market.
