1. Oil Markets

Crude oil is the largest commodity market by traded value and the benchmark for much of the broader energy complex. Oil markets are characterised by a global physical trade in many distinct grades of crude, a sophisticated downstream refining industry that converts crude into a slate of products, and a supply side dominated by a small number of large producers whose output decisions move global prices.

Crude Benchmarks: WTI and Brent

Definition — WTI and Brent

West Texas Intermediate (WTI) is a light, sweet (low-sulphur) crude oil that serves as the pricing benchmark for North American crude. The NYMEX WTI futures contract specifies physical delivery at Cushing, Oklahoma, a major pipeline and storage hub.

Brent is a light, sweet crude blend produced from North Sea fields, used as the benchmark for roughly two-thirds of internationally traded crude. ICE Brent futures are cash-settled (or settle into a forward contract for physical delivery, EFP), and Brent's price is also referenced via a complex of dated Brent, Brent CFDs, and the Dated-to-Frontline swap market.

The WTI–Brent spread reflects relative supply/demand balances in the U.S. versus the Atlantic Basin and global markets, transportation logistics (pipeline capacity out of the U.S. shale basins, tanker availability), and U.S. crude export policy. The spread can swing from WTI trading at a premium to Brent (rare in recent years) to WTI trading at a substantial discount during periods of U.S. oversupply.

Crude Grades and Quality Differentials

Remark — Grade Characteristics

API gravity measures the density of crude oil relative to water; higher API gravity indicates a "lighter" crude that yields a greater proportion of high-value products (gasoline, diesel) on refining. Sulphur content determines whether a crude is "sweet" (low sulphur, easier and cheaper to refine to meet environmental fuel specifications) or "sour" (high sulphur, requiring additional desulphurisation capacity).

Each crude grade trades at a differential to the relevant benchmark reflecting its quality, location, and the refining capacity configured to process it. Heavy, sour grades (e.g., Maya, Western Canadian Select) typically trade at a discount to light, sweet benchmarks, with the discount widening when sour-crude refining capacity is constrained or light-heavy product spreads compress.

Refining Margins and Crack Spreads

Refiners purchase crude oil and sell refined products; their economics depend on the spread between product prices and crude cost — the refining margin. The most widely quoted proxy for refining profitability is the crack spread, which approximates the gross margin from "cracking" a barrel of crude into its constituent products.

Definition — 3-2-1 Crack Spread

The 3-2-1 crack spread approximates the margin from refining 3 barrels of crude oil into 2 barrels of gasoline and 1 barrel of distillate (heating oil/diesel): $$\text{3-2-1 Crack} = \frac{2 \times P_{\text{gasoline}} + 1 \times P_{\text{distillate}} - 3 \times P_{\text{crude}}}{3}$$ where all prices are expressed per barrel (gasoline and distillate futures, quoted per gallon, must be converted using 42 gallons per barrel). The result is an approximate gross margin per barrel of crude processed, before accounting for other operating costs, energy use, and yield differences across refinery configurations.

Example — Computing a 3-2-1 Crack Spread

Suppose WTI crude trades at $\$75$/bbl, RBOB gasoline futures trade at $\$2.30$/gallon, and ULSD (heating oil) futures trade at $\$2.50$/gallon. Converting product prices to per-barrel terms ($\times 42$):

$P_{\text{gasoline}} = 2.30 \times 42 = \$96.60$/bbl, $\quad P_{\text{distillate}} = 2.50 \times 42 = \$105.00$/bbl.

$$\text{3-2-1 Crack} = \frac{2(96.60) + 105.00 - 3(75)}{3} = \frac{298.20 - 225}{3} \approx \$24.40 \text{ per barrel}.$$ A widening crack spread signals improving refining economics and tends to encourage refiners to run at higher utilisation rates, increasing crude demand and product supply — a feedback mechanism that links crude and product markets.

Product Markets: Gasoline and Distillates

Gasoline (RBOB futures in the U.S.) and distillates (heating oil/ULSD futures, used as a proxy for diesel and jet fuel) exhibit pronounced seasonal demand patterns that drive their own term structures, often independent of the crude curve.

Remark — Seasonal Demand Patterns

Gasoline demand peaks in the Northern Hemisphere summer driving season (roughly Memorial Day to Labor Day in the U.S.), and refiners switch to summer-grade (lower Reid vapor pressure) gasoline blends in spring, which can temporarily tighten supply and widen cracks.

Distillates (heating oil, diesel) see winter demand from heating in colder climates, plus year-round demand from diesel-powered freight and agricultural/construction equipment. Diesel demand also tracks industrial activity, making the diesel crack a useful barometer of economic activity.

OPEC+ and Supply Dynamics

Unlike most commodities, a substantial share of global crude oil production is coordinated by a cartel-like body. The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries and its allies (OPEC+, including Russia and other major producers) periodically agree on production targets intended to manage global supply relative to demand.

Remark — OPEC+ Mechanics and Market Impact

Spare capacity: the difference between a producer's maximum sustainable output and current production. OPEC+ spare capacity (concentrated in a handful of Gulf producers) acts as a buffer against supply disruptions; when spare capacity is low, markets become highly sensitive to outages.

Compliance: announced production cuts or increases are not always fully implemented; the market closely monitors actual output (via tanker tracking, satellite data, secondary source estimates) versus quotas.

Non-OPEC supply response: U.S. shale production is comparatively short-cycle (wells can be drilled and brought online in months rather than years), making it more responsive to price signals than conventional OPEC+ production, and acting as a partial counterweight to OPEC+ supply management.

Storage Economics and the Cushing Hub

Cushing, Oklahoma — the delivery point for NYMEX WTI futures — is a major pipeline interconnection and storage hub. Inventory levels at Cushing, reported weekly by the U.S. Energy Information Administration, are closely watched because they directly affect the deliverable supply underlying the futures contract.

Example — Storage and the Curve at Cushing

When Cushing inventories approach tank capacity (as occurred dramatically in April 2020), the marginal cost of storing an additional barrel rises sharply — there may simply be nowhere to put it. This pushes the prompt futures price down relative to deferred contracts (steep contango), since holders of expiring contracts who cannot arrange storage or transport must sell at a steep discount, or even pay to have the oil taken away, as seen in the negative WTI prices of April 20, 2020.

Conversely, when Cushing inventories are low, the convenience yield of holding physical barrels rises, and the curve tends toward backwardation as immediate supply becomes relatively scarce. Cushing storage levels and the WTI calendar spread (e.g., the front-month minus second-month contract) are therefore tightly linked.
