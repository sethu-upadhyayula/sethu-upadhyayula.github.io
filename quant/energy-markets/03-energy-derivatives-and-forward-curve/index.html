3. Energy Derivatives and the Forward Curve

Having established the physical and seasonal drivers of crude oil and natural gas prices, this chapter turns to the derivative instruments traded on top of these physical markets and the shape of the resulting forward curve. We compare futures and swaps as the two principal vehicles for taking forward price exposure in energy, and develop the economic drivers of contango and backwardation. A key feature unique to commodity term structures is the Samuelson effect — the tendency for volatility to rise as a contract approaches expiry — which has direct consequences for option pricing and hedge ratios across the curve.

The second section develops the cross-commodity spread structures that are the bread and butter of energy hedging desks: the crack spread (the refiner's hedge of crude against refined products) and the spark spread (the power generator's hedge of electricity against the fuel used to produce it). We work through the mechanics of structuring these spreads as tradable instruments and computing the hedge ratios that make them effective risk management tools.
