2.2 LNG, Pipeline Economics, and Storage/Injection-Withdrawal Cycles

The previous section established that natural gas trades at regionally distinct hubs connected by transport infrastructure with finite capacity. This section examines that infrastructure in more detail - liquefied natural gas (LNG), the flexible long-distance link between regional markets, and pipeline capacity, the rigid short-distance link - and then turns to underground storage, the mechanism that allows gas produced relatively steadily through the year to meet sharply seasonal demand.

LNG: Liquefaction, Shipping, and Regasification

Definition - The LNG Value Chain

Converting pipeline gas into a globally tradable commodity requires three stages: liquefaction (cooling gas to approximately $-162^\circ\text{C}$, reducing its volume by a factor of about 600, at a dedicated liquefaction terminal); shipping (transport in specialised cryogenic tankers); and regasification (warming the LNG back to a gaseous state at an import terminal for injection into the destination market's pipeline grid). Each stage has its own capital-intensive, long-lived infrastructure with capacity that cannot be quickly expanded in response to price signals - liquefaction trains and import terminals typically take years to permit and construct.

Remark - The LNG Netback and Regional Price Convergence

The "netback" value of gas at the liquefaction terminal is the destination market price minus shipping cost minus regasification cost minus a liquefaction fee/margin: $$\text{Netback}_{\text{export}} = P_{\text{destination}} - C_{\text{shipping}} - C_{\text{regas}} - C_{\text{liquefaction}}$$ When this netback exceeds the price available in the domestic market of the exporting country, cargoes are routed to the higher-netback destination - this is the mechanism by which LNG flows arbitrage price differences between, say, Asia (JKM, the Japan-Korea Marker) and Europe (TTF). Because the global LNG fleet is finite and cargoes can be redirected mid-voyage, LNG provides a degree of global price convergence that pipeline-only markets lack - but only up to the limits of available liquefaction and shipping capacity, which, as discussed in 2.1, can itself become the binding constraint during periods of regional stress.

Pipeline Capacity Constraints

Within a continent, gas moves primarily by pipeline. Pipeline capacity between two points is fixed by physical infrastructure (pipe diameter, compressor stations) and, unlike LNG shipping, cannot be redirected - gas flows where the pipes go. When demand at one end of a pipeline corridor exceeds the capacity available to serve it, the local price can decouple sharply from prices elsewhere on the network, even over relatively short distances.

Remark - Basis Blowouts from Pipeline Constraints

A classic example is the price relationship between Appalachian production hubs and downstream demand centers during periods of high local production growth: if pipeline takeaway capacity out of a production region is fully subscribed, additional production has nowhere to go, and the local price can fall to a steep discount to Henry Hub - in extreme cases, even to near-zero or negative prices, as producers pay to have associated gas (a byproduct of oil drilling) taken away rather than shutting in oil production. Conversely, demand hubs at the end of constrained pipeline corridors - for example, New England in winter, which has limited pipeline connectivity to the rest of the U.S. gas grid - can see prices spike to many multiples of Henry Hub during cold weather events, because no amount of price increase at Henry Hub can bring more physical gas through a pipe that is already at capacity.

Underground Storage and the Injection/Withdrawal Cycle

Because production is relatively stable through the year while demand is highly seasonal, the gas market relies on underground storage facilities (depleted reservoirs, aquifers, and salt caverns) to bridge the gap. Gas is injected into storage during the lower-demand "injection season" (roughly April through October in the Northern Hemisphere) and withdrawn during the higher-demand "withdrawal season" (November through March).

Definition - Storage No-Arbitrage Bound

Let $P_S$ be the price of gas in the injection season (summer) and $P_W$ the price in the withdrawal season (winter), and let $C_{\text{store}}$ be the all-in cost of injecting, holding, and withdrawing one unit of gas (including financing cost over the holding period). A storage operator can buy gas in summer, store it, and sell it in winter; this trade is profitable whenever: $$P_W - P_S > C_{\text{store}}$$ Operators compete to exploit this spread by bidding for storage capacity, which tends to compress the summer-winter spread toward $C_{\text{store}}$ in a competitive market with abundant storage capacity. When storage capacity itself is the binding constraint (all capacity contracted), the spread can persist above $C_{\text{store}}$, since the marginal unit of additional storage capacity is unavailable at any price.

Example - A Storage Arbitrage Trade

Suppose the August (summer) Henry Hub futures contract trades at $\$2.50$/MMBtu and the January (winter) contract trades at $\$4.20$/MMBtu. A storage operator estimates the all-in cost of injecting in August, holding through the season, and withdrawing in January - including injection/withdrawal fees charged by the storage facility and the financing cost of the gas held in inventory - at $\$1.00$/MMBtu.

The summer-winter spread is $4.20 - 2.50 = \$1.70$/MMBtu, which exceeds the $\$1.00$/MMBtu storage cost by $\$0.70$/MMBtu. The operator can lock in this margin by simultaneously buying the August contract, selling the January contract, and contracting the necessary storage capacity - a "summer-winter spread" trade that is one of the most common structures in gas storage trading.

If many operators identify the same opportunity and storage capacity is available, increased buying of the August contract and selling of the January contract will narrow the spread toward $\$1.00$/MMBtu (plus any residual scarcity premium on storage capacity itself). The observed summer-winter spread in the market is therefore informative about the marginal cost and availability of storage capacity at any given time.
