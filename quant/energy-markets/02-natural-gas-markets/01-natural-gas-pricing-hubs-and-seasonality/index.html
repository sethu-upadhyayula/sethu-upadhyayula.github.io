2.1 Natural Gas Pricing, Hubs, and Seasonality

Natural gas is far more expensive to transport and store, relative to its energy content, than crude oil. A barrel of crude can be loaded onto a tanker and shipped anywhere in the world at modest cost; moving natural gas any meaningful distance requires either a pipeline or liquefaction into LNG, both involving large fixed infrastructure investments. As a result, natural gas does not have a single global price - instead, it is priced at a number of regional hubs, with prices that can diverge substantially depending on local supply, demand, and the infrastructure connecting (or failing to connect) that hub to the rest of the world.

Major Pricing Hubs

Definition - Henry Hub, TTF, and NBP

Henry Hub, located in Erath, Louisiana, is the delivery point for the NYMEX Natural Gas futures contract and the benchmark price for U.S. natural gas. It sits at the confluence of multiple interstate and intrastate pipelines, giving it deep connectivity to U.S. production basins (Appalachia, Permian, Haynesville) and demand centers.

TTF (Title Transfer Facility) is a virtual trading point in the Netherlands that has become the benchmark for continental European natural gas. Unlike Henry Hub, TTF is not a physical location but an entry in the books of the Dutch gas transmission system operator - gas "at TTF" can be injected or withdrawn at any connected physical entry/exit point.

NBP (National Balancing Point) is the equivalent virtual trading point for the UK gas market, historically the dominant European gas benchmark before TTF's rise to prominence following increased UK-Continent interconnection and LNG import growth.

Basis Differentials Between Hubs

The price difference between two hubs - the basis - reflects the cost and availability of moving gas between them, plus any local supply/demand imbalance that pipeline or shipping capacity cannot fully arbitrage away.

Definition - Basis Differential

For two hubs $A$ and $B$ connected by a pipeline or shipping route, the basis is: $$\text{Basis}_{A,B} = P_A - P_B$$ In a market with sufficient transport capacity and no binding constraints, the basis should be bounded by the cost of transport between the hubs (a no-arbitrage condition analogous to cost-of-carry): if $P_A - P_B$ exceeds the transport cost from $B$ to $A$, it becomes profitable to ship gas from $B$ to $A$, which increases supply at $A$ and demand at $B$, narrowing the basis. When transport capacity is constrained (fully utilised), the basis can exceed the marginal transport cost indefinitely, reflecting the scarcity value of pipeline or LNG capacity itself.

Example - Henry Hub-TTF Basis and the Global LNG Arbitrage

Suppose Henry Hub is trading at $\$3.00$/MMBtu and TTF is trading at $\$10.00$/MMBtu. The basis is $\text{Basis}_{\text{TTF,HH}} = 10.00 - 3.00 = \$7.00$/MMBtu. If the all-in cost of liquefying U.S. gas, shipping it to Europe, and regasifying it (the "LNG netback cost") is, say, $\$4.00$/MMBtu, then an exporter shipping U.S. gas to Europe nets approximately $10.00 - 3.00 - 4.00 = \$3.00$/MMBtu - a strong incentive to maximise LNG exports.

This arbitrage is precisely what occurred at scale following Europe's loss of Russian pipeline gas in 2022: the TTF-Henry Hub basis widened dramatically, U.S. LNG export terminals ran at maximum utilisation, and the basis only partially narrowed because liquefaction and shipping capacity - not the underlying gas price difference - became the binding constraint. This illustrates why basis differentials in gas markets can persist at levels that look like "arbitrage" from a pure price perspective: the arbitrage is capacity-constrained, not price-constrained.

Seasonal Demand Patterns

Natural gas demand is strongly seasonal, driven primarily by its use for heating (winter) and, increasingly, for power generation to meet air-conditioning load (summer). This seasonality is the dominant feature of the gas forward curve, far more pronounced than the seasonal patterns seen in crude oil.

Remark - Winter Heating and Summer Cooling Demand

Winter heating demand is the larger and more volatile of the two seasonal peaks in most Northern Hemisphere markets. Residential and commercial heating load is highly sensitive to temperature - a colder-than-normal winter (measured in "heating degree days," HDDs) can drive demand sharply above the level implied by storage withdrawal plans, causing prompt prices to spike well above the forward curve's implied winter price.

Summer cooling demand has grown substantially as natural gas-fired generation has become the marginal fuel for power systems in many regions; hot summers increase electricity demand for air conditioning, which increases gas demand for power generation ("cooling degree days," CDDs). This summer demand peak is generally smaller than the winter peak but has become an increasingly important driver of summer gas prices and of summer injection rates into storage.

These seasonal demand patterns are the fundamental reason the natural gas forward curve typically exhibits a pronounced "sawtooth" shape - winter contracts trading at a premium to the surrounding shoulder months (spring and fall), when heating and cooling demand are both low and the system is rebuilding storage for the next winter.
