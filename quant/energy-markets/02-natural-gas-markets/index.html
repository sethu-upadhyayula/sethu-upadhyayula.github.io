2. Natural Gas Markets

Natural gas markets differ from oil markets in a fundamental way: gas is far more costly to transport and store relative to its value, so the global gas market is really a collection of regional markets connected only loosely by pipeline and LNG trade. This chapter begins with the pricing of natural gas at the major regional hubs - Henry Hub in the U.S., TTF in Europe, and NBP in the UK - and the basis differentials between them, together with the strong seasonal demand patterns (winter heating, summer cooling for power generation) that dominate the shape of the gas forward curve.

The second section addresses the physical infrastructure that connects these regional markets - liquefied natural gas (LNG) liquefaction, shipping, and regasification - and the pipeline capacity constraints that can cause regional prices to diverge sharply even when arbitrage in principle should align them. We close with the underground storage injection/withdrawal cycle, the dominant seasonal pattern in gas markets, and the no-arbitrage bounds it places on the relationship between summer and winter gas prices.
