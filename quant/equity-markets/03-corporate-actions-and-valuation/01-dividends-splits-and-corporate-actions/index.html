3.1 Dividends, Splits, and Corporate Actions

Corporate actions are events initiated by a company that change the terms or structure of its outstanding shares. Unlike ordinary price moves driven by trading, corporate actions are mechanical: they redistribute or restate value in ways that must be precisely accounted for in price histories, index calculations, and derivative contracts. This section covers the most common corporate actions and the conventions used to handle them.

Dividend Mechanics

A cash dividend is a distribution of a portion of a company's earnings (or, occasionally, capital) to its shareholders. The process unfolds through a sequence of dates, each with a specific legal and economic meaning.

Definition — The Dividend Timeline

Declaration date: the company's board of directors formally announces the dividend amount per share, and the ex-dividend, record, and payment dates.

Ex-dividend (ex-date): the first trading day on which a share trades without the right to the declared dividend. An investor who buys on or after the ex-date does not receive the upcoming dividend; an investor who buys before the ex-date does. The stock's price is expected to adjust downward by approximately the dividend amount at the open of the ex-date.

Record date: the date used by the company's transfer agent to determine which shareholders of record are entitled to the dividend. Given standard settlement conventions, the record date typically falls one business day after the ex-date.

Payment date: the date on which the dividend is actually credited to shareholders' accounts, typically several weeks after the record date.

Remark — Why the Price Drops on the Ex-Date

On the ex-date, the company's cash balance will decrease by the total dividend payment once it is made, so the enterprise's value to a new buyer (who will not receive the dividend) is mechanically lower by that per-share amount. A rational market therefore opens the ex-date at approximately $S_{\text{ex}} \approx S_{\text{cum}} - D$, where $S_{\text{cum}}$ is the previous closing price (which still included the right to the dividend) and $D$ is the dividend per share. In practice the observed drop can differ slightly from $D$ due to the differential tax treatment of dividends versus capital gains for various classes of investors, and due to ordinary market noise.

Worked Example: Ex-Dividend Price Adjustment

Example — Ex-Dividend Adjustment

A stock closes at $S_{\text{cum}} = \$100.00$ on the day before its ex-dividend date. The company has declared a dividend of $D = \$0.50$ per share.

All else equal, the stock should open on the ex-date at approximately $$S_{\text{ex}} \approx S_{\text{cum}} - D = 100.00 - 0.50 = \$99.50$$

Historical price series that are not dividend-adjusted will show an apparent $-0.50\%$ "return" on the ex-date that has nothing to do with the stock's economic performance. For this reason, total-return price series (used for performance measurement and most quantitative research) are constructed by adding back dividends: the adjusted closing price on the ex-date and all subsequent dates is scaled up by a factor of $\frac{S_{\text{cum}}}{S_{\text{cum}} - D}$ relative to the raw price, so that the cumulative total return correctly reflects both price appreciation and dividends received.

Stock Splits

A stock split changes the number of shares outstanding and the price per share without changing the company's total market capitalisation or any shareholder's proportional ownership.

Definition — Stock Split and Reverse Split

In an $n$-for-1 (forward) stock split, each existing share is converted into $n$ shares. If the pre-split price is $S_0$ and shares outstanding is $Q_0$, then immediately after the split: $$S_0' = \frac{S_0}{n}, \qquad Q_0' = n \, Q_0, \qquad S_0' \times Q_0' = S_0 \times Q_0$$ Market capitalisation is unchanged. A shareholder who held 100 shares at $\$300$ before a 3-for-1 split holds 300 shares at $\$100$ after — the same $\$30{,}000$ position.

A reverse split ($1$-for-$n$) consolidates $n$ existing shares into one: $S_0' = n S_0$ and $Q_0' = Q_0 / n$. Reverse splits are often used by companies whose share price has fallen to a level that risks delisting (many exchanges impose minimum price requirements) or that is perceived as signalling distress.

Remark — Why Companies Split Their Stock

A stock split has no effect on fundamental value, but it can have practical effects: a lower nominal share price may be more accessible to retail investors trading in round lots, and — as discussed in Section 2.1 — a lower price increases the relative size of the minimum tick, which can affect spreads and trading costs. Exchange-listed options and futures on a stock are adjusted at the time of a split: the strike price is divided by $n$ and the contract multiplier (number of shares per contract) is multiplied by $n$, so that the economic exposure of each contract is unchanged.

Rights Issues and Other Corporate Actions

A rights issue is a method by which a company raises additional equity capital by offering existing shareholders the right to purchase new shares, typically at a discount to the prevailing market price, in proportion to their existing holdings.

Definition — Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP)

If a company with $N$ shares outstanding at price $S_0$ offers $M$ new shares at issue price $P_{\text{issue}}$, the theoretical ex-rights price is the weighted-average price of all shares after the issue, assuming no new information: $$\text{TERP} = \frac{N \cdot S_0 + M \cdot P_{\text{issue}}}{N + M}$$ Because $P_{\text{issue}} < S_0$ (the issue is at a discount), $\text{TERP} < S_0$: existing shareholders who do not participate experience dilution of the per-share price, but — because they hold a right with positive value — they are compensated if they sell that right or exercise it.

The theoretical value of one right is $S_0 - \text{TERP}$, representing the value transferred to whoever exercises (or sells) the right rather than letting it lapse.

Remark — Other Common Corporate Actions

Stock dividends / bonus issues: the company issues additional shares to existing holders in proportion to their holdings (e.g. a 10% stock dividend gives one new share for every ten held), economically similar to a small stock split.

Spin-offs: a company distributes shares of a subsidiary to its existing shareholders, creating a new independently-traded entity. The parent's share price drops by approximately the value of the distributed subsidiary shares.

Mergers and acquisitions: in a cash acquisition, shareholders receive a fixed cash amount per share and the stock is delisted; in a stock-for-stock merger, shareholders receive shares of the acquirer according to an exchange ratio. Both require adjustment of any open derivative positions and index weights.
