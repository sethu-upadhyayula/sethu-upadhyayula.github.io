3.2 Fundamental Valuation Approaches

While Section 3.1 dealt with the mechanical adjustments that corporate actions impose on share prices, this section addresses a more fundamental question: what should a share be worth in the first place? Fundamental valuation methods estimate intrinsic value from a company's expected future cash flows or from comparisons to similar companies, providing a benchmark against which the market price can be assessed.

The Dividend Discount Model

The dividend discount model (DDM) values a share as the present value of all future dividends it is expected to pay - the most direct cash flow an equity investor receives.

Definition - General Dividend Discount Model

The fair value of a share is the present value of its expected future dividend stream, discounted at the required rate of return on equity $r_e$: $$P_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{E[D_t]}{(1+r_e)^t}$$ where $D_t$ is the dividend expected to be paid at the end of period $t$. This is the equity analogue of valuing a bond as the present value of its coupon stream, with the key difference that dividends are uncertain and the horizon is infinite (or until the firm is dissolved or acquired).

Theorem - Gordon Growth Model

If dividends are assumed to grow at a constant rate $g < r_e$ in perpetuity, $D_t = D_0(1+g)^t$, the infinite sum has a closed form: $$P_0 = \frac{D_0(1+g)}{r_e - g} = \frac{D_1}{r_e - g}$$ This is the Gordon growth model. It requires $g < r_e$ for convergence - a growth rate equal to or exceeding the discount rate implies an infinite (or undefined) valuation, signalling that the constant-growth assumption is unrealistic over an infinite horizon.

Remark - Multi-Stage Models

Real companies rarely grow at a single constant rate forever. Multi-stage DDMs project dividends explicitly for an initial high-growth period (e.g. 5-10 years) and then apply the Gordon growth formula to value the "terminal" perpetuity of dividends beyond that horizon, discounted back to the present. The terminal value often represents the majority of the total valuation, making the choice of terminal growth rate $g$ and discount rate $r_e$ the dominant drivers of the result - and a key source of valuation disagreement between analysts.

Worked Example: Gordon Growth Valuation

Example - Applying the Gordon Growth Model

A company just paid an annual dividend of $D_0 = \$2.00$ per share. Dividends are expected to grow at $g = 4\%$ per year indefinitely, and the required return on equity is $r_e = 9\%$.

The next dividend is $D_1 = D_0(1+g) = 2.00 \times 1.04 = \$2.08$. The fair value is: $$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r_e - g} = \frac{2.08}{0.09 - 0.04} = \frac{2.08}{0.05} = \$41.60$$

If the stock currently trades at $\$35$, the DDM suggests it is undervalued relative to this set of assumptions - but the result is highly sensitive to $g$ and $r_e$: raising $g$ to $5\%$ would give $P_0 = \frac{2.10}{0.04} = \$52.50$, a 26% change in fair value from a one-percentage-point change in the assumed growth rate.

Discounted Cash Flow Valuation

The DDM works well for mature companies with stable, predictable dividend policies, but many companies - especially growth companies - pay little or no dividend, retaining cash to reinvest in the business. Discounted cash flow (DCF) valuation generalises the approach by discounting the cash flows available to all capital providers, not just the dividends actually distributed to shareholders.

Definition - Free Cash Flow to Firm and Enterprise Value

Free cash flow to the firm (FCFF) is the cash generated by operations after reinvestment needs, available to both debt and equity holders. The enterprise value (EV) is the present value of all future FCFF discounted at the weighted average cost of capital (WACC): $$EV = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{FCFF_t}{(1+WACC)^t}$$ The equity value is then obtained by subtracting net debt: $\text{Equity Value} = EV - \text{Net Debt}$, and dividing by shares outstanding gives an intrinsic value per share.

Remark - DDM vs DCF

The DDM and DCF approaches are conceptually equivalent - both are present-value-of-future-cash-flow methods - but they differ in which cash flows are discounted and at what rate. The DDM discounts dividends actually paid at the cost of equity $r_e$; the DCF/EV approach discounts cash flows available to the whole firm at the WACC (a blend of the cost of debt and cost of equity), and is preferred when dividend policy is a poor proxy for cash-generating capacity - for example, for companies that retain all earnings or that have significant leverage.

Relative Valuation: Multiples

Rather than estimating intrinsic value from first principles, relative valuation infers a company's value by comparing its price to a financial metric, using the ratio (multiple) observed for comparable companies as a benchmark.

Definition - Common Valuation Multiples

Price-to-Earnings (P/E): $\dfrac{P_0}{E_1}$, the price per share divided by (trailing or forward) earnings per share. A P/E of 20 means investors are paying 20 times the company's annual earnings for one share. P/E is closely linked to the Gordon growth model: rearranging $P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r_e-g}$ and writing $D_1 = E_1 \times \text{payout ratio}$ shows that the P/E ratio is itself a function of the payout ratio, growth rate, and required return.

Enterprise Value to EBITDA (EV/EBITDA): $\dfrac{EV}{EBITDA}$, comparing total firm value (equity plus net debt) to earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation. Because EV/EBITDA is capital-structure-neutral (EBITDA is computed before interest expense, and EV includes debt), it is often preferred for comparing companies with different leverage or depreciation policies, such as across industries with heavy fixed-asset investment.

Remark - Using Multiples in Practice

Relative valuation requires a set of genuinely comparable companies - similar in growth prospects, margins, capital intensity, and risk - since multiples embed all of these factors implicitly. A stock trading at a P/E discount to its peers may be cheap, or it may simply have lower expected growth or higher risk; the multiple alone cannot distinguish between these explanations. For this reason, multiples are typically used alongside DCF or DDM analysis as a cross-check, and as a quick way to flag stocks for further fundamental research rather than as a standalone valuation methodology.
