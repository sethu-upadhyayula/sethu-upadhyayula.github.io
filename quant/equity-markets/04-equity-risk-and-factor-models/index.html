4. Equity Risk and Factor Models

Why do some stocks earn higher average returns than others, and how much of a stock's risk can be diversified away? This chapter develops the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) as the foundational framework for decomposing a stock's risk into a systematic component, driven by movements in the overall market, and an idiosyncratic component specific to the firm - and shows how beta measures a stock's sensitivity to the former.

We then move beyond the single-factor CAPM to multi-factor models that have come to dominate quantitative equity investing in practice. The Fama-French framework and its descendants identify systematic sources of return - value, momentum, quality, and low volatility - that are not fully captured by market beta alone, and we discuss how these factors are constructed into investable portfolios and the crowding risks that arise when too much capital chases the same factor exposures.
