1.2 Stock Indices

An equity index aggregates the prices of a basket of stocks into a single number that summarises the performance of a market, sector, or investment style. Indices serve three roles: as performance benchmarks for active managers, as the reference underlying for derivatives and exchange-traded funds (ETFs), and as the basis for passive index-replication strategies that now represent a substantial share of invested assets.

Index Construction Methodologies

The way an index combines its constituent prices into a single level fundamentally determines how individual stock moves translate into index moves. The three dominant weighting schemes are price weighting, market-capitalisation weighting, and equal weighting.

Definition — Price-Weighted Index

A price-weighted index is the sum of constituent prices divided by a divisor: $$I = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} S_i}{D}$$ where $S_i$ is the price of stock $i$ and $D$ is the index divisor, adjusted over time to absorb the effect of stock splits and constituent changes so that the index level is continuous across such events. The Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) is the best-known example, with $N=30$.

A high-priced stock has a disproportionately large influence on the index level and its returns, regardless of the issuing company's market value — an artefact widely viewed as a methodological weakness.

Definition — Market-Capitalisation-Weighted Index

A market-cap-weighted index assigns each constituent a weight proportional to its total market value: $$I_t = I_0 \times \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} P_{i,t} \, Q_{i,t}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} P_{i,0} \, Q_{i,0}}$$ where $P_{i,t}$ is the price and $Q_{i,t}$ is the number of shares (or free-float-adjusted shares) of constituent $i$ at time $t$, and $I_0$ is the base index level at the base date. The S&P 500, NASDAQ Composite, and most broad regional benchmarks use this scheme.

Equivalently, the weight of stock $i$ is $w_{i,t} = \dfrac{P_{i,t}Q_{i,t}}{\sum_j P_{j,t}Q_{j,t}}$, and the index return is the weight-average of constituent returns: $R_I = \sum_i w_i R_i$.

Definition — Equal-Weighted Index

An equal-weighted index assigns each of the $N$ constituents the same weight $w_i = 1/N$ at each rebalancing date. Between rebalances, weights drift as prices move, so the index must be periodically rebalanced back to equal weights — typically quarterly. Equal-weighted versions of cap-weighted benchmarks (e.g. the S&P 500 Equal Weight Index) tilt toward smaller constituents relative to the cap-weighted version and historically exhibit a small-cap and value tilt.

Free-Float Adjustment

Most major cap-weighted indices use free-float shares outstanding rather than total shares outstanding when computing weights. Free float excludes shares held by governments, founders, controlling shareholders, or other strategic holders that are not available for trading.

Remark — Why Free-Float Matters

If a company has 1 billion shares outstanding but only 300 million are freely tradeable (a free-float factor of 30%), weighting by total shares would overstate the stock's investable weight relative to the capital that can actually flow into it via index funds. Free-float adjustment ensures the index weight reflects the portion of market capitalisation actually available to investors, which keeps index funds from chasing illiquid stock and reduces tracking costs. The free-float factor is typically rounded to the nearest 5% band and reviewed periodically.

Rebalancing and Reconstitution

Index providers periodically update both the composition (which stocks are included — reconstitution) and the weights (how much of each — rebalancing) of an index according to a published, rules-based methodology.

Definition — Rebalancing vs Reconstitution

Rebalancing adjusts constituent weights back toward target levels (e.g. resetting an equal-weighted index to $1/N$, or updating free-float factors and share counts for cap-weighted indices) without necessarily changing membership. Most major indices rebalance quarterly.

Reconstitution changes the set of constituents — adding companies that now meet eligibility criteria (market cap, liquidity, sector classification, profitability screens) and removing those that no longer qualify, e.g. due to acquisition, bankruptcy, or falling below a size threshold. The S&P 500 reconstitutes on an as-needed basis with quarterly reviews; the Russell indices reconstitute annually each June.

Remark — Index Effect

Stocks added to a widely-tracked index tend to experience abnormal positive returns around the announcement and effective dates, as passive funds must buy the new constituent to track the index — and the reverse for deletions. This index effect creates a predictable, if shrinking, source of short-term price pressure that index-arbitrage and event-driven strategies attempt to capture.

Total Return, Price Return, and Net Return

An index can be published in several variants that differ only in how dividends paid by constituents are treated. This distinction is critical: derivatives reference specific variants, and confusing them leads to material valuation errors.

Definition — Index Return Variants

Price Return (PR) index: tracks only price changes of constituents; dividends are ignored entirely. Most headline-quoted indices (e.g. "the S&P 500 closed at...") are price return indices.

Total Return (TR) / Gross Total Return (GTR) index: assumes all dividends are reinvested in the index at the gross (pre-tax) amount on the ex-dividend date. The TR index level evolves as $$I^{TR}_{t} = I^{TR}_{t-1} \times \left(1 + \frac{\sum_i w_{i,t-1}(P_{i,t} - P_{i,t-1} + D_{i,t})}{P_{i,t-1}}\right)$$ where $D_{i,t}$ is any dividend paid by constituent $i$ on date $t$.

Net Total Return (NTR) index: reinvests dividends net of a withholding tax rate, typically the rate applicable to a non-resident institutional investor in the relevant jurisdiction. NTR indices are the standard underlying for many index futures and total return swaps because they better reflect the economics achievable by a real investor.

Example — TR vs PR Divergence

Suppose the S&P 500 price index is flat over a year while constituents pay an average dividend yield of 1.5%. The PR index ends the year at the same level it started, but the TR index rises by approximately 1.5% (compounded reinvestment of dividends). Over multi-decade horizons this gap compounds to a very large difference: since 1990, the cumulative total return of the S&P 500 has been several times the cumulative price return.

Replication: Futures and ETFs

Most investors do not buy every constituent of an index directly. Instead they gain exposure through index futures or exchange-traded funds (ETFs), both of which are designed to replicate the economic performance of the underlying index, typically on a total-return basis net of financing costs and fees.

Definition — Index Futures Fair Value

For an index with continuous dividend yield $q$, the fair (no-arbitrage) futures price for maturity $T$ is $$F_0 = I_0 \, e^{(r-q)T}$$ where $r$ is the financing (risk-free) rate. The futures price embeds the cost of carry: long the future is economically equivalent to borrowing at $r$ to buy the index basket and collecting dividends at rate $q$. Index futures (e.g. E-mini S&P 500) are among the most liquid instruments in global markets and are widely used for both hedging and obtaining leveraged, capital-efficient index exposure.

Theorem — ETF Creation/Redemption and Tracking

An ETF tracks its reference index via an in-kind creation/redemption mechanism: authorised participants (APs) can exchange a basket of the underlying securities for new ETF shares (creation) or vice versa (redemption). This mechanism keeps the ETF's market price closely anchored to its net asset value (NAV), since any persistent premium or discount creates an arbitrage opportunity for APs.

Despite this mechanism, an ETF's realised return will generally differ slightly from the index's return due to tracking error — the standard deviation of the difference between ETF and index returns, driven by management fees, sampling (holding a subset of constituents rather than the full basket), cash drag, rebalancing timing, and — for net-return-tracking funds — withholding tax differences.

Example — Tracking Error

An ETF charges an expense ratio of 0.05% per year and tracks a TR index. If the index returns 12.00% over a year and the ETF returns 11.93%, the realised tracking difference is $-0.07\%$, slightly worse than the fee alone would suggest due to small frictions from sampling and cash drag. The annualised standard deviation of daily (ETF $-$ index) return differences is the tracking error, often just a few basis points for large, liquid ETFs on broad indices.
