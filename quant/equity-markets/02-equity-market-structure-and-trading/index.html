2. Equity Market Structure and Trading

Beneath the price quoted on a screen lies a complex web of exchanges, alternative trading systems, and intermediaries that determine how orders are matched, what prices participants actually pay, and who profits from the act of trading itself. This chapter examines the institutional plumbing of equity markets: the limit order book mechanics that govern price formation, the menu of order types traders use to express their intentions, and the costs implicit in the bid-ask spread.

We then turn to the fragmented, largely electronic landscape of modern equity trading — dark pools that allow large orders to be worked away from the public order book, and the high-frequency trading firms and designated market makers whose strategies and obligations shape the liquidity available to every other participant. Understanding this market structure is essential for interpreting transaction costs, designing execution algorithms, and assessing the realism of any model that treats trading as frictionless.
