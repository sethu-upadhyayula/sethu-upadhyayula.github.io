2.2 Cash-3M Spreads, Tom-Next, and Carry Trades

The LME's daily-prompt structure means that the curve between today and three months forward can, in principle, be quoted at a different price for every business day. In practice, liquidity concentrates at a small number of reference points — cash (T+2), the 3M date, and a handful of broker-quoted "carries" between consecutive prompts — and traders express views on the shape of the curve through spreads between these reference points rather than through outright prices.

The Cash-3M Spread

Definition — Cash-3M Spread

The cash-3M spread is the difference between the cash (T+2) price $C$ and the 3-month forward price $F_{3M}$: $$\text{Spread} = C - F_{3M}.$$ By LME convention, a positive spread (cash above 3M) is called backwardation, and a negative spread (cash below 3M) is called contango — note this is the opposite sign convention from simply quoting "$F-S$" as in Section 1.2, so care is needed when comparing LME-quoted spreads to cost-of-carry formulas written in terms of $F(t,T)-S_t$.

The cash-3M spread is the LME's primary barometer of near-term physical tightness. A widening backwardation (cash rising relative to 3M) signals that immediately available metal is becoming scarce relative to metal for delivery in three months — exactly the convenience-yield story from Section 1.2, but expressed as a spread between two specific, heavily-traded reference dates rather than a generic forward curve.

Tom-Next and Carries

Because LME positions are quoted against specific prompt dates, a trader who wants to maintain a constant-maturity exposure (e.g., "always be long the 3-month forward") must continuously roll positions forward as each day's 3M prompt rolls to the next business day. This rolling is executed via carries — simultaneous purchase and sale of the same metal for two different prompt dates.

Definition — Tom-Next (TN) and Carry

A carry between prompt dates $t_1 < t_2$ is a spread trade: buy (or sell) the metal for prompt $t_1$ and simultaneously sell (or buy) for prompt $t_2$, at a net price equal to the spread $P(t_1)-P(t_2)$. The tom-next (TN) carry is the carry between "tomorrow" and the next business day ("next") — the shortest possible carry, analogous to an overnight FX swap. A trader holding a cash position who wants to roll it one day forward without changing the net economic exposure executes a tom-next carry: sell tomorrow, buy next — locking in the one-day roll cost (or benefit) implied by the prevailing cash-3M spread and short-term financing rates.

Remark — Carries as the Building Blocks of the Curve

Because every adjacent pair of prompt dates has a quotable carry, the entire LME forward curve can in principle be built up by chaining together carries from cash out to the 3M date and beyond — directly analogous to bootstrapping a discount curve from a sequence of forward rate agreements (as in the Rates Fundamentals subject). In practice, liquidity is concentrated in cash, 3M, and a handful of "broken date" carries quoted by brokers for specific calendar dates that matter to commercial hedgers (e.g., quarter-ends, specific shipment dates).

Cash-and-Carry Trades

A cash-and-carry trade exploits a mispricing between the cash-3M spread and the implied financing cost of holding physical metal. If the 3M forward is "too cheap" relative to cash given prevailing interest rates and storage costs (i.e., the curve is in too-steep backwardation, or insufficiently in contango), a trader can buy metal at cash, finance it, store it, and sell it forward for 3M delivery — capturing the difference as a near risk-free profit, subject to warehouse rent and financing costs.

Theorem — Implied Financing Rate from the Cash-3M Spread

If the cash price is $C$ and the 3M forward price is $F_{3M}$, the implied annualized carry rate $\rho$ over the 3-month period (ignoring convenience yield, i.e., the rate that would prevail if the curve were pure cost-of-carry) satisfies: $$F_{3M} = C\, e^{\rho \cdot 0.25} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \rho = \frac{1}{0.25}\ln\left(\frac{F_{3M}}{C}\right) = 4\ln\left(\frac{F_{3M}}{C}\right).$$ If the metal's actual financing-plus-storage cost $r+u$ exceeds $\rho$, the 3M forward is cheap relative to fair value (the curve is in too much backwardation, or not enough contango) and a cash-and-carry (buy cash, sell 3M, store) is theoretically profitable. If $r+u < \rho$, the reverse ("reverse cash-and-carry": sell cash, buy 3M, borrow the metal to deliver against the cash sale) is favoured — though reverse cash-and-carry in metals requires the ability to borrow physical metal, which can be constrained, particularly during squeezes (Section 1.2 and Section 2.1's discussion of lending guidance).

Worked Example: Pricing a Copper Cash-and-Carry

Example — Copper Cash-and-Carry Trade

Suppose LME copper cash trades at $C = \$9{,}000$/tonne and the 3M forward trades at $F_{3M} = \$9{,}050$/tonne — a contango of $\$50$ (a negative cash-3M spread of $-\$50$ under the LME sign convention). The implied annualized carry rate is: $$\rho = 4 \ln\left(\frac{9050}{9000}\right) = 4 \ln(1.005556) = 4 \times 0.005540 = 0.02216 \approx 2.22\%.$$

Suppose a trader's actual cost of financing (3-month USD borrowing rate) is $r = 5.0\%$ annualized, and LME warehouse rent plus insurance for three months adds a further $u \approx 1.0\%$ annualized, for a total carrying cost of $r+u \approx 6.0\%$. Since $\rho = 2.22\% < r+u = 6.0\%$, the 3M forward is cheap relative to the cost of physically carrying the metal — the market is not compensating a holder of physical copper enough to justify buying cash and storing it for delivery in three months. A reverse cash-and-carry — sell copper at cash for $\$9{,}000$, borrow copper from the lending market to make delivery, invest the $\$9{,}000$ proceeds at $r=5\%$, and buy back the 3M forward at $\$9{,}050$ to return the borrowed metal — would be more attractive, provided physical copper can be borrowed at a lease-like rate below $r+u-\rho \approx 3.78\%$ annualized.

In practice, copper is rarely available to borrow at attractive rates, so persistent gaps between $\rho$ and $r+u$ — particularly when the forward curve is "too flat" relative to financing costs — are common and reflect the convenience yield embedded in physical metal access rather than a pure arbitrage opportunity. This gap is precisely the convenience yield $y$ from the cost-of-carry identity in Section 1.2: $\rho \approx r+u-y$, so $y \approx (r+u)-\rho \approx 3.78\%$ in this example.
