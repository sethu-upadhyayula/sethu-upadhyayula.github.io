2.1 LME Contract Structure, Warehousing, and Warrants

The London Metal Exchange's contract structure is unusual among major futures exchanges. Rather than trading a handful of fixed quarterly expiries, LME base metal contracts (copper, aluminum, zinc, lead, nickel, tin) trade against a continuum of prompt dates — the dates on which a given contract becomes deliverable. This structure dates back to the exchange's origins as a forward market for physically traded metal arriving in London by ship, where delivery dates needed to align with shipping schedules rather than a standardized calendar.

The Prompt Date Structure

Definition — LME Prompt Dates

An LME prompt date is the maturity/delivery date of a forward or futures position. The LME offers:

  • Daily prompts from tomorrow (tom) out to three months ("3M") — every business day has its own tradeable prompt date.
  • Weekly prompts from 3 months out to 6 months — every Wednesday is a prompt date.
  • Monthly prompts from 6 months out to (typically) 123 months for some contracts — the third Wednesday of each month is a prompt date.

This dense daily structure near the front of the curve allows commercial participants — producers, consumers, and merchants — to align their hedges precisely with physical shipment dates, rather than being forced onto a fixed quarterly cycle that may mismatch their underlying exposure by weeks. It also means that, unlike on exchanges such as the CME, "rolling" a position from one prompt to the next can in principle be done one day at a time.

Definition — Cash and 3M (Three-Month) Prices

Two reference prices dominate LME quoting conventions:

  • Cash (or "spot") — the price for delivery two business days forward (prompt date = today + 2 business days), the LME's settlement convention analogous to T+2 spot in FX markets.
  • 3M (three-month) — the price for delivery three calendar months forward from today, a fixed rolling reference point used as the standard benchmark for quoting the shape of the curve and as the basis for most industrial pricing contracts and hedges.

The difference between the cash and 3M prices — the cash-3M spread — is the LME's standard quote for the near-end curve shape, examined in detail in Section 2.2.

The LME Warehousing Network

The LME does not itself own or operate warehouses; instead, it licenses a global network of independently operated warehouses across locations including the UK, the Netherlands, the US, Singapore, Malaysia, South Korea, and Taiwan. Metal can be deposited into any LME-approved warehouse, at which point it becomes "on-warrant" and eligible to satisfy delivery against a short futures position.

Definition — On-Warrant, Off-Warrant, and Cancelled Warrants

On-warrant metal is physically present in an LME warehouse and represented by a live warrant — it counts as part of LME "registered stocks" and is available for delivery. Cancelled warrants represent metal that has been earmarked for physical withdrawal (typically because an industrial consumer has purchased it) but has not yet physically left the warehouse; cancelled tonnage no longer counts as available exchange supply even though it remains physically on-site until removed. Off-warrant metal sits outside the LME system entirely (e.g., in a producer's own storage or in transit) and is invisible to exchange reporting.

Warehouse Rent, Load-Out Rates, and Queues

LME warehouse operators charge daily rent on stored metal, and — critically — also charge (and earn revenue from) load-out: the physical removal of metal from the warehouse once a warrant is cancelled. In the early-to-mid 2010s, this created a notorious distortion: at certain large warehousing hubs, load-out rates were capped well below the rate at which new metal could be warranted in, causing queues of cancelled metal awaiting physical withdrawal to stretch for many months.

Remark — The Warehouse Queue Problem and LME Reforms

When load-out queues are long, a consumer who cancels a warrant and pays to take physical delivery may have to wait many months to actually receive the metal, while continuing to pay rent during the wait. This created an incentive for warehouse operators (some owned by major trading houses and banks) to attract inbound metal — boosting rent revenue — even as outbound queues lengthened, widening the gap between the LME cash price (based on warranted, but not necessarily promptly deliverable, metal) and the physical premium consumers actually paid to obtain metal in hand. In response, the LME introduced Queue-Based Rent Capping and minimum load-out rate requirements tied to queue length, intended to ensure that rent charged on metal awaiting load-out cannot exceed a regulated cap once queues exceed certain thresholds — directly linking warehouse economics to queue length so that operators have less incentive to let queues build.

Backwardation Limits and Lending Guidance

Because a small number of participants can sometimes accumulate a dominant share of on-warrant stock in a particular location, the LME maintains lending guidance rules intended to prevent dominant position-holders from forcing extreme backwardations (squeezes) onto shorts who must deliver.

Remark — Lending Guidance

Under LME lending guidance, a participant holding a sufficiently large proportion of a particular cash position relative to the warranted stock and tomorrow/next-day positions in that metal is required to lend out the excess at capped backwardation rates (e.g., no more than a specified amount, such as a fraction of a percent per day, depending on the size of the dominant position). This prevents a dominant long from refusing to lend metal to shorts who need it to make delivery, which would otherwise let them extract an unbounded backwardation premium. These rules are a direct, rules-based analogue to the squeeze dynamics discussed qualitatively in Section 1.2.
