3. Mining Economics and Metals Derivatives

So far we have treated metals prices as exogenous and focused on the relationships between spot, forward, and lease/financing rates. This chapter turns to the supply side: how mining companies' cost structures determine the marginal economics of production, and how those companies use derivatives to manage the substantial revenue risk that comes from being unhedged sellers of a volatile commodity. Section 3.1 develops the cash cost and all-in sustaining cost (AISC) framework used throughout the mining industry, the concept of an industry cost curve, and the forward sales and collar structures producers use to lock in or floor revenue.

Section 3.2 covers the derivatives themselves in more depth — vanilla options on metals futures, the distinctive volatility characteristics of metals markets (including the smile/skew effects driven by squeeze risk and lease-rate dynamics), and the structured products — zero-cost collars, accumulators, and similar — that banks structure for producers and consumers. A worked option-pricing example ties these together with a Black-76-style calculation on a metals futures option.
