4.1 Battery Metals: Lithium, Nickel, and Cobalt

Lithium, nickel, and cobalt are the principal metals consumed in the cathodes and electrolytes of lithium-ion batteries, and demand for all three has been transformed over the past decade by the growth of electric vehicles (EVs) and grid-scale energy storage. Unlike copper or aluminum, however, these metals historically traded in opaque, contract-based physical markets with little or no exchange involvement — a structure that is only gradually converging toward the exchange-traded model familiar from the LME (Chapter 2).

Market Structure

Remark — Lithium

Lithium is produced from two main resource types: hard-rock spodumene ore (concentrated then converted into lithium chemicals, predominantly in China) and brine deposits (evaporated and processed, predominantly in South America's "Lithium Triangle" — Chile, Argentina, Bolivia). The traded commodity is not metallic lithium but lithium chemicals — primarily lithium carbonate ($\text{Li}_2\text{CO}_3$) and lithium hydroxide ($\text{LiOH}$), the latter increasingly preferred for high-nickel cathode chemistries. Because supply is concentrated among a relatively small number of producers and demand is dominated by a relatively small number of battery and cathode manufacturers (heavily concentrated in China, Korea, and Japan), lithium has historically been sold under bilateral annual or quarterly contracts rather than on a spot exchange, though spot and short-term contract volumes have grown substantially as the market has matured.

Remark — Nickel

Nickel is unusual among battery metals in that it already had an established exchange-traded market (LME nickel, Chapter 2) long before battery demand became significant — its largest historical end use is stainless steel production. Battery-grade nickel, however, requires a much higher purity ("Class 1" nickel, suitable for conversion to nickel sulfate for cathodes) than the nickel pig iron and ferronickel ("Class 2" nickel) consumed by the stainless steel industry. The LME nickel contract specifies Class 1 nickel as deliverable, but the rapid growth of Indonesian nickel pig iron and matte production (much of it subsequently upgraded to battery-grade intermediates via high-pressure acid leach, or HPAL, processing) has shifted the marginal source of battery nickel supply toward material that does not directly correspond to the LME's deliverable specification — a divergence that contributed to the extreme volatility and eventual suspension of LME nickel trading in March 2022, discussed further below.

Remark — Cobalt

Cobalt is overwhelmingly produced as a by-product of copper and nickel mining, with the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC) accounting for the large majority of global mined supply — a geographic concentration that creates significant supply-chain and ESG (artisanal mining, governance) risk premia. Because cobalt is a by-product, its supply responds primarily to copper and nickel production economics (Section 3.1's cost-curve logic) rather than to cobalt prices directly, making cobalt supply unusually price-inelastic in the short run. Battery manufacturers have responded by developing lower-cobalt and cobalt-free cathode chemistries (e.g., NMC 811 and lithium iron phosphate, LFP), which has been a major demand-side driver of cobalt price weakness even as overall battery production has grown rapidly.

EV Demand Drivers

Battery metals demand is driven less by traditional industrial-production indicators (as for copper or steel) and more directly by EV sales volumes, the average battery size (kWh) per vehicle, and the cathode chemistry mix.

Definition — Cathode Chemistry and Metal Intensity

The dominant lithium-ion cathode chemistries are denoted by their active metal content: NMC (nickel-manganese-cobalt, in ratios such as 6:2:2 or 8:1:1) and NCA (nickel-cobalt-aluminum) offer higher energy density (longer range per kg of battery), while LFP (lithium iron phosphate) uses no nickel or cobalt at all, trading some energy density for lower cost and improved safety/longevity. The metals intensity of a given EV therefore depends not just on battery size but on chemistry choice: a shift in the market toward LFP (which has been pronounced in the Chinese EV market) reduces nickel and cobalt demand per vehicle even as total EV unit sales rise, while a shift toward high-nickel NMC/NCA chemistries (more common in premium, long-range vehicles in Europe and North America) increases nickel intensity per vehicle and reduces cobalt intensity relative to older NMC formulations.

Pricing Benchmarks and the Absence of Deep Futures Liquidity

Unlike copper, aluminum, and (mostly) nickel, none of the three battery metals has a deep, liquid, exchange-traded futures market comparable to LME base metals. This has significant consequences for how prices are set, how risk is hedged, and how volatile reported prices can be.

Remark — Why Battery Metals Lack Deep Futures Markets

Several factors have historically limited exchange liquidity in battery metals. First, the traded commodities are often chemical compounds (lithium carbonate, lithium hydroxide, nickel sulfate) rather than a single standardized metal, with quality specifications (impurity levels, particle size, battery-grade vs. technical-grade) that vary by end use and are harder to standardize into a single deliverable grade than LME copper or aluminum. Second, the market has historically been dominated by a small number of large producers and consumers transacting under long-term bilateral contracts, which limits the free float available for exchange trading and price discovery. Third, for lithium in particular, the rapid pace of demand growth and supply response has made multi-year forward price discovery inherently difficult — there is limited consensus on a "fair" long-run price around which a futures curve could anchor. Exchanges including the LME, CME, and the Guangzhou Futures Exchange have launched lithium hydroxide/carbonate and cobalt contracts in recent years, but volumes and open interest remain modest relative to LME copper or aluminum, and bid-ask spreads are correspondingly wider.

Definition — Price Reporting Agency (PRA) Benchmarks

In the absence of deep futures markets, battery metals prices are predominantly referenced to indices published by price reporting agencies (PRAs) such as Fastmarkets, Benchmark Mineral Intelligence, and Argus. These agencies survey market participants for transaction prices and bids/offers across defined product specifications (e.g., "lithium hydroxide CIF Asia, battery grade, min 56.5% $\text{LiOH·H}_2\text{O}$") and publish assessed prices, typically weekly or monthly, that serve as the reference point for contract pricing formulas. Unlike an exchange settlement price, a PRA assessment reflects a methodology-driven estimate of where transactions are occurring, based on a panel of reported deals and bids/offers, rather than a single observable market-clearing trade.

Contract Pricing Mechanisms

Because most battery metals volume still moves under bilateral contracts rather than exchange delivery, the specific pricing mechanism written into those contracts is itself an important object of negotiation between miners, refiners/converters, and battery or cathode manufacturers.

Definition — Common Contract Pricing Structures

Index-linked (floating) pricing. The contract price for a given delivery period is set as the average of a specified PRA index over a defined pricing period (e.g., the average of the monthly Fastmarkets lithium hydroxide CIF Asia assessment over the month prior to shipment), often with a contractually agreed premium or discount for quality, volume, or relationship factors: $$P_{\text{contract}} = \overline{\text{Index}}_{\text{pricing period}} \times (1 + \text{premium/discount \%})$$ Fixed-price contracts. Less common in the current environment but historically used for long-term offtake agreements, particularly where a buyer is financing new mine or refinery capacity and both parties want certainty over the life of the financing.

Price participation / floor-and-ceiling contracts. A hybrid structure in which the contract specifies a floor price (protecting the seller/producer if the index falls sharply, similar in spirit to the put leg of a producer collar in Section 3.1) and a ceiling price (capping the buyer's cost if the index spikes), with the contract price floating with the index between those bounds: $$P_{\text{contract}} = \min\left(\max\left(\overline{\text{Index}},\ P_{\text{floor}}\right),\ P_{\text{ceiling}}\right)$$ This is economically equivalent to the index price plus a collar (Sections 3.1–3.2) embedded directly in the offtake agreement, but because there is no liquid options market in the underlying metal, the "collar" cannot easily be unwound or re-hedged separately from the physical contract — the floor and ceiling are simply terms of the commercial agreement, carried as embedded optionality on both parties' books rather than as separately tradable instruments.

Cost-plus / margin-sharing contracts. Used in some integrated supply chains (e.g., between a converter and a cathode maker), where the price is set as the converter's input cost (linked to spodumene concentrate or brine feedstock prices) plus an agreed processing margin, shifting most of the raw-material price risk onto the buyer while leaving processing-margin risk with the converter.
