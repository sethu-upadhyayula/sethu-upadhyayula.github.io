4. Battery Metals, Steel, and Bulk Commodities

The first three chapters focused on precious metals, base metals, and the exchange-traded ecosystem (LME, COMEX) that prices and hedges them. This final chapter turns to a group of commodities that are central to the energy transition and to global industrial activity but that trade in markets structured very differently from copper or gold: lithium, nickel, and cobalt for batteries and electric vehicles, and iron ore, steel, and the dry bulk freight market that connects them. Section 4.1 covers the battery metals — their market structure, the demand drivers created by the EV and energy-storage buildout, the index-based pricing benchmarks that have emerged in the absence of deep, liquid futures markets, and the contract pricing mechanisms (price participation, floors/ceilings, and benchmark-linked formulas) used between miners, refiners, and battery makers.

Section 4.2 covers steel and iron ore — how iron ore is priced off daily indices rather than an exchange floor, how steel products such as rebar and hot-rolled coil (HRC) trade on futures exchanges, and how the dry bulk freight market sits between the mine and the steel mill, materially affecting the landed cost of ore at different ports. A worked landed-cost example ties index price, freight, and quality adjustments together into the delivered cost that a steel producer actually pays.
