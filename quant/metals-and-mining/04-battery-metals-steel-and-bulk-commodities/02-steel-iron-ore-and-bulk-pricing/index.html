4.2 Steel, Iron Ore, and Bulk Commodity Pricing

Iron ore and steel form the largest commodity complex by tonnage in the world, yet their pricing conventions look quite different from the metals covered in the rest of this subject. Iron ore is priced almost entirely off published indices rather than an exchange-cleared spot market, steel products trade on futures exchanges in some regions but remain heavily influenced by local supply-demand balances, and the dry bulk freight market that physically moves the ore from mine to mill is itself a major, separately-traded source of price variation in the delivered cost of the commodity.

Iron Ore Index Pricing

Remark - From Annual Benchmarks to Daily Indices

Until around 2010, the seaborne iron ore trade was priced through annual benchmark negotiations between major producers (Vale, Rio Tinto, BHP) and major Asian steelmakers, with the resulting benchmark price applied to the bulk of global trade for the following year. This system broke down amid the rapid demand growth and price volatility of the 2000s commodity boom, and the industry shifted to index-linked pricing: contracts reference the average of a published daily or weekly price index over an agreed pricing period, much as described for battery metals contracts in Section 4.1. The dominant indices - published by agencies such as Platts (S&P Global Commodity Insights), Fastmarkets (MBIO), and Mysteel - assess the price of iron ore fines of a reference specification (commonly 62% Fe content, delivered CFR to a major Chinese port such as Qingdao) based on surveyed transactions, bids, and offers, analogous in methodology to the PRA benchmarks discussed for battery metals.

Definition - Iron Ore Quality Adjustments

Because the reference index is assessed for a specific iron content (typically 62% Fe) and physical specification, cargoes of different grade trade at a premium or discount to the index, generally applied roughly proportionally to the difference in Fe content (with adjustments also made for impurities such as silica, alumina, and phosphorus, which affect smelting costs and yield at the steel mill). Higher-grade ores (e.g., 65% Fe "premium" fines or pellets) typically command a premium per percentage point of Fe above the reference grade that exceeds the premium for the equivalent point below the reference grade for lower-grade ores - i.e., the price-quality relationship is convex - because higher-grade ore allows steelmakers to either increase output for a given furnace volume or reduce coke consumption and associated emissions, an effect that becomes more valuable when steel margins are wide or when carbon costs are material.

Steel Futures: Rebar and HRC

Unlike iron ore, finished steel products do trade on futures exchanges in significant volume, though liquidity and the role of futures versus physical pricing differ substantially by region and product.

Remark - Rebar and Hot-Rolled Coil (HRC) Futures

Rebar (reinforcing bar, used in concrete construction) futures trade in large volume on the Shanghai Futures Exchange (SHFE), reflecting China's position as by far the largest steel producer and construction market; SHFE rebar futures are widely used both by Chinese steel producers and traders for hedging and by macro traders as a liquid proxy for Chinese construction activity and infrastructure stimulus expectations. Hot-rolled coil (HRC) - a flat-rolled steel product used in automotive, appliance, and general manufacturing applications - trades on the CME (U.S. Midwest HRC futures) as well as on SHFE, with the CME contract widely referenced by North American steel producers, service centers, and industrial consumers for hedging domestic steel price exposure. U.S. HRC prices have historically been considerably more volatile than international benchmarks, reflecting a domestic market that is partially insulated from global trade flows by tariffs and trade remedy actions, and that can become acutely tight or oversupplied depending on domestic capacity utilization.

Bulk Shipping (Freight) and Landed Cost

Iron ore (and coal, grain, and other bulk commodities) moves between continents on dry bulk vessels - predominantly Capesize ships for iron ore given the scale of the trade - and the cost of that freight is itself a volatile, separately-traded market that can move the delivered cost of ore by a significant fraction of the underlying commodity price.

Definition - Baltic Dry Index and Freight Rates

The Baltic Dry Index (BDI), published by the Baltic Exchange, is a composite index of dry bulk shipping rates across vessel classes (Capesize, Panamax, Supramax) and major routes, widely used as a barometer of global trade activity and as a reference for freight derivatives (forward freight agreements, or FFAs) that allow charterers and shipowners to hedge freight rate risk. Freight rates are quoted either as a flat rate per tonne for a specific route (e.g., $/tonne from Port Hedland, Australia to Qingdao, China) or, for time charters, as a daily hire rate per vessel. Freight rates are highly volatile relative to the commodity prices they affect, driven by global fleet capacity (which adjusts slowly, as new vessel orders take years to deliver), bunker fuel costs, port congestion, and the overall pace of dry bulk trade - meaning the freight component of landed cost can swing independently of, and sometimes in the opposite direction from, the underlying commodity index.

Definition - Landed Cost

The landed cost of a bulk commodity cargo at a destination port is the sum of the reference index price (for the cargo's benchmark specification), the freight cost to move it from origin to destination, and any quality premium or discount for the specific cargo's grade relative to the index specification: $$\text{Landed Cost} = \text{Index Price} + \text{Freight} + \text{Premium/Discount}$$ Landed cost is the figure that actually matters to a steel mill's input costs and to a miner's netback (the price the miner effectively realizes after shipping costs, when selling on a delivered basis rather than free-on-board, or FOB, at the loading port). Because freight and quality adjustments can each be a meaningful fraction of the index price, two cargoes referencing the same index can have materially different economics for buyer and seller depending on origin, destination, and grade.

Worked Example: Landed Cost of Iron Ore at a Chinese Port

Example - Landed Cost Calculation

A trader is evaluating a cargo of 65% Fe iron ore fines loaded at an Australian port for delivery to Qingdao, China. The relevant 62% Fe index price (CFR Qingdao) is currently $\$105$/tonne. The cargo's grade of 65% Fe commands a quality premium of $\$0.85$ for each percentage point of Fe above 62% (reflecting the convex quality-price relationship discussed above), so: $$\text{Premium} = (65 - 62) \times \$0.85 = 3 \times \$0.85 = \$2.55/\text{tonne}$$

However, the $\$105$/tonne index is already quoted CFR (i.e., it already includes a "typical" freight assumption embedded by the index methodology for the reference route). The trader instead has an FOB offer at the loading port and needs to compute the all-in CFR-equivalent cost independently as a cross-check. The FOB price for the 65% Fe cargo is $\$96.00$/tonne, and the current Capesize freight rate for the Australia-to-Qingdao route, from an FFA quote, is $\$8.50$/tonne: $$\text{Landed Cost (CFR equivalent)} = \text{FOB Price} + \text{Freight} = \$96.00 + \$8.50 = \$104.50/\text{tonne}$$

This spread could reflect a softer freight market than the index assumes, a seller willing to transact below prevailing levels, or simply normal bid-ask variation around the index assessment; in any case, it is exactly this kind of landed-cost comparison - decomposing a delivered price into index, quality, and freight components - that determines whether a given cargo represents good value relative to the broader market, and is the everyday arithmetic underlying physical iron ore trading desks' decisions.
