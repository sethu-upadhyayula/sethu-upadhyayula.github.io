1.1 Gold and Silver: Monetary Metals, Lease Rates, and Forward Pricing

Unlike most commodities, gold - and to a lesser extent silver - is held in enormous above-ground stockpiles relative to annual mine production: central bank reserves, ETF holdings, jewellery, coin and bar investment, and industrial inventories. Estimates put total above-ground gold stocks at roughly 80-100 times annual mine production, whereas for most industrial commodities annual consumption is a large multiple of visible inventory. This means gold supply is, for practical purposes, highly elastic at prices anywhere near the prevailing market price: a modest price increase can draw large quantities of gold out of vaults and into the market. Consequently, gold behaves much more like a financial asset or currency than an industrial commodity whose price is set by current physical scarcity.

Remark - Gold as a "Currency"

Because above-ground stocks vastly exceed annual flow (production and fabrication demand), gold's price is determined primarily by its role as a store of value, an inflation hedge, and a portfolio diversifier - driven by real interest rates, currency movements (especially the U.S. dollar), and central bank reserve policy - rather than by industrial supply/demand imbalances. This is why gold's cost-of-carry relationship is unusually clean: gold is cheap to store relative to its value, has negligible convenience yield from physical industrial use, and the forward price is determined almost entirely by the time value of money and the gold lease rate. Silver shares many of gold's monetary characteristics but has a meaningfully larger industrial demand component (electronics, solar photovoltaics, and brazing alloys), so its price exhibits somewhat more sensitivity to industrial cycles than gold's.

The Gold Lease Market

Gold can be lent and borrowed in a wholesale market much like cash can be lent in the interbank money market. Central banks and other large holders of physical gold lease it to bullion banks, which use the borrowed metal to facilitate market-making, cover short positions, and supply jewellery fabricators, in exchange for a fee - the gold lease rate. The lease rate plays a role in the gold forward pricing formula analogous to a dividend yield on a stock or a convenience yield on an industrial commodity: it is a benefit that accrues to the holder of the physical metal but not to the holder of a forward contract.

Definition - Gold Lease Rate

The gold lease rate $\ell(t,T)$ for the period $[t,T]$ is the annualized rate a borrower of physical gold pays to the lender, typically denominated and paid in gold or settled in cash terms. Historically, the lease rate was closely related to the spread between LIBOR (or another unsecured funding rate) and the Gold Forward Offered Rate (GOFO), the rate at which bullion banks were willing to lend dollars against gold collateral: $\ell \approx \text{LIBOR} - \text{GOFO}$. GOFO was discontinued in 2015, but the underlying economic relationship - that the lease rate is the implicit yield earned by lending out physical gold rather than holding it idle - remains central to gold forward pricing.

Forward Pricing with the Lease Rate

The cost-of-carry relationship for gold forwards mirrors the standard relationship for an asset with a continuous "dividend" yield, where the lease rate plays the role of the dividend yield.

Definition - Gold Forward Price with Lease Rate

Let $S_t$ be the spot gold price, $r$ the continuously compounded risk-free (funding) interest rate over $[t,T]$, and $\ell$ the continuously compounded gold lease rate over the same period. The theoretical forward price for delivery at $T$ is $$F(t,T) = S_t\, e^{(r-\ell)(T-t)}.$$ The quantity $r - \ell$ governs whether gold trades in contango ($r > \ell$, the typical case, so $F > S_t$) or backwardation ($\ell > r$, so $F < S_t$). Backwardation in gold is unusual and signals that holders of physical metal are unwilling to lend it except at a rate exceeding the risk-free rate - typically a sign of acute physical scarcity or stress in the gold lending market.

Theorem - No-Arbitrage Bounds via the Gold Carry Trade

If $F(t,T) > S_t e^{(r-\ell)(T-t)}$, an arbitrageur can borrow gold at the lease rate $\ell$, sell it spot for $S_t$, invest the proceeds at $r$ until $T$, and simultaneously sell the forward at $F(t,T)$ and buy gold forward to return the leased metal at $T$. This locks in a riskless profit equal to the difference between $F(t,T)$ and the theoretical forward, scaled by the position size. The reverse trade (borrow cash, buy gold spot, lend the gold out at $\ell$, sell forward) bounds $F(t,T)$ from below. Together these replication arguments pin $F(t,T)$ to $S_t e^{(r-\ell)(T-t)}$ up to the bid-offer spreads and transaction costs in the gold lending and lending-cash markets, which are typically very tight for gold relative to other commodities.

Worked Example: Gold Carry Trade

Example - Pricing a One-Year Gold Forward

Suppose spot gold trades at $S_t = \$2{,}300$/oz, the one-year USD interest rate is $r = 5.0\%$ (continuously compounded), and the one-year gold lease rate is $\ell = 0.5\%$. The theoretical one-year forward price is: $$F = 2300 \times e^{(0.05-0.005)\times 1} = 2300 \times e^{0.045} \approx 2300 \times 1.04603 \approx \$2{,}405.9.$$

A bullion bank observing a market forward quote of, say, $\$2{,}415$ - above the theoretical $\$2{,}405.9$ - could execute the carry trade: borrow 1 oz of gold from a central bank lessor at $\ell = 0.5\%$, sell it spot for $\$2{,}300$, invest the cash at $r = 5\%$ to accumulate $2300 e^{0.05} \approx \$2{,}417.9$ at $T$, and sell gold forward at $\$2{,}415$ to lock in delivery of 1 oz (plus accrued lease fee of $2300(e^{0.005}-1)\approx \$11.5$) to the lessor. The arbitrage profit per ounce is approximately $2417.9 - 2415 - 11.5 \times \text{(adjustment)} $, which is small but riskless before transaction costs - exactly the kind of trade that keeps $F(t,T)$ pinned to the theoretical value.

If gold lease rates spike sharply above the risk-free rate - for example during a period when central banks recall leased gold and bullion banks scramble to source physical metal to meet delivery obligations - the term $r-\ell$ can turn negative, and the forward curve inverts into backwardation, with $F(t,T) < S_t$. Such episodes are rare for gold precisely because the above-ground stock is so large relative to any plausible demand shock, but they are far more common in silver and, especially, in industrial base metals (Section 1.2).

Silver: A Hybrid Monetary-Industrial Metal

Silver's forward pricing follows the same lease-rate framework as gold - $F(t,T) = S_t e^{(r-\ell_{Ag})(T-t)}$ - but silver lease rates tend to be more volatile and, on average, higher than gold lease rates, reflecting silver's larger industrial demand base and its smaller, less liquid above-ground investment stock relative to its industrial consumption.

Remark - Why Silver Lease Rates Are More Volatile

Roughly half of annual silver demand comes from industrial and fabrication uses (electronics, solar panels, brazing and soldering alloys, photography historically), compared to a small single-digit percentage for gold. Silver's gold-to-silver stock ratio (investment stock relative to annual flow) is therefore much lower, so shocks to industrial demand or mine supply can tighten the silver lending market more readily than the gold lending market, producing sharper and more frequent spikes in silver lease rates - and occasional episodes of silver forward backwardation - than is typical for gold.
