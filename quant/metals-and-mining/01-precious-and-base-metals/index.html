1. Precious and Base Metals

Metals markets span a continuum from gold and silver, which trade largely as monetary and store-of-value assets with deep, liquid lease markets, to industrial base metals such as copper and aluminum, whose prices are driven primarily by global manufacturing and construction demand. Both segments use the cost-of-carry framework, but the economic interpretation of the carry components differs sharply between the two.

Gold and Silver as Monetary Metals

Unlike most commodities, gold (and to a lesser extent silver) is held in enormous above-ground stockpiles relative to annual mine production — central bank reserves, ETF holdings, jewellery, and private investment vaults. This means gold supply is, for practical purposes, near-perfectly elastic at prices anywhere near the prevailing market price: a small price increase can draw enormous quantities of gold out of vaults and into the market. Consequently, gold behaves much more like a financial asset than an industrial commodity whose price is set by current physical scarcity.

Remark — Gold as a "Currency"

Because above-ground stocks vastly exceed annual flow (production and consumption), gold's price is determined primarily by its role as a store of value, an inflation hedge, and a portfolio diversifier — driven by real interest rates, currency movements (especially the U.S. dollar), and central bank reserve policy — rather than by industrial supply/demand imbalances. This is why gold's cost-of-carry relationship is unusually clean: gold is easy and cheap to store, has negligible convenience yield from physical use, and the forward price is determined almost entirely by the time value of money and the gold lease rate.

Lease Rates and Gold Forward Pricing

Gold can be lent and borrowed in the wholesale market — central banks and large holders lease gold to bullion banks, who use it to facilitate market-making, in exchange for a lease rate. This lease rate plays a role analogous to a "convenience yield" or dividend yield in the cost-of-carry formula for gold forwards.

Definition — Gold Forward Price with Lease Rate

Let $S_t$ be the spot gold price, $r$ the risk-free (funding) interest rate, and $\ell$ the gold lease rate, both continuously compounded. The theoretical forward price for delivery at $T$ is $$F(t,T) = S_t \, e^{(r - \ell)(T-t)}.$$ The quantity $r - \ell$, often called the gold forward offered rate (GOFO) differential or implied lease rate spread, governs whether gold trades in contango ($r > \ell$, the typical case) or backwardation ($\ell > r$, which can occur during episodes of acute physical gold scarcity or stress in the gold lending market).

Example — Gold Carry Trade

Suppose spot gold trades at $S_t = \$2{,}300$/oz, the one-year USD interest rate is $r = 5\%$, and the one-year gold lease rate is $\ell = 0.5\%$. The theoretical one-year forward price is $$F = 2300 \times e^{(0.05 - 0.005)(1)} = 2300 \times e^{0.045} \approx \$2{,}406.$$ A bullion bank can borrow gold at the lease rate, sell it spot for dollars, invest the dollars at $r$, and simultaneously buy gold forward at $F$ to return the leased gold at maturity — locking in the spread $r - \ell$ as a near risk-free profit, which is precisely why $F$ cannot deviate materially from this formula without arbitrage.

If gold lease rates spike sharply above the risk-free rate (signalling that holders of physical gold are reluctant to lend it out — often a sign of tight physical supply or stress), the forward curve can invert into backwardation, with $F(t,T) < S_t$.

Industrial Base Metals: Copper and Aluminum

In contrast to gold, base metals such as copper and aluminum are consumed in industrial processes (construction wiring, electronics, vehicles, packaging) and have annual consumption that is large relative to visible inventories. Their prices are therefore much more sensitive to the current state of physical supply and demand, and exhibit the classic convenience-yield-driven contango/backwardation dynamics introduced earlier in this subject.

Remark — Demand Drivers for Base Metals

Copper demand is closely tied to construction activity, electrical infrastructure, and increasingly the energy transition (electric vehicles, renewable power generation, and grid expansion all use copper intensively), making it a widely watched macroeconomic bellwether (sometimes nicknamed "Dr. Copper" for its perceived ability to diagnose the health of the global economy).

Aluminum demand is driven by transportation (lightweighting of vehicles), packaging, and construction, while supply is heavily influenced by electricity costs — aluminum smelting is extremely energy-intensive, so smelter output is sensitive to regional power prices and can be curtailed when electricity costs rise sharply.

LME Warehousing and Warrants

The London Metal Exchange (LME) is the principal global venue for base metals futures and operates a worldwide network of licensed warehouses where physical metal can be deposited against exchange contracts.

Definition — LME Warrant

An LME warrant is a document of title to a specific lot of metal held in an LME-registered warehouse. Warrants can be "cancelled" (earmarked for physical withdrawal) or remain "live" (available to satisfy delivery against a futures position). Reported LME inventory levels — and the split between live and cancelled warrants — are closely watched indicators of available exchange-deliverable supply, analogous to Cushing inventories for crude oil.

Contango and Backwardation in Metals

Example — Copper Curve and Convenience Yield

When LME copper inventories are low and spot demand is strong (e.g., during a period of supply disruptions at major mines combined with robust manufacturing activity), the copper curve can move into backwardation: the cash (spot) price trades above the 3-month forward price. This reflects a high convenience yield — the benefit to industrial consumers of holding physical copper on hand outweighs the financing and storage costs of holding a forward position instead.

Conversely, when inventories are abundant relative to consumption (e.g., during a manufacturing slowdown), the curve typically reverts to contango, with forward prices exceeding spot by approximately the cost of carry: $F(t,T) \approx S_t e^{(r+u-y)(T-t)}$, where $u$ is the storage cost and $y$ is the convenience yield, consistent with the cost-of-carry model developed in Chapter 1. Large, sudden moves into backwardation — sometimes called a "squeeze" — can occur when a small number of participants control a large share of deliverable warehouse stock, forcing short futures holders to pay up to acquire physical metal for delivery.
