1. Precious and Base Metals Fundamentals

This chapter establishes the foundational distinction between monetary and industrial metals, and how that distinction shapes the cost-of-carry relationship for each. Gold and silver are held in vast above-ground stockpiles relative to annual production, trade in deep wholesale lending markets, and behave more like currencies than industrial commodities - their forward curves are governed almost entirely by interest rates and lease rates. Section 1.1 develops the gold and silver lease rate framework, the forward pricing relation that links spot price, funding rates, and lease rates, and works through a numerical lease-rate arbitrage example.

Section 1.2 turns to the industrial base metals - copper and aluminum - whose prices are driven by global manufacturing and construction demand and whose exchange-deliverable inventories sit on the London Metal Exchange (LME). We introduce the LME as a market structure, the role of warehousing and warrants in setting visible supply, and the contango/backwardation dynamics that result from the interaction of inventories, convenience yield, and financing costs. Together these two sections give the vocabulary and pricing relationships - lease rates, forward prices, convenience yield, and contango/backwardation - that recur throughout the rest of this subject.
