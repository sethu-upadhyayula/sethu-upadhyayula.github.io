1.2 Base Metals: Copper, Aluminum, and the LME

In contrast to gold, base metals such as copper and aluminum are consumed in industrial processes - construction wiring, electronics, transportation, packaging - and annual consumption is large relative to visible inventories. Their prices are therefore far more sensitive to the current state of physical supply and demand than to interest rate differentials, and they exhibit the classic convenience-yield-driven contango/backwardation dynamics that distinguish industrial commodities from monetary metals.

Demand Drivers for Copper and Aluminum

Remark - Copper as a Macroeconomic Bellwether

Copper demand is closely tied to construction activity, electrical and electronic infrastructure, and increasingly the energy transition - electric vehicles, charging infrastructure, renewable generation, and grid expansion all use copper far more intensively per unit of output than the technologies they replace. Because copper demand tracks global industrial activity so closely, it is widely watched as a leading indicator of the health of the global economy, earning it the nickname "Dr. Copper" for its perceived diagnostic ability. Roughly half of global copper demand now comes from China, making Chinese construction and manufacturing data a key driver of copper price moves.

Remark - Aluminum and Energy Costs

Aluminum demand is driven by transportation (lightweighting of vehicles and aircraft), packaging (cans, foil), and construction (cladding, window frames), while supply is unusually sensitive to electricity costs - primary aluminum smelting via the Hall-Héroult process is extremely energy-intensive, consuming on the order of 13-15 MWh per tonne of metal produced. Smelter output is therefore directly linked to regional power prices: when electricity costs spike (as occurred in Europe during the 2021-2022 energy crisis), smelters can become loss-making and curtail or permanently close production, tightening global supply and supporting prices even amid weak end-demand.

The London Metal Exchange

The London Metal Exchange (LME) is the principal global venue for base metals futures and forwards, trading copper, aluminum, zinc, lead, nickel, and tin (along with newer contracts on cobalt, lithium, and steel scrap discussed in later chapters). Unlike most futures exchanges, which trade contracts with fixed monthly or quarterly expiries, the LME's core "ring" contracts trade on a rolling daily-prompt basis out to three months, supplemented by weekly prompts out to six months and monthly prompts out to longer tenors - a structure examined in detail in Chapter 2.

Definition - LME Warrant

An LME warrant is a document of title to a specific lot of metal (a defined brand, shape, and quantity) held in an LME-registered warehouse. Warrants can be "cancelled" - earmarked for physical withdrawal from the warehouse, typically because a consumer has bought the metal for delivery - or remain "live" (on-warrant), meaning they are available to satisfy delivery obligations against short futures positions. The reported split between live and cancelled warrants, and the absolute level of on-warrant stock, are closely watched indicators of immediately deliverable exchange supply, analogous to crude oil inventories at Cushing, Oklahoma for WTI futures.

Contango and Backwardation in the Copper Curve

The shape of the LME forward curve for an industrial metal reflects the balance between financing/storage costs (which push the curve toward contango) and convenience yield (which can pull the curve into backwardation when physical metal is scarce relative to immediate consumption needs).

Definition - Cost-of-Carry with Convenience Yield

For an industrial metal with spot price $S_t$, the forward price for delivery at $T$ satisfies $$F(t,T) \approx S_t\, e^{(r+u-y)(T-t)}$$ where $r$ is the financing (interest) rate, $u$ is the proportional cost of physical storage and insurance, and $y$ is the convenience yield - the non-monetary benefit accruing to a holder of physical metal from being able to use it in production or sell it immediately without the lead time of taking delivery against a forward. When $y > r+u$, the curve inverts into backwardation ($F(t,T) < S_t$); when $y < r+u$, the curve is in contango ($F(t,T) > S_t$).

Example - Copper Curve Under Tight and Loose Inventory Regimes

Tight inventory (backwardation). Suppose LME copper on-warrant stocks fall sharply due to a major mine strike, while manufacturing activity remains robust. Spot copper trades at $S_t = \$9{,}500$/tonne, but the 3-month forward trades at only $\$9{,}380$/tonne - a backwardation of $\$120$, or about $1.26\%$ over three months (roughly $5.1\%$ annualized). This implies an annualized convenience yield $y$ exceeding $r+u$ by approximately 5 percentage points: industrial consumers are willing to pay a substantial premium for metal they can use today rather than receive in three months.

Abundant inventory (contango). Now suppose a global manufacturing slowdown causes copper to flow back into LME warehouses, with on-warrant stocks rising sharply. Spot trades at $\$8{,}800$/tonne and the 3-month forward at $\$8{,}890$/tonne - a contango of $\$90$, or about $1.02\%$ over three months ($\approx 4.1\%$ annualized), broadly consistent with $r+u \approx 4\text{-}5\%$ and a near-zero convenience yield when stocks are ample. A sudden, sharp move from contango into steep backwardation - sometimes called a squeeze - can occur when a small number of participants control a large share of on-warrant stock, forcing shorts who must deliver to pay up sharply for nearby metal; LME rules on lending and backwardation limits (discussed in Chapter 2) exist specifically to limit the severity of such squeezes.
