4. Replication and Consistency Models

Replicating data across multiple machines (introduced practically in Chapter 8 of Software Engineering & Systems Design) improves availability and read performance, but immediately raises a question this chapter addresses directly: what exactly can a client expect a read to return, relative to recent writes that may not yet have reached every replica?

Strong Consistency (Linearizability)

Definition — Linearizability

Linearizability (strong consistency) guarantees that every operation appears to take effect instantaneously at some single point between its invocation and completion, and that all operations across all clients respect a single, globally agreed real-time order — the strongest practical consistency guarantee, behaving as though there were only ever one copy of the data, at the cost of requiring coordination (often consensus, Chapter 3) that can add latency and reduce availability during a network partition.

Eventual Consistency

Eventual consistency, introduced in Chapter 8 of Database Systems, guarantees only that replicas converge to the same value eventually, absent further writes — a much weaker but often more available and lower-latency guarantee, appropriate when an application can tolerate briefly reading stale data in exchange for better availability and performance.

Causal Consistency

Definition — Causal Consistency

Causal consistency sits between these two extremes — it guarantees that causally related operations (in the happens-before sense of Chapter 2) are seen by every process in the same order, while operations with no causal relationship (concurrent operations) may be seen in different orders by different processes without violating the guarantee. This is often strong enough to avoid confusing application-visible behaviour (a reply is never seen before the message it replies to) while remaining considerably cheaper to implement than full linearizability.

Read-Your-Writes and Session Guarantees

Several weaker, pragmatic session guarantees are common in practice — read-your-writes consistency ensures a client always sees its own prior writes reflected in subsequent reads, even if the system as a whole is only eventually consistent — addressing one of the most user-visible and confusing symptoms of weak consistency (a user updating something and then not seeing their own change) without requiring the full cost of strong consistency for every operation.

Choosing a Consistency Model

Remark — Matching the Model to the Actual Requirement

Choosing a consistency model is a genuine design decision balancing correctness needs against availability and performance, directly connected to the CAP theorem's tradeoff (Chapter 8 of Database Systems) — a banking ledger balance likely needs strong consistency, while a social media "like" count can often tolerate eventual consistency without any real user-facing harm, and conflating the two situations by defaulting to one consistency model everywhere is a common, costly design mistake.
