3. Consensus: Paxos and Raft

Consensus — getting a group of machines to agree on a single value, even if some of them fail or messages between them are delayed — is one of distributed systems' most fundamental problems, underlying everything from leader election to the replicated logs behind many distributed databases.

The Consensus Problem

Definition — Consensus

A consensus algorithm must satisfy: agreement (all correct processes decide on the same value), validity (the agreed value was actually proposed by some process, not fabricated), and termination (every correct process eventually decides) — all while tolerating the failure model in play (Chapter 1) and working around the FLP impossibility result (also Chapter 1) in practice.

Paxos

Definition — Paxos

Paxos, developed by Leslie Lamport, is the foundational consensus algorithm most later practical systems are built on or compared against. It proceeds through proposers suggesting values, acceptors voting on proposals in numbered rounds, and a value becoming chosen once a majority of acceptors accept it — using proposal numbers and a majority-quorum requirement to ensure agreement even if some acceptors fail or messages are delayed, at the cost of being notoriously difficult to fully understand and correctly implement.

Raft

Definition — Raft

Raft was explicitly designed as a more understandable alternative to Paxos, achieving equivalent guarantees through a clearer structural decomposition: leader election (nodes hold randomized-timeout-triggered elections to choose a single leader), log replication (the elected leader appends entries to a replicated log and replicates them to followers), and safety (mechanisms ensuring a new leader always has all previously committed log entries, so committed data is never lost across a leadership change).

Why a Single Leader Simplifies Things

Both Paxos and Raft benefit substantially from (though don't strictly require) electing a single leader to sequence proposals — without a leader, multiple processes proposing values simultaneously can repeatedly conflict and delay agreement (a "dueling proposers" problem in Paxos); a stable leader avoids this by having only one process actually propose new values during normal operation, resuming a new election only when the current leader is suspected to have failed (Chapter 6).

Consensus in Practice

Remark — Where Consensus Is Actually Used

Consensus algorithms rarely appear directly in application code — instead, they underlie coordination services (Chapter 8) and the replicated logs of many distributed databases (Chapter 4), which applications use as a building block rather than reimplementing consensus themselves, reflecting how subtle and easy to get wrong these algorithms are even for experienced distributed systems engineers.
