2. Design Principles

Beyond any specific pattern (Chapter 3) or architecture (Chapter 4), a handful of general design principles guide how individual classes and modules should be structured to stay maintainable as a codebase grows — this chapter covers the most widely referenced of them.

The SOLID Principles

Definition — SOLID

Single Responsibility — a class should have exactly one reason to change.
Open/Closed — code should be open for extension but closed for modification (extend behaviour by adding new code, not editing existing, working code).
Liskov Substitution — a subtype should be usable anywhere its supertype is expected, without breaking correctness.
Interface Segregation — prefer several small, specific interfaces over one large, general-purpose one.
Dependency Inversion — depend on abstractions, not concrete implementations (Chapter 3's dependency injection pattern is the most common practical technique for achieving this).

DRY: Don't Repeat Yourself

The DRY principle holds that every piece of knowledge or logic should have a single, unambiguous representation within a system — duplicated logic means duplicated maintenance burden and the real risk that a bug fix or change is applied to one copy but forgotten in another, silently reintroducing the very problem it was meant to fix.

KISS and YAGNI

Two Restraint-Oriented Principles
  • KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid) — prefer the simplest design that correctly solves the actual problem, resisting unnecessary cleverness or complexity.
  • YAGNI (You Aren't Gonna Need It) — don't build functionality or flexibility for hypothetical future requirements that may never materialize, since speculative generality has a real cost (complexity, maintenance burden) paid immediately for a benefit that may never actually be realized.

Separation of Concerns

Separation of concerns divides a system so that each distinct part addresses a separate, well-defined responsibility — the layered architectures covered in Chapter 4 are essentially separation of concerns applied at the level of an entire application's structure, and the same principle applies recursively down to individual functions and classes.

Principles as Guidelines, Not Absolute Rules

Remark — Applying Principles with Judgment

These principles frequently pull in different directions in a specific real situation — strict adherence to DRY can sometimes conflict with KISS if eliminating a small duplication requires a more complex abstraction than the duplication itself would have cost — and experienced engineers generally treat them as guidelines to weigh against each other and the specific context, rather than absolute rules to follow mechanically regardless of circumstance.
