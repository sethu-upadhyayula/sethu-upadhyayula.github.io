4. Software Architecture Patterns

Beyond the class-level design patterns of Chapter 3, this chapter covers architecture patterns — how an entire application's major components are organized and connected to one another.

Layered Architecture

Definition — Layered Architecture

A layered architecture organizes an application into horizontal layers — commonly presentation, business logic, and data access — each layer depending only on the layer directly beneath it, a direct application of the separation of concerns principle (Chapter 2) at the whole-application scale, and the traditional default structure for many business applications.

The Monolith

A monolithic application deploys all of its functionality as a single unit, typically (though not necessarily) organized internally using layers as above. Monoliths are simpler to develop, test, and deploy for a small team and codebase, but can become increasingly difficult to work on and independently scale as both the codebase and the team working on it grow substantially larger.

Microservices

Definition — Microservices

A microservices architecture splits an application into many small, independently deployable services, each typically owning its own data and communicating with other services over the network (Computer Networks) — allowing independent teams to develop, deploy, and scale their own services separately, at the cost of introducing genuinely distributed-systems complexity (Distributed Systems) — network failures, data consistency across service boundaries — that a monolith simply does not have to contend with.

Event-Driven Architecture

An event-driven architecture has components communicate by publishing and subscribing to events (often through a message broker) rather than through direct, synchronous calls — decoupling producers and consumers of information from needing to know about each other directly, and naturally supporting asynchronous processing, though at the cost of a system whose overall control flow is often considerably harder to trace and reason about than a comparable design built from direct calls.

Choosing an Architecture

Remark — There Is No Universally Correct Architecture

Microservices are often reached for by default in modern discussion, but a well-structured monolith remains a perfectly reasonable, often preferable choice for many applications and team sizes — the operational and consistency overhead microservices introduce (Distributed Systems) is a real cost that should be justified by an actual organizational or scaling need, not adopted simply because it is currently fashionable.
