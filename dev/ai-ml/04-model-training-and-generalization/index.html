4. Model Training and Generalization

Chapters 2 and 3 described what supervised and unsupervised models predict — this chapter surveys how a model's internal parameters actually get fit to training data, and how well the result can be expected to generalize to new data.

Loss Functions and Gradient Descent

Definition — Loss Function and Gradient Descent

A loss function measures how wrong a model's current predictions are compared to the true, known outputs in the training set — training then becomes an optimization problem: adjust the model's parameters to minimize this loss. Gradient descent is the standard optimization approach used, repeatedly adjusting parameters a small step in the direction that decreases the loss fastest (its negative gradient), continuing until the loss stops decreasing meaningfully.

Overfitting and Underfitting

Definition — Overfitting and Underfitting
  • Overfitting — a model fits its training data very closely, including its noise and idiosyncrasies, but generalizes poorly to new data.
  • Underfitting — a model is too simple to capture even the genuine underlying pattern in the training data, performing poorly on both training and new data alike.

Regularization

Regularization techniques discourage a model from fitting the training data too closely, directly combatting overfitting above — commonly by adding a penalty term to the loss function that grows with the model's parameter magnitudes, encouraging the optimization to prefer simpler models unless the extra complexity is genuinely justified by a meaningfully better fit.

Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Remark — Bias versus Variance

A model's generalization error can be understood as a tradeoff between bias (error from a model too simple to capture the true pattern — underfitting) and variance (error from a model too sensitive to the specific training data it happened to see — overfitting) — model complexity, training data quantity, and regularization above all shift this tradeoff, and much of practical machine learning is about finding a good balance point for the problem at hand.

Cross-Validation

Cross-validation gives a more reliable estimate of how well a model generalizes than a single train/test split alone — repeatedly partitioning the training data into different train and validation subsets, training and evaluating on each partition in turn, and averaging the results — commonly used to compare different models or different settings of a model's own configuration parameters ("hyperparameters") before final evaluation on a truly held-out test set.
