6. Attention and Large Language Models

The transformer architecture, introduced briefly in Chapter 5, underlies essentially every modern large language model — this chapter surveys the attention mechanism at its core, and roughly how such models are trained and used.

The Attention Mechanism

Definition — Attention (Informal)

Attention lets each position in a sequence look at every other position and decide, via a learned weighting, how much to draw on each one when computing its own updated representation — for instance, letting the representation for a pronoun draw heavily on the representation of the noun it refers to, regardless of how far apart they are in the sequence, unlike an RNN (Chapter 5) which must carry that information forward step by step.

Tokens and Embeddings

Text input to a language model is first broken into tokens (a similar idea to the tokens introduced in Compilers, Chapter 2, though produced by a different algorithm suited to natural language rather than a fixed programming-language grammar), each mapped to a numeric embedding vector — a learned representation placing tokens with similar meaning or usage near each other in a high-dimensional vector space.

Pretraining and Fine-Tuning

Definition — Pretraining and Fine-Tuning

A large language model is typically first pretrained on a very large, general corpus of text using a simple self-supervised objective, such as predicting the next token in a sequence — no manual labeling required, since the "label" at each position is just the next token in the existing text. The model may then be further fine-tuned on a smaller, more specific dataset (or via human feedback) to better follow instructions or specialize to a particular task.

Scale

Much of the recent progress in this area has come from scaling up three things roughly together — model size (number of parameters), training data quantity, and training compute — with empirically observed relationships ("scaling laws") describing how model performance tends to improve fairly predictably as all three increase together, though the topic of exactly why and how far this trend continues remains an active area of ongoing research and debate.

Limitations

Remark — What This Approach Doesn't Guarantee

A model trained to predict plausible-sounding next tokens has no built-in guarantee of factual accuracy — it can produce confident, fluent, and entirely incorrect statements (sometimes called "hallucination"), a limitation that stems directly from the next-token-prediction training objective above, not merely an occasional bug to be patched, and one actively addressed through fine-tuning, retrieval of external information, and other techniques.
