8. Modern Architectural Trends

This closing chapter surveys several current directions in computer architecture, building on the constraints and tradeoffs covered throughout this subject — particularly the end of easy single-core scaling (Chapter 7) that has driven much of this recent innovation.

Specialized Accelerators

Definition — Domain-Specific Accelerator

A domain-specific accelerator is hardware purpose-built for one particular class of computation — GPUs (Chapter 7) for data-parallel workloads, and more recently dedicated AI accelerators (such as tensor processing units) specifically optimized for the matrix-multiplication-heavy computations underlying neural network training and inference (AI/ML) — trading general-purpose flexibility for substantially higher efficiency on their specific target workload than a general-purpose CPU could achieve.

Heterogeneous Computing

Modern systems increasingly combine multiple different types of processing units — general-purpose CPU cores, a GPU, and specialized accelerators — on a single system or even a single chip, with software explicitly directing different parts of a computation to whichever unit handles it most efficiently, a design philosophy called heterogeneous computing.

Chiplets and Advanced Packaging

Remark — Beyond a Single Monolithic Chip

Rather than manufacturing an entire processor as one large, monolithic piece of silicon, modern high-end designs increasingly use chiplets — smaller, separately manufactured silicon dies combined within a single processor package — improving manufacturing yield (a defect in a small chiplet wastes far less silicon than the same defect in one enormous monolithic die) and allowing different chiplets to even be manufactured using different, independently optimized process technologies.

Power Efficiency as a First-Class Design Goal

Particularly since the shift toward multicore designs (Chapter 7), power efficiency (performance achieved per watt consumed) has become as central a design consideration as raw performance itself — driven by mobile devices' battery constraints and, at the opposite extreme, the enormous aggregate electricity costs of large data centers running thousands of processors continuously.

Architecture's Continuing Relevance

Remark — Why Architecture Still Matters

Even as software increasingly runs atop layers of virtualization and abstraction (Distributed Systems, Operating Systems), the underlying hardware architecture covered throughout this subject still fundamentally shapes what is efficiently possible — a well-written compiler (Compilers) and a well-designed operating system (Operating Systems) both ultimately succeed or fail at extracting performance based on how well they work with, rather than against, the processor architecture actually running the code.
