8. NoSQL and Modern Database Systems

This closing chapter covers NoSQL databases - a family of systems that depart from the relational model (Chapter 1) and, often, from strict ACID guarantees (Chapter 4), in exchange for different tradeoffs around scalability, flexibility, and performance.

Why NoSQL Emerged

Remark - Motivations Behind NoSQL

NoSQL systems emerged largely to address workloads where a rigid, normalized relational schema (Chapter 3) or strict ACID transactions (Chapter 4) were either a poor fit for the data's natural structure (deeply nested or highly variable documents, for instance) or an obstacle to horizontal scaling across many machines (Distributed Systems), rather than being a strictly superior replacement for relational databases in every context.

Major NoSQL Categories

The Major NoSQL Data Models
  • Key-value stores - the simplest model, mapping keys directly to opaque values; extremely fast and easy to scale, but offering minimal query capability beyond direct key lookup.
  • Document stores - store semi-structured documents (typically JSON-like), allowing flexible, nested schemas that can vary from document to document without an upfront rigid schema definition.
  • Column-family stores - organize data by column rather than by row on disk, well suited to analytical workloads reading a few columns across very many rows.
  • Graph databases - store data as nodes and edges, optimized for efficiently traversing relationships (e.g., "friends of friends") that would require many expensive joins (Chapter 2) in a relational model.

The CAP Theorem

The CAP Theorem

The CAP theorem states that a distributed data store can provide at most two of three properties simultaneously during a network partition: consistency (every read receives the most recent write), availability (every request receives a non-error response), and partition tolerance (the system continues operating despite network communication failures between nodes). Since partition tolerance is generally non-negotiable for any genuinely distributed system (Distributed Systems), the real practical choice most such systems face is between prioritizing consistency or availability when a partition actually occurs.

Eventual Consistency

Many NoSQL systems favour availability over strict consistency, offering eventual consistency instead - guaranteeing that, absent further updates, all replicas will eventually converge to the same value, without guaranteeing that any single read reflects the most recent write immediately - a materially weaker guarantee than the isolation and consistency properties covered in Chapters 4–5, chosen deliberately for workloads where availability under partition matters more than always reading the absolute latest value.

NewSQL and the Convergence of Models

Remark - Blending Relational Guarantees with NoSQL-Style Scale

More recently, NewSQL systems aim to provide the horizontal scalability associated with NoSQL systems while retaining full relational semantics and ACID transactions (Chapter 4) - reflecting a broader trend of relational and non-relational database design converging somewhat, rather than remaining two permanently separate, mutually exclusive categories of database technology.
