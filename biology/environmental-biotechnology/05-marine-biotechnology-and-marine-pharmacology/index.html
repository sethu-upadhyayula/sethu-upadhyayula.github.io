5. Marine Biotechnology and Marine Pharmacology

The ocean harbours an enormous, comparatively underexplored diversity of organisms and microbial metabolites - this chapter covers the distinctive biology of marine life and its biotechnological potential, particularly as a source of new pharmaceuticals.

Marine Organisms and Their Biology

Marine organisms face physical and chemical conditions markedly different from terrestrial life - high salinity, high pressure at depth, and in many regions, permanent darkness or extreme cold - driving the evolution (Chapter 4 of Evolution's discussion of adaptive radiation and convergent evolution) of distinctive physiological and biochemical adaptations rarely seen among terrestrial organisms.

Marine Microbes and Their Metabolites

Definition - Marine Natural Products

Marine microorganisms - often living under unusual chemical or physical pressures relative to terrestrial microbes - produce a distinctive range of secondary metabolites (conceptually related to the plant secondary metabolites of Chapter 7 of Plant Biology, but chemically often quite different), some of which have proven to be valuable leads for drug discovery (Chapter 7 of Structural & Computational Biology's discussion of drug design), a field known as marine pharmacology.

Marine Resource Assessment and Protection

Sustainably exploiting marine biotechnological resources requires accurate marine resource assessment - surveying population sizes and distributions of harvested or bioprospected marine species (Chapter 2 of Population & Community Ecology) - paired with marine environment protection measures to prevent overexploitation, connecting marine biotechnology directly to marine conservation concerns (Conservation Biology).

Seafood Microbiology

Seafood microbiology studies the microorganisms naturally associated with, or contaminating, harvested seafood - relevant both for food safety (detecting pathogenic or toxin-producing microbes, Chapter 8 of Microbiology) and food spoilage (understanding and controlling the microbial processes that limit seafood shelf life).

Oceanography and Marine Biology

Marine biotechnology is inseparable from basic oceanography - understanding ocean currents, temperature stratification, and nutrient distribution is essential context for interpreting where and why particular marine organisms and their associated microbial communities occur, and for predicting how marine ecosystems and their biotechnological resources will respond to the ocean acidification and warming covered in Chapter 4 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology.
