6. Biofouling, Corrosion and Biofilms in Marine Environments

This closing chapter covers a distinctly practical marine biotechnology problem - the unwanted colonization of submerged surfaces by marine organisms, and the biologically mediated corrosion this colonization can accelerate.

Biofouling

Definition - Biofouling

Biofouling is the accumulation of microorganisms, plants, algae, and larger sessile marine animals (such as barnacles) on submerged structures - ship hulls, pipelines, and offshore infrastructure - increasing hydrodynamic drag on vessels (raising fuel consumption), blocking pipes and intake systems, and providing a route for the introduction of invasive marine species (Chapter 6 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology) transported on the hulls of ships travelling between regions.

Marine Biofilms as the First Stage of Fouling

Biofouling typically begins with the rapid formation of a microbial biofilm (Chapter 3 of Microbiology) on any newly submerged surface - this initial biofilm alters the surface's chemical and physical properties in ways that subsequently promote attachment by larger fouling organisms, meaning effective anti-fouling strategies often specifically target this early microbial colonization stage before macrofouling can become established.

Microbially Influenced Corrosion

Definition - Microbially Influenced Corrosion

Certain bacteria, particularly sulfate-reducing bacteria (Chapter 5 of Microbiology's discussion of anaerobic respiration), can directly accelerate the corrosion of submerged metal structures - either through their metabolic byproducts (such as corrosive hydrogen sulfide) or by altering local chemical conditions at the metal surface within a biofilm - a economically significant concern for marine and offshore infrastructure that must be specifically accounted for in materials selection and maintenance planning.

Managing Biofouling and Corrosion

Anti-fouling strategies range from physical (regular cleaning, protective coatings) to chemical (biocidal anti-fouling paints, though increasingly regulated due to their own environmental toxicity) and, increasingly, biological approaches exploiting natural anti-fouling compounds produced by some marine organisms themselves (Chapter 5) as a more environmentally sustainable alternative to conventional biocidal coatings.

Closing the Curriculum

Biofouling and corrosion draw together microbiology (Microbiology), ecology (Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology), and applied biotechnology in a single practical problem - a fitting closing note for this subject, and for the biology curriculum as a whole, which has consistently shown how the molecular, cellular, organismal, and ecological perspectives developed across earlier subjects converge on the real-world problems biotechnology exists to solve.
