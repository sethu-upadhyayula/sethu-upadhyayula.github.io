4. Biogas and Biofuel Production

Organic waste - from agriculture, wastewater treatment (Chapter 1), and other sources - represents an energy resource that microbial fermentation can convert into usable fuel, the subject of this chapter.

Biogas Production

Definition - Anaerobic Digestion

Biogas (predominantly methane and CO₂) is produced by anaerobic digestion - a multi-stage microbial process (Chapter 5 of Microbiology) in which complex organic matter is broken down sequentially by different microbial groups, culminating in methanogenic archaea converting simple intermediate products into methane. This process can treat organic waste (from agriculture, food processing, or wastewater treatment, Chapter 1) while simultaneously recovering its energy content as a usable fuel.

Integrated Waste Management

Biogas production is increasingly incorporated into broader integrated waste management systems, in which biological, physical, and chemical waste treatment methods are combined to maximize both waste volume reduction and resource (energy, nutrient) recovery, rather than treating waste disposal and energy production as entirely separate concerns.

Liquid Biofuels

Example - Bioethanol and Biodiesel

Bioethanol is produced by yeast fermentation (Chapter 6 of Bioprocess Engineering) of sugars derived from crops such as sugarcane or corn, or increasingly from cellulosic (non-food) plant biomass using specialized cellulose-degrading enzymes. Biodiesel is chemically produced from plant oils or animal fats, though microbial and algal oil production is an active area of research aiming to reduce reliance on conventional crop-based feedstocks that compete with food production.

Biofuel from Waste

Producing biofuel specifically from waste streams - rather than from dedicated energy crops - avoids the land-use and food-security tradeoffs associated with first-generation biofuels, aligning fuel production directly with the waste treatment goals of Chapter 1 rather than competing with agricultural land otherwise used for food production.
