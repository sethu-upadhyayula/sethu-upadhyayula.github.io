1. Wastewater Treatment and Pollution Control

Wastewater treatment is, at its core, an applied microbiology problem - using naturally occurring microbial metabolism (Chapter 5 of Microbiology) at engineered scale to break down waste before it is released back into the environment.

Wastewater Treatment Systems

Stages of Wastewater Treatment
  • Primary treatment - physical removal of large solids by settling and screening.
  • Secondary treatment - biological treatment, using microbial communities (often as an activated sludge or biofilm, Chapter 6) to aerobically or anaerobically break down dissolved and suspended organic matter.
  • Tertiary treatment - further polishing steps (e.g., disinfection, nutrient removal) to meet water quality standards before discharge or reuse.

Aerobic and Anaerobic Treatment Processes

Secondary treatment can use aerobic processes (requiring active aeration, analogous to the aeration challenges covered in Chapter 5 of Bioprocess Engineering) that fully oxidize organic waste, or anaerobic processes (Chapter 5 of Microbiology) that instead convert organic waste into biogas (Chapter 4) - the choice depending on waste characteristics, energy considerations, and whether biogas recovery is a treatment goal in its own right.

Eutrophication and Nutrient Removal

Inadequately treated wastewater is a major contributor to the nutrient pollution and eutrophication covered in Chapter 5 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology - modern wastewater treatment increasingly includes dedicated biological nitrogen and phosphorus removal steps specifically to prevent this downstream ecological harm, rather than treating organic matter removal alone as sufficient.

Environment-Friendly Pollution Control Technologies

Remark - Biological Alternatives to Chemical Pollution Control

Biological pollution control approaches are increasingly favoured over purely chemical or physical methods where feasible, since they typically generate fewer secondary waste products and can often be operated at lower energy cost - a preference reflected across every biological technology covered in the remainder of this chapter and subject.
