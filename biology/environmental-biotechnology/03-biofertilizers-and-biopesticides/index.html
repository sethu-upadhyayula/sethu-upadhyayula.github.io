3. Biofertilizers, Biopesticides and Sustainable Agricultural Inputs

This chapter covers biological products developed as alternatives to conventional synthetic chemical agricultural inputs, aiming to reduce the environmental footprint of intensive agriculture while maintaining crop productivity.

Biofertilizers

Definition - Biofertilizer

A biofertilizer is a formulation of living microorganisms that, when applied to soil or seed, enhances nutrient availability to a crop - most commonly nitrogen-fixing bacteria (such as Rhizobium, Chapter 4 of Plant Biology, or free-living nitrogen fixers) and phosphate-solubilizing microorganisms that convert soil phosphorus into a form plants can actually absorb, reducing dependence on energy-intensive synthetic fertilizer manufacture.

Biopesticides

Definition - Biopesticide

A biopesticide controls agricultural pests using a biological agent rather than a synthetic chemical - including microbial biopesticides (such as the Bacillus thuringiensis toxin underlying Bt crops, Chapter 5 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding, applied here as a sprayed formulation rather than expressed transgenically), and biocontrol agents (natural predators or parasites of a target pest, Chapter 4 of Population & Community Ecology's discussion of predation applied deliberately for pest management).

Biosurfactants

Biosurfactants - surface-active compounds produced by certain microorganisms - have applications spanning bioremediation (Chapter 2, helping mobilize hydrophobic contaminants for microbial degradation), enhanced oil recovery (Chapter 2), and as a more biodegradable alternative to synthetic surfactants in various industrial applications.

Advantages and Limitations

Remark - Tradeoffs of Biological Agricultural Inputs

Biofertilizers and biopesticides generally have a smaller environmental footprint and lower risk of harming non-target organisms than their synthetic counterparts, but often act more slowly, are more sensitive to environmental conditions (temperature, soil moisture, existing microbial competition), and can show more variable performance across different fields and seasons - factors that continue to limit, though not prevent, their broader agricultural adoption alongside conventional inputs.
