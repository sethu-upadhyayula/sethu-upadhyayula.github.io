2. Bioremediation and Phytoremediation

Where pollution has already contaminated soil or water, biological organisms - rather than purely physical or chemical remediation methods - can often be exploited to break down or remove the contaminant directly, at lower cost and with less environmental disruption than excavation or chemical treatment.

Bioremediation

Definition - Bioremediation

Bioremediation uses microorganisms (naturally present or deliberately introduced) to degrade organic pollutants - most commonly petroleum hydrocarbons - into less harmful products, exploiting microbial metabolic pathways (Chapter 5 of Microbiology) capable of using the pollutant itself as a carbon and energy source.

In Situ versus Ex Situ Bioremediation

Bioremediation can be conducted in situ (treating contamination where it lies, often by stimulating existing microbial populations with added nutrients or oxygen, "biostimulation") or ex situ (excavating or pumping contaminated material to a controlled treatment facility) - the choice depending on contamination depth, site accessibility, and how quickly remediation is required.

Microbially Enhanced Oil Recovery

Related microbial techniques are also used proactively rather than only remedially - microbially enhanced oil recovery uses microorganisms (or their metabolic products, such as biosurfactants, Chapter 3) to improve extraction of residual oil from a reservoir that conventional extraction methods can no longer efficiently recover.

Phytoremediation

Definition - Phytoremediation

Phytoremediation uses plants, rather than microorganisms, to remove or stabilize contaminants - some plants can accumulate heavy metals to unusually high concentrations in their tissue (exploiting the metal-sequestering mechanisms covered in Chapter 8 of Plant Biology), after which the contaminated plant material is harvested and safely disposed of, gradually reducing the contaminant's concentration in the surrounding soil over successive growing seasons.

Limitations of Bioremediation

Both bioremediation and phytoremediation are generally slower than physical or chemical remediation and are effective only for contaminants that the chosen organism can actually metabolize or accumulate - meaning they are best suited to sites where speed is less critical than cost, environmental disruption, and long-term sustainability of the remediation approach.
