4. Translation and Protein Synthesis

Translation converts the nucleotide sequence of messenger RNA into the amino acid sequence of a protein, read three bases (a codon) at a time according to the genetic code.

The Ribosome and the Genetic Code

The ribosome, a large ribonucleoprotein complex of two subunits (large and small), is the site of translation. Its catalytic core, which forms peptide bonds, is composed of RNA - making the ribosome a ribozyme. Each mRNA codon specifies one of 20 amino acids (or a stop signal), with most amino acids specified by more than one codon (the code is degenerate), read by transfer RNAs bearing complementary anticodons.

Stages of Translation

Initiation, Elongation, and Termination
  • Initiation - the small ribosomal subunit, initiator tRNA, and mRNA assemble at the start codon (usually AUG), aided by initiation factors, before the large subunit joins.
  • Elongation - aminoacyl-tRNAs are delivered to the ribosome's A site, a peptide bond forms with the growing chain in the P site, and the ribosome translocates one codon along the mRNA, cycle after cycle.
  • Termination - a stop codon is recognized by release factors rather than a tRNA, triggering release of the completed polypeptide.

Regulation and Inhibition of Translation

Translation is regulated at initiation most commonly - for instance, through phosphorylation of initiation factors in response to cellular stress, which can globally reduce protein synthesis, or through regulatory sequences in the mRNA's untranslated regions bound by specific proteins or microRNAs (Chapter 7).

Example - Antibiotic Translational Inhibitors

Many clinically important antibiotics work by selectively inhibiting bacterial (prokaryotic) ribosomes while sparing eukaryotic ones - streptomycin causes misreading of the genetic code, tetracycline blocks aminoacyl-tRNA binding, and chloramphenicol inhibits peptide bond formation.

Post-Translational Modification and Degradation

Newly synthesized proteins are frequently modified - by proteolytic cleavage, phosphorylation, glycosylation, or other chemical additions - before reaching their mature, active form (Chapter 3 of Cell Biology covers their subsequent trafficking). Proteins are eventually degraded, often via the ubiquitin-proteasome system, in which a protein tagged with a chain of ubiquitin molecules is recognized and destroyed by the proteasome, a major mechanism controlling protein lifespan and levels of key regulators like cyclins.
