2. DNA Damage, Repair and Recombination

DNA is under constant chemical and physical assault, both from the environment and from a cell's own metabolism. A suite of repair pathways continually corrects this damage, and dedicated recombination machinery both repairs certain lesions and drives genetic exchange.

Sources and Types of DNA Damage

DNA damage arises from ultraviolet light (forming pyrimidine dimers), reactive oxygen species and other reactive metabolites (causing base oxidation and alkylation), ionizing radiation (causing single- and double-strand breaks), and spontaneous chemical instability (e.g., depurination, cytosine deamination).

DNA Repair Pathways

Major Repair Mechanisms
  • Base excision repair (BER) - a glycosylase removes a single damaged base, and the resulting gap is filled and sealed.
  • Nucleotide excision repair (NER) - removes a short stretch of DNA containing bulky, helix-distorting lesions, such as UV-induced thymine dimers.
  • Mismatch repair (MMR) - corrects base-pairing errors that escape polymerase proofreading, distinguishing the new strand from the template to know which base to correct.
  • Double-strand break repair - via non-homologous end joining (fast, error-prone, direct rejoining) or homologous recombination (slower, accurate, uses a sister chromatid as template).
Remark - Repair Deficiency and Disease

Inherited defects in these pathways cause characteristic disease syndromes - for example, xeroderma pigmentosum (defective NER, extreme UV sensitivity) and hereditary non-polyposis colorectal cancer/Lynch syndrome (defective MMR).

Homologous and Site-Specific Recombination

Definition - Homologous Recombination

The exchange of genetic material between two DNA molecules sharing extensive sequence similarity, mediated by proteins such as RecA in bacteria (or its eukaryotic homolog RAD51). It underlies accurate double-strand break repair, the segregation-linked crossing-over of meiosis (Chapter 7 of Cell Biology), and, historically, was central to classical genetic mapping.

Site-specific recombination, by contrast, occurs between short, specific DNA sequences recognized by a dedicated recombinase, independent of extended homology - the mechanism behind bacteriophage lambda integration into the host genome and, more recently, exploited in the Cre-Lox system for engineering genomes in the laboratory.
