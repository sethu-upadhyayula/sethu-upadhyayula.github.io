7. Non-Coding RNA and RNA Interference

Not all RNA codes for protein. A large and functionally diverse set of non-coding RNAs regulate gene expression, catalyze reactions, and defend cells against foreign nucleic acids - a layer of biology only fully appreciated in the last few decades.

Classes of Non-Coding RNA

Major Non-Coding RNA Types
  • Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and transfer RNA (tRNA) - the core machinery of translation (Chapter 4).
  • microRNA (miRNA) - short (~22 nt) RNAs that guide sequence-specific repression of target mRNAs, encoded in the genome and processed from longer hairpin precursors.
  • small interfering RNA (siRNA) - similarly short, but typically derived from long double-stranded RNA (e.g., from viral infection or experimentally introduced), guiding highly sequence-specific target cleavage.
  • Ribozymes - catalytic RNAs, such as the ribosome's peptidyl transferase center and self-splicing introns.
  • Riboswitches - regulatory RNA elements, usually in the untranslated region of a bacterial mRNA, that directly bind a small-molecule metabolite and change conformation to control expression of the gene they are part of, without any protein intermediary.

The RNA Interference (RNAi) Pathway

Definition - RNA Interference

Double-stranded RNA is cleaved by the enzyme Dicer into short duplexes (miRNA or siRNA), one strand of which is loaded into the RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC). The RISC-bound small RNA then guides the complex to complementary mRNAs, leading to their cleavage, translational repression, or destabilization.

Remark - RNAi as a Laboratory Tool

Because siRNAs can be designed to target virtually any gene of known sequence, RNAi has become a standard experimental tool for selectively "knocking down" gene expression to study gene function, and underlies several RNAi-based therapeutics now in clinical use.

Gene Silencing in Plants

In plants, related small-RNA pathways mediate transgene silencing and post-transcriptional gene silencing (PTGS), often as a natural antiviral defense mechanism, and are exploited experimentally to suppress specific genes in transgenic plants (Chapter 6 of Plant Biology).
