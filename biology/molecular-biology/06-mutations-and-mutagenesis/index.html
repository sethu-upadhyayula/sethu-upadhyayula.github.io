6. Mutations and Mutagenesis

Mutations are heritable changes in DNA sequence - the ultimate source of all genetic variation, but also a cause of genetic disease when they disrupt essential gene function.

Types of Mutation

Classifying Mutations by Molecular Change
  • Point mutations - a single base substitution; may be silent (no amino acid change, due to code degeneracy), missense (a different amino acid), or nonsense (a premature stop codon).
  • Insertions and deletions (indels) - addition or loss of one or more bases; if not a multiple of three, causes a frameshift, scrambling the entire downstream reading frame.

Causes of Mutation

Mutations arise spontaneously, from rare replication errors or chemical instability of DNA bases, or are induced by mutagens - chemical agents (base analogs, alkylating agents, intercalating agents) or physical agents (UV and ionizing radiation) that damage DNA or interfere with accurate replication.

Mutant Phenotypes

Classifying Mutations by Phenotypic Effect
  • Loss-of-function mutations reduce or eliminate gene activity; if a single functional copy is insufficient, the phenotype is termed lethal or conditional (visible only under specific conditions, e.g., temperature-sensitive alleles).
  • Gain-of-function mutations confer a new or enhanced activity.
  • Dominant-negative mutations produce a product that actively interferes with the function of the remaining normal gene copy.
  • Mutations are further classed as germinal (in gametes, heritable to offspring) or somatic (in body cells, not transmitted to offspring but potentially significant for the individual, e.g., in cancer).

Detecting Mutagens: The Ames Test

Example - The Ames Test

A standard assay for mutagenicity uses histidine-auxotrophic Salmonella strains that can only grow without histidine supplementation if a reverse mutation restores the gene's function. A test compound that increases the rate of these reversions above background is flagged as a potential mutagen - and, since many carcinogens act via DNA damage, as a possible carcinogen as well.
