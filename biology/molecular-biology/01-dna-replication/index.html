1. DNA Replication

Before a cell divides, its entire genome must be copied exactly once. DNA replication achieves this with remarkable speed and accuracy, coordinated by a large complex of enzymes acting at the replication fork.

Semiconservative Replication

The Meselson-Stahl Experiment

Using density-gradient centrifugation (Chapter 9 of Biochemistry) with ¹⁵N-labelled DNA, Meselson and Stahl showed that after one round of replication, each daughter duplex contains one parental strand and one newly synthesized strand - confirming Watson and Crick's proposed semiconservative mechanism over competing conservative or dispersive models.

Origins and the Replication Fork

Replication begins at one or more origins of replication, where initiator proteins unwind the double helix. In bacteria, a single origin (oriC) suffices for the whole (typically circular) chromosome; eukaryotic chromosomes, being far larger, use many origins fired in a coordinated fashion across S phase.

Key Enzymes at the Replication Fork
  • Helicase - unwinds the parental duplex ahead of the fork.
  • Single-strand binding proteins - stabilize the separated single strands.
  • Primase - synthesizes short RNA primers to initiate DNA synthesis.
  • DNA polymerase - extends primers, synthesizing new DNA in the 5′→3′ direction only.
  • DNA ligase - seals nicks between adjacent DNA fragments.

Leading and Lagging Strands

Because DNA polymerase synthesizes only 5′→3′, and the two parental strands are antiparallel, replication is asymmetric. The leading strand is synthesized continuously toward the fork; the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously, away from the fork, as a series of Okazaki fragments that are later joined by ligase after their RNA primers are removed and replaced with DNA.

Fidelity of Replication

DNA polymerases achieve very high fidelity through accurate base selection and a built-in 3′→5′ proofreading exonuclease activity that removes mismatched nucleotides immediately after they are added, reducing the overall error rate to roughly one mistake per billion bases replicated - with any remaining errors subject to further correction by the mismatch repair system (Chapter 2).
