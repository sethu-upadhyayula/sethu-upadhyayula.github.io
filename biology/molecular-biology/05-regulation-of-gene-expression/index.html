5. Regulation of Gene Expression

Cells constantly adjust which genes they express, in response to internal signals and the external environment. This chapter surveys regulatory strategies across prokaryotes, bacteriophages, and eukaryotes.

Gene Regulation in Bacteria

Repression and Induction

In an inducible operon (e.g., the lac operon), a repressor normally blocks transcription until an inducer molecule (e.g., a lactose derivative) binds the repressor and removes it from the operator. In a repressible operon (e.g., the trp operon), transcription proceeds by default until a co-repressor (e.g., tryptophan itself) activates the repressor, shutting expression off once the end product is abundant.

Gene Regulation in Bacteriophages

Bacteriophage lambda provides a classic model of a genetic switch: following infection, a regulatory decision between the lytic cycle (immediate phage replication and host cell lysis) and the lysogenic cycle (integration into the host genome as a dormant prophage) is governed by a small set of competing regulatory proteins (CI and Cro) that mutually repress each other's promoters.

Gene Regulation in Eukaryotes

Eukaryotic gene regulation is layered and more elaborate than in prokaryotes, involving combinatorial control by multiple transcription factors at enhancers and promoters (Chapter 3), alternative splicing (Chapter 3), and extensive chromatin-level control.

Chromatin and Gene Silencing

Genes packaged into heterochromatin (Chapter 6 of Cell Biology) are generally inaccessible to the transcription machinery. Histone-modifying enzymes add or remove chemical marks (acetylation, methylation) that loosen or tighten chromatin packing, while DNA methylation at gene promoters is independently associated with stable, heritable gene silencing - together forming the basis of epigenetic regulation.
