3. Viruses

Viruses occupy a strange position in any survey of life - most biologists do not consider them fully "alive" (they cannot replicate independent of a host cell), yet they profoundly shape the evolution, ecology, and health of every domain of cellular life.

Viral Structure

Definition - Virion Structure

A virus particle (virion) consists minimally of a nucleic acid genome (DNA or RNA, single- or double-stranded) enclosed in a protective protein coat, the capsid. Many viruses additionally have an envelope - a lipid membrane, typically derived from the host cell, studded with viral surface proteins used for attachment and entry.

Classification

Viruses are classified largely by genome type (DNA versus RNA, single- versus double-stranded, and, for RNA viruses, positive- versus negative-sense) together with capsid structure and the host organisms they infect - a scheme that reflects fundamentally different replication strategies (Chapter 3 of Molecular Biology's discussion of RNA and DNA synthesis) more than any shared evolutionary ancestry across all viruses.

Viral Replication and Life Cycles

Example - Lytic versus Lysogenic Cycles

Bacteriophages (viruses infecting bacteria) illustrate two contrasting replication strategies: the lytic cycle, in which the phage immediately hijacks the host to produce new virions and lyses the cell to release them, and the lysogenic cycle, in which the phage genome instead integrates into the host chromosome as a dormant prophage (Chapter 5 of Molecular Biology's discussion of bacteriophage lambda), potentially reactivating to a lytic cycle later.

Impact on Life

Viruses are a major force in microbial ecology and evolution - bacteriophages regulate bacterial population sizes and drive horizontal gene transfer via transduction (Chapter 6 of Genetics) - and, in animals and plants, cause an enormous range of disease (Chapter 8 of Microbiology and Chapter 8 of Plant Biology). Retroviruses and other viral elements have also left a lasting genomic legacy, having inserted sequences into host genomes over evolutionary time that now make up a substantial fraction of many eukaryotic genomes (Chapter 6 of Cell Biology's discussion of transposable elements).
