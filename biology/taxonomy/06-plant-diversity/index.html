6. Plant Diversity

Land plants evolved from a freshwater algal ancestor and subsequently diversified into the major lineages that dominate most terrestrial ecosystems today, each representing a distinct set of solutions to the challenges of life on land.

Bryophytes

Definition - Bryophytes

Mosses, liverworts, and hornworts - collectively bryophytes - were among the earliest land plant lineages, lacking true vascular tissue (Chapter 2 of Plant Biology) and true roots, and consequently restricted to consistently moist habitats. Their life cycle is unusual among plants in that the haploid gametophyte generation, rather than the diploid sporophyte, is the larger and more conspicuous stage.

Ferns

Ferns were the first plant lineage to evolve true vascular tissue, allowing greater size and more efficient water transport, but - like bryophytes - still require water for the motile sperm to swim to the egg during fertilization, restricting them to relatively moist environments despite their vascular advantage.

Gymnosperms

Definition - Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms ("naked seed" plants, including conifers, cycads, and ginkgo) were the first plants to evolve the seed - a desiccation-resistant, dispersible structure protecting the plant embryo - and pollen, eliminating the need for external water during fertilization and enabling colonization of much drier habitats than ferns or bryophytes can tolerate.

Angiosperms

Angiosperms (flowering plants) are by far the most diverse living plant lineage, distinguished by enclosed seeds (within a fruit derived from the ovary) and the flower - a specialized reproductive structure that, particularly through coevolution with animal pollinators (Chapter 5 of Evolution), is credited with driving angiosperms' rapid and extensive diversification since their appearance.

Plant Morphology, Anatomy, and Reproduction

Across these lineages, increasing structural complexity - true roots, vascular tissue, seeds, flowers - closely tracks increasing independence from consistently wet environments, connecting the diversity covered in this chapter directly to the anatomical and reproductive detail covered in Chapters 1 and 3 of Plant Biology.
