5. Fungi

Fungi form their own eukaryotic kingdom, distinct from plants and animals, unified by a distinctive mode of nutrition - absorbing nutrients from their surroundings rather than ingesting (like animals) or photosynthesizing (like plants).

Fungal Biology

Most fungi grow as networks of thread-like hyphae, collectively forming a mycelium, and possess cell walls built from chitin (Chapter 1 of Cell Biology) rather than the cellulose found in plants. Fungi are decomposers, parasites, or mutualists (as in lichens and mycorrhizae, Chapter 8 of Plant Biology) rather than primary producers.

Major Fungal Groups

The Major Fungal Phyla
  • Chytrids - the only fungi retaining flagellated spores, generally considered the earliest-diverging fungal lineage.
  • Zygomycetes - includes common bread molds, characterized by sexual reproduction producing a resistant zygospore.
  • Glomeromycetes - form arbuscular mycorrhizae, a widespread and ecologically crucial mutualism with plant roots.
  • Ascomycetes - the largest fungal group ("sac fungi"), including yeasts, morels, and many important plant pathogens and antibiotic-producing species.
  • Basidiomycetes - "club fungi," including mushrooms, rusts, and smuts, characterized by spore production on a specialized club-shaped structure.

Important Fungal Pathogens

Example - Fungal Disease in Plants and Humans

Fungal pathogens cause some of the most economically damaging plant diseases (Chapter 8 of Plant Biology), including rusts, smuts, and blights capable of devastating entire crop harvests. In humans, fungal pathogens range from superficial infections (e.g., dermatophyte skin infections) to serious systemic disease in immunocompromised patients (Chapter 8 of Immunology's discussion of immunodeficiency), reflecting the fact that, as fellow eukaryotes, fungi are harder to selectively target with antifungal drugs than bacteria are with antibiotics.
