2. Microbial Diversity: Bacteria and Archaea

Bacteria and Archaea together make up two of the three fundamental domains of life, and represent an enormous share of the planet's total genetic and metabolic diversity - far more than their small size would suggest.

Bacteria and Archaea as Separate Domains

Definition - The Three-Domain System

Though both are prokaryotic (lacking a membrane-bound nucleus, Chapter 1 of Cell Biology), Bacteria and Archaea are as distantly related to each other as either is to Eukarya, differing in fundamental molecular features such as membrane lipid chemistry (Chapter 1 of Cell Biology), RNA polymerase structure, and ribosomal RNA sequence - the basis on which Carl Woese first proposed splitting prokaryotes into two separate domains.

Broad Classification

Bacteria are broadly grouped by criteria such as Gram stain reaction (Chapter 1 of Cell Biology), shape (cocci, bacilli, spirilla), and metabolic strategy. Archaea, though less diverse in described species than Bacteria, show striking metabolic and ecological breadth, including many extremophiles - organisms thriving in high temperature, high salinity, or extreme pH.

Ecological Roles

Example - Microbial Contributions to Biogeochemical Cycles

Bacteria and Archaea drive much of the planet's nutrient cycling - nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Chapter 7 of Biochemistry) convert atmospheric nitrogen into usable forms, nitrifying and denitrifying bacteria cycle nitrogen compounds through soil and water, and methanogenic archaea produce a significant share of the world's methane, connecting microbial diversity directly to the biogeochemical cycles discussed in Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology.

Economically and Medically Important Microbes

Beyond their ecological roles, specific bacteria and archaea are significant for human affairs - as agents of infectious disease (Chapter 8 of Microbiology), as workhorses of industrial fermentation and biotechnology (Recombinant DNA Technology and Bioprocess Engineering), and, increasingly, as members of the human microbiome influencing health (Chapter 7 of Animal Physiology's discussion of the gut microbiome).
