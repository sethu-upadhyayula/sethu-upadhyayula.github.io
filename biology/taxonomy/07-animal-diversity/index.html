7. Animal Diversity

Animals - multicellular, heterotrophic organisms typically capable of movement at some life stage - comprise dozens of major phyla, of which only one, the chordates, includes the vertebrates most familiar in everyday experience.

Major Grades of Body Organization

Key Organizational Features Distinguishing Animal Phyla
  • Symmetry - radial symmetry (e.g., cnidarians) versus bilateral symmetry (most other animals), the latter associated with directional movement and a distinct head end (cephalization).
  • Body cavity - acoelomate (no body cavity, e.g., flatworms), pseudocoelomate (partially lined cavity, e.g., roundworms), or coelomate (fully lined cavity, e.g., annelids, arthropods, chordates).
  • Development pattern - protostome (mouth develops first, e.g., mollusks, arthropods, annelids) versus deuterostome (anus develops first, e.g., echinoderms, chordates), a distinction supported by both embryology and molecular phylogenetics.

Major Invertebrate Phyla

Example - Arthropods and Mollusks

Arthropods (insects, crustaceans, arachnids) are, by species count, the most diverse animal phylum by far, characterized by a chitinous exoskeleton and jointed appendages. Mollusks (snails, bivalves, cephalopods) show remarkable structural diversity built around a shared body plan of a muscular foot, visceral mass, and mantle - cephalopods in particular have independently evolved camera-type eyes and sophisticated nervous systems, a striking case of convergent evolution (Chapter 4 of Evolution) with vertebrates.

Vertebrates

Vertebrates - fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals - are a subgroup of the chordates, unified by a vertebral column protecting the dorsal nerve cord. Evolutionary relationships among vertebrate classes broadly track the transition from water to land (the evolution of limbs and lungs in the tetrapod lineage) and, within amniotes, the further divergence into the reptile/bird lineage and the mammalian lineage, each independently evolving solutions to reproduction and temperature regulation away from water (Chapter 7 of Animal Physiology's discussion of thermoregulation).

Molecular Phylogenetics and Animal Relationships

Molecular data (Chapter 5 of Structural & Computational Biology) has substantially revised earlier, morphology-based views of animal relationships - for example, confirming that arthropods, not annelids, are the closest relatives of a small marine phylum called Onychophora, and resolving long-standing uncertainty about the placement of several minor phyla whose morphology alone gave few reliable clues to their ancestry.
