4. Species Interactions

Populations rarely exist in isolation - their dynamics are shaped substantially by interactions with other species, ranging from open conflict over resources to close, mutually beneficial partnerships.

Competition

Definition - Interspecific Competition

Interspecific competition occurs when two species rely on the same limited resource, reducing each other's growth, survival, or reproduction. The competitive exclusion principle holds that two species with identical ecological niches (Chapter 1) cannot indefinitely coexist - one will eventually outcompete and displace the other, unless niche differentiation reduces the overlap in resource use enough to allow stable coexistence.

Predation

The Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey Model

The classic Lotka-Volterra model describes coupled oscillations in predator and prey population size - prey population growth is suppressed by predation, while predator population growth depends on prey availability, producing out-of-phase cyclical fluctuations in both populations over time, a pattern that has been observed (with variable fidelity to the simple model) in several real predator-prey systems.

Mutualism

Mutualism benefits both interacting species - examples span the mycorrhizal fungus-plant root associations (Chapter 5 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics), the legume-Rhizobium nitrogen-fixing symbiosis (Chapter 4 of Plant Biology), and the plant-pollinator relationships whose coevolutionary dynamics were covered in Chapter 5 of Evolution.

Parasitism

Parasitism benefits the parasite at the host's expense, typically without immediately killing the host (Chapter 8 of Microbiology covers the biology of specific parasites in detail) - from a population-dynamics perspective, parasite load can itself regulate host population size in a manner conceptually similar to predation, just operating on a slower timescale and rarely causing immediate death.
