1. Introduction to Ecology

Ecology studies the relationships between organisms and their environment, at scales ranging from a single individual to the entire biosphere. This chapter introduces the levels of organization ecology works across, and the basic factors that determine where a given organism can live.

Levels of Ecological Organization

The Ecological Hierarchy
  • Individual - a single organism, and its physiological and behavioural responses to the environment.
  • Population - all individuals of one species in a given area, the focus of Chapters 2–4.
  • Community - all populations of different species co-occurring in a given area, interacting with one another (Chapters 5–6).
  • Ecosystem - a community together with its physical (abiotic) environment, covered in Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology.
  • Biosphere - the sum of all of Earth's ecosystems.

Abiotic and Biotic Factors

An organism's distribution and abundance are shaped by abiotic factors (temperature, water availability, light, soil chemistry, and other non-living components of the environment) and biotic factors (interactions with other organisms - competitors, predators, mutualists, and pathogens, covered in Chapter 5). Both must fall within a tolerable range for a species to persist in a given location.

Ecological Adaptations

Example - Ecological Adaptation to Abiotic Stress

Organisms living in physiologically demanding environments often show specific adaptations to their local abiotic conditions - the water-conserving physiology of desert plants (Chapter 8 of Plant Biology) and the thermoregulatory strategies of animals in extreme climates (Chapter 6 of Animal Physiology) both illustrate how abiotic factors shape not just where a species can live, but its physiology once it does.

Ecological Niche

The ecological niche is the full set of biotic and abiotic conditions and resources a species needs to survive and reproduce, together with its functional role in the community (e.g., its position in a food web, Chapter 6) - a concept that becomes central once competition and species interactions (Chapter 5) are considered.
