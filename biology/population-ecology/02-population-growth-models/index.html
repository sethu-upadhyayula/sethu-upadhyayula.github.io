2. Population Growth Models

Population ecology begins with simple mathematical models of how a population's size changes over time - models that, despite their simplicity, capture much of the basic logic behind real population dynamics.

Exponential Growth

The Exponential Growth Model

When resources are unlimited, a population grows at a constant per-capita rate, described by $$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN$$ where $N$ is population size and $r$ is the intrinsic growth rate - the same exponential growth law introduced for microbial populations in Chapter 3 of Microbiology, here applied at the scale of whole organisms.

Logistic Growth

The Logistic Growth Model

Real populations cannot grow exponentially forever - resources eventually become limiting. The logistic model, $$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right)$$ introduces a carrying capacity $K$, the population size an environment can sustain indefinitely; growth slows as $N$ approaches $K$, and the population approaches a stable equilibrium at $K$ rather than growing without bound.

Density-Dependent and Density-Independent Factors

Density-dependent factors (such as competition for food or space, and the spread of disease) intensify as population density increases, providing the negative feedback that produces logistic-type growth. Density-independent factors (such as a severe storm or extreme temperature event) affect a population regardless of its size, and can cause sharp population changes unrelated to the logistic model's assumptions.

Life Tables and Survivorship Curves

Definition - Life Table and Survivorship Curve

A life table tracks the proportion of a cohort surviving to each successive age, and the average number of offspring produced at each age. Plotting survivorship against age produces a survivorship curve, broadly classified into three types: Type I (high survival until old age, typical of large mammals with few offspring and high parental investment), Type II (constant mortality rate across the lifespan), and Type III (very high early mortality, typical of species producing many offspring with little individual investment) - connecting directly to the life history strategies covered in Chapter 3.
