3. Population Dynamics and Life History Strategies

Beyond simple population size, ecologists track a population's internal structure and the reproductive strategy its members follow - factors that strongly influence how a population will respond to future environmental change.

Age Structure and Sex Ratio

A population's age structure - the relative number of individuals in each age class - strongly predicts its near-future growth trajectory: a population weighted toward pre-reproductive individuals will tend to keep growing even if birth rates immediately fell to replacement level, simply because more individuals have yet to reach reproductive age. Sex ratio similarly affects a population's reproductive potential, particularly in species where only one sex provides significant parental care.

Life History Strategies: r-Selection and K-Selection

Definition - r-Selected and K-Selected Species

r-selected species are adapted to exploit unstable or unpredictable environments, favouring rapid reproduction, many offspring, and little parental investment per offspring (corresponding to a Type III survivorship curve, Chapter 2) - the strategy favoured when population density is typically well below carrying capacity. K-selected species are adapted to stable environments near carrying capacity, favouring fewer offspring, larger body size, and substantial parental investment (Type I survivorship) - the strategy favoured when competition for limited resources is the dominant selective pressure.

Remark - r/K as a Continuum

Real species fall along a continuum between these two extremes rather than into two discrete categories, and the same species can shift its strategy somewhat depending on local conditions - the r/K framework is best understood as a useful conceptual axis rather than a strict binary classification.

Metapopulations

Definition - Metapopulation

A metapopulation is a set of spatially separated populations of the same species, connected by occasional migration between patches of suitable habitat. Individual local populations may go extinct and be re-established via migration from other patches - meaning the metapopulation as a whole can persist stably even while individual patches experience turnover, provided migration between patches is sufficient.

Habitat Fragmentation and Connectivity

Habitat fragmentation - the breaking up of continuous habitat into smaller, more isolated patches, typically by human land use - reduces connectivity between metapopulation patches, decreasing the migration rate that would otherwise rescue a locally extinct patch, and consequently increasing overall extinction risk (a theme developed further in Conservation Biology).
