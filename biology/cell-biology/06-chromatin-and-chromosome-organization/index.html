6. Chromatin and Chromosome Organization

The genome of a cell is not simply a bare strand of DNA - it is packaged, folded, and organized in ways that affect which genes can be expressed and how faithfully the genome is copied and segregated at division.

Nucleosomes and Chromatin

Definition - Nucleosome

The basic repeating unit of eukaryotic chromatin: about 147 base pairs of DNA wrapped nearly twice around a histone octamer (two copies each of histones H2A, H2B, H3, and H4). Linker DNA and histone H1 connect successive nucleosomes, and further coiling compacts chromatin into higher-order structures.

Euchromatin versus Heterochromatin

Euchromatin is loosely packed and transcriptionally active; heterochromatin is densely packed and largely silent, either permanently (constitutive, e.g., at centromeres) or in a cell-type-specific manner (facultative, e.g., the inactivated X chromosome).

Genome Organization: Genes and Non-Coding DNA

Eukaryotic genes are frequently interrupted - coding exons are separated by non-coding introns removed during RNA splicing. Genomes also contain extensive unique and repetitive DNA, ranging from tandemly repeated satellite DNA at centromeres to dispersed transposable elements that make up a large fraction of many eukaryotic genomes, and gene families arising from gene duplication over evolutionary time.

Example - The Bacterial Operon

In prokaryotes, functionally related genes are often organized into an operon, transcribed together as a single polycistronic mRNA from one promoter and regulated as a unit (e.g., the lac operon) - a compact organizational strategy largely absent in eukaryotic genomes.

Chromosome Structure

Each eukaryotic chromosome has a centromere, where kinetochore proteins assemble to attach spindle microtubules during division, and telomeres - repetitive sequences at each end that protect against progressive DNA loss and are maintained by the enzyme telomerase in germline and stem cells.
