8. Cell Death, Aging and Senescence

Cell death is not always accidental damage - much of it is a controlled, genetically programmed process essential for normal development and tissue homeostasis. This closing chapter distinguishes the major forms of cell death and introduces the cellular basis of aging.

Apoptosis

Definition - Apoptosis

Programmed cell death characterized by cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation, DNA fragmentation, and membrane blebbing into apoptotic bodies that are cleared by phagocytosis without triggering inflammation. Apoptosis proceeds via two convergent pathways: the extrinsic pathway (triggered by external death-receptor signals) and the intrinsic (mitochondrial) pathway (triggered by internal stress, releasing cytochrome c) - both ultimately activating a cascade of caspase proteases that dismantle the cell.

Necrosis

In contrast to apoptosis, necrosis is an uncontrolled form of cell death, typically resulting from acute injury (ischemia, toxins, trauma), characterized by cell swelling and membrane rupture that spills cellular contents and triggers local inflammation.

Autophagy

Definition - Autophagy

A regulated degradative process in which a double-membrane structure (the autophagosome) engulfs cytoplasmic components - damaged organelles or protein aggregates - and delivers them to the lysosome for breakdown. Autophagy can support cell survival during starvation by recycling internal building blocks, but excessive autophagy can also contribute to cell death.

Aging and Senescence

Cellular senescence is a stable, largely irreversible exit from the cell cycle triggered by factors such as telomere shortening, DNA damage, or oncogene activation. Senescent cells remain metabolically active and secrete inflammatory signalling molecules (the senescence-associated secretory phenotype), and their accumulation with age is thought to contribute to tissue aging and age-related disease, even though senescence itself likely evolved as a tumour-suppressive mechanism to halt the division of damaged cells.
