7. The Cell Cycle and Cell Division

Cell division must duplicate the genome exactly once and distribute it accurately to two daughter cells - a process tightly controlled at several checkpoints to prevent errors that could lead to cell death or, in multicellular organisms, cancer.

Phases of the Cell Cycle

Definition - The Four Phases

$G_1$ (growth and preparation), $S$ (DNA synthesis/replication), $G_2$ (further growth and preparation for division), and $M$ (mitosis and cytokinesis). Cells that exit the cycle to a non-dividing state are said to enter $G_0$.

Regulation and Checkpoints

Progression through the cycle is driven by cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs), active only when bound to their regulatory partner cyclins, whose levels rise and fall at specific points in the cycle. Checkpoints at the $G_1$/S, $G_2$/M, and metaphase/anaphase transitions monitor DNA integrity and chromosome attachment, halting the cycle if problems are detected - failure of these checkpoints is a hallmark of cancer (see Chapter 4 of Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology).

Mitosis

Stages of Mitosis

Prophase (chromosome condensation, spindle formation), metaphase (chromosomes align at the spindle equator), anaphase (sister chromatids separate to opposite poles), and telophase (nuclear envelopes reform), followed by cytokinesis dividing the cytoplasm - producing two genetically identical daughter cells.

Meiosis

Meiosis produces haploid gametes through two successive divisions following a single round of DNA replication. Meiosis I separates homologous chromosome pairs (with crossing-over generating genetic recombination during prophase I), while meiosis II resembles mitosis, separating sister chromatids - together halving the chromosome number and generating genetic diversity among offspring.
