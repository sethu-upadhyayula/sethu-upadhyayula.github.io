4. Mitochondria and Other Organelles

Beyond the nucleus, ER, and Golgi, eukaryotic cells contain several other membrane-bound compartments, each dedicated to a specialized biochemical task.

Mitochondria

Definition - Mitochondrial Structure

Mitochondria have a smooth outer membrane and a highly folded inner membrane (forming cristae) that houses the electron transport chain and ATP synthase (Chapter 5 of Biochemistry). The innermost compartment, the matrix, contains the enzymes of the TCA cycle and the mitochondrion's own circular DNA and ribosomes.

Remark - Endosymbiotic Origin

Mitochondria (and chloroplasts) are thought to have originated as free-living bacteria engulfed by an ancestral host cell, evidenced by their own genome, double membrane, and bacteria-like ribosomes - the endosymbiotic theory.

Lysosomes and Peroxisomes

Lysosomes are acidic, enzyme-filled organelles that degrade macromolecules delivered by endocytosis, phagocytosis, or autophagy (Chapter 8); their roughly 50 hydrolytic enzymes function optimally at the lysosome's low internal pH, protecting the rest of the cell if one leaks. Peroxisomes carry out oxidative reactions, including fatty acid β-oxidation and detoxification of reactive oxygen species via catalase, and - unlike mitochondria - are not derived by endosymbiosis but form by growth and division from existing peroxisomes or budding from the ER.

Plastids and the Vacuole

Example - Plant-Specific Organelles

Plastids are a family of related organelles in plants and algae, including chloroplasts (photosynthesis), chromoplasts (pigment storage, e.g., in fruit), and amyloplasts (starch storage). The large central vacuole found in mature plant cells regulates turgor pressure, stores metabolites and pigments, and serves a degradative role analogous to the lysosome.
