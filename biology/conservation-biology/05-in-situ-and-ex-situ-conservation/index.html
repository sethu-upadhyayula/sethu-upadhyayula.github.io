5. In-Situ and Ex-Situ Conservation Strategies

Conservation practitioners draw on two broad, complementary strategies for protecting threatened species - conserving them in their natural habitat, or maintaining them outside it - this chapter covers both, along with the growing recognition of local communities' role in conservation success.

In-Situ Conservation

Definition - In-Situ Conservation

In-situ conservation protects species within their natural habitat, most commonly through protected areas (national parks, wildlife reserves) that limit habitat destruction and unsustainable exploitation (Chapter 2). This approach preserves not just the target species but the entire ecological community and the species interactions (Chapter 4 of Population & Community Ecology) it depends on, generally considered the preferred strategy wherever feasible.

Ex-Situ Conservation

Definition - Ex-Situ Conservation

Ex-situ conservation maintains species outside their natural habitat - zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks (Chapter 7), and captive breeding programs - providing a safeguard against extinction in the wild and, in some cases, a source population for later reintroduction. Ex-situ populations require careful genetic management (Chapter 3) to avoid the inbreeding and diversity loss that can occur rapidly in a small captive population.

Reintroduction Programs

Species conserved ex-situ can sometimes be reintroduced to restored or protected habitat, though success depends heavily on first removing the original threat that caused decline, ensuring reintroduced individuals (often captive-bred, and potentially lacking wild survival skills) receive adequate preparation, and applying the population viability principles of Chapter 4 to determine an adequate founding population size.

Community-Based Conservation

Remark - Indigenous Knowledge and Community-Based Conservation

Conservation strategies increasingly recognize that local and indigenous communities often possess detailed ecological knowledge accumulated over generations, and that conservation efforts are typically far more effective and sustainable when they actively involve, rather than exclude, the communities living alongside the species and habitats being protected - a shift from earlier, more exclusionary approaches to protected area management.
