6. Restoration Ecology

Where habitat has already been degraded or lost, conservation can move beyond simply protecting what remains to actively rebuilding what has been damaged - the focus of restoration ecology.

Habitat Restoration

Definition - Habitat Restoration

Habitat restoration actively intervenes to return a degraded ecosystem toward its former structure and function - for example, replanting native vegetation, removing invasive species (Chapter 6 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology), or reintroducing natural disturbance regimes (such as periodic fire) that a degraded system may have lost.

Restoration and Ecological Succession

Restoration projects work directly with the successional processes covered in Chapter 6 of Population & Community Ecology, often attempting to accelerate or redirect a site's natural successional trajectory toward a desired target community rather than waiting for unassisted secondary succession to run its course over a much longer timeframe.

Afforestation

Afforestation - establishing forest cover on land that was not previously forested, or reforestation on land that has lost its forest cover - is used both to restore habitat directly and, given forests' substantial carbon storage capacity (Chapter 2 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology), as a climate mitigation strategy, though poorly planned afforestation using non-native monocultures can itself reduce local biodiversity rather than restoring it.

Ecological Hotspots and Prioritization

Example - Biodiversity Hotspots

Given limited conservation resources, biodiversity hotspots - regions combining exceptional species richness and endemism (Chapter 1) with significant existing habitat loss - are used to prioritize where conservation and restoration investment will protect the greatest amount of otherwise-irreplaceable biodiversity per unit of effort or funding.
