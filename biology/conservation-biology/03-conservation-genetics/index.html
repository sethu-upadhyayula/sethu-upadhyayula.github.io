3. Conservation Genetics

Conservation genetics applies the population genetics principles covered in Population & Quantitative Genetics directly to the practical problem of managing small, endangered populations, for which genetic factors can pose as serious a threat as demographic ones.

Genetic Threats in Small Populations

Genetic Risks of Small Population Size
  • Loss of genetic diversity - small populations lose genetic variation faster to genetic drift (Chapter 2 of Population & Quantitative Genetics), reducing their capacity to adapt to future environmental change.
  • Inbreeding depression - reduced survival or fertility resulting from increased mating between close relatives in a small population, exposing deleterious recessive alleles (Chapter 1 of Genetics).
  • Accumulation of deleterious mutations - small population size can allow mildly harmful mutations to drift to higher frequency than selection alone would permit in a larger population.

Measuring Genetic Diversity for Conservation

Molecular markers (Chapter 2 of Genetics) allow direct measurement of genetic diversity within a population and genetic differentiation between populations (Chapter 2 of Population & Quantitative Genetics's discussion of $F_{ST}$), informing decisions about which populations most urgently need genetic management and which populations could serve as a source for genetic rescue.

Genetic Rescue and Managed Breeding

Example - Genetic Rescue

Genetic rescue - deliberately introducing individuals from a genetically distinct population to increase diversity and reduce inbreeding in a threatened population - has reversed population decline in several documented cases, though it must be balanced against the risk of outbreeding depression, in which mixing genetically or locally adapted lineages disrupts co-adapted gene complexes and reduces, rather than improves, fitness.

DNA Barcoding in Conservation

DNA barcoding (Chapter 7 of Genomics) is increasingly applied in conservation to detect illegal wildlife trade (identifying the species of origin from processed animal products), verify captive breeding records, and confirm species identity in cases where morphological identification is ambiguous or impossible.
