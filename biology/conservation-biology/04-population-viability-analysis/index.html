4. Population Viability Analysis

Population viability analysis translates the demographic and genetic risks facing a threatened species into a quantitative estimate of its actual extinction risk, providing conservation managers with a rigorous basis for prioritizing action.

Population Viability Analysis (PVA)

Definition - Population Viability Analysis

Population viability analysis (PVA) uses demographic data (birth rates, death rates, age structure, Chapter 3 of Population & Community Ecology) together with estimates of environmental variability to model a population's probability of persistence over a specified time horizon, typically expressed as the probability of extinction within, say, 100 years under current conditions.

Minimum Viable Population Size

PVA models are often used to estimate a minimum viable population (MVP) size - the smallest population size with an acceptably low probability of extinction over a defined period, accounting for demographic stochasticity (chance variation in individual survival and reproduction, disproportionately impactful in small populations), environmental stochasticity, and the genetic risks covered in Chapter 3.

Stochasticity and Extinction Vortices

Remark - The Extinction Vortex

Small populations can enter a self-reinforcing extinction vortex, in which reduced population size increases genetic and demographic risk (Chapter 3), which further reduces population size, accelerating the decline toward extinction - a dynamic that highlights why conservation intervention is generally far more effective, and less costly, when applied before a population becomes critically small rather than after.

Applications of PVA in Conservation Planning

PVA outputs directly inform practical conservation decisions - how large a protected area needs to be to support a viable population, how many individuals are needed to found a new population via reintroduction (Chapter 5), and which of several at-risk populations warrants the most urgent intervention given limited conservation resources.
