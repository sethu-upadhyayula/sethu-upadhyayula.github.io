1. Biodiversity: Value and Measurement

Before discussing how to conserve biodiversity, this chapter covers what biodiversity actually means, why it is considered valuable, and how it can be measured and compared across different communities and regions.

Dimensions of Biodiversity

Three Levels of Biodiversity
  • Genetic diversity - variation within a species (Chapter 2 of Population & Quantitative Genetics), providing the raw material for future adaptation.
  • Species diversity - the number and relative abundance of species within a community (Chapter 5 of Population & Community Ecology).
  • Ecosystem diversity - the variety of distinct ecosystem types across a landscape or region.

Why Biodiversity Matters

Biodiversity is valued for several distinct reasons: its direct role in providing ecosystem services (Chapter 3 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology), its economic value (food, medicine, materials), its scientific and educational value, and - for many people - its intrinsic or ethical value, independent of any direct human benefit.

Measuring Species Diversity

Definition - Diversity Indices

Species diversity combines species richness (the number of species present) with species evenness (how equally individuals are distributed among those species); diversity indices such as the Shannon index combine both components into a single comparable number, allowing quantitative comparison of biodiversity between different communities or over time within the same community.

Global Patterns of Biodiversity

Species richness is not evenly distributed globally - it generally increases toward the tropics, a pattern (the latitudinal diversity gradient) attributed to some combination of greater available energy, longer evolutionary time without disruption by glaciation, and more stable climatic conditions, though its precise causes remain an active area of ecological research.
