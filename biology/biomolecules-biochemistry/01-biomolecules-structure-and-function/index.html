1. Biomolecules: Structure and Function

Living systems are built from a small set of chemical building blocks: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids, along with water and a handful of vitamins and minerals. This chapter surveys the composition and organization of each class, and the non-covalent forces that hold larger biomolecules in their functional shapes.

Water and Biophysical Chemistry

Water is not a passive solvent - its polarity, hydrogen-bonding capacity, and high heat capacity shape nearly every biomolecular interaction. Hydrophobic effects (the tendency of nonpolar groups to minimize contact with water) drive protein folding, membrane assembly, and ligand binding.

Definition - pH and Buffers

pH is defined as $-\log_{10}[\text{H}^+]$. A buffer resists changes in pH upon addition of acid or base, and is most effective within about one pH unit of its pKa, described by the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation: $\text{pH} = \text{p}K_a + \log_{10}\frac{[\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}$.

Reaction kinetics, thermodynamics (free energy, enthalpy, entropy), and colligative properties (freezing-point depression, osmotic pressure) provide the physical-chemistry vocabulary used throughout biochemistry - most importantly in describing enzyme catalysis (Chapter 4) and bioenergetics (Chapter 5).

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates range from simple monosaccharides to complex polysaccharides, serving roles in energy storage, structure, and cell recognition.

Classification

Monosaccharides (e.g., glucose, fructose, ribose) are single sugar units. Disaccharides (sucrose, lactose, maltose) are two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond. Polysaccharides are long chains - starch and glycogen for storage, cellulose and chitin for structure.

Example - Glycosidic Bonds

Glucose molecules linked by $\alpha(1\to4)$ bonds form starch and glycogen (readily digestible, energy storage), while $\beta(1\to4)$ links form cellulose (structural, resistant to most digestive enzymes). The stereochemistry of a single bond changes the molecule's biological role entirely.

Lipids

Lipids are a chemically diverse group unified by their hydrophobicity. Key categories include fatty acids, triacylglycerols (energy storage), phospholipids and sphingolipids (membrane structure), and steroids (signaling and membrane rigidity).

Remark - Amphipathic Behaviour

Phospholipids have a polar head group and nonpolar tails. In water, this amphipathic character drives spontaneous self-assembly into bilayers - the physical basis of all biological membranes (see Chapter 4 of Cell Biology).

Proteins: Building Blocks

Proteins are polymers of 20 standard amino acids, joined by peptide bonds. Each amino acid has a central (α) carbon bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, a hydrogen, and a distinctive side chain (R group) that determines its chemical character - nonpolar, polar uncharged, acidic, or basic.

Definition - Peptide Bond

A peptide bond is an amide linkage formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next, with loss of water. The resulting C–N bond has partial double-bond character, making it planar and rigid - a constraint that shapes all higher-order protein structure (Chapter 2).

Nucleic Acids: Building Blocks

DNA and RNA are polymers of nucleotides, each composed of a nitrogenous base (purine or pyrimidine), a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose or ribose), and one or more phosphate groups. Chapter 3 covers their higher-order structure in detail.

Stabilizing Interactions

The three-dimensional structures of proteins and nucleic acids are held together not by covalent bonds alone, but by a combination of weaker, non-covalent forces acting collectively:

The Four Stabilizing Interactions
  • Van der Waals forces - weak, short-range attractions between all atoms in close proximity.
  • Electrostatic (ionic) interactions - attraction between oppositely charged groups (e.g., salt bridges between Asp/Glu and Lys/Arg side chains).
  • Hydrogen bonding - directional interactions between a donor (N–H, O–H) and an acceptor (O, N), central to secondary structure and base pairing.
  • Hydrophobic interactions - the burial of nonpolar groups away from water, the dominant driving force in protein folding and membrane formation.

Vitamins

Vitamins are organic micronutrients the body cannot synthesize in sufficient quantity. Water-soluble vitamins (B-complex, C) largely function as coenzyme precursors (Chapter 4), while fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are involved in vision, calcium regulation, antioxidant defense, and blood clotting respectively.
