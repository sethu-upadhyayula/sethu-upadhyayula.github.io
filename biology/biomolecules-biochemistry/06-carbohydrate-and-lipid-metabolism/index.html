6. Carbohydrate and Lipid Metabolism

This chapter follows glucose from initial breakdown through complete oxidation, along with the pathways that build glucose back up and that store and mobilize the body's two major fuel reserves: glycogen and fat.

Glycolysis

Definition - Glycolysis

A ten-step cytosolic pathway converting one glucose (6 carbons) into two pyruvate (3 carbons each), with net production of 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation) and 2 NADH. The pathway has an energy-investment phase (phosphorylation of glucose and fructose-6-phosphate, costing 2 ATP) followed by an energy-payoff phase.

Key regulatory steps occur at phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1), the pathway's committed and rate-limiting step, allosterically inhibited by ATP and citrate and activated by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate.

The Citric Acid (TCA) Cycle

Pyruvate is oxidatively decarboxylated to acetyl-CoA (by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex), which enters the TCA cycle in the mitochondrial matrix. Each turn of the cycle fully oxidizes one acetyl group to two CO₂, generating 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 GTP/ATP directly, with the bulk of the pathway's energy captured later during oxidative phosphorylation (Chapter 5).

Remark - Amphibolic Role

The TCA cycle is amphibolic - besides oxidizing fuel, its intermediates (e.g., oxaloacetate, α-ketoglutarate) are drawn off for amino acid and nucleotide biosynthesis (Chapter 7), which is why anaplerotic reactions (like pyruvate carboxylase replenishing oxaloacetate) are needed to keep the cycle running.

Pentose Phosphate Pathway

An alternative fate for glucose-6-phosphate, the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) runs in two phases: an oxidative phase generating NADPH (for reductive biosynthesis and antioxidant defense) and ribulose-5-phosphate, and a non-oxidative phase interconverting sugars to produce ribose-5-phosphate (for nucleotide synthesis) or feed back into glycolysis.

Gluconeogenesis

Gluconeogenesis synthesizes glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (lactate, glycerol, glucogenic amino acids), largely reversing glycolysis but bypassing its three irreversible steps with distinct enzymes (pyruvate carboxylase and PEP carboxykinase, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, glucose-6-phosphatase) to avoid a futile cycle.

Glycogen Metabolism

Glycogenesis (synthesis, via glycogen synthase) and glycogenolysis (breakdown, via glycogen phosphorylase) are reciprocally regulated by hormones - glucagon and epinephrine promote breakdown via cAMP-dependent phosphorylation cascades, while insulin promotes synthesis.

Fatty Acid Metabolism

β-Oxidation and Fatty Acid Synthesis

β-oxidation breaks fatty acids down two carbons at a time as acetyl-CoA, in the mitochondrial matrix, generating NADH and FADH₂ for the electron transport chain. Fatty acid synthesis runs in the cytosol, building palmitate two carbons at a time from malonyl-CoA, using NADPH (largely supplied by the PPP) as the reducing agent - the two pathways are chemically similar but use different cofactors, enzymes, and cellular compartments, allowing independent regulation.
