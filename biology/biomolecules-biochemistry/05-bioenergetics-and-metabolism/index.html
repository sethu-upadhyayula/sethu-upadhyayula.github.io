5. Bioenergetics and Metabolism

Every metabolic pathway ultimately runs on the thermodynamics of energy release and capture. This chapter introduces the free-energy framework for coupled reactions, the central role of ATP, and the electron transport chain that generates most of a cell's usable energy.

Free Energy and Coupled Reactions

Definition - Gibbs Free Energy Change

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$ A reaction proceeds spontaneously when $\Delta G < 0$ (exergonic). Many biologically necessary reactions are endergonic ($\Delta G > 0$) on their own, and are driven forward by coupling to a highly exergonic reaction - most often ATP hydrolysis.

ATP as the Energy Currency

ATP hydrolysis to ADP and inorganic phosphate releases a large negative free-energy change, due to electrostatic repulsion relief between adjacent phosphates and resonance stabilization of the products. Cells use this energy for mechanical work (muscle contraction, molecular motors), active transport across membranes, and biosynthesis.

Remark - Group Transfer Potential

ATP occupies an intermediate position on the scale of phosphoryl-group transfer potential - it can accept phosphate from higher-energy compounds (like phosphoenolpyruvate, in glycolysis) and donate it to lower-energy acceptors, making it a universal energy-transfer intermediary rather than a long-term energy store.

Electron Transport Chain and Oxidative Phosphorylation

The electron transport chain (ETC) is a series of membrane-embedded protein complexes (Complexes I–IV) in the inner mitochondrial membrane that pass electrons from NADH and FADH₂ down a redox gradient to molecular oxygen, the final electron acceptor.

The Chemiosmotic Theory

As electrons flow through Complexes I, III, and IV, protons are pumped from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space, creating an electrochemical gradient (the proton-motive force). ATP synthase uses the energy released as protons flow back down this gradient to catalyze ATP synthesis from ADP and Pi - a mechanism proposed by Peter Mitchell and confirmed by Boyer's rotary-catalysis model of ATP synthase.

Example - Uncouplers

Chemical uncouplers (e.g., 2,4-dinitrophenol) dissipate the proton gradient without passing through ATP synthase, allowing electron transport to continue while ATP synthesis stops - the energy is released instead as heat, the physiological basis of thermogenesis by brown adipose tissue (via the natural uncoupling protein, thermogenin/UCP1).

Ligand Binding and Bioenergetic Context

The same free-energy framework describes non-covalent ligand binding (e.g., oxygen to hemoglobin, Chapter 2) and membrane potential, where the electrochemical gradient across a membrane - combining a chemical concentration gradient and an electrical potential difference - stores energy that cells exploit for transport and, in excitable cells, for generating action potentials.
