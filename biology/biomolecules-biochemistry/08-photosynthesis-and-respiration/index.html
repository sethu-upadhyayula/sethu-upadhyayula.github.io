8. Photosynthesis and Respiration

Photosynthesis captures light energy and fixes atmospheric CO₂ into organic carbon, while respiration releases that stored energy again - the two processes are mirror images that, together, drive the flow of both carbon and energy through most ecosystems.

Light Reactions

In the chloroplast thylakoid membrane, light-harvesting complexes (arrays of chlorophyll and accessory pigments) funnel absorbed light energy to reaction centers in Photosystem II and Photosystem I.

Definition - The Z-Scheme

Electrons excited in PSII are passed through an electron transport chain (plastoquinone, cytochrome b6f, plastocyanin) to PSI, where they are re-excited by a second photon and ultimately reduce NADP⁺ to NADPH. Water is split at PSII to replace the electrons lost, releasing O₂ as a byproduct. Electron flow through cytochrome b6f pumps protons into the thylakoid lumen, and the resulting gradient drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase - the same chemiosmotic principle as mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (Chapter 5).

Remark - Photoprotection

Under excess light, plants dissipate surplus absorbed energy as heat via the xanthophyll cycle and non-photochemical quenching, preventing damage from reactive oxygen species that would otherwise form when light-harvesting complexes are over-excited.

The Calvin Cycle

ATP and NADPH generated by the light reactions power the Calvin cycle in the stroma, which fixes CO₂ into organic carbon in three phases: carboxylation (CO₂ + ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate, catalyzed by RuBisCO), reduction (using ATP and NADPH to form glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate), and regeneration of ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate to continue the cycle.

C3, C4, and CAM Pathways

Three Carbon-Fixation Strategies
  • C3 plants fix CO₂ directly via RuBisCO in the Calvin cycle - the ancestral and most common strategy, but RuBisCO's competing oxygenase activity leads to wasteful photorespiration, especially in hot, dry conditions.
  • C4 plants (e.g., maize, sugarcane) first fix CO₂ into a four-carbon acid in mesophyll cells, then shuttle it to bundle-sheath cells where it is released at high local concentration for RuBisCO - spatially separating initial fixation from the Calvin cycle to suppress photorespiration.
  • CAM plants (e.g., cacti, pineapple) separate the same two steps temporally instead of spatially: CO₂ is fixed at night when stomata are open (minimizing water loss) and released for the Calvin cycle during the day when stomata close.

Photorespiration

When RuBisCO acts on O₂ instead of CO₂, it produces a toxic two-carbon byproduct that must be recycled through a costly pathway spanning the chloroplast, peroxisome, and mitochondrion, consuming energy and releasing CO₂ without producing ATP - a direct metabolic cost of C3 photosynthesis's imperfect enzyme.

Respiration and Photorespiration's Mitochondrial Counterpart

Plant mitochondrial respiration follows the same core glycolysis/TCA cycle/electron-transport logic described in Chapters 5 and 6, but plants additionally possess an alternate oxidase pathway that bypasses part of the standard electron transport chain, releasing energy as heat rather than capturing it as ATP - used, for instance, to generate the heat that volatilizes floral scent compounds in some flowering plants.
