7. Nitrogen Metabolism

Unlike carbon, most organisms cannot use atmospheric nitrogen directly. This chapter covers how nitrogen enters the biosphere, how it is incorporated into amino acids, and how amino acids and nucleotides are subsequently synthesized and broken down.

Nitrogen Fixation

Definition - Biological Nitrogen Fixation

The reduction of atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃), catalyzed by the enzyme nitrogenase found only in certain prokaryotes - free-living bacteria (e.g., Azotobacter) and symbiotic bacteria (Rhizobium, in root nodules of legumes). The reaction is highly energy-intensive, consuming at least 16 ATP per N₂ fixed, and nitrogenase is irreversibly inactivated by oxygen, requiring specialized protective mechanisms (e.g., leghemoglobin in root nodules).

Ammonium Assimilation

Once fixed, ammonium is incorporated into organic molecules primarily via glutamine synthetase (forming glutamine from glutamate and NH₄⁺) and glutamate synthase (GOGAT), which together funnel nitrogen into glutamate - the central amino-group donor for the rest of amino acid metabolism.

Amino Acid Biosynthesis and Catabolism

Definition - Transamination

The transfer of an amino group from an amino acid to an α-keto acid, catalyzed by aminotransferases using pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) as a cofactor - the principal route by which amino groups are both distributed among amino acids and collected for excretion.

Excess amino groups, collected largely as glutamate, are converted to ammonia via oxidative deamination (glutamate dehydrogenase) and then detoxified. In humans and other ureotelic organisms, this ammonia is converted to urea via the urea cycle, a pathway spanning the mitochondrion and cytosol that safely disposes of nitrogen as a neutral, highly soluble excretion product.

Remark - Essential versus Nonessential Amino Acids

Humans can synthesize 11 of the 20 standard amino acids from central metabolic intermediates; the remaining 9 "essential" amino acids must come from the diet, since the biosynthetic pathways for their carbon skeletons were lost over the course of animal evolution.

Nucleotide Metabolism

Purine (adenine, guanine) and pyrimidine (cytosine, uracil, thymine) nucleotides are synthesized by two distinct classes of pathways:

De Novo versus Salvage Pathways
  • De novo synthesis builds purine and pyrimidine rings from simple precursors - amino acids (glycine, aspartate, glutamine), CO₂, and ribose-5-phosphate (supplied by the pentose phosphate pathway) - and is energetically expensive.
  • Salvage pathways recycle free bases and nucleosides released by nucleic acid turnover, at a fraction of the energetic cost, and are the dominant source of nucleotides in most tissues.

Purine catabolism in humans terminates in uric acid; excessive accumulation causes gout. Pyrimidine catabolism, by contrast, yields highly soluble products (β-alanine, β-aminoisobutyrate) that are readily excreted or further metabolized.
