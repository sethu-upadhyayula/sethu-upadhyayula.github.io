3. Nucleic Acid Structure

DNA and RNA store and transmit genetic information, but their function depends on structural features well beyond the sequence of bases - the helical conformation, base-pairing geometry, and higher-order folding of RNA all matter for how these molecules are read, copied, and regulated.

DNA Conformations: A, B, and Z Forms

Double-stranded DNA can adopt several distinct helical geometries depending on sequence, hydration, and ionic conditions.

The Three Major Forms
  • B-DNA - the standard right-handed double helix under physiological conditions, with ~10.5 base pairs per turn; the form Watson and Crick originally described.
  • A-DNA - a right-handed but more compact, wider helix favoured under dehydrating conditions; also the typical conformation of RNA-DNA hybrids and double-stranded RNA.
  • Z-DNA - a left-handed, zigzag helix that can form at alternating purine-pyrimidine sequences (e.g., GC repeats), possibly playing a role in transcriptional regulation.

RNA Conformation

Unlike DNA, RNA is typically single-stranded, which allows it to fold back on itself into complex secondary and tertiary structures - hairpins, stem-loops, bulges, and pseudoknots - stabilized by Watson-Crick and non-canonical base pairing.

Example - Functional RNA Folding

Transfer RNA folds into a characteristic cloverleaf secondary structure that further folds into an L-shaped tertiary structure. Ribozymes (catalytic RNAs) and riboswitches depend on precise tertiary folds to perform chemistry or sense metabolite concentrations directly, without protein involvement.

Stability of Nucleic Acids and Proteins

The stability of a folded nucleic acid or protein reflects the balance between the favourable interactions holding it together (Chapter 1) and the entropic cost of restricting a flexible chain to one conformation.

Definition - Melting Temperature ($T_m$)

The temperature at which half of a double-stranded nucleic acid population has denatured (separated) into single strands. $T_m$ increases with GC content, since G–C base pairs form three hydrogen bonds versus two for A–T, and with ionic strength, which screens backbone phosphate repulsion.

Remark - Protein Denaturation

Proteins similarly denature under heat, extremes of pH, or chaotropic agents (urea, guanidinium), losing secondary and tertiary structure while the primary sequence remains intact - the basis of Anfinsen's refolding experiments discussed in Chapter 2.
