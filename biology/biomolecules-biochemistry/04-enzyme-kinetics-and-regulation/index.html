4. Enzyme Kinetics and Regulation

Enzymes are biological catalysts that accelerate reactions by many orders of magnitude without being consumed. This chapter develops the quantitative framework for describing enzyme catalysis, the major ways enzymes are regulated and inhibited, and the coenzymes many of them depend on.

Principles of Catalysis

Enzymes work by stabilizing the transition state of a reaction, lowering the activation energy ($E_a$) required, and thereby increasing the reaction rate without altering the reaction's equilibrium or overall free-energy change.

Definition - Active Site

The active site is the region of an enzyme, typically a cleft or pocket, where substrate binds and catalysis occurs. Catalytic strategies include acid-base catalysis, covalent catalysis, metal-ion catalysis, and proximity/orientation effects.

Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

For a simple enzyme-catalyzed reaction $E + S \rightleftharpoons ES \rightarrow E + P$, the initial reaction velocity as a function of substrate concentration is described by the Michaelis-Menten equation:

The Michaelis-Menten Equation

$$v_0 = \frac{V_{max}[S]}{K_m + [S]}$$ where $V_{max}$ is the maximum velocity at saturating substrate, and $K_m$ (the Michaelis constant) is the substrate concentration at which $v_0 = V_{max}/2$. A low $K_m$ indicates high apparent affinity between enzyme and substrate.

Remark - Linear Transformations

The Lineweaver-Burk (double-reciprocal) plot, $1/v_0$ against $1/[S]$, linearizes the Michaelis-Menten equation and was historically used to extract $K_m$ and $V_{max}$ graphically, and remains useful for visually distinguishing modes of inhibition.

Enzyme Inhibition

Types of Reversible Inhibition
  • Competitive - inhibitor binds the active site, competing with substrate; increases apparent $K_m$, $V_{max}$ unchanged.
  • Non-competitive - inhibitor binds a site distinct from the active site, on either free enzyme or the ES complex; decreases $V_{max}$, $K_m$ unchanged.
  • Uncompetitive - inhibitor binds only the ES complex; decreases both $K_m$ and $V_{max}$.

Enzyme Regulation

Beyond simple inhibition, cells regulate enzyme activity through several mechanisms:

Regulatory Strategies
  • Allosteric regulation - binding of an effector at a site distinct from the active site shifts the enzyme between low- and high-activity conformations (e.g., feedback inhibition of a pathway's first committed enzyme by its end product).
  • Covalent modification - reversible phosphorylation, for instance, switches many metabolic enzymes on or off.
  • Zymogen activation - proteolytic cleavage irreversibly activates enzymes synthesized in an inactive precursor form (e.g., chymotrypsinogen, Chapter 2).
  • Isozymes - different gene products catalyzing the same reaction with distinct kinetic properties, allowing tissue-specific regulation (e.g., lactate dehydrogenase isozymes in heart versus skeletal muscle).

Vitamins and Coenzymes

Many enzymes require a non-protein cofactor to function. Coenzymes are typically derived from water-soluble vitamins:

Common Coenzymes
  • NAD⁺/NADH and FAD/FADH₂ (from niacin and riboflavin) - carry electrons in metabolic redox reactions (Chapters 5–6).
  • Coenzyme A (from pantothenic acid) - carries acyl groups, central to fatty acid and pyruvate metabolism.
  • Thiamine pyrophosphate (from thiamine/B1), pyridoxal phosphate (from B6), and biotin - each support specific classes of reactions such as decarboxylation, transamination, and carboxylation.
