2. Protein Structure and Folding

A protein's function depends almost entirely on its three-dimensional shape, which is itself dictated by its amino acid sequence. This chapter works through the hierarchy of protein structure, the physical constraints that make certain shapes favourable, and what happens when folding goes wrong - illustrated with several proteins that recur constantly in the biochemistry literature.

Levels of Protein Structure

The Four Levels
  • Primary structure - the linear sequence of amino acids, joined by peptide bonds.
  • Secondary structure - local, regular folding patterns stabilized by backbone hydrogen bonds: the α-helix and β-sheet (parallel or antiparallel).
  • Tertiary structure - the overall 3D fold of a single polypeptide, driven largely by burial of hydrophobic side chains.
  • Quaternary structure - the arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits into a functional complex (e.g., hemoglobin's four subunits).

The Ramachandran Plot

Because the peptide bond is planar, the backbone conformation at each residue is fully described by two dihedral angles: $\phi$ (phi, around the N–Cα bond) and $\psi$ (psi, around the Cα–C bond).

Definition - Ramachandran Plot

A plot of $\psi$ against $\phi$ for every residue in a protein. Steric clashes between side chains and backbone atoms forbid most $(\phi, \psi)$ combinations, leaving distinct allowed regions corresponding to α-helices, β-sheets, and left-handed helices - a standard tool for validating solved protein structures.

Domains, Motifs, and Folds

Large proteins are often organized into domains - compact, independently-folding structural units that may also correspond to distinct functions. Recurring small arrangements of secondary structure elements are called motifs (e.g., the helix-turn-helix motif in DNA-binding proteins), while a fold describes a larger, evolutionarily conserved arrangement of secondary structure shared across many unrelated proteins (e.g., the TIM barrel).

Protein Folding and Misfolding

Anfinsen's classic experiments on ribonuclease A showed that a protein's native structure is determined entirely by its amino acid sequence - folding is thermodynamically directed toward the lowest free-energy state, not dictated by an external template.

Remark - Chaperones

Inside cells, molecular chaperones (e.g., Hsp70, the GroEL/GroES chaperonin system) assist folding by preventing aggregation, rather than specifying the final structure themselves.

When folding fails, proteins can misfold and aggregate - the basis of diseases such as Alzheimer's (amyloid-β), Parkinson's (α-synuclein), and prion diseases, where a misfolded conformation can template the misfolding of correctly-folded copies.

Case Studies

Myoglobin and Hemoglobin

Myoglobin is a single-chain, oxygen-storage protein with a simple hyperbolic oxygen-binding curve. Hemoglobin, its close relative, is a tetramer (α₂β₂) whose subunits communicate - binding of oxygen at one heme increases the affinity of the others, producing a sigmoidal binding curve (cooperativity), classically explained by the concerted (MWC) and sequential (KNF) models of allostery.

Lysozyme and Ribonuclease A

Lysozyme, an antibacterial enzyme found in tears and saliva, hydrolyzes bonds in bacterial peptidoglycan. Ribonuclease A, a small, highly stable enzyme that degrades RNA, was the protein used in Anfinsen's refolding experiments and remains a standard model for studying protein stability and folding kinetics.

Chymotrypsin and Carboxypeptidase

Chymotrypsin is a serine protease that uses a catalytic triad (Ser–His–Asp) to cleave peptide bonds after bulky hydrophobic residues; it is synthesized as an inactive zymogen (chymotrypsinogen) and activated by proteolytic cleavage. Carboxypeptidase, a zinc metalloenzyme, removes amino acids one at a time from the C-terminus of a peptide chain.
