9. Biochemical Separation and Characterization Techniques

Studying a biomolecule first requires isolating it from a complex mixture, then confirming its identity and purity. This closing chapter surveys the standard separation and characterization techniques referenced throughout the rest of the subject.

Chromatography

Types of Chromatography
  • Ion exchange chromatography - separates molecules by net surface charge, using a charged stationary phase and an increasing salt gradient to elute bound molecules.
  • Size exclusion (gel filtration) chromatography - separates by size, as a porous resin allows small molecules to enter pores and take a longer path, while large molecules are excluded and elute first.
  • Affinity chromatography - exploits a specific, reversible binding interaction (e.g., antigen-antibody, His-tag–Ni²⁺, substrate analog–enzyme) for highly selective purification in a single step.
  • HPLC, GC, and FPLC - high-performance/gas/fast-protein liquid chromatography apply the same underlying separation principles (partition, ion exchange, size exclusion) under precisely controlled, automated, high-resolution conditions.

Centrifugation

Differential centrifugation separates cellular components by size and density using successive rounds of increasing centrifugal force, pelleting progressively smaller organelles at each step (nuclei first, then mitochondria, then microsomes, then ribosomes). Density gradient centrifugation (e.g., sucrose or CsCl gradients) achieves finer separation, banding particles at the point in the gradient matching their own density - the technique behind the classic Meselson-Stahl experiment confirming semiconservative DNA replication.

Electrophoresis

Definition - Electrophoretic Separation

Electrophoresis separates charged molecules migrating through a gel matrix under an applied electric field. SDS-PAGE denatures proteins and coats them uniformly with negative charge (via SDS), so migration depends on size alone. Native PAGE preserves folded structure and charge, separating by a combination of size, shape, and charge. Agarose gel electrophoresis is the standard method for separating DNA and RNA fragments by size.

Spectroscopic Methods

UV-visible spectroscopy exploits the characteristic absorbance of nucleic acids (260 nm) and aromatic amino acids in proteins (280 nm) for rapid concentration and purity estimates (e.g., the A260/A280 ratio as a nucleic-acid purity check). Fluorescence spectroscopy is far more sensitive, detecting light re-emitted by fluorophores (intrinsic, like tryptophan, or attached labels) and is widely used to study protein conformation, binding, and dynamics.

Mass Spectrometry

Mass spectrometry ionizes molecules and measures their mass-to-charge ratio, providing highly accurate molecular weight determination and, when combined with fragmentation (tandem MS/MS), sequence information for peptides and proteins - now a standard tool for large-scale proteomics identification alongside the genomic and transcriptomic methods covered elsewhere in this curriculum.
