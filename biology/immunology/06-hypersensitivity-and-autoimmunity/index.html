6. Hypersensitivity and Autoimmunity

The same immune mechanisms that protect the body can, misdirected, cause disease - either by overreacting to otherwise harmless substances, or by mistakenly targeting the body's own tissues.

Hypersensitivity Reactions

Classifying Hypersensitivity Reactions
  • Type I (immediate) - IgE-mediated, triggering rapid release of histamine and other mediators from mast cells; the basis of common allergies and, in severe cases, anaphylaxis.
  • Type II (antibody-mediated) - antibodies target antigens on the surface of the body's own cells, as in some autoimmune blood disorders.
  • Type III (immune complex-mediated) - antibody-antigen complexes deposit in tissues (e.g., blood vessels, kidneys) and trigger damaging local inflammation.
  • Type IV (delayed-type) - mediated by T cells rather than antibodies, developing over one to several days, as in the tuberculin skin test reaction and contact dermatitis.

Autoimmunity

Definition - Autoimmunity

Autoimmunity arises when the immune system fails to distinguish self from non-self and mounts an adaptive response (Chapters 3–4) against the body's own antigens. This normally requires overcoming multiple layers of self-tolerance - mechanisms, established primarily during B and T cell development, that eliminate or inactivate immune cells strongly reactive to self antigens before they can cause harm.

Immune Response During Infection

The severity and character of disease during bacterial, viral, and parasitic infection often reflects not only the pathogen's direct effects but the immune response mounted against it - an excessive or poorly targeted inflammatory response (Chapter 5) can itself cause significant tissue damage, a balance particularly relevant to the host-pathogen interactions covered in Chapter 6 of Microbiology.

Congenital and Acquired Immunodeficiencies

Failures of immune tolerance sit at one extreme of immune dysfunction; failures of immune capability - covered in more depth in Chapter 7 - sit at the other, together framing the immune system as requiring careful calibration rather than simply "more" or "less" activity to function properly.
