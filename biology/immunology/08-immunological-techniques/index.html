8. Immunological Techniques

Immunology's precise, high-affinity antibody-antigen recognition (Chapter 2) has itself become one of biology's most widely used laboratory tools, exploited across virtually every other subject in this curriculum for detecting and quantifying specific molecules.

Immunodiffusion and Immunoelectrophoresis

Immunodiffusion allows antigen and antibody to diffuse toward each other through a gel, forming a visible precipitation line where they meet at optimal proportions - a simple, classic method for detecting antigen-antibody reactions and estimating relative concentrations. Immunoelectrophoresis combines this principle with an initial electrophoretic separation step (Chapter 9 of Biochemistry), improving resolution when a sample contains multiple distinct antigens.

ELISA and RIA

Definition - ELISA

The enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) uses an antibody linked to an enzyme that produces a measurable colour change, allowing sensitive, quantitative detection of a specific antigen (or antibody) in a sample - now the standard immunoassay format across research, clinical diagnostics, and quality control. Radioimmunoassay (RIA), an older, functionally similar technique, uses a radioactively labelled tracer instead of an enzyme, largely superseded by ELISA outside of a few specialized applications due to the practical difficulties of working with radioactivity (Chapter 14 of Methods in Biology).

Western Blotting and Immunoprecipitation

Western blotting combines gel electrophoresis (Chapter 9 of Biochemistry) with antibody-based detection to identify a specific protein within a complex mixture, based on both its size and its reactivity with a chosen antibody. Immunoprecipitation instead uses an antibody to physically pull a specific protein (and anything bound to it) out of solution, a method frequently used to identify protein-protein interactions (Chapter 4 of Genomics's discussion of the interactome).

Flow Cytometry and Immunofluorescence Microscopy

Example - Flow Cytometry

Flow cytometry uses fluorescently labelled antibodies to rapidly characterize and count individual cells within a mixed population, based on which surface markers each cell displays - the standard method for enumerating specific immune cell subsets (e.g., distinguishing CD4+ from CD8+ T cells, Chapter 4) in a blood or tissue sample. Immunofluorescence microscopy applies a similar labelling principle to directly visualize the location of a specific molecule within fixed cells or tissue sections.

Monoclonal Antibodies as Research Tools

Nearly all of the techniques in this chapter depend on having a reliable, specific antibody against the molecule of interest - a need largely met today by monoclonal antibody technology (Chapter 2), which can generate a essentially unlimited, perfectly reproducible supply of antibody against almost any chosen target.
