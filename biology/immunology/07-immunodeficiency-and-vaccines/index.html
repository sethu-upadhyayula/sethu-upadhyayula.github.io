7. Immunodeficiency and Vaccines

This chapter covers two contrasting situations: what happens when the immune system cannot mount an adequate defense, and how that same system's capacity for memory can be deliberately harnessed to prevent disease before it occurs.

Congenital Immunodeficiencies

Congenital (primary) immunodeficiencies arise from inherited genetic defects (Chapter 1 of Genetics) affecting the development or function of specific immune components - for example, defects in the machinery for V(D)J recombination (Chapter 2) can prevent functional B and T cell receptors from forming at all, leaving an individual severely vulnerable to infection from birth.

Acquired Immunodeficiencies

Example - HIV and Acquired Immunodeficiency

Acquired immunodeficiencies develop after birth, whether from infection (most notably HIV, which specifically infects and depletes CD4+ helper T cells, Chapter 4, progressively crippling the coordination of both humoral and cell-mediated immunity), malnutrition, or immunosuppressive medical treatment.

Vaccines

Types of Vaccine
  • Live attenuated vaccines - a weakened form of the pathogen, capable of eliciting a strong, broad immune response similar to natural infection, but with the potential for reversion to a more virulent form.
  • Killed (inactivated) vaccines - a whole, non-replicating pathogen; safer but typically eliciting a weaker response, often requiring multiple doses.
  • Subunit vaccines - a specific, purified antigen (e.g., a single surface protein) rather than the whole pathogen, minimizing side effects at some cost to the breadth of immune response elicited.
  • Recombinant nucleic acid vaccines - deliver genetic instructions (DNA or mRNA) for the host's own cells to transiently produce a pathogen antigen, combining strong immune stimulation with the safety of not introducing any actual pathogen material.

Why Vaccines Work: Immunological Memory

All vaccine types work by deliberately inducing memory B and T cells (Chapters 3–4) against a pathogen without requiring the risk of an actual infection, so that a real subsequent exposure triggers the fast, effective secondary immune response described in Chapter 3 - the central principle connecting this chapter back to the basic mechanisms of adaptive immunity covered earlier in the subject.
