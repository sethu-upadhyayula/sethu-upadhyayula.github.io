3. B Cells and Humoral Immunity

B cells are the source of all antibodies, and this chapter covers how a B cell recognizing its specific antigen is transformed into a factory for producing that antibody at scale.

The B Cell Receptor

Each B cell displays on its surface a B cell receptor (BCR) - essentially a membrane-bound version of the antibody that cell is genetically committed to produce (Chapter 2), generated by the same V(D)J recombination process during B cell development, before any antigen exposure has occurred.

B Cell Activation and Differentiation

Definition - B Cell Activation

Antigen binding to the BCR, typically together with a second ("helper") signal from an activated T cell (Chapter 4), triggers a B cell to proliferate and differentiate into plasma cells - antibody-secreting factories - and long-lived memory B cells, which persist after the infection clears and mount a faster, stronger response upon re-exposure to the same antigen.

Humoral Immune Responses

Humoral immunity refers to this antibody-mediated arm of adaptive immunity, effective particularly against extracellular pathogens and toxins - antibodies can neutralize pathogens directly (blocking their ability to infect cells), opsonize them (coating them to enhance phagocytosis), or trigger the complement cascade (Chapter 5).

Primary and Secondary Immune Responses

Example - Primary versus Secondary Response

A primary immune response, upon first exposure to an antigen, is relatively slow to develop and modest in magnitude. A secondary response, upon re-exposure, is faster, stronger, and dominated by higher-affinity antibodies - a direct consequence of memory B cells generated during the primary response, and the biological basis for vaccination (Chapter 7).
