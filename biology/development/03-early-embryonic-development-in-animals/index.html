3. Early Embryonic Development in Animals

The earliest stages of animal development transform a single large cell - the zygote - into a multicellular, multi-layered embryo with the basic body plan already sketched out.

Cleavage

Definition - Cleavage

A rapid series of cell divisions (mitosis, Chapter 7 of Cell Biology) that partitions the zygote's cytoplasm into progressively smaller cells (blastomeres), without an accompanying increase in overall embryo size. Cleavage pattern varies with the amount and distribution of yolk in the egg, ranging from complete (holoblastic) cleavage in yolk-poor eggs to incomplete (meroblastic) cleavage in yolk-rich eggs such as those of birds.

Blastula Formation

Continued cleavage produces the blastula, typically a hollow ball of cells surrounding a fluid-filled cavity (the blastocoel). By this stage, cytoplasmic determinants inherited unevenly during cleavage (Chapter 1) have already begun to establish regional differences among the blastomeres.

Embryonic Fields

Before cells become irreversibly committed, they exist within embryonic fields - regions of the embryo within which cells can still regulate and compensate for the loss or addition of other cells, reflecting a degree of developmental flexibility that narrows as gastrulation and organogenesis proceed.

Gastrulation and the Germ Layers

Definition - Gastrulation

Gastrulation is a dramatic period of coordinated cell movement that converts the simple blastula into a multi-layered embryo, establishing the three germ layers: ectoderm (gives rise to skin and nervous system), mesoderm (muscle, skeleton, circulatory system), and endoderm (lining of the digestive and respiratory tracts) - the foundational tissue layers from which all subsequent organogenesis (Chapter 5) proceeds.
