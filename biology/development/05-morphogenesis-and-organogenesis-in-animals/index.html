5. Morphogenesis and Organogenesis in Animals

Following gastrulation (Chapter 3), the three germ layers must be sculpted and patterned into the specific organs and structures of the adult body - a process studied extensively using a handful of experimentally convenient model organisms.

Axes and Pattern Formation in Drosophila

Example - The Drosophila Body Plan

The fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster is the classic model for pattern formation: maternally deposited mRNA gradients establish the anterior-posterior axis (Chapter 1's morphogenetic gradients), activating a cascade of gap, pair-rule, and segment-polarity genes that progressively subdivide the embryo, culminating in Hox genes that assign distinct identities to each body segment - a patterning logic broadly conserved across animals, including vertebrates.

Organogenesis in Model Organisms

Classic Organogenesis Systems
  • Vulva formation in Caenorhabditis elegans - a small, fixed number of cells respond to an inductive signal from a single anchor cell, providing an unusually precise, cell-by-cell model of induction (Chapter 1).
  • Eye lens induction in vertebrates - a textbook example of sequential induction, in which the developing optic vesicle induces overlying ectoderm to form the lens, which in turn induces the cornea.
  • Limb development and regeneration - vertebrate limb outgrowth is directed by specialized signaling centers at the limb bud tip and posterior margin; some vertebrates (e.g., salamanders) retain the capacity to regenerate entire lost limbs, a capacity largely lost in mammals.

Neuronal Differentiation and Aggregative Development

Differentiation of neurons involves not just acquiring a specialized cell shape but extending axons that must navigate, often over long distances, to their correct targets, guided by attractive and repulsive cues. A conceptually distinct model of multicellular organization is provided by Dictyostelium (Chapter 4 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics), whose free-living amoebae aggregate under starvation to form a coordinated multicellular structure entirely without an embryo.

Post-Embryonic Development, Metamorphosis, and Sex Determination

Development does not end at hatching or birth - many animals undergo further larval stages and metamorphosis (e.g., the transformation of a tadpole into a frog, or a caterpillar into a butterfly), often involving extensive tissue remodelling and programmed cell death (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology) to eliminate larval structures no longer needed in the adult. Sex determination itself can be established genetically (e.g., by sex chromosomes) or, in some reptiles, environmentally (by incubation temperature) - and, alongside metamorphosis, is subject to significant environmental regulation of otherwise genetically programmed development.
