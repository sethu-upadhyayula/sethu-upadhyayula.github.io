7. Stem Cells, Model Organisms and Developmental Cell Death

This closing chapter draws together three threads that run through the whole subject: the special properties of stem cells, the small set of organisms that have taught us most of what we know about development, and the perhaps counterintuitive fact that cell death is itself an essential developmental tool.

Stem Cells Revisited

Building on the definition in Chapter 1, embryonic stem cells (derived from the early blastula, Chapter 3) are pluripotent, capable of forming any cell type in the body, while adult (tissue-specific) stem cells are typically more restricted, maintaining and repairing a specific tissue throughout life. Induced pluripotent stem cell technology (Chapter 2 of Animal & Medical Biotechnology) can reprogram adult, differentiated cells back to a pluripotent state, demonstrating that differentiation - despite appearing stable and irreversible in Chapter 1's terms - can, with the right combination of factors, be experimentally reversed.

Mutants and Transgenics in Developmental Analysis

Remark - Why Model Organisms

Much of what is known about development comes from a small number of genetically tractable model organisms - Drosophila, C. elegans, zebrafish, mouse, and Arabidopsis among them - chosen for short generation times, ease of genetic manipulation, and, in several cases, essentially transparent embryos that allow direct microscopic observation of development as it happens. Mutant analysis (disrupting a gene and observing the developmental consequence) and transgenic techniques (introducing reporter genes to visualize where and when a gene is expressed) remain the core experimental tools of the field.

Programmed Cell Death in Development

Example - Sculpting the Body Through Cell Death

Apoptosis (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology covers its molecular mechanism) is not merely a response to damage - it is a routinely scheduled, essential developmental tool. In humans, the webbing between embryonic fingers and toes is removed by apoptosis to separate individual digits; in frogs, apoptosis drives the resorption of the tadpole tail during metamorphosis (Chapter 5); and in the developing nervous system, a substantial fraction of neurons generated are eliminated by apoptosis if they fail to make appropriate synaptic connections, refining neural circuits after their initial, over-produced wiring.

Aging and Senescence in a Developmental Context

Just as cell death is developmentally programmed rather than purely accidental, aspects of aging and senescence (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology) are increasingly understood as continuous with developmental programs - the same signaling pathways that drive growth and patterning earlier in life often contribute, later in life, to the decline associated with aging, a connection of active ongoing research interest in both plants and animals.
