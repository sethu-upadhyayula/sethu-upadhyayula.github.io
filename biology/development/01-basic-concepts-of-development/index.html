1. Basic Concepts of Development

Development is the process by which a single cell becomes a complex, multicellular body with many distinct cell types, all sharing an identical genome. This chapter introduces the conceptual vocabulary used to describe how a cell progressively narrows down what it will become.

From Potency to Differentiation

A Progression of Commitment
  • Potency - the range of cell types a cell is capable of becoming; decreases progressively over development (totipotent → pluripotent → multipotent → unipotent).
  • Specification - a reversible, early bias toward a particular fate, which a cell will adopt if left undisturbed, but can still be redirected by a new environment.
  • Determination - a stable, essentially irreversible commitment to a fate, which persists even when a cell is moved to a new environment.
  • Differentiation - the actual process of acquiring the specialized structure and function of a particular cell type.

Induction and Competence

Definition - Induction

Induction is the process by which one group of cells (the inducer) signals to a neighbouring group, influencing their developmental fate - a specific application of the cell signaling mechanisms covered in Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology. A responding tissue must have competence - the ability to respond appropriately to a given inductive signal - which itself changes over developmental time.

Morphogenetic Gradients and Cell Fate

Many developmental decisions are guided by morphogen gradients - diffusible signaling molecules present at different concentrations across a tissue, such that cells interpret their local concentration to adopt position-appropriate fates, a mechanism that helps translate a one-dimensional concentration signal into precisely positioned, distinct structures.

Genomic Equivalence and Cytoplasmic Determinants

Remark - Genomic Equivalence

Classic nuclear transfer experiments established that most differentiated cells retain a complete, unaltered genome (genomic equivalence) despite their restricted fate - differentiation reflects differential gene expression (Chapter 5 of Molecular Biology), not loss of genetic material. Early asymmetries are often set up by cytoplasmic determinants - maternally deposited molecules unevenly distributed in the egg, whose inheritance by different daughter cells after cleavage biases their subsequent fate.

Stem Cells

Stem cells combine self-renewal (the capacity to divide and produce more stem cells) with the potential to differentiate into one or more specialized cell types - covered further, alongside the model organisms used to study these processes, in Chapter 7.
