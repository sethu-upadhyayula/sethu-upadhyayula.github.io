6. Morphogenesis and Organogenesis in Plants

Unlike most animals, plants continue generating new organs - leaves, branches, flowers - throughout their life, from persistent pools of stem-cell-like tissue called meristems, rather than completing their full complement of organs during embryonic development alone.

Shoot and Root Apical Meristems

Definition - Apical Meristem

The shoot apical meristem (SAM) and root apical meristem (RAM) are small populations of continuously dividing, undifferentiated cells at the tips of the shoot and root, respectively, that generate all new above- and below-ground tissue throughout the plant's life - functionally analogous to the stem cells of Chapter 7, but organized as a stable, persistent structure rather than dispersed individual cells.

Leaf Development and Phyllotaxy

Leaves are initiated as small bumps (primordia) on the flanks of the shoot apical meristem, in a strikingly regular spatial arrangement - phyllotaxy - thought to arise from a self-organizing pattern in which each new primordium is positioned to avoid the local inhibitory influence of the hormone auxin (Chapter 4 of Plant Biology) around existing primordia.

Transition to Flowering

Definition - Floral Transition

At an appropriate developmental and environmental stage - often cued by day length (photoperiodism) or a period of cold exposure (vernalization), both sensed via the photoreceptor and hormonal pathways of Chapter 4 of Cell Signaling & Chapter 4 of Plant Biology - the shoot apical meristem switches from producing vegetative organs (leaves) to producing floral organs, converting into a floral meristem.

The ABC Model of Floral Organ Identity

The ABC Model

Floral organ identity - sepal, petal, stamen, or carpel - is determined by the combinatorial expression of three classes of homeotic genes (A, B, and C) in four concentric floral whorls: A alone specifies sepals, A+B specifies petals, B+C specifies stamens, and C alone specifies carpels. This model, established through classic homeotic mutant analysis, is a plant parallel to the Hox gene patterning logic seen in Drosophila (Chapter 5), despite plants and animals having evolved multicellularity and body patterning independently (Chapter 2 of Evolution).
