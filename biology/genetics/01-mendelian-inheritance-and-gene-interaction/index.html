1. Mendelian Inheritance and Gene Interaction

Gregor Mendel's experiments with pea plants established the basic rules by which discrete hereditary factors - now called genes - are passed from parents to offspring, laying the foundation for all of modern genetics.

Mendel's Laws

The Law of Segregation and the Law of Independent Assortment

The law of segregation states that the two alleles at a gene locus separate during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only one. The law of independent assortment states that alleles of different genes assort into gametes independently of one another - though, as Chapter 2 shows, this second law holds only for genes on different chromosomes, or far apart on the same one.

Dominance Relationships

Beyond Simple Dominance
  • Codominance - both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygote (e.g., the AB blood type).
  • Incomplete dominance - the heterozygote phenotype is intermediate between the two homozygotes.
  • Penetrance - the proportion of individuals with a given genotype who actually show the expected phenotype.
  • Expressivity - the degree to which a phenotype is expressed, which can vary among individuals sharing the same genotype.

Gene Interaction and Pleiotropy

Many phenotypes depend on more than one gene. Epistasis occurs when one gene's product masks or modifies the phenotypic effect of another gene, producing phenotypic ratios in crosses that deviate from the simple Mendelian expectation. Pleiotropy describes the reverse situation - a single gene influencing multiple, seemingly unrelated phenotypic traits.

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Example - X-Linked Inheritance

Genes located on the X chromosome show characteristic inheritance patterns distinct from autosomal genes, since males (XY) have only one copy and therefore express any X-linked recessive allele they carry - the basis of conditions such as red-green color blindness and hemophilia occurring far more often in males than females.
