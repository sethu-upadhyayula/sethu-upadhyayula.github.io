4. Chromosomal Structural and Numerical Variation

Beyond point mutations (Chapter 6 of Molecular Biology), whole segments or even entire chromosomes can be gained, lost, or rearranged - changes with consequences ranging from silent to lethal, depending on which genes are affected and how.

Structural Chromosomal Alterations

Four Types of Structural Rearrangement
  • Deletion - loss of a chromosome segment; often unmasks recessive alleles on the remaining homolog.
  • Duplication - an extra copy of a segment; a major source of new genetic material for evolution (via subsequent divergence of the duplicate).
  • Inversion - a segment is reversed in orientation; can suppress recombination across the inverted region in heterozygotes.
  • Translocation - a segment moves to a different, non-homologous chromosome; can be reciprocal (segments exchanged between two chromosomes) or non-reciprocal.

Numerical Alterations: Ploidy

Definition - Euploidy and Aneuploidy

Euploidy refers to a change in the complete chromosome set (e.g., triploidy, tetraploidy) - common and often tolerated in plants, but generally lethal in animals. Aneuploidy refers to a change in the number of one specific chromosome (e.g., trisomy, monosomy), usually arising from nondisjunction during meiosis (Chapter 7 of Cell Biology), and is a major cause of human developmental disorders and pregnancy loss.

Example - Trisomy 21

Down syndrome results from trisomy of chromosome 21 - three copies instead of the usual two - illustrating how a single extra small chromosome can nonetheless produce substantial developmental effects through the resulting imbalance in gene dosage.

Genetic Implications

All of these rearrangements can disrupt genes at their breakpoints, alter gene dosage, or bring genes under the control of new regulatory elements (an important mechanism in cancer, Chapter 5 of Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology) - and, when they occur in the germline, become heritable structural variants subject to the same segregation and linkage principles covered in Chapters 1 and 2.
