6. Microbial Genetics

Bacteria reproduce asexually, but they are far from genetically static - several distinct mechanisms allow genetic material to move between bacterial cells, driving genetic diversity and, notably, the spread of traits such as antibiotic resistance.

Transformation

Definition - Transformation

The uptake of free DNA from the environment by a bacterial cell, which can then recombine into the recipient's chromosome (if homologous) or persist as a separate replicating plasmid. Naturally transformable species take up DNA released by lysed cells; the phenomenon is also exploited in the laboratory to introduce recombinant plasmids into bacteria (Chapter 1 of Recombinant DNA).

Conjugation

Conjugation transfers DNA directly between two bacterial cells in physical contact, typically via a sex pilus encoded by a conjugative plasmid (e.g., the F plasmid). A donor cell carrying the plasmid can transfer a copy of it - and, in the case of Hfr ("high frequency of recombination") strains where the plasmid has integrated into the chromosome, a copy of chromosomal DNA as well - to a recipient cell.

Remark - Sex-Duction

When an integrated F plasmid excises imprecisely, it can carry adjacent chromosomal genes with it, producing an F′ plasmid that transfers those genes to a new recipient by conjugation - a process called sex-duction, historically useful for mapping and manipulating specific chromosomal regions.

Transduction

Definition - Transduction

Bacteriophage-mediated transfer of bacterial DNA between cells. In generalized transduction, a phage accidentally packages random host DNA instead of its own genome during assembly, and delivers it into a new host cell upon infection. In specialized transduction, imprecise excision of an integrated (lysogenic) phage genome carries adjacent host genes along with it, analogous to sex-duction.

Fine-Structure Genetic Analysis

These gene-transfer mechanisms historically enabled fine-structure analysis of individual bacterial and phage genes - mapping mutations to positions within a single gene at a resolution far finer than classical eukaryotic recombination mapping (Chapter 2) could achieve, and contributing directly to the establishment of the gene as a linear, divisible structure rather than an indivisible unit.
