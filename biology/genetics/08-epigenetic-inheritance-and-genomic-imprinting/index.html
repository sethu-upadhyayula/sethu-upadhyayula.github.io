8. Epigenetic Inheritance and Genomic Imprinting

Not all heritable differences in gene expression are due to differences in DNA sequence. This closing chapter covers epigenetic mechanisms - stable, sometimes heritable, changes in gene activity that leave the underlying DNA sequence untouched.

Chromatin-Based Epigenetic Marks

As introduced in Chapter 6 of Cell Biology and Chapter 5 of Molecular Biology, histone modifications and DNA methylation can stably mark a gene as active or silent. Critically, these marks can sometimes be copied along with the DNA at replication and passed to daughter cells - allowing a pattern of gene expression established once (for instance, during development) to be maintained through many subsequent cell divisions without further instruction.

Genomic Imprinting

Definition - Genomic Imprinting

A parent-of-origin effect in which a gene is expressed only from the copy inherited from one specific parent - the copy from the other parent is epigenetically silenced, typically via DNA methylation established during gamete formation. Unlike ordinary Mendelian genes, an imprinted gene's phenotypic effect depends on which parent transmitted the mutant allele, not simply on genotype alone.

Example - Imprinting Disorders

Prader-Willi and Angelman syndromes both result from loss of the same small region of human chromosome 15, but produce entirely different phenotypes depending on whether the deletion occurs on the paternally or maternally inherited chromosome - a direct clinical illustration of genomic imprinting.

X-Inactivation

In female mammals, one of the two X chromosomes in each cell is largely and stably silenced early in development - X-inactivation - to equalize X-linked gene dosage with males. The choice of which X is inactivated is random from cell to cell but then clonally maintained, making female mammals natural mosaics for X-linked gene expression.

Relationship to RNA-Based Silencing

Epigenetic silencing frequently works together with the small-RNA-guided mechanisms covered in Chapter 7 of Molecular Biology - for example, a long non-coding RNA is itself responsible for recruiting the silencing machinery across the inactive X chromosome - illustrating that chromatin-based and RNA-based gene regulation are complementary layers of the same broader regulatory system, rather than independent mechanisms.
