7. Alleles, Complementation and Genetic Analysis

Before a mutation's molecular basis can be studied directly, classical genetics developed purely phenotypic tests to answer a deceptively simple question: are two mutations affecting the same gene, or different genes?

Alleles, Multiple Alleles, and Pseudoalleles

Definition - Allele and Multiple Alleles

An allele is one of the alternative sequence variants at a given gene locus. While a single individual carries at most two alleles of an autosomal gene, a population may harbor many - multiple alleles - as in the three-allele ABO blood group system. A pseudoallele describes two mutations that behave as alleles of the same gene in classical crosses but are later shown to lie in distinct, closely linked genes - a discrepancy historically important in refining the definition of the gene itself.

The Complementation Test

Definition - Complementation Test

Two recessive mutations with the same phenotype are combined in a heterozygote (or, in bacteria/phage, a merodiploid). If the phenotype is restored to normal ("complementation"), the mutations most likely lie in different genes, each provided in a functional copy by the other chromosome. If the mutant phenotype persists ("failure to complement"), the mutations most likely lie in the same gene.

Remark - Limits of the Complementation Test

Complementation testing can be misleading for genes encoding multi-subunit proteins (intragenic complementation) or genes with dominant-negative alleles (Chapter 6 of Molecular Biology), so results are typically interpreted alongside other genetic and molecular evidence.

Classifying Mutant Alleles

Alleles identified through genetic screens are further classified by their behaviour - lethal alleles that prevent survival, conditional alleles visible only under specific conditions, and biochemical (auxotrophic) mutants that fail to synthesize a required nutrient, the class exploited directly in bacterial genetic mapping (Chapter 6) and in classic mutagenesis screens such as the Ames test (Chapter 6 of Molecular Biology).
