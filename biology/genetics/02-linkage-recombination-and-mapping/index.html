2. Linkage, Recombination and Genetic Mapping

Genes located near each other on the same chromosome do not assort independently - they tend to be inherited together, a phenomenon called linkage, which can itself be exploited to map the relative positions of genes along a chromosome.

Linkage and Crossing-Over

Definition - Linkage and Recombination Frequency

Genes on the same chromosome are linked and tend to be inherited together, except when separated by a crossover during meiosis I (Chapter 7 of Cell Biology). The recombination frequency between two loci - the proportion of recombinant offspring in a cross - increases with the physical distance between them, providing the basis for genetic mapping.

Genetic Maps

A genetic (linkage) map orders genes along a chromosome and estimates the distances between them in map units (centimorgans), where one map unit corresponds to a 1% recombination frequency. Modern mapping increasingly uses molecular markers - such as short tandem repeats or single-nucleotide polymorphisms - rather than visible phenotypic traits, in plants, animals, and bacteria alike.

Tetrad Analysis

Example - Tetrad Analysis in Fungi

In fungi such as Neurospora, all four products of a single meiosis are retained together in an ascus. Analyzing the arrangement of alleles within such a tetrad allows recombination frequency, and even the point in meiosis at which crossing-over occurred, to be determined directly from a single meiotic event - a level of resolution not available in organisms whose gametes disperse independently.

Developing Mapping Populations in Plants

Plant genetics makes extensive use of purpose-built mapping populations - such as F2 populations, recombinant inbred lines, and doubled haploids (Chapter 6 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding) - crossed and genotyped specifically to construct high-resolution genetic maps for traits of agricultural interest.
