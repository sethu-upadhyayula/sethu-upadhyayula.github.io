2. Genetic Transformation of Plants

Introducing foreign DNA into a plant genome requires overcoming the plant cell wall (Chapter 1 of Plant Biology), a barrier animal and bacterial transformation methods do not face - this chapter covers the two broad strategies plant biotechnology uses to do so.

Vector-Mediated Transformation: Agrobacterium

Agrobacterium-Mediated Transformation

The soil bacterium Agrobacterium tumefaciens naturally transfers a defined segment of its Ti plasmid (T-DNA, Chapter 1 of Recombinant DNA Technology) into a plant cell's genome as part of its own infection strategy, normally causing crown gall disease. Biotechnologists exploit this natural gene-transfer mechanism by replacing the plasmid's disease-causing genes with a gene of interest, turning a plant pathogen's own infection machinery into the most widely used plant transformation method.

Direct Gene Transfer Methods

Direct (Non-Vector) Transformation Methods
  • Particle bombardment (biolistics) - DNA-coated microscopic metal particles are physically shot into plant cells at high velocity, bypassing the need for any biological vector entirely.
  • Protoplast-based transformation - DNA is introduced into wall-free protoplasts (Chapter 1) via chemical treatment or electroporation, before cell wall regeneration and plant regeneration.

Direct methods are particularly valuable for species or tissue types poorly susceptible to Agrobacterium infection, including many cereal crops that were historically difficult to transform by the Agrobacterium route alone.

Generating and Confirming Transgenic Plants

Following DNA delivery by either route, transformed cells are typically selected using a co-introduced selectable marker gene, then regenerated into whole plants via the tissue culture methods of Chapter 1 - with transgene integration and expression subsequently confirmed by molecular methods (PCR, Chapter 4 of Recombinant DNA Technology, and sequencing, Chapter 5).

Plastid Transformation

Remark - Chloroplast Transformation

DNA can also be introduced directly into the chloroplast genome (Chapter 3 of Genetics's discussion of cytoplasmic inheritance) rather than the nuclear genome - since chloroplasts are typically maternally inherited and not present in pollen, plastid transformation offers a natural strategy for limiting transgene spread via pollen, a biosafety consideration relevant to genetically modified crop regulation (Chapter 5).
