8. Plant-Microbe Interactions

Plants exist in constant, close association with microorganisms - some beneficial, some harmful, and some occupying a more ambiguous middle ground - this closing chapter surveys that full spectrum of relationships.

Symbiotic Mutualisms

Definition - Mycorrhizae and Rhizobial Symbiosis

Mycorrhizal fungi (Chapter 5 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics) form a widespread mutualistic association with plant roots, extending the effective root system for water and mineral (particularly phosphorus) uptake in exchange for photosynthetically fixed carbon. The legume-Rhizobium symbiosis (Chapter 4 of Plant Biology and Chapter 10 of Microbiology) provides a comparable service for nitrogen, together representing two of the most agriculturally and ecologically important plant-microbe mutualisms.

Endophytes

Endophytes - fungi or bacteria living within plant tissue without causing apparent disease symptoms - occupy a spectrum between clear mutualism and latent pathogenicity, in some cases providing the host plant with enhanced stress tolerance or pathogen resistance, and in others becoming pathogenic only under specific triggering conditions.

Pathogenic Interactions

Pathogenic plant-microbe interactions were covered in detail in Chapter 7 - this chapter's broader framing emphasizes that pathogenicity is only one point along a continuum of possible plant-microbe relationships, not a categorically distinct type of interaction from the mutualisms above.

Host-Parasitic Plant Interactions

Example - Parasitic Plants

Some plants (such as Cuscuta, dodder) are themselves parasitic on other plants, forming specialized haustorial connections that tap directly into a host plant's vascular tissue (Chapter 2 of Plant Biology) to withdraw water, nutrients, and photoassimilates - an unusual case in which the "pathogen" is itself a fellow plant rather than a microbe, sensed and, in some cases at least partially resisted through defense signaling pathways related to those used against microbial pathogens (Chapter 7).

Epiphytes

Epiphytes grow non-parasitically on the surface of another plant, using it purely for physical support (typically to gain better access to light) rather than drawing any resources directly from the host - a further point along the spectrum of plant-plant and plant-microbe association covered in this closing chapter, illustrating that physical association between organisms does not, on its own, imply either mutualism or exploitation.
