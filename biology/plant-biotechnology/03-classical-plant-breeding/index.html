3. Classical Plant Breeding

Long before molecular tools became available, plant breeders improved crops using purely phenotypic selection and controlled crosses - methods that remain foundational, and are now typically used alongside, rather than replaced by, the molecular techniques of Chapter 4.

Selection

Selection - choosing which individuals reproduce based on their phenotype - is the most fundamental breeding tool, directly exploiting the heritability principles covered in Chapter 5 of Population & Quantitative Genetics: the more heritable a trait, the more effectively selection improves it across successive generations.

Hybridization

Definition - Hybridization in Plant Breeding

Hybridization deliberately crosses two genetically distinct parent lines to combine desirable traits from each, or to generate novel genetic combinations for subsequent selection - requiring careful control of pollination (often via emasculation or male sterility, below) to ensure the cross is made as intended rather than through uncontrolled self- or open-pollination.

Heterosis (Hybrid Vigour)

Example - Heterosis in Hybrid Crops

Heterosis, or hybrid vigour, describes the frequently observed phenomenon that a first-generation (F1) hybrid between two distinct, often inbred parent lines outperforms both parents for traits such as yield - a major driver of commercial hybrid seed production in crops such as maize, though the specific genetic mechanisms underlying heterosis remain an area of ongoing research.

Male Sterility

Male sterility - a plant's inability to produce viable pollen, sometimes controlled by cytoplasmic (mitochondrial, Chapter 3 of Genetics) genes - is exploited in hybrid seed production to prevent unwanted self-pollination of the female parent line, ensuring that all seed collected from that line results from the intended cross with the chosen male parent.

Breeding Population Development

Classical breeding programs generate structured breeding populations - segregating F2 populations, backcross populations, and others - providing the genetic variation from which desirable recombinant genotypes are subsequently selected, populations that are also the substrate for the genetic mapping approaches covered in Chapter 2 of Genetics and molecular marker development covered in Chapter 4.
