7. Plant Pathology

Plant diseases threaten crop yield and food security worldwide - this chapter covers how such diseases are classified, the molecular basis of plant resistance to them, and the strategies used to control them.

Classification of Plant Diseases

Plant diseases are caused by fungi (Chapter 5 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics), bacteria (Chapter 2), nematodes, and viruses (Chapter 3), each requiring somewhat different diagnostic and management approaches, and are classified by the pathogen type, the affected plant part, and the specific symptoms produced (e.g., blights, rusts, smuts, wilts).

Mechanisms of Pathogenesis

Plant pathogens cause disease through mechanisms including direct tissue destruction by pathogen-secreted enzymes, toxin production, and vascular blockage disrupting water and nutrient transport (Chapter 3 of Plant Biology) - the specific combination of mechanisms determining a disease's characteristic symptoms and severity.

Plant Resistance: The Gene-for-Gene Concept

The Gene-for-Gene Concept

Plant disease resistance is often governed by a gene-for-gene relationship - a plant resistance gene product specifically recognizes a corresponding pathogen effector (avirulence) protein, triggering a strong, localized defense response, most dramatically the hypersensitive response, in which cells around the infection site rapidly undergo programmed cell death (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology) to contain the pathogen before it can spread further.

Systemic and Induced Resistance

Beyond this localized response, plants mount systemic acquired resistance - a heightened, broad-spectrum defensive state that spreads beyond the initial infection site to the whole plant, primed by salicylic acid signaling (Chapter 5 of Plant Biology) - and induced systemic resistance, a related but jasmonic-acid-mediated response often triggered by beneficial root-associated microbes rather than pathogens directly.

Molecular Detection of Plant Pathogens

Modern plant disease diagnosis increasingly uses molecular methods (PCR, Chapter 4 of Recombinant DNA Technology, and sequencing-based detection) alongside traditional symptom-based diagnosis, allowing faster, more sensitive, and more specific pathogen identification, particularly valuable for detecting infection before visible symptoms appear.
