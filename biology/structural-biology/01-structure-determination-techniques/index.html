1. Structure Determination Techniques

Everything covered in this subject - modelling, prediction, drug design - ultimately depends on structures determined experimentally. Three techniques dominate structural biology, each with distinct strengths and limitations.

X-Ray Crystallography

Definition - X-Ray Crystallography

A purified molecule is first coaxed into forming an ordered crystal, then exposed to an X-ray beam. The resulting diffraction pattern, once its "phase problem" is solved (traditionally via heavy-atom derivatives, more often today via molecular replacement using a known related structure), is used to compute an electron density map, into which an atomic model is built. It remains the source of the majority of solved protein structures, though it requires the protein to crystallize - not always possible, especially for membrane proteins.

NMR Spectroscopy

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy determines structure in solution, avoiding the need for crystallization, by measuring interactions between magnetically active nuclei (commonly ¹H, ¹³C, ¹⁵N) that depend on their distance and environment. NMR is largely limited to smaller proteins, but has the unique advantage of capturing molecular dynamics and flexibility directly, rather than a single static structure.

Cryo-Electron Microscopy

Definition - Cryo-EM

Purified molecules are rapidly frozen in a thin layer of vitreous (non-crystalline) ice and imaged directly by an electron microscope. Thousands of individual particle images, each a noisy 2D projection, are computationally aligned and averaged to reconstruct a 3D density map. Advances in detector technology and image-processing algorithms have made cryo-EM the method of choice for large complexes and membrane proteins that resist crystallization.

Complementary Biophysical Methods

Additional techniques provide lower-resolution but still valuable structural information: light scattering reports on a molecule's overall size and oligomeric state, circular dichroism spectroscopy estimates secondary structure content, and surface plasmon resonance measures binding kinetics and affinity between interacting molecules in real time.
