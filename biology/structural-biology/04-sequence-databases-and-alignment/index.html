4. Sequence Databases and Alignment

Comparing sequences - to find related genes, infer function, or reconstruct evolutionary history - is one of the most fundamental tasks in computational biology, and depends on large public databases and well-established alignment algorithms.

Major Bioinformatic Resources

Public repositories organize biological data for reuse: sequence databases (e.g., GenBank) store nucleotide and protein sequences; 3D structure databases (e.g., the Protein Data Bank) store experimentally solved coordinates; gene expression databases store transcriptomic datasets; and specialized pattern/motif databases catalog conserved sequence signatures associated with particular functions.

Homology Terminology

Orthologues, Paralogues, and Homologues

Homologues are sequences descended from a common ancestor. Orthologues arise by speciation (the same gene in different species, usually retaining the same function), while paralogues arise by gene duplication within a single lineage (often diverging in function over time).

Pairwise Sequence Alignment

Aligning two sequences requires balancing matches, mismatches, and gaps, guided by a scoring matrix (e.g., PAM or BLOSUM matrices for proteins, reflecting how often one amino acid is substituted for another over evolutionary time) and a gap penalty. Global alignment (Needleman-Wunsch) aligns sequences end to end, while local alignment (Smith-Waterman) finds the best-matching subregion - more appropriate when comparing sequences that share only a domain.

Database Searching: BLAST and FASTA

Definition - BLAST

Exhaustively aligning a query sequence against every sequence in a large database is too slow for routine use. BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) and FASTA instead use fast heuristics - first finding short exact-match "seeds" between query and database sequences, then extending only the most promising seeds into full local alignments - trading a small amount of sensitivity for enormous gains in speed.
