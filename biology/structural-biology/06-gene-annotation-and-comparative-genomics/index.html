6. Gene Annotation and Comparative Genomics

A newly sequenced genome is, on its own, just a long string of bases. Annotation is the process of identifying its genes and inferring what those genes do - increasingly done computationally, at genome scale.

Predicting Gene Function

Strategies for Functional Annotation
  • Homology-based annotation - transferring functional information from a well-characterized homologue found via BLAST (Chapter 4).
  • Genomic context - in bacteria, genes of related function are often physically clustered (e.g., in an operon, Chapter 5 of Molecular Biology), so a gene's neighbours can hint at its role.
  • Structural inference - a predicted structure (Chapter 3) can suggest function even when sequence similarity to characterized proteins is too weak for confident homology-based annotation.
  • Network-based inference - inferring function from a gene's position within protein-interaction or co-expression networks, on the principle that functionally related genes tend to cluster together in such networks.

Genetic Variation

Comparing individual genomes reveals polymorphisms - sequence variants present at appreciable frequency in a population - a small fraction of which are deleterious mutations affecting protein function or gene regulation. Distinguishing likely-neutral variation from functionally significant variation is a central challenge in interpreting genomic data, particularly in a clinical context.

Comparative Genomics

Remark - What Comparative Genomics Reveals

Aligning whole genomes across species highlights which regions are under strong evolutionary constraint (and therefore likely functionally important, even outside protein-coding genes) versus which have diverged freely. This approach has been central to identifying regulatory elements, tracing gene family expansions and losses, and reconstructing the gene content of ancestral genomes.
