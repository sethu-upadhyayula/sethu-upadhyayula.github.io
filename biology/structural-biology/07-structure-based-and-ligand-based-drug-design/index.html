7. Structure-Based and Ligand-Based Drug Design

Computational methods now play a central role in early drug discovery, helping to identify and refine candidate molecules well before they reach a laboratory bench.

Receptor-Ligand Interactions and Chemical Databases

Drug design begins from the same principles governing any receptor-ligand interaction (Chapter 1 of Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology) - a small molecule binding a target protein's active or allosteric site. Large chemical databases (e.g., PubChem, the NCI database) catalog millions of candidate compounds and their properties, providing the starting pool for virtual screening.

Structure-Based Drug Design

Definition - Structure-Based Drug Design

When the 3D structure of a target protein is known (Chapter 1) or can be predicted (Chapter 3), candidate molecules can be computationally docked into its binding site to estimate how well they fit and bind, and existing compounds can be modified in silico to improve fit, guiding synthesis toward the most promising candidates before any wet-lab testing.

Ligand-Based Drug Design

When a target's structure is unavailable, ligand-based approaches instead work from a set of molecules already known to be active (or inactive), inferring what chemical features are needed for activity.

QSAR and Pharmacophores

Structure-Activity Relationship (SAR) analysis and Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) models statistically relate a molecule's chemical descriptors to its measured biological activity, allowing prediction of activity for untested compounds. A pharmacophore is the minimal spatial arrangement of chemical features (e.g., a hydrogen-bond donor at one position, a hydrophobic group at another) shared by known active compounds, believed to be responsible for their activity.

Predicting Drug Behaviour: ADMET

Beyond activity against the intended target, computational methods increasingly predict a candidate's ADMET profile - Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, and Toxicity - helping to flag compounds likely to fail later in development for reasons unrelated to target binding, before significant resources are committed to them.
