3. Protein Structure Prediction

Experimental structure determination (Chapter 1) is slow and not always successful, while the number of known protein sequences vastly outpaces the number of solved structures. Structure prediction methods aim to close this gap computationally.

Homology (Comparative) Modelling

Definition - Homology Modelling

Since structure is far more conserved than sequence over evolutionary time, a protein of unknown structure can be modelled by aligning its sequence to a homologous protein of known structure (a template) and building coordinates based on that alignment. Accuracy depends heavily on the sequence identity to the template - modelling is most reliable above roughly 30% identity.

Fold Recognition and Threading

When no clearly homologous template exists, threading (fold recognition) instead evaluates how well a target sequence fits into each of a library of known structural folds, based on the observation that the total number of distinct protein folds in nature is far smaller than the number of protein sequences.

Ab Initio Prediction

Ab initio methods predict structure from sequence alone, without reference to any known structure, by searching for the conformation of lowest free energy according to physical principles - historically the least accurate approach, and computationally the most expensive, but necessary for sequences with no structural relatives at all.

Deep Learning and AlphaFold

Remark - AlphaFold and AI-Based Prediction

Deep-learning systems such as AlphaFold reframed structure prediction as a pattern-recognition problem, training neural networks on the entire body of solved structures and their corresponding sequence alignments to directly predict atomic coordinates, with accuracy for many proteins now rivalling experimental methods - a result widely regarded as one of the most significant recent advances in computational biology, and one that has substantially reduced (though not eliminated) the need for the template- and fold-based methods above.
