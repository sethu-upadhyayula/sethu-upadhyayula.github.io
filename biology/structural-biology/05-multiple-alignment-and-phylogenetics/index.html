5. Multiple Sequence Alignment and Phylogenetics

Extending alignment from a pair of sequences to many at once reveals conserved positions across an entire protein family, and provides the raw material for reconstructing how those sequences are evolutionarily related.

Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA)

Definition - Multiple Sequence Alignment

An alignment of three or more sequences into a common set of columns, positioning homologous residues together across all sequences simultaneously. Because finding the mathematically optimal MSA is computationally very expensive as the number of sequences grows, practical tools use heuristic strategies - commonly progressive alignment, which builds up the full alignment by successively adding sequences in order of similarity, guided by a preliminary phylogenetic tree.

MSAs reveal conserved positions - often functionally or structurally critical residues under strong evolutionary constraint - and are a required input for both protein structure prediction (Chapter 3) and phylogenetic reconstruction.

Building Phylogenetic Trees

Tree-Building Methods
  • Distance-based methods (e.g., neighbor-joining) compute a pairwise distance matrix from the alignment and cluster sequences to minimize total branch length.
  • Character-based methods - maximum parsimony (finding the tree requiring the fewest evolutionary changes) and maximum likelihood (finding the tree most likely to have produced the observed sequences, under an explicit model of sequence evolution) - use the alignment's individual columns directly rather than a summary distance.
  • Bayesian methods sample a distribution of plausible trees, providing a natural measure of confidence in the resulting relationships.

Applications in Taxonomy and Comparative Genomics

Molecular phylogenetics has become the primary tool for classifying organisms (Chapter 4 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics) and underlies comparative genomics - identifying which genomic features are shared by common descent versus acquired independently, and calibrating rates of molecular evolution.
