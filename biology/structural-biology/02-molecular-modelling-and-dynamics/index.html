2. Molecular Modelling and Dynamics

Once a structure is known, computational modelling allows researchers to visualize it, probe its physical behaviour, and explore states not directly captured by experiment.

Molecular Mechanics and Force Fields

Definition - Force Field

Rather than solving the full quantum mechanics of a molecule (computationally intractable for large biomolecules), molecular mechanics treats atoms as classical particles connected by springs, with a force field - a parameterized set of equations describing bond stretching, angle bending, torsional rotation, and non-bonded (van der Waals and electrostatic) interactions - used to compute the potential energy of any given conformation.

Molecular Dynamics Simulation

Molecular dynamics simulations use a force field to compute the forces on every atom, then numerically integrate Newton's equations of motion forward in very small time steps (typically femtoseconds), generating a trajectory that shows how the molecule moves, flexes, and interacts with solvent or ligands over time - from nanoseconds up to (with modern hardware) milliseconds of simulated time.

Remark - What Simulation Reveals

Simulations can reveal conformational changes too fast, too rare, or too subtle to capture with static experimental structures - such as the opening and closing of an enzyme's active site, or the pathway by which a ligand enters a binding pocket - complementing the experimental techniques from Chapter 1.

3D Structure Visualization

Dedicated molecular visualization software renders atomic coordinates as interactive 3D models - as ribbons highlighting secondary structure, surfaces showing shape and electrostatic potential, or stick representations showing individual atoms and bonds - an essential tool for interpreting structural data and communicating structural hypotheses.
