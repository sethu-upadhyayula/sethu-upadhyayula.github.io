4. Extinction, Adaptive Radiation and Macroevolution

Beyond the origin of individual species (Chapter 3), evolutionary biology also studies patterns that play out over much longer timescales and across many lineages at once - collectively, macroevolution.

Extinction

Background versus Mass Extinction

Background extinction refers to the ordinary, continuous rate at which species disappear over geological time. Mass extinctions are comparatively rare episodes of extinction affecting a large fraction of species across many unrelated lineages simultaneously, typically attributed to sudden, severe environmental disruption (e.g., the asteroid impact linked to the end-Cretaceous extinction of the non-avian dinosaurs).

Mass extinctions have repeatedly reshaped the subsequent course of evolution, clearing ecological space that survivors then diversify to fill - a pattern illustrated directly by adaptive radiation.

Adaptive Radiation

Example - Darwin's Finches

Adaptive radiation is the relatively rapid diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many descendant species, each adapted to a different ecological niche. The Galápagos finches Darwin studied diversified from a common ancestor into species with strikingly different beak shapes, each suited to a distinct food source - a textbook example of ecological opportunity driving rapid diversification.

Convergent Evolution

Definition - Convergent Evolution

The independent evolution of similar traits in unrelated lineages facing similar selective pressures - for example, the streamlined body shape shared by sharks, ichthyosaurs, and dolphins, three unrelated lineages independently adapted to fast swimming in water. Convergence demonstrates that natural selection can arrive at similar solutions repeatedly, constrained more by the physical demands of an environment than by any particular lineage's evolutionary history.

Human Impact on Extinction and Diversification

Contemporary human activity is now recognized as driving an extinction event of unusual speed and scale - a subject taken up in more detail in Conservation Biology - while simultaneously creating novel selective environments (urban landscapes, pesticide and antibiotic exposure) in which rapid, human-driven adaptive change can itself be observed.
