2. Origin of Life and Early Evolution

Before natural selection could act on living organisms, life itself had to arise from non-living chemistry - one of biology's most difficult open questions, though several plausible pathways have been proposed and partially tested.

Prebiotic Chemistry

Classic experiments (e.g., Miller-Urey) demonstrated that simple organic molecules - amino acids among them - can form spontaneously under conditions thought to resemble early Earth's atmosphere, given an energy source such as electrical discharge. This showed that the building blocks of life are not themselves difficult to generate abiotically, shifting the harder question to how those building blocks assembled into self-replicating systems.

The RNA World Hypothesis

Definition - RNA World Hypothesis

A leading hypothesis proposing that early life relied on RNA alone to both store genetic information and catalyze reactions (as a ribozyme, Chapter 4 of Molecular Biology), before the later evolution of DNA (a more chemically stable information carrier) and proteins (more versatile catalysts) - the ribosome's own RNA-based catalytic core is often cited as a molecular "fossil" of this earlier RNA-dominated stage.

Evolution of Cellular Structures

The transition from self-replicating molecules to true cells required, at minimum, a bounding membrane (Chapter 1 of Cell Biology) to concentrate reaction products and maintain a distinct internal chemistry - plausibly preceded by simpler, non-living lipid vesicles that could form spontaneously and grow/divide under suitable conditions.

Example - The Origin of the Eukaryotic Cell

The eukaryotic cell itself represents a major evolutionary transition, combining an archaeal host lineage with bacterial endosymbionts that became mitochondria and (in the plant/algal lineage) chloroplasts (Chapter 4 of Cell Biology) - a merger of two previously separate lineages into a single, more complex cell type.

The Evolution of Multicellularity

Multicellularity has evolved independently multiple times across the tree of life, generally through cells failing to separate after division and subsequently specializing - a transition that requires evolving mechanisms for cell-cell communication and adhesion (Chapter 3 of Cell Signaling) and coordinated, regulated cell death (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology) to sculpt and maintain a multicellular body.
