6. Molecular Evolution and the Neutral Theory

Once DNA sequencing made it possible to directly observe evolutionary change at the molecular level, a striking pattern emerged: much of it did not look like it was driven by natural selection at all.

The Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution

The Neutral Theory (Kimura)

Motoo Kimura's neutral theory proposed that the great majority of molecular changes observed between species - particularly synonymous substitutions that do not alter protein sequence - are selectively neutral, and become fixed in a population primarily through genetic drift (Chapter 2 of Population & Quantitative Genetics) rather than positive selection. This was a deliberately narrower claim than suggesting evolution overall is non-adaptive - it concerns the molecular record specifically, and is compatible with adaptive evolution continuing to shape morphology and behaviour via a comparatively small number of selected sites.

The Molecular Clock

Definition - Molecular Clock

If neutral substitutions accumulate at a roughly constant rate over time (a direct consequence of the neutral theory), the number of sequence differences between two lineages can be used as a "clock" to estimate how long ago they diverged from a common ancestor - calibrated against fossil or geological dates for at least one node in a phylogenetic tree (Chapter 5 of Structural & Computational Biology).

Remark - Rate Variation

In practice, the molecular clock does not tick at a strictly constant rate across all genes or lineages - mutation rate itself varies with generation time, DNA repair efficiency, and other factors - so modern applications use "relaxed" molecular clock models that allow rate variation across the tree, rather than assuming strict constancy.

Detecting Selection at the Molecular Level

Because most molecular change is neutral, sites or genes that deviate from neutral expectations - for instance, showing an excess of amino-acid-changing substitutions relative to silent ones - stand out as candidates for positive selection, the same logic underlying the sequence-based signatures of selection introduced in Chapter 6 of Population & Quantitative Genetics.
