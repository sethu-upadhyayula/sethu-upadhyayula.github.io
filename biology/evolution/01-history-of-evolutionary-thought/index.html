1. History of Evolutionary Thought and the Modern Synthesis

The idea that species change over time predates Darwin, but it was Darwin and Wallace who identified the mechanism - natural selection - that could explain why species change, and change in ways that appear so well suited to their environment.

Before Darwin

Jean-Baptiste Lamarck proposed one of the earliest comprehensive theories of evolutionary change, built around the inheritance of characteristics acquired during an organism's lifetime - an idea now known to be incorrect for the vast majority of traits (though epigenetic inheritance, Chapter 8 of Genetics, has revived limited interest in analogous mechanisms), but historically significant as one of the first serious proposals that species are not fixed.

Darwin, Wallace, and Natural Selection

The Logic of Natural Selection

Darwin and Wallace independently arrived at the same argument: (1) individuals within a population vary; (2) at least some of this variation is heritable; (3) populations produce more offspring than the environment can support; and (4) individuals whose heritable traits better suit the environment leave more surviving offspring. Given these premises, the population's traits will shift over generations toward those that improve survival and reproduction - natural selection - without requiring any guiding intention or acquired-characteristic inheritance.

The Modern Synthesis

Darwin lacked a correct mechanism for heredity - a gap filled only decades later, when Mendelian genetics (rediscovered around 1900) was combined with Darwinian selection and population-level mathematics (Chapters 1–3 of Population & Quantitative Genetics) into the Modern Synthesis of the 1930s–40s. This unification resolved an apparent contradiction - Mendelian inheritance was initially thought to be incompatible with the gradual, continuous change Darwin envisioned - by showing that discrete genes, acting in large numbers, readily produce the continuous variation selection acts upon (Chapter 4 of Population & Quantitative Genetics).

Remark - Beyond the Modern Synthesis

Molecular biology (discovery of DNA's structure, sequencing technology) and the neutral theory of molecular evolution (Chapter 6) have since extended, though not overturned, the Modern Synthesis, adding a molecular and, more recently, a genomic dimension to a framework originally built on morphology and classical genetics alone.
