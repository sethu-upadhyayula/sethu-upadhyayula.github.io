7. Human Evolution

Human evolution applies every concept covered so far in this subject - speciation, adaptive radiation, molecular evolution - to reconstruct the specific evolutionary history of our own species.

The Hominin Fossil Record

Fossil evidence documents a series of hominin species over roughly the last several million years, showing a gradual (though not strictly linear) trend toward bipedalism, increased brain size, and tool use - with multiple hominin species often coexisting at the same time rather than a single, simple lineage progressing directly to modern humans.

The Out-of-Africa Model

Definition - Out-of-Africa Model

Genetic and fossil evidence together support an African origin for anatomically modern Homo sapiens, with subsequent dispersal to the rest of the world; this is supported by the observation that genetic diversity is highest within African populations, consistent with Africa having the longest history of accumulating human genetic variation, with non-African populations representing more recent, smaller founding groups (Chapter 2's founder effect) that carry only a subset of that diversity.

Interbreeding with Other Hominins

Ancient DNA sequencing has shown that modern humans interbred with at least two other hominin groups - Neanderthals and Denisovans - as they dispersed out of Africa, leaving a small but functionally significant fraction of archaic hominin DNA in the genomes of many present-day human populations, including variants associated with immune function and high-altitude adaptation.

Recent Human Evolution

Example - Lactase Persistence

Human evolution has continued well within the timeframe of recorded history - the ability to digest lactose into adulthood (lactase persistence) evolved independently in multiple human populations following the domestication of dairy animals, a clear case of culturally driven natural selection (Chapter 3 of Population & Quantitative Genetics) acting on our own species within only the last several thousand years.
