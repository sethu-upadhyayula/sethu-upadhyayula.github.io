5. Coevolution and Evolutionary Arms Races

Species rarely evolve in isolation. When two species interact closely and repeatedly over evolutionary time, each can become a significant selective force acting on the other - a reciprocal process called coevolution.

Defining Coevolution

Definition - Coevolution

Coevolution occurs when a genetic change in one species produces a selective response in a second, interacting species, whose own evolutionary response in turn selects for further change in the first - an ongoing reciprocal process, rather than a single one-way adaptation. It can occur between predators and prey, hosts and parasites, or mutualistic partners.

Antagonistic Coevolution: Arms Races

Example - The Red Queen Hypothesis

In antagonistic relationships - predator/prey or host/parasite - each side is under continual selection to counter the other's adaptations, a dynamic captured by the Red Queen hypothesis: species must constantly evolve merely to maintain their relative fitness against a coevolving antagonist, since standing still while the opponent adapts is equivalent to falling behind. Host-pathogen coevolution (Chapter 6 of Organismal Biology and Chapter 4 of Immunology) is a particularly active and medically relevant example.

Mutualistic Coevolution

Coevolution is not always antagonistic. Many flowering plants and their pollinators have coevolved highly specific, mutually reinforcing traits - flower shape, color, and reward matched to a pollinator's morphology and sensory biases, and pollinator morphology and behaviour matched in turn to the flower - a mutually beneficial relationship shaped by reciprocal selection rather than conflict.

Coevolution and Diversification

Tightly coevolved relationships can themselves drive further diversification - as one partner's lineage diversifies (e.g., through the adaptive radiation of Chapter 4), a closely coevolved partner lineage may diversify alongside it, producing parallel, cospeciating trees of relationship between two entirely different groups of organisms.
