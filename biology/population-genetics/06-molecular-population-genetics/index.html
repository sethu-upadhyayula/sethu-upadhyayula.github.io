6. Molecular Population Genetics

With genome sequencing now routine, population genetics increasingly works directly with DNA sequence variation itself, rather than inferring allele frequencies indirectly from phenotype.

Measures of Sequence Diversity

Nucleotide diversity within a population, and divergence between populations or species, can be quantified directly from aligned sequences (Chapter 4 of Structural & Computational Biology) - providing a molecular window onto the same drift, selection, and migration processes covered earlier in this subject, but now measurable at every individual base pair across the genome.

Detecting Selection in Sequence Data

Signatures of Selection
  • Reduced diversity around a selected site (a "selective sweep"), as a favored allele drags nearby linked variants to high frequency along with it.
  • Excess of rare variants relative to neutral expectation, often associated with recent population expansion or purifying selection against deleterious alleles.
  • Elevated ratio of non-synonymous to synonymous substitutions in protein-coding genes, suggesting positive selection has favored amino-acid-changing mutations.

Molecular Mapping

Dense panels of molecular markers across the genome allow far finer-resolution genetic mapping (Chapter 2 of Genetics) than was possible with visible phenotypic markers alone, underlying both modern human disease-gene mapping and marker-assisted selection in agricultural breeding.

Remark - Molecular Data and Classical Population Genetics

Every concept introduced earlier in this subject - Hardy-Weinberg deviation, drift, $F_{ST}$, selection coefficients - can now be estimated directly from sequence data at genome scale, turning population genetics from a largely theoretical framework into an empirically rich, data-driven field.
