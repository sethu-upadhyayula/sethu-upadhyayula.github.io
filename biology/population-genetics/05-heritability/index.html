5. Heritability

Heritability quantifies how much of the variation in a trait, within a specific population and environment, is attributable to genetic differences among individuals - a concept frequently misunderstood, and worth defining precisely.

Broad-Sense and Narrow-Sense Heritability

Definition - Heritability

Broad-sense heritability, $H^2 = V_G / V_P$, is the proportion of total phenotypic variance due to all genetic variance. Narrow-sense heritability, $h^2 = V_A / V_P$, uses only the additive genetic variance, and is the more useful quantity for predicting resemblance between relatives and the response to artificial or natural selection.

Measuring Heritability

Heritability is commonly estimated from the resemblance between relatives - for example, comparing trait correlation in monozygotic versus dizygotic twins, or performing parent-offspring regression - and, for agricultural and laboratory populations, from the observed response to selective breeding over generations.

What Heritability Does Not Mean

Remark - Common Misconceptions

Heritability is a property of a specific population in a specific environment, not a fixed, universal property of a trait - the same trait can have different heritability in different environments or populations, since heritability depends on both genetic and environmental variance present in that setting. A heritability of, say, 0.6 does not mean 60% of an individual's trait value is "caused by genes" - it describes the proportion of population-level variance explained by genetic differences, a statement about variation between individuals, not about the development of any one individual.

Heritability and Response to Selection

Narrow-sense heritability directly predicts a population's response to selection - the breeder's equation, $R = h^2 S$, relates the response $R$ (change in trait mean per generation) to the selection differential $S$ (how strongly parents are selected relative to the population mean), a relationship used extensively in agricultural breeding programs (Chapter 6 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding).
