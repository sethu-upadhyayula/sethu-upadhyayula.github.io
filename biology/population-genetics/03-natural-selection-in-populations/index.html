3. Natural Selection in Populations

Unlike drift, natural selection changes allele frequencies in a consistent, non-random direction, driven by genotype-associated differences in survival and reproduction.

Fitness and Selection Coefficients

Definition - Relative Fitness and Selection Coefficient

The relative fitness ($w$) of a genotype measures its reproductive success relative to the most successful genotype in the population (set to $w = 1$). The selection coefficient, $s = 1 - w$, measures the relative disadvantage of a genotype. Selection changes an allele's frequency each generation in proportion to both $s$ and the current genotype frequencies, shifting the population away from Hardy-Weinberg proportions.

Modes of Selection

Classifying Modes of Selection
  • Directional selection - favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population mean over time.
  • Stabilizing selection - favors the intermediate phenotype, reducing variance without shifting the mean.
  • Disruptive selection - favors both extremes over the intermediate, potentially increasing variance or driving speciation.
  • Balancing selection - actively maintains multiple alleles in a population, for instance through heterozygote advantage.
Example - Heterozygote Advantage and Sickle Cell Trait

The sickle-cell allele is maintained at surprisingly high frequency in malaria-endemic regions because heterozygotes (carriers) have greater resistance to malaria than either homozygote - a classic example of balancing selection maintaining genetic variation that would otherwise be selected against (in the disease-causing homozygous state).

Selection versus Drift

The relative importance of selection and drift (Chapter 2) in shaping allele frequencies depends on the product of population size and the selection coefficient: in large populations even weak selection dominates over drift, while in small populations drift can overwhelm all but the strongest selection - a central and still actively debated question when interpreting real genomic variation data.
