4. Quantitative and Polygenic Traits

Many important traits - height, crop yield, blood pressure - do not sort into the discrete categories of a Mendelian cross, but vary continuously across a population. Quantitative genetics provides the statistical tools to study the inheritance of such traits.

Polygenic Inheritance

Definition - Polygenic Trait

A trait influenced by many genes, each typically contributing a small additive effect, together with environmental influence - producing a roughly continuous, often approximately normal, distribution of phenotypes in a population, rather than the discrete phenotypic classes seen for single-gene traits (Chapter 1 of Genetics).

Partitioning Phenotypic Variance

Definition - Phenotypic Variance

The total observed (phenotypic) variance in a trait, $V_P$, is partitioned as $$V_P = V_G + V_E$$ where $V_G$ is genetic variance and $V_E$ is environmental variance. Genetic variance is further split into additive ($V_A$), dominance ($V_D$), and epistatic/interaction ($V_I$) components, reflecting different ways gene effects combine.

Genome-Wide Approaches to Polygenic Traits

Modern quantitative genetics increasingly maps polygenic traits directly to genomic variation, using genome-wide association studies to identify individual variants of small effect and combining many such variants into polygenic scores that predict an individual's trait value or disease risk from genotype alone - extending classical quantitative genetics with the molecular mapping tools introduced in Chapter 2 of Genetics.
