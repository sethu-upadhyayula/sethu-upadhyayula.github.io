1. Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Before asking why allele frequencies change, population genetics first establishes a baseline: the conditions under which they would not change at all. This baseline is the Hardy-Weinberg principle.

The Hardy-Weinberg Principle

Theorem - Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

For a gene with two alleles at frequencies $p$ and $q$ (where $p + q = 1$), the genotype frequencies at equilibrium are $$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$$ corresponding to the homozygote, heterozygote, and other homozygote genotype frequencies. A population reaches and maintains these frequencies after a single generation of random mating, provided no other evolutionary forces are acting.

The Assumptions Behind Equilibrium

Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
  • No mutation altering allele frequencies.
  • No migration (gene flow) into or out of the population.
  • Random mating with respect to the gene in question.
  • No natural selection (all genotypes have equal fitness).
  • Infinitely large population size (no genetic drift, Chapter 2).

Real populations violate at least one of these assumptions almost always - which is precisely what makes the principle useful: it serves as a null hypothesis. Observing that a population's genotype frequencies deviate significantly from Hardy-Weinberg proportions is itself evidence that one of these forces (selection, non-random mating, drift, etc.) is actively operating.

Testing for Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Observed genotype counts in a sample are compared against those expected under Hardy-Weinberg proportions using a chi-squared goodness-of-fit test; a significant deviation flags the locus for further investigation - for instance, as a candidate under selection, or as evidence of population substructure (Chapter 2) or genotyping error.
