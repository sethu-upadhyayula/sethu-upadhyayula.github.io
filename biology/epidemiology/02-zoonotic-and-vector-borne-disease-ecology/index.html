2. Zoonotic and Vector-Borne Disease Ecology

Many of the most significant infectious diseases in human history did not originate in humans at all - this chapter covers diseases that cross from animal populations into humans, and those requiring a vector organism to complete their transmission cycle.

Zoonotic Disease

Definition - Zoonosis and Spillover

A zoonosis is a disease that can transmit from animals to humans. A spillover event occurs when a pathogen circulating in an animal reservoir population successfully jumps into and establishes infection in a new host species, sometimes followed by sustained human-to-human transmission if the pathogen adapts sufficiently to spread efficiently within the new host.

Factors Driving Spillover

Spillover risk increases with the frequency and closeness of contact between humans and wildlife reservoirs - factors including habitat encroachment and land-use change (Chapter 3 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology), wildlife trade, and intensive livestock farming (which can also serve as an intermediate amplifying host) all increase opportunities for a pathogen to encounter and adapt to new host species.

Vector-Borne Disease

Example - Vector-Borne Transmission

A vector is an organism (often a mosquito or tick) that transmits a pathogen between hosts without itself necessarily suffering disease. Vector-borne disease transmission depends not only on pathogen and host biology but on vector ecology - the vector's abundance, biting behaviour, and geographic range, all of which are themselves sensitive to climate (Chapter 4 of Ecosystem & Global Change Ecology), meaning climate change can directly shift the geographic range of vector-borne disease risk.

Reservoir Dynamics

Understanding a zoonotic disease's ecology requires understanding the population dynamics of its animal reservoir (Chapter 2 of Population & Community Ecology) - for instance, whether reservoir population booms (often tied to resource availability or climate conditions) predictably precede human outbreak seasons, information that can support targeted, proactive surveillance (Chapter 5) rather than purely reactive response.

Connections to Parasitology

Several vector-borne diseases are caused by parasitic (rather than viral or bacterial) pathogens - the biology of the parasites themselves, including their complex multi-host life cycles, is covered in Chapter 8 of Microbiology.
