3. Vaccination and Herd Immunity

Vaccination's immunological basis - how a vaccine trains the adaptive immune system - was covered in Chapter 7 of Immunology. This chapter covers vaccination from a population, rather than individual, perspective.

Herd Immunity

Building directly on Chapter 1's herd immunity threshold, population-level vaccination programs aim to push the proportion immune in a population above this threshold, indirectly protecting even unvaccinated individuals (those medically unable to be vaccinated, for instance) by sufficiently reducing the pathogen's opportunities for transmission within the wider population.

Vaccination Coverage and Program Design

Definition - Vaccination Coverage

Vaccination coverage - the proportion of a target population actually vaccinated - must be evaluated against the herd immunity threshold (Chapter 1) for a given disease's $R_0$; diseases with higher $R_0$ (more transmissible) require correspondingly higher coverage to achieve herd immunity, explaining why highly transmissible diseases require particularly high vaccination rates to control at the population level.

Vaccine Hesitancy and Population Heterogeneity

Achieving adequate population-level coverage depends not just on vaccine availability but on uptake, which can be undermined by vaccine hesitancy or unequal access - and vaccinated individuals are rarely distributed uniformly across a population, meaning pockets of low coverage can sustain local transmission and outbreaks even when national or regional average coverage appears adequate.

Eradication and Elimination

Example - Disease Eradication

Sustained, sufficiently high vaccination coverage can, in principle, drive a pathogen's effective reproduction number persistently below 1 across an entire region or globally, leading to elimination (no ongoing local transmission in a defined region) or, if achieved worldwide and the pathogen has no significant animal reservoir (Chapter 2) to reintroduce it, eradication - smallpox remains the clearest example of a human disease eradicated through vaccination.
