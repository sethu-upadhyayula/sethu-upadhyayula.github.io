5. Outbreak Investigation and Disease Surveillance

Detecting and responding to a disease outbreak quickly and accurately requires a systematic process - this chapter covers the standard framework public health investigators use, and the surveillance systems that detect an outbreak in the first place.

Disease Surveillance Systems

Definition - Disease Surveillance

Disease surveillance is the ongoing, systematic collection and analysis of health data to detect unusual patterns that might indicate an emerging outbreak - ranging from mandatory reporting of specific diseases by clinicians, to syndromic surveillance (tracking non-specific symptom patterns for early warning), to genomic surveillance (Chapter 6 of Genomics) tracking pathogen genetic variants as they spread and evolve.

Steps in Outbreak Investigation

The Standard Outbreak Investigation Sequence
  • Confirm that an actual outbreak is occurring, rather than a normal statistical fluctuation in baseline disease incidence (Chapter 1).
  • Establish a case definition, and systematically identify and count cases meeting it.
  • Characterize cases by person, place, and time to generate hypotheses about the source and mode of transmission.
  • Test those hypotheses, often using case-control or cohort study designs comparing affected and unaffected individuals.
  • Implement and evaluate control measures based on the confirmed source and transmission route.

Contact Tracing

Contact tracing - identifying and monitoring individuals who may have been exposed to a confirmed case - allows early detection and isolation of secondary cases before they can transmit further, most effective for diseases with a sufficiently long incubation period (Chapter 1) to allow intervention before an exposed contact becomes infectious themselves.

Genomic Epidemiology

Remark - Tracking Transmission with Pathogen Genomes

Sequencing pathogen genomes from different cases (Chapter 3 of Genomics's discussion of RNA-seq and sequencing technology more broadly) allows investigators to reconstruct transmission chains directly - closely related pathogen sequences suggest a recent, direct transmission link between cases - a molecular complement to the traditional epidemiological methods described above, now routinely used to confirm or refute suspected transmission routes during real outbreaks.
