1. Epidemiological Concepts and Models

Epidemiology begins with a small set of precisely defined quantities for describing how common a disease is and how quickly it spreads - the vocabulary this chapter introduces before the rest of the subject builds on it.

Incidence and Prevalence

Definition - Incidence and Prevalence

Incidence is the rate of new cases of a disease arising in a population over a given time period. Prevalence is the total proportion of a population currently affected at a given point in time, reflecting both how quickly new cases arise (incidence) and how long each case persists (duration) - a chronic, long-lasting disease can have high prevalence despite comparatively low incidence, and vice versa for a rapidly resolving one.

The Basic Reproduction Number

Definition - Basic Reproduction Number ($R_0$)

The basic reproduction number, $R_0$, is the average number of new infections generated by a single infected individual in an entirely susceptible population. When $R_0 > 1$, an outbreak tends to grow; when $R_0 < 1$, it tends to decline and eventually die out - making $R_0$ the central quantity determining whether an emerging infection will spread widely or fizzle out on its own.

Compartmental (SIR-Type) Models

The SIR Model

A basic SIR model divides a population into Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered compartments, and models the flow of individuals between them over time based on contact rates and recovery rates - conceptually similar to the population growth models of Chapter 2 of Population & Community Ecology, but tracking disease status rather than simple population size. More elaborate variants (e.g., adding an Exposed but not-yet-infectious compartment, or compartments for vaccination status, Chapter 3) extend this basic framework to more realistic disease dynamics.

Herd Immunity Threshold

Compartmental models predict a herd immunity threshold - the proportion of a population that must be immune (through prior infection or vaccination, Chapter 3) for $R_0$'s effective value to drop below 1, at which point an outbreak can no longer sustain exponential growth even though some susceptible individuals remain.
