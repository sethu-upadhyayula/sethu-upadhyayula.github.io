1. Foundations of Animal Behaviour

Before surveying specific categories of behaviour, this chapter introduces the conceptual framework behavioural ecology uses to ask "why" an animal behaves the way it does - a question that, perhaps surprisingly, has more than one valid kind of answer.

Proximate and Ultimate Causation

Definition - Proximate versus Ultimate Explanation

A proximate explanation describes the immediate mechanistic cause of a behaviour - the neural, hormonal (Chapter 8 of Animal Physiology), or developmental machinery producing it. An ultimate explanation describes why that behaviour evolved - what fitness advantage (Chapter 3 of Population & Quantitative Genetics) it confers. Both are simultaneously valid and complementary - a bird's migratory restlessness has both a proximate hormonal trigger and an ultimate explanation in terms of seasonal resource availability (Chapter 4).

Classical Ethology

Early ethologists (the founders of behavioural biology as a discipline) emphasized fixed action patterns - stereotyped, largely innate behavioural sequences triggered by a specific environmental stimulus (a "releaser"), studied through careful, systematic observation of animals in as close to natural conditions as possible, an approach that established behaviour as a subject amenable to the same rigorous biological study as morphology or physiology.

Instinct, Learning, and Memory

Innate versus Learned Behaviour

Instinctive behaviour is largely genetically pre-programmed, requiring little or no environmental input to develop correctly, while learned behaviour is shaped by individual experience - the two are not strictly separate categories, since many behaviours combine an innate predisposition with substantial fine-tuning through learning and memory over an individual's lifetime.

Sensory Ecology

How an animal behaves depends fundamentally on what it can perceive - sensory ecology studies how an animal's sensory systems (Chapter 4 of Animal Physiology) are tuned to the specific information most relevant to its ecological niche (Chapter 1 of Population & Community Ecology), a perspective that underlies the study of communication (Chapter 3) in particular.
