2. Foraging Behaviour

Finding and acquiring food is one of the most fundamental behavioural challenges any animal faces, and one of the most extensively modelled areas of behavioural ecology.

Optimal Foraging Theory

Definition - Optimal Foraging Theory

Optimal foraging theory models foraging as a cost-benefit problem, predicting that natural selection favours foraging strategies maximizing net energy intake (or another relevant currency) per unit time or risk - for example, predicting which prey types a predator should include in its diet based on their relative abundance and the energy gained versus the time cost of pursuing and handling them.

The Marginal Value Theorem

When food occurs in discrete patches that deplete as an animal forages within them, the marginal value theorem predicts the optimal time to leave a patch and move to a new one - an animal should depart once the patch's current intake rate falls to match the average intake rate achievable across the whole habitat, accounting for the travel time cost of finding a new patch.

Foraging Trade-offs

Example - The Predation Risk Trade-off

Real foraging decisions rarely optimize energy intake alone - animals must also weigh the risk of predation while foraging, often accepting a lower food intake rate in a safer location over a higher intake rate in a riskier one, illustrating that "optimal" foraging behaviour reflects a balance among multiple simultaneous fitness costs and benefits, not energy maximization in isolation.

Foraging and Social Behaviour

Foraging strategy connects directly to other behavioural categories covered in this subject - group foraging can improve prey detection or reduce individual predation risk (Chapter 6), while competition over food resources is often a central driver of the aggressive and territorial behaviours covered in Chapter 7.
