3. Communication

Communication - the transfer of information from a signaling individual to a receiver, in a way that (at least on average) benefits the signaler - underlies courtship, coordination, and conflict resolution across the animal kingdom.

Channels of Communication

Sensory Channels Used in Animal Signaling
  • Visual signals - body postures, coloration, and displays, effective at range but limited by line of sight and light availability.
  • Acoustic signals - vocalizations and other sounds, able to travel around obstacles and over greater distances than visual signals, at the cost of being detectable by unintended eavesdroppers, including predators.
  • Chemical signals (pheromones) - persist over time and can travel efficiently in the absence of direct contact, but degrade more slowly and carry direction/timing information less precisely than acoustic signals.
  • Tactile signals - require direct contact, used extensively in close social interactions such as grooming (Chapter 6).

Pheromones

Example - Pheromone Communication

Pheromones are chemical signals released by one individual that trigger a specific behavioural or physiological response in another individual of the same species - used for purposes ranging from marking territory boundaries, to signaling reproductive receptivity, to the trail-marking and alarm pheromones central to insect social organization (Chapter 6).

Honest Signals and Signal Reliability

Because signaling and receiving can have conflicting interests (a signaler may benefit from exaggerating its quality or intentions), evolutionary theory predicts that reliable signals are often costly to produce in a way only genuinely high-quality individuals can afford - a handicap that keeps the signal honest on average, a concept especially important for interpreting the elaborate ornaments and displays covered in Chapter 5's discussion of sexual selection.

Communication and Sensory Ecology

The evolution of any communication signal is inseparable from the sensory ecology (Chapter 1) of its intended receiver - a signal can only be effective if it matches the perceptual capabilities of the species it is meant to reach, a constraint that has shaped signal design across every sensory channel listed above.
