7. Conflict, Aggression and Parental Care

This closing chapter covers two behavioural categories that, at first glance, seem opposite - aggressive conflict and parental self-sacrifice - but both are governed by the same underlying cost-benefit logic used throughout this subject.

Aggression and Resource Contests

Definition - Ritualized Aggression

Conflicts over resources, mates, or territory (Chapter 5) are frequently resolved through ritualized aggression - stereotyped displays and contests that settle a dispute with reduced risk of physical injury to either party - rather than escalating to potentially lethal physical combat, since even the eventual winner of an all-out fight risks significant injury that would reduce its own subsequent fitness.

Game-Theoretic Models of Conflict

Evolutionary game theory models predict when an individual should escalate a contest versus retreat, based on factors such as resource value, an individual's own fighting ability relative to its opponent, and the cost of injury - providing a framework for understanding why aggressive displays so often substitute for actual physical fighting, without requiring any assumption of deliberate strategic reasoning by the animals themselves.

Parental Care

Definition - Parental Care

Parental care - any parental behaviour that improves offspring survival or fitness, from simply guarding eggs to extended feeding and teaching of independent young - represents a direct investment (Chapter 5) that trades off against a parent's own future reproduction, since resources and time devoted to current offspring cannot simultaneously be devoted to producing or caring for future offspring.

Determinants of Parental Investment

Remark - Why Care Varies So Much Across Species

The amount of parental care a species exhibits reflects a balance between offspring need (how helpless young are at birth or hatching, connecting to the developmental biology of altriciality versus precociality) and the parent's own prospects for future reproduction - species with low expectation of future breeding opportunities are predicted to invest more heavily in current offspring than species with strong prospects for future reproductive success, a prediction that connects parental care directly back to the r/K life history framework introduced in Chapter 3 of Population & Community Ecology.
