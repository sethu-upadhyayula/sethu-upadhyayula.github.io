6. Social Behaviour, Eusociality and Kin Selection

Group living carries both costs (competition, disease transmission, conspicuousness to predators) and benefits (cooperative foraging and defense, Chapter 2), and this chapter covers the evolutionary logic behind the most extreme forms of animal social organization.

Benefits and Costs of Group Living

Social living can reduce individual predation risk through group vigilance and the "dilution effect" (each individual bearing a smaller share of overall predation risk within a larger group), and can improve foraging efficiency through information sharing and cooperative hunting - benefits that must outweigh the costs of increased resource competition and pathogen transmission within a group for sociality to be favoured by selection.

Kin Selection and Inclusive Fitness

Hamilton's Rule

Since relatives share genes by common descent (Chapter 1 of Genetics), a gene favouring helping a relative can spread even at some cost to the helper's own direct reproduction - formalized in Hamilton's rule, $$rB > C$$ where $r$ is the genetic relatedness between actor and recipient, $B$ is the fitness benefit to the recipient, and $C$ is the fitness cost to the actor. This concept of inclusive fitness - an individual's own reproduction plus its effect on relatives' reproduction, weighted by relatedness - explains how apparently altruistic behaviour can nonetheless be favoured by natural selection.

Eusociality

Example - Eusocial Insects

Eusociality - the most extreme form of social organization, seen in ants, bees, wasps, and termites - involves cooperative brood care, overlapping generations, and a reproductive division of labour in which most individuals (workers) forgo their own direct reproduction to help raise the offspring of a small number of reproductive individuals (queens). The unusual genetics of haplodiploidy in bees and ants (where males develop from unfertilized eggs) produces unusually high relatedness among sisters, historically proposed as a factor favouring the repeated evolution of eusociality in this group.

Dominance Hierarchies

Many social species establish stable dominance hierarchies that reduce the cost of repeated conflict over resources or mates (Chapter 7) - once relative rank is established, subordinate individuals typically defer rather than repeatedly re-contest an already-settled outcome, reducing the energetic and injury costs of conflict for the group as a whole.
