4. Migration, Dispersal and Navigation

Many animals undertake substantial movements - some cyclical and seasonal, others a one-way departure from their birthplace - this chapter covers both the ecological reasons for such movement and the remarkable sensory mechanisms that make it possible.

Migration

Definition - Migration

Migration is regular, often seasonal, long-distance movement between distinct habitats, typically tracking resource availability, breeding opportunity, or favourable climatic conditions - for example, avoiding the reduced day length and resource scarcity of temperate winters by moving toward more productive conditions elsewhere.

Dispersal

Dispersal - movement away from an individual's birthplace or existing home range, typically without return - is distinct from migration in being generally a one-way event rather than cyclical, and is a major determinant of gene flow (Chapter 2 of Population & Quantitative Genetics) between populations, connecting behavioural ecology directly to population genetics and metapopulation dynamics (Chapter 3 of Population & Community Ecology).

Mechanisms of Navigation

Navigational Cues Used by Migrating Animals
  • Celestial cues - the position of the sun or stars, requiring an internal clock (Chapter 6 of Plant Biology's discussion of circadian rhythms has an animal parallel) to correct for their movement across the sky over the course of a day or night.
  • Magnetic field detection - some migratory species can sense Earth's magnetic field directly, providing a reliable directional reference independent of visibility conditions.
  • Olfactory cues - used by some species (e.g., salmon returning to their natal stream) to identify a specific remembered location at close range.
  • Landmark-based navigation - reliance on learned visual landmarks, generally effective only over more limited spatial scales than the mechanisms above.

The Costs and Benefits of Long-Distance Movement

Both migration and dispersal carry substantial costs - energy expenditure, predation risk during transit, and the possibility of arriving at an unsuitable destination - meaning natural selection favours these behaviours only when the benefits (access to resources, escape from unfavourable conditions, avoidance of inbreeding or local competition) reliably outweigh those costs, the same basic cost-benefit logic underlying the foraging decisions of Chapter 2.
