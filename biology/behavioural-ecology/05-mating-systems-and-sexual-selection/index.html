5. Mating Systems and Sexual Selection

Reproduction imposes its own distinct selective pressures, separate from - and sometimes in tension with - ordinary natural selection for survival, a distinction formalized as sexual selection.

Sexual Selection

Definition - Sexual Selection

Sexual selection is selection arising specifically from variance in mating success, operating through two main mechanisms: intrasexual selection (competition among members of one sex, typically males, for access to mates) and intersexual selection (mate choice, typically by females, favouring certain traits in potential mates) - together able to favour traits, such as elaborate ornaments or weaponry, that may even be disadvantageous for ordinary survival.

Mating Systems

Classifying Mating Systems
  • Monogamy - one male and one female form an exclusive pair bond, often associated with substantial biparental care (Chapter 7).
  • Polygyny - one male mates with multiple females, common when resources or receptive females can be effectively monopolized by dominant males.
  • Polyandry - one female mates with multiple males, comparatively rare, but found where male parental investment is unusually high.
  • Promiscuity - multiple mating by both sexes without stable pair bonds.

Parental Investment Theory

Which sex competes more intensely for mates, and which sex is choosier, is predicted substantially by relative parental investment - the sex investing more in each offspring (often, though not universally, females, given the greater energetic cost of producing eggs versus sperm, Chapter 2 of Developmental Biology) is typically the more selective sex, while the lower-investing sex faces stronger competitive pressure for mating opportunities.

Courtship and Territoriality

Example - Courtship Displays and Territory Defense

Elaborate courtship displays often function as honest signals (Chapter 3) of mate quality, while territoriality - defending an area against rivals - frequently serves to secure resources or breeding sites attractive to potential mates, linking mating system evolution directly to the aggressive and resource-defense behaviours covered further in Chapter 7.
