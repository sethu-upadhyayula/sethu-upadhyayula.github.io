1. Energy Flow in Ecosystems

Unlike matter, which cycles repeatedly through an ecosystem (Chapter 2), energy flows through it in one direction only - captured by producers, transferred (with substantial loss) through consumers, and ultimately dissipated as heat.

Primary Production

Definition - Gross and Net Primary Production

Gross primary production (GPP) is the total rate at which producers (via photosynthesis, Chapter 8 of Biochemistry) capture energy. Net primary production (NPP) is GPP minus the energy producers themselves consume in respiration - the energy actually available to be transferred to the next trophic level (Chapter 5 of Population & Community Ecology), and the quantity ecologists usually mean by an ecosystem's "productivity."

Trophic Transfer and Energy Pyramids

The Ten Percent Rule

On average, only around 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is captured in the biomass of the level above it - most is lost as metabolic heat or remains unconsumed and unassimilated. This consistent, substantial loss at each transfer is why energy pyramids are always narrowest at the top, and why food chains rarely extend beyond four or five trophic levels before too little energy remains to support a further level.

Ecosystem Productivity Across Environments

Net primary production varies enormously between ecosystem types - tropical rainforests and coral reefs rank among the most productive systems on Earth, while deserts and the open ocean are comparatively unproductive per unit area - differences driven by the availability of light, water, and nutrients (Chapter 2) rather than by any fundamental difference in the underlying photosynthetic machinery (Chapter 8 of Biochemistry).

Secondary Production

Secondary production refers to the rate at which consumers convert consumed energy into their own biomass, itself subject to the same substantial transfer losses described above - meaning the total biomass an ecosystem can support declines sharply at each successive trophic level, a direct energetic constraint on food web structure (Chapter 5 of Population & Community Ecology).
