2. Biogeochemical Cycles

Unlike energy, the chemical elements essential to life are neither created nor destroyed - they cycle repeatedly between living organisms and the physical (abiotic) environment, in patterns collectively called biogeochemical cycles.

The Carbon Cycle

Carbon moves between the atmosphere (as CO₂), living organisms (via photosynthesis and respiration, Chapters 6 and 8 of Biochemistry), the oceans (as dissolved inorganic carbon), and long-term geological storage (fossil fuels, carbonate rocks) - a cycle substantially perturbed in the modern era by the combustion of fossil carbon that had been removed from active circulation for millions of years (Chapter 5).

The Nitrogen Cycle

Definition - The Nitrogen Cycle

Atmospheric nitrogen gas (N₂), largely inaccessible to most organisms, is converted to usable forms by nitrogen-fixing bacteria and archaea (Chapter 2 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics), assimilated into organic molecules (Chapter 7 of Biochemistry and Chapter 4 of Plant Biology), and eventually returned to the atmosphere by denitrifying microorganisms - a cycle depending almost entirely on microbial activity at multiple steps (Chapter 10 of Microbiology).

The Phosphorus Cycle

Unlike carbon and nitrogen, phosphorus has no significant atmospheric gas phase - it cycles primarily through rock weathering, uptake by organisms, and sedimentation, making it a comparatively slow-moving cycle and, consequently, often the nutrient limiting primary production (Chapter 1) in freshwater and some terrestrial ecosystems.

The Water Cycle

Water moves between the atmosphere, land, and oceans through evaporation, transpiration (Chapter 3 of Plant Biology), precipitation, and runoff - a cycle that interacts closely with the others covered in this chapter, since water availability strongly constrains rates of both primary production and microbial nutrient cycling.

Ecosystem Services

Remark - Ecosystem Services

The functioning of these cycles, together with primary production (Chapter 1), underlies a wide range of ecosystem services - benefits humans derive from ecosystems, including provisioning services (food, timber, fresh water), regulating services (climate regulation, water purification, pollination), and cultural services - a framework increasingly used to make the practical value of ecosystem function explicit in conservation and policy decisions (Conservation Biology).
