6. Invasive Species

Species introduced, whether deliberately or accidentally, to a region outside their native range can sometimes become ecologically disruptive invaders - this closing chapter covers what distinguishes a successful, damaging invasion from a harmless introduction.

Defining Invasive Species

Definition - Invasive Species

Not every non-native species becomes invasive - the term is generally reserved for introduced species that establish self-sustaining populations and cause significant ecological or economic harm in their new range, as distinct from introduced species that either fail to establish at all or integrate without substantial disruptive effect.

Why Introduced Species Succeed as Invaders

Successful invaders often benefit from "enemy release" - arriving in a new range without the predators, parasites, or competitors (Chapter 4 of Population & Community Ecology) that constrained their population in their native range - and frequently possess traits associated with r-selected life history strategies (Chapter 3 of Population & Community Ecology), such as rapid reproduction and broad environmental tolerance, that favour rapid establishment.

Ecological Impacts of Invasive Species

Mechanisms of Ecological Disruption
  • Competitive displacement - outcompeting native species for shared resources (Chapter 4 of Population & Community Ecology).
  • Novel predation - preying on native species that evolved no defenses against this new predator.
  • Hybridization - interbreeding with related native species, potentially eroding the genetic distinctiveness of the native population (Chapter 3 of Evolution's discussion of hybrid zones).
  • Ecosystem engineering - physically altering habitat structure or nutrient cycling (Chapter 2) in ways that disadvantage native species adapted to the pre-invasion conditions.

Managing Invasive Species

Management strategies range from prevention (biosecurity measures limiting new introductions) to early detection and rapid response, and - once a species is well established - long-term control or, rarely, complete eradication, all considered core tools within the broader conservation strategy toolkit covered in Conservation Biology.
