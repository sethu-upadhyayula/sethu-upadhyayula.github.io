4. Climate Change: Causes and Consequences

Human alteration of the global carbon cycle (Chapter 2) has driven a rapid and well-documented change in Earth's climate - this chapter covers the underlying physical mechanism and its ecological consequences.

The Greenhouse Effect

Definition - The Greenhouse Effect

Certain atmospheric gases (CO₂, methane, and others) are transparent to incoming shortwave solar radiation but absorb and re-emit outgoing longwave radiation from Earth's surface, retaining heat within the atmosphere - the greenhouse effect. This effect is not itself artificial (without it, Earth would be far colder and largely uninhabitable) - anthropogenic climate change instead reflects a rapid intensification of this natural effect, driven by rising atmospheric concentrations of these gases.

Anthropogenic Drivers

Fossil fuel combustion (releasing carbon that had been removed from the active carbon cycle over geological time, Chapter 2) and land-use change (Chapter 3, particularly deforestation) are the dominant human contributions to rising atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations, with agriculture (methane from livestock and rice cultivation, nitrous oxide from fertilizer use) as further significant contributors.

Ecological Consequences

Documented Ecological Responses to Climate Change
  • Phenological shifts - many species now flower, breed, or migrate earlier than in past decades, tracking warming temperatures.
  • Range shifts - species distributions are moving poleward and to higher elevations as local climate conditions shift.
  • Ocean acidification - increased atmospheric CO₂ dissolves into oceans as carbonic acid, lowering ocean pH and impairing shell and skeleton formation in many marine organisms.
  • Mismatch effects - when interacting species (e.g., a plant and its pollinator, Chapter 5 of Evolution) shift their timing at different rates, previously synchronized relationships can become disrupted.

Climate Change and Biodiversity

Climate change interacts with the other pressures covered in this subject - land-use change (Chapter 3), pollution (Chapter 6), and invasive species (Chapter 7) - often compounding their individual effects, and is considered alongside these other threats in modern biodiversity conservation assessment (Conservation Biology).
