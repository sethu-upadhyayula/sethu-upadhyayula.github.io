1. Restriction Enzymes and Vector Systems

Recombinant DNA technology begins with the ability to cut DNA at precise, predictable locations and to deliver the resulting fragments into a living cell - the subject of this opening chapter.

Restriction and Modification Enzymes

Definition - Restriction Endonuclease

A restriction endonuclease recognizes a specific, usually palindromic, DNA sequence and cleaves the DNA backbone at or near that site, generating either blunt ends or overhanging "sticky" ends that can subsequently re-anneal with any other DNA fragment cut by the same enzyme. Bacteria naturally use these enzymes, paired with a corresponding modification (methylation) system that protects the bacterium's own DNA from cleavage, as a defense against invading bacteriophage DNA (Chapter 3 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics).

Plasmid Vectors

A plasmid - a small, circular, self-replicating DNA molecule naturally found in many bacteria (Chapter 3 of Genetics covers their inheritance) - can be engineered into a vector: a plasmid modified to carry a selectable marker (typically antibiotic resistance) and one or more restriction sites into which foreign DNA can be inserted, then reintroduced into bacterial cells by transformation (Chapter 6 of Genetics).

Other Vector Systems

Vectors for Larger DNA Inserts
  • Bacteriophage vectors - engineered from bacteriophages such as lambda, able to carry larger DNA inserts than a typical plasmid.
  • Cosmids - hybrid vectors combining plasmid features with phage packaging signals, extending insert capacity further.
  • Bacterial and yeast artificial chromosomes (BACs and YACs) - capable of stably carrying very large DNA inserts (hundreds of kilobases), essential for early genome sequencing projects (Chapter 5 of Genomics).
  • The Ti plasmid - a natural plasmid from the plant pathogen Agrobacterium tumefaciens, engineered as the primary vector for introducing foreign DNA into plant genomes (Chapter 6 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding).

Gene Targeting and Transposon-Based Methods

Beyond simple insertion, vectors can be designed for gene targeting - directing an inserted sequence to a specific genomic location via homologous recombination (Chapter 2 of Molecular Biology) - while engineered transposons (Chapter 6 of Cell Biology) provide an alternative route for inserting DNA at semi-random genomic locations, useful for large-scale mutagenesis screens.
