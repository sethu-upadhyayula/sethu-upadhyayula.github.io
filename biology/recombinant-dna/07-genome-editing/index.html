7. Genome Editing: CRISPR-Cas9 and Other Tools

Genome editing technologies allow a specific genomic sequence to be altered directly within a living cell's own chromosome, at a level of precision and ease that has transformed both basic research and the prospects for genetic therapies.

The CRISPR-Cas9 System

How CRISPR-Cas9 Works

CRISPR-Cas9, adapted from a natural bacterial antiviral immune system, uses a short guide RNA to direct the Cas9 enzyme to a specific genomic sequence complementary to that guide, where Cas9 introduces a targeted double-strand break. The cell's own DNA repair machinery (Chapter 2 of Molecular Biology) then resolves this break, either through error-prone non-homologous end joining (often disrupting the gene, achieving a knockout, Chapter 6) or, if a repair template is supplied, through homologous recombination (allowing a precise, designed sequence change to be introduced instead).

Comparison to Earlier Genome-Editing Tools

Before CRISPR-Cas9 became widely available, genome editing relied on zinc-finger nucleases (ZFNs) and TALENs - engineered proteins combining a programmable, sequence-specific DNA-binding domain with a nuclease domain - which achieve a similar targeted double-strand break but require far more complex protein engineering for each new target sequence than CRISPR's comparatively simple guide-RNA redesign, explaining CRISPR's rapid and near-total adoption once it became available.

The Cre-Lox System

Definition - Cre-Lox Recombination

The Cre-Lox system uses the site-specific recombinase Cre to excise, invert, or translocate DNA flanked by short loxP recognition sequences (Chapter 2 of Molecular Biology's discussion of site-specific recombination) - widely used to generate conditional knockouts, in which a gene is disrupted only in specific tissues or at a specific developmental time, achieved by restricting Cre expression itself to the desired tissue or time window.

Applications and Considerations

Genome editing tools are used to create disease models, correct disease-causing mutations directly (in both research and, increasingly, clinical gene therapy contexts), and generate the genetically modified crops and organisms covered in Chapter 6 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding and Chapter 3 of Animal & Medical Biotechnology - applications that also raise the biosafety and bioethics considerations discussed more fully in that context.
