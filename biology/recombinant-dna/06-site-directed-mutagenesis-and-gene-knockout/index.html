6. Site-Directed Mutagenesis and Gene Knockout

Understanding what a gene does often requires deliberately changing or removing it and observing the consequence - this chapter covers the classical methods for making such precise, planned genetic changes, prior to the CRISPR-based tools covered in Chapter 7.

Site-Directed Mutagenesis

Definition - Site-Directed Mutagenesis

Site-directed mutagenesis introduces a specific, planned sequence change - a single point mutation, small insertion, or deletion - at a precise, predetermined location within a cloned gene, typically using PCR with primers carrying the desired sequence change (Chapter 4). This allows researchers to test the functional consequence of a specific amino acid change directly, connecting a gene's sequence to its protein's structure and function (Chapters 1–2 of Structural & Computational Biology).

In Vitro Mutagenesis and Deletion Techniques

Beyond single-point changes, larger deletions or insertions can be engineered into a cloned sequence in vitro before reintroduction into a host organism - an approach used to systematically probe which regions of a gene or regulatory element are functionally essential.

Gene Knockout

Definition - Gene Knockout

A gene knockout eliminates a gene's function entirely - in bacteria and yeast, typically by homologous recombination (Chapter 2 of Molecular Biology) replacing the target gene with a disrupted or marker-containing version; in mice and other model organisms, classically achieved via homologous recombination in embryonic stem cells (Chapter 7 of Developmental Biology), which are then used to generate a whole knockout animal for phenotypic study.

Reverse Genetics

Knockout and mutagenesis approaches together constitute reverse genetics - starting from a known gene sequence and working forward to determine its function by observing the effect of disrupting it - the logical complement to classical (forward) genetics, which starts from an observed phenotype and works backward to identify the responsible gene (Chapter 1 of Genetics).
