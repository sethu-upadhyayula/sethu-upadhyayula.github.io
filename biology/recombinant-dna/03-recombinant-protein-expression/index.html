3. Recombinant Protein Expression Systems

Cloning a gene (Chapter 2) is often only the first step - many applications require producing large quantities of the encoded protein itself, which requires an expression system matched to the protein's specific requirements.

Expression Vectors

Definition - Expression Vector

Unlike a simple cloning vector, an expression vector includes regulatory elements - a strong promoter, appropriate for the chosen host's transcription machinery (Chapter 3 of Molecular Biology), and translation signals - positioned to drive high-level production of the cloned gene's protein product, often under the control of an inducible promoter allowing expression to be switched on only when desired.

Bacterial Expression Systems

Bacterial hosts (typically E. coli) offer fast growth, low cost, and simple genetics, but lack the machinery for eukaryotic post-translational modifications (Chapter 4 of Molecular Biology) such as glycosylation, and can struggle to correctly fold complex eukaryotic proteins - sometimes producing insoluble aggregates (inclusion bodies) that require additional purification steps to recover functional protein.

Eukaryotic Expression Systems

Choosing a Eukaryotic Host
  • Yeast - combines eukaryotic protein processing with bacteria-like ease of culture and genetic manipulation, a common intermediate choice.
  • Insect cells - often used with baculovirus vectors, providing high protein yields with mostly correct eukaryotic folding and modification.
  • Mammalian cells - the most accurate choice for producing complex human therapeutic proteins (Chapter 2 of Animal & Medical Biotechnology) requiring precisely human-type post-translational modification, at higher cost and complexity than the alternatives above.

Purification of Recombinant Protein

Recombinant proteins are typically engineered with an affinity tag (such as a polyhistidine tag) enabling rapid, highly selective purification via affinity chromatography (Chapter 9 of Biochemistry), before the tag is often removed by specific proteolytic cleavage to yield the native protein sequence.
