5. DNA and Protein Sequencing Methods

Determining a molecule's exact sequence - rather than simply detecting or amplifying it - is the foundation of essentially all of modern genomics (Genomics), and this chapter covers the underlying experimental methods.

Sanger Sequencing

Definition - Sanger (Chain-Termination) Sequencing

Sanger sequencing extends a primer using DNA polymerase in the presence of both normal nucleotides and a small proportion of fluorescently labelled, chain-terminating dideoxynucleotides - each incorporation of a terminator halts extension at that exact position, producing a mixture of fragments of every possible length up to the full template, which are then separated by size (via capillary electrophoresis, Chapter 9 of Biochemistry) and read off to reconstruct the sequence.

Next-Generation Sequencing

Next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies sequence millions of DNA fragments simultaneously rather than one at a time, dramatically reducing cost and increasing throughput compared to Sanger sequencing - the technology underlying whole-genome sequencing, RNA-seq (Chapter 3 of Genomics), and the metagenomic sequencing covered in Chapter 6 of Genomics, and now the standard approach for essentially all large-scale sequencing projects.

Genome Sequencing Strategies

Remark - Assembling a Genome from Short Reads

Because sequencing technologies typically read only short fragments at a time, sequencing an entire genome requires fragmenting it, sequencing many overlapping pieces, and computationally reassembling them based on sequence overlap - a process aided by the alignment algorithms covered in Chapter 4 of Structural & Computational Biology, and complicated considerably by repetitive genomic regions (Chapter 1 of Genomics) that can span multiple reads and confound unambiguous assembly.

Protein Sequencing

Direct protein sequencing (Edman degradation, sequentially removing and identifying one N-terminal amino acid at a time) has been largely superseded by mass-spectrometry-based methods (Chapter 4 of Genomics's discussion of proteomics) for most applications, though direct sequencing remains useful for confirming a purified protein's identity or characterizing modifications not easily inferred from the corresponding gene sequence alone.
