4. PCR and Nucleic Acid Amplification

The polymerase chain reaction transformed molecular biology by making it possible to generate billions of copies of a specific DNA sequence from a vanishingly small starting sample, without needing to clone it into a living host at all.

The PCR Cycle

The Three Steps of a PCR Cycle

Each PCR cycle repeats three temperature-controlled steps: denaturation (heating to separate the DNA duplex into single strands), annealing (cooling to allow short, sequence-specific primers to bind their complementary target sequence), and extension (a heat-stable DNA polymerase, most commonly Taq polymerase from a thermophilic bacterium, extends each primer, Chapter 1 of Molecular Biology). Because each cycle doubles the amount of target sequence, roughly 30 cycles can amplify a single target molecule more than a billion-fold.

Real-Time (Quantitative) PCR

Real-time PCR (qPCR) monitors amplification as it happens, using a fluorescent reporter whose signal increases in proportion to the amount of DNA produced each cycle - allowing not just detection but accurate quantification of the original target sequence's abundance, widely used for microbial quantification (Chapter 1 of Microbiology) and gene expression analysis (Chapter 3 of Genomics's discussion of differential expression, though qPCR is typically used for a small number of specific genes rather than genome-wide profiling).

PCR Applications

Common Applications of PCR
  • Diagnostic detection of specific pathogens directly from a clinical sample, without requiring culture (Chapter 1 of Microbiology).
  • Amplifying a specific region for subsequent cloning (Chapter 2) or sequencing (Chapter 5).
  • Forensic and paternity DNA profiling, amplifying the short tandem repeat regions used in DNA fingerprinting (Chapter 7 of Genomics).
  • Site-directed mutagenesis (Chapter 6), using primers carrying a deliberate sequence change.

DNA Microarrays

DNA microarrays, an earlier hybridization-based technology for surveying many sequences simultaneously (introduced alongside RNA-seq in Chapter 3 of Genomics), rely on similar sequence-specific hybridization principles as PCR primer annealing, but detect binding to a fixed, pre-designed probe set rather than amplifying a target sequence.
