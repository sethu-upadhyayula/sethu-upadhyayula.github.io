3. Transgenic Animals and Animal Cloning

This chapter covers two distinct animal biotechnologies that both manipulate an animal's genome or genetic origin - introducing new genetic material (transgenics) and producing a genetically identical copy of an existing animal (cloning).

Generating Transgenic Animals

Definition - Transgenic Animal

A transgenic animal carries a deliberately introduced foreign gene, classically produced by microinjecting DNA directly into a fertilized egg pronucleus before implantation, or via the embryonic-stem-cell-based homologous recombination approach used to generate the gene knockouts covered in Chapter 6 of Recombinant DNA Technology - the same core technique, whether the goal is disrupting an existing gene (knockout) or adding a new one (transgenic).

Applications of Transgenic Animals

Transgenic animals serve as disease models (carrying a human disease-associated gene or mutation to study its physiological effects), as "bioreactors" producing a valuable recombinant protein in an easily collected body fluid such as milk, and as tools for basic research into gene function, directly complementing the reverse-genetics approach introduced in Chapter 6 of Recombinant DNA Technology.

Animal Cloning by Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer

Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer

Somatic cell nuclear transfer - the technique behind Dolly the sheep, the first mammal cloned from an adult cell - removes the nucleus from an unfertilized egg and replaces it with the nucleus from a donor somatic (body) cell, then stimulates the reconstructed egg to begin development as though newly fertilized. Successful cloning demonstrates the genomic equivalence of differentiated cells (Chapter 1 of Developmental Biology) directly - the transferred nucleus retains the full genetic information needed to direct development of an entire new organism, despite originating from an already differentiated adult cell.

Cell Cloning and Selection

At a smaller scale, cell cloning - isolating and expanding a single cell into a genetically uniform population - underlies both the hybridoma technology of Chapter 2 and the establishment of stable, genetically defined cell lines (Chapter 1) more generally, a basic technique threading through much of this subject.
