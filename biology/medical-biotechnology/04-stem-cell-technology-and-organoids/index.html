4. Stem Cell Technology, Organoids and Regenerative Medicine

Building on the stem cell biology introduced in Chapter 7 of Developmental Biology, this chapter covers the specific biotechnological achievement of reprogramming cells to a pluripotent state on demand, and the increasingly sophisticated tissue models this makes possible.

Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells

Reprogramming to Pluripotency

Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) are generated by introducing a small set of reprogramming transcription factors into an ordinary adult (somatic) cell, reverting it to an embryonic-stem-cell-like pluripotent state - demonstrating, alongside somatic cell nuclear transfer (Chapter 3), that cellular differentiation (Chapter 1 of Developmental Biology) can be experimentally reversed, and providing a source of pluripotent cells that avoids the ethical considerations associated with deriving stem cells from embryos.

Guided Differentiation

Once generated, iPSCs can be guided, through carefully timed exposure to specific signaling molecules (Chapters 1–2 of Cell Signaling) that mimic normal developmental cues, to differentiate into essentially any desired cell type - a technique used both to model disease in a dish (using patient-derived iPSCs carrying a disease-causing mutation) and to screen candidate drugs for efficacy or toxicity before clinical testing.

Organoids

Definition - Organoid

An organoid is a self-organizing, three-dimensional miniature tissue structure grown from stem cells (either iPSCs or adult tissue-specific stem cells), which spontaneously recapitulates some of the architecture and cell-type diversity of the corresponding organ - a substantially more physiologically realistic model than traditional flat, single-cell-type culture (Chapter 1), used extensively to study developmental biology and disease processes that depend on realistic 3D tissue organization.

Regenerative Medicine

Stem cell and organoid technologies together underlie the emerging field of regenerative medicine - repairing or replacing damaged tissue using cell-based therapies, ranging from established treatments (haematopoietic stem cell transplantation) to experimental approaches still being tested for organ repair and tissue engineering more broadly.
