2. Hybridoma Technology and Monoclonal Antibody Production

Chapter 2 of Immunology covered antibody structure and engineering; this chapter covers the classical cell-biology technique - hybridoma technology - that first made monoclonal antibodies available at practical scale.

The Hybridoma Technique

Producing a Hybridoma

An animal is immunized with an antigen of interest, and antibody-producing B cells (Chapter 3 of Immunology) are isolated from its spleen. Because normal B cells cannot divide indefinitely in culture (Chapter 1), each B cell is fused with an immortalized myeloma (cancerous plasma cell) cell line to create a hybridoma - a hybrid cell combining the B cell's specific antibody-producing capability with the myeloma cell's capacity for unlimited division.

Selection and Screening

Following fusion, cells are grown in a selective medium that kills unfused myeloma cells while allowing only successfully fused hybridomas to survive, since unfused B cells die naturally in culture on their own. Individual surviving hybridoma clones are then screened to identify those producing antibody with the desired specificity, and the single best-performing clone is expanded into a stable, immortal cell line.

Monoclonal versus Polyclonal Antibodies

Definition - Monoclonal Antibody

Because a hybridoma derives from a single original B cell, every antibody molecule it produces is identical - a monoclonal antibody - in contrast to the polyclonal mixture of many different antibodies (recognizing different epitopes of the same antigen, Chapter 2 of Immunology) present in an immunized animal's whole serum. Monoclonal antibodies' defined, reproducible specificity makes them invaluable for the immunological techniques covered in Chapter 8 of Immunology, and for antibody-based therapeutics.

Beyond Hybridomas: Modern Antibody Production

While hybridoma technology remains widely used, many modern monoclonal antibody therapeutics are now produced using the recombinant expression and antibody engineering methods covered in Chapters 2–3 of Recombinant DNA Technology and Chapter 2 of Immunology, particularly for generating humanized or fully human antibodies with reduced immunogenicity in patients.
