8. Molecular Diagnostics

This closing chapter covers how the molecular tools introduced across Recombinant DNA Technology, Immunology, and earlier chapters of this subject converge in the clinic - diagnosing disease and identifying pathogens with a speed and specificity unavailable to older diagnostic methods.

Nucleic Acid-Based Diagnostics

PCR and its real-time quantitative variant (Chapter 4 of Recombinant DNA Technology) allow direct, highly sensitive detection of a specific pathogen's genetic material from a clinical sample, without requiring the pathogen to first be cultured (Chapter 1 of Microbiology) - dramatically reducing diagnostic turnaround time for many infections, and enabling detection of pathogens that are difficult or dangerous to culture in the laboratory.

Immunoassay-Based Diagnostics

Antibody-based diagnostic tests (ELISA and related formats, Chapter 8 of Immunology) detect either pathogen antigens directly or the host antibody response to a pathogen, providing a complementary diagnostic approach to nucleic acid detection - antibody tests, for instance, can reveal past infection even after the pathogen itself has been cleared, information nucleic-acid-based tests alone cannot provide.

Molecular Diagnosis of Pathogens in Veterinary and Agricultural Contexts

Remark - Beyond Human Medicine

Molecular diagnostic methods are equally central to veterinary medicine and animal breeding (Chapter 7) - rapid, specific pathogen identification supports disease control in livestock populations, while molecular strain-typing methods (also applied to plant pathogen diagnosis, Chapter 7 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding) help track the spread of a specific pathogen strain across a region or production system.

Sequencing-Based Diagnostics

Next-generation sequencing (Chapter 5 of Recombinant DNA Technology) is increasingly used diagnostically, particularly when a patient presents with an unusual or unidentified infection - sequencing all nucleic acid present in a clinical sample (an approach closely related to the metagenomic sequencing covered in Chapter 6 of Genomics) can identify a causative pathogen even without any prior hypothesis about what it might be, a capability unmatched by targeted PCR or immunoassay approaches that require the pathogen to be at least suspected in advance.
