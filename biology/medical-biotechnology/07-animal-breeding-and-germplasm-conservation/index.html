7. Animal Breeding and Germplasm Conservation

Just as classical and molecular tools improve crop plants (Plant Biotechnology & Breeding), similar principles are applied to improve livestock and conserve valuable or threatened animal genetic resources - the subject of this chapter.

Animal Breeding

Animal breeding applies the same selection and heritability principles covered in Chapter 3 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding and Chapter 5 of Population & Quantitative Genetics to livestock - selecting breeding animals based on desirable traits (growth rate, milk yield, disease resistance) and, increasingly, using molecular markers (Chapter 4 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding's discussion of marker-assisted selection has a direct livestock parallel) to accelerate genetic improvement.

Assisted Reproductive Technologies

Reproductive Technologies in Animal Breeding
  • Artificial insemination - allows a single genetically superior male's genetic contribution to be distributed across a far larger number of offspring than natural mating would permit.
  • Embryo transfer - allows a genetically superior female to contribute many more offspring than she could bear herself, by transferring her embryos into surrogate mothers.
  • Cloning (Chapter 3) - used to propagate an exceptional individual animal's exact genome directly, rather than through sexual reproduction and its associated genetic recombination (Chapter 2 of Genetics).

Conservation of Animal Germplasm

Example - Germplasm Cryopreservation

Semen, embryos, and, for some species, oocytes can be cryopreserved (Chapter 1) to conserve genetic material from valuable breeding animals or endangered species indefinitely - an animal-genetics parallel to the seed gene banks covered in Chapter 7 of Conservation Biology, providing a secure, long-term backup of genetic diversity that might otherwise be lost.

Genetic Health Monitoring

Both commercial livestock breeding and conservation breeding programs increasingly use molecular genetic testing (Chapter 3 of Conservation Biology's discussion of conservation genetics) to monitor genetic diversity and screen for known deleterious alleles within a breeding population, helping to avoid the inbreeding depression risks that can arise in any small, closed breeding population.
