6. Medical Devices, Biosensors and Neurobiological Techniques

This chapter covers a different category of medical biotechnology - devices and sensors engineered to interface directly with living biological systems, whether to monitor a physiological signal, treat a condition mechanically, or study the nervous system's function directly.

Medical Devices and Implants

Medical devices and implants range from relatively simple prosthetic and orthopedic implants (requiring biocompatible materials that the immune system, Chapter 1 of Immunology, will not aggressively reject) to sophisticated active implanted devices such as cardiac pacemakers, which directly interface with the heart's own electrical conduction system (Chapter 2 of Animal Physiology).

Biosensors

Definition - Biosensor

A biosensor combines a biological recognition element (an enzyme, antibody, or nucleic acid probe) with a physical transducer that converts a specific binding or catalytic event into a measurable electrical, optical, or other signal - the glucose biosensor used in home blood-glucose monitors, which couples an enzyme (Chapter 4 of Biochemistry) specific for glucose to an electrochemical signal, remains the most widely deployed example.

Electrophysiological Studies of the Brain

Neurobiological research and clinical neurology both depend on techniques for directly recording the brain's electrical activity (Chapter 4 of Animal Physiology) - from single-neuron recording capturing the activity of an individual cell, to the electroencephalogram (EEG) recording aggregate electrical activity across the scalp, to functional imaging techniques (fMRI, PET) that infer neural activity indirectly via associated changes in blood flow or metabolism.

Behavioural Tests in Neurobiology

Remark - Linking Neural Activity to Behaviour

Electrophysiological and imaging data are typically interpreted alongside standardized behavioural tests - controlled tasks or paradigms (many originally developed in the context of the animal behaviour studies covered in Chapter 1 of Behavioural Ecology) used to probe specific cognitive or sensory functions, allowing researchers to connect a specific measured neural signal to a specific, well-defined behavioural or cognitive outcome.
