5. Cell and Gene Therapy

Cell and gene therapies treat disease not with a conventional small-molecule or protein drug, but by introducing living cells or genetic material directly - an approach drawing together nearly every technology covered so far in this subject and in Recombinant DNA Technology.

Cell Therapy

Definition - Cell Therapy

Cell therapy introduces living cells into a patient to replace damaged tissue, deliver a therapeutic function, or modulate an immune response - ranging from long-established haematopoietic stem cell transplantation to newer approaches using stem-cell-derived (Chapter 4) or immune cell-based therapies.

Immunotherapy and Engineered Immune Cells

Example - CAR-T Cell Therapy

CAR-T cell therapy genetically engineers a patient's own T cells (Chapter 4 of Immunology) to express a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) - an artificial receptor combining an antibody-derived recognition domain (Chapter 2 of Immunology) with T cell signaling machinery - redirecting the engineered T cells to specifically recognize and destroy cancer cells bearing a chosen surface marker, one of the most clinically successful applications of engineered cell therapy to date.

Gene Therapy

Gene therapy introduces genetic material directly into a patient's cells to correct a disease-causing genetic defect, replace a missing gene function, or introduce a new therapeutic function - using viral vectors (engineered from naturally infectious viruses, Chapter 3 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics, but disabled for further replication) or non-viral delivery methods to introduce DNA or RNA into target cells, and the genome-editing tools of Chapter 7 of Recombinant DNA Technology when direct genomic correction, rather than gene addition, is the goal.

rDNA-Based Therapy and Antibody Engineering

Beyond cell and gene therapy narrowly defined, recombinant DNA technology (Recombinant DNA Technology) more broadly underlies the production of therapeutic recombinant proteins, engineered antibodies (Chapter 2 of Immunology), and phage-display-derived antibody fragments (Chapter 2 of Immunology) - a family of related "biologic" therapeutics distinct from traditional small-molecule pharmaceuticals.
