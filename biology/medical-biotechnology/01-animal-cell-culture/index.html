1. Animal Cell Culture

Nearly every technology covered in this subject depends on the ability to grow animal cells reliably outside the body - the foundational technique this chapter covers.

Culture Media

Definition - Animal Cell Culture Medium

Animal cell culture medium supplies the nutrients (amino acids, glucose, vitamins), buffering capacity, and growth factors cells need to survive and divide outside the body - typically supplemented with serum (a complex, undefined mixture of growth-promoting proteins and factors) or, increasingly, chemically defined serum-free formulations that avoid the batch-to-batch variability and biosafety concerns associated with animal-derived serum.

Anchorage-Dependent versus Suspension Culture

Many animal cell types are anchorage-dependent, requiring attachment to a solid surface (often coated with extracellular matrix proteins, Chapter 3 of Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology) to survive and proliferate, reflecting the same adhesion-dependent signaling that operates in intact tissue. Other cell types, including many blood cells and some engineered cell lines, can grow in suspension culture without requiring surface attachment, generally simplifying large-scale culture for biomanufacturing purposes (Chapter 3 of Bioprocess Engineering).

Primary Culture versus Cell Lines

Primary cultures, derived directly from animal or human tissue, typically have a limited replicative lifespan before undergoing senescence (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology). Established cell lines have acquired (through spontaneous transformation or deliberate immortalization) the ability to divide indefinitely, providing a stable, renewable, and genetically consistent resource for repeated experiments - though at the cost of no longer perfectly representing normal, non-transformed cell physiology.

Cell and Tissue Preservation

Remark - Cryopreservation

Cell lines and primary cultures are commonly preserved long-term by cryopreservation - freezing cells in the presence of a cryoprotectant (which limits damaging ice-crystal formation) at ultra-low temperature - allowing a genetically stable stock to be stored indefinitely and thawed as needed, essential for maintaining consistent, low-passage-number cell stocks for research and biomanufacturing over long timeframes.
