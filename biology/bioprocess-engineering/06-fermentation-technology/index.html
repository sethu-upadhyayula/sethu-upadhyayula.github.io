6. Fermentation Technology

This chapter surveys the range of commercially important products manufactured through large-scale microbial fermentation, each built on the growth kinetics (Chapter 3) and bioreactor engineering (Chapter 4) covered earlier in this subject.

Antibiotic Fermentation

Antibiotics (Chapter 6 of Microbiology) are produced industrially by fermenting the natural producing organism (often a filamentous fungus or actinomycete bacterium) under carefully optimized conditions, typically as a secondary metabolite produced predominantly once growth has slowed - meaning antibiotic fermentation processes are often specifically designed around a distinct production phase following an initial growth phase, rather than continuous production throughout.

Organic Acids and Alcohol

Example - Fermentative Production of Organic Acids and Ethanol

Organic acids (such as citric acid, produced industrially by fungal fermentation) and ethanol (produced by yeast fermentation of sugars, Chapter 6 of Biochemistry) are among the oldest and highest-volume industrial fermentation products, exploiting fermentative metabolic pathways (Chapter 5 of Microbiology) that would otherwise be considered a less energetically efficient alternative to full aerobic respiration.

Bioplastics, Vitamins, and Enzymes

Fermentation also produces bioplastics (biodegradable polymers accumulated by certain bacteria as an internal carbon/energy store), vitamins (many industrially produced via engineered microbial fermentation rather than chemical synthesis), and industrial enzymes (Chapter 7) themselves - the recombinant protein expression systems of Chapter 3 of Recombinant DNA Technology are frequently deployed at bioprocess scale for exactly this purpose.

Biotransformation

Definition - Biotransformation

Biotransformation uses living cells or isolated enzymes (Chapter 7) to carry out a single, specific chemical modification of an already largely synthesized substrate - for example, microbial biotransformation of steroid precursors into pharmaceutically active steroid hormones - exploiting an enzyme's precise chemo-, regio-, or stereoselectivity to perform a transformation that would be difficult or costly to achieve through conventional chemical synthesis alone.

Process Flow Sheets and Economics

Each of these fermentation processes is ultimately organized around a process flow sheet - a systematic diagram of every step from raw material to final purified product - and evaluated on process economics (Chapter 9), since a scientifically successful fermentation must also be cost-competitive to be commercially viable.
