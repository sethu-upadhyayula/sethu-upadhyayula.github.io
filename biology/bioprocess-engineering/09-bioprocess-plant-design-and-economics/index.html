9. Bioprocess Plant Design and Economics

This closing chapter addresses the practical challenge of taking a bioprocess proven at laboratory scale and reliably reproducing its performance at full industrial scale - and the economic considerations that ultimately determine whether the whole enterprise is worthwhile.

Process Flow Sheets

A complete bioprocess is documented as a process flow sheet, integrating every unit operation covered across this subject - fermentation (Chapter 6), mass transfer considerations (Chapter 5), and downstream processing (Chapter 8) - into a single coherent diagram showing material and energy flows (Chapter 1) between each stage.

Scale-Up and Scale-Down

Definition - Scale-Up Challenges

Scale-up - moving a process from small laboratory scale to large industrial scale - is rarely a simple matter of building a bigger version of the same equipment, since key physical parameters (mixing time, oxygen transfer rate, Chapter 5, and heat removal capacity) do not all scale with vessel size in the same proportion, meaning conditions optimized at small scale often perform differently once scaled up. Scale-down - designing small-scale laboratory experiments that deliberately reproduce specific large-scale conditions (such as expected gradients in oxygen or nutrient concentration within a very large industrial bioreactor) - is used to study and anticipate these scale-up challenges before committing to full-scale production.

Scale-Up of Downstream Processes

Downstream processing steps (Chapter 8) also require their own scale-up considerations, often distinct from those of the fermentation itself - a chromatography step effective at laboratory scale, for instance, may become prohibitively expensive or slow at the volumes required for industrial-scale purification, sometimes requiring a substantially redesigned downstream strategy for large-scale production.

Equipment Selection and Specification

Selecting and specifying appropriate bioprocess equipment - bioreactor type and size (Chapter 4), heat exchangers, centrifuges, chromatography columns (Chapter 8) - requires balancing performance requirements against capital and operating cost, a central part of the overall plant design process.

Process Economics

Remark - What Determines Commercial Viability

Ultimately, a bioprocess's commercial viability depends on its overall process economics - capital costs (equipment, plant construction) weighed against operating costs (raw materials, energy, labour) and achievable product yield and market price - meaning even a scientifically elegant and well-optimized bioprocess can fail commercially if its economics do not compare favourably against alternative production methods or competing products.
