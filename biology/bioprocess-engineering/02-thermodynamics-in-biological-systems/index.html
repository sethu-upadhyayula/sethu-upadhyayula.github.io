2. Thermodynamics in Biological Systems

Classical thermodynamics was developed largely for closed systems approaching equilibrium - but living systems are neither closed nor at equilibrium, requiring a somewhat extended thermodynamic framework, the subject of this chapter.

Biological Systems as Open, Non-Equilibrium Systems

Definition - Open Non-Equilibrium Systems

Living cells continuously exchange both matter and energy with their environment (an open system) and are maintained in a stable but non-equilibrium state by this continuous flux - unlike a closed system relaxing toward equilibrium, a living cell at "steady state" is actively sustained away from equilibrium by ongoing metabolic work (Chapter 5 of Biochemistry), and would rapidly decay toward equilibrium (death) if that flux stopped.

Entropy Production and Thermodynamic Flux and Force

Non-equilibrium thermodynamics describes a system's behaviour in terms of thermodynamic forces (such as a concentration or electrochemical gradient) and the fluxes they drive (such as the corresponding rate of diffusion or reaction) - with entropy production providing a general measure of how far a process operates from reversibility, relevant to understanding the fundamental efficiency limits of any bioprocess.

Thermodynamics of Coupled Biochemical Reactions

Remark - Coupled Reactions Revisited

The coupling of an endergonic reaction to ATP hydrolysis (Chapter 5 of Biochemistry) is a specific instance of a more general non-equilibrium thermodynamic principle - energetically unfavourable fluxes can be sustained indefinitely provided they are thermodynamically coupled to a sufficiently favourable driving flux, a principle underlying the thermodynamic analysis of oxidative phosphorylation and other coupled biological energy transductions.

Biological Oscillations

Some coupled metabolic systems produce self-sustained oscillations rather than a simple steady state - glycolytic oscillations, in which metabolite concentrations rhythmically rise and fall, are a classic experimentally observed example - and biological clocks more broadly (Chapter 6 of Plant Biology) represent a related phenomenon in which non-equilibrium biochemical feedback loops generate stable, self-sustaining periodic behaviour.
