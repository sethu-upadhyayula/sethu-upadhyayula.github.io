7. Enzyme Engineering and Immobilization

Many industrial bioprocesses rely on isolated enzymes rather than whole living cells - this chapter covers how such enzymes are engineered for improved performance and physically immobilized for efficient, repeated industrial use.

Enzymes in Non-Aqueous Environments

Industrially useful reactions often require conditions far from an enzyme's natural aqueous environment - some enzymes retain useful activity in organic solvents or ionic liquids, expanding the range of substrates and reaction conditions (including reactions that would be thermodynamically disfavoured in water) accessible to enzymatic catalysis.

Protein Engineering Approaches

Rational versus Random Approaches to Enzyme Engineering
  • Rational design - using structural knowledge (Chapter 1 of Structural & Computational Biology) to make targeted mutations (Chapter 6 of Recombinant DNA Technology) at specific residues predicted to improve a desired property.
  • Directed evolution - a random approach generating many mutant variants (via random mutagenesis or gene shuffling) and screening or selecting for the desired improved property, without requiring detailed structural knowledge in advance.

Immobilization Techniques

Methods of Enzyme (or Whole-Cell) Immobilization
  • Adsorption - binding an enzyme non-covalently to a solid support surface.
  • Covalent attachment - chemically bonding the enzyme to a support, generally more stable than adsorption but risking some loss of activity from the bonding chemistry itself.
  • Entrapment - physically trapping the enzyme (or whole cells) within a porous gel or fibre matrix.
  • Encapsulation - enclosing the enzyme within a semi-permeable membrane capsule.

Design and Kinetics of Immobilized Enzyme Reactors

Remark - Diffusional Resistance and the Thiele Modulus

Immobilized enzymes allow easy separation from product (enabling reuse across many production batches) but introduce diffusional resistance - substrate must physically diffuse into the immobilization matrix to reach the enzyme, and product must diffuse back out - quantified by the Thiele modulus, which compares the rate of reaction to the rate of diffusion and indicates whether an immobilized enzyme reactor's overall performance is limited by intrinsic reaction kinetics or by mass transfer, directly informing bioreactor design (Chapter 4).
