5. Mass Transfer and Aeration

For any aerobic fermentation, delivering enough oxygen to a dense, actively respiring culture is frequently the single most challenging engineering problem - the focus of this chapter.

Oxygen Transfer

Definition - Oxygen Transfer Rate

Because oxygen has low aqueous solubility, it must be continuously transferred from gas bubbles into the liquid medium fast enough to match the culture's oxygen consumption rate - the oxygen transfer rate depends on the interfacial area between gas and liquid (increased by finer bubbles and more vigorous agitation) and the concentration gradient driving diffusion across that interface.

Aeration and Agitation

Aeration (sparging air or oxygen-enriched gas into the culture) and mechanical agitation (via an impeller) work together to maximize oxygen transfer - agitation breaks large bubbles into smaller ones with greater total surface area and keeps the culture well-mixed, preventing oxygen or nutrient gradients from forming within the bioreactor.

Fluid Rheology

Definition - Fluid Rheology in Fermentation

Rheology - the study of how a fluid deforms and flows - matters directly for bioprocessing because many fermentation broths, particularly those with filamentous organisms or high biomass/product concentration, become substantially more viscous (and often non-Newtonian, meaning their apparent viscosity changes with agitation intensity) as fermentation proceeds, directly reducing achievable oxygen transfer and requiring correspondingly greater agitation power as a fermentation progresses.

Effects of Dissolved Oxygen on the Process

Dissolved oxygen concentration is monitored and controlled in real time (Chapter 4), since insufficient oxygen can shift microbial metabolism toward less efficient anaerobic or fermentative pathways (Chapter 5 of Microbiology), reducing biomass or product yield, while these mass-transfer constraints are frequently the primary factor limiting how large a fermentation can practically be scaled up (Chapter 9).
