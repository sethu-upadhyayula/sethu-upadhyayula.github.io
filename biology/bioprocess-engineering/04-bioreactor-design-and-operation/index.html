4. Bioreactor Design and Operation

A bioreactor provides the controlled physical and chemical environment within which the microbial growth kinetics of Chapter 3 are actually realized at production scale - this chapter covers the major configurations and the sterilization step that must precede any run.

Bioreactor Configurations

Major Bioreactor Types
  • Stirred-tank (batch and fed-batch) bioreactors - the most common configuration, using mechanical agitation for mixing and aeration (Chapter 5).
  • Plug-flow bioreactors - culture moves continuously through a tubular reactor with minimal back-mixing, such that composition varies along the reactor's length rather than being uniform throughout, as in a well-mixed stirred tank.
  • Continuous stirred-tank bioreactors - operated as a chemostat (Chapter 3 of Microbiology) at industrial scale, maintaining steady-state conditions indefinitely.
  • Enzyme reactors - configured for a cell-free, immobilized-enzyme-catalyzed process (Chapter 7) rather than living cell growth.

Sterilization

Because an industrial fermentation must typically be dominated by a single desired organism, all media and equipment must be sterilized (Chapter 4 of Microbiology) before inoculation - most commonly via batch or continuous heat sterilization, engineered to reliably kill contaminating organisms, including resistant endospores, while minimizing thermal damage to heat-sensitive nutrients in the growth medium.

Process Monitoring and Control

Remark - Maintaining Optimal Conditions

Industrial bioreactors continuously monitor and control temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen (Chapter 5), and agitation rate, adjusting each in real time to keep the culture within the narrow range of conditions under which the organism's growth kinetics (Chapter 3) and product formation are optimal - deviations outside this range can substantially reduce yield or, in severe cases, cause culture failure.
