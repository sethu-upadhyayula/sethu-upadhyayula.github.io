3. Microbial Growth Kinetics in Bioprocesses

The qualitative growth curve introduced in Chapter 3 of Microbiology can be modelled quantitatively - a necessity for designing and predicting the performance of an industrial fermentation, the focus of this chapter.

The Monod Model

The Monod Equation

The Monod equation describes microbial specific growth rate as a function of limiting substrate concentration: $$\mu = \mu_{max}\frac{S}{K_s + S}$$ where $\mu_{max}$ is the maximum specific growth rate and $K_s$ is the substrate concentration at which growth rate is half-maximal - a functional form directly analogous to the Michaelis-Menten equation (Chapter 4 of Biochemistry), reflecting a similar underlying saturation kinetics.

Substrate Utilization and Yield

The relationship between substrate consumed and biomass (or product) produced is captured by a yield coefficient, allowing an engineer to predict, via the material balances of Chapter 1, how much substrate a fermentation will require to achieve a target biomass or product quantity.

Growth in Batch, Fed-Batch, and Continuous Systems

Cultivation Modes and Growth Kinetics
  • Batch - substrate is provided once at the start; the population passes through the full growth curve from Chapter 3 of Microbiology as substrate is progressively depleted.
  • Fed-batch - substrate is added incrementally during cultivation, allowing extended growth and higher final biomass/product concentration than a simple batch process while avoiding substrate levels high enough to inhibit growth.
  • Continuous - fresh medium is fed and culture is withdrawn continuously (as in a chemostat, Chapter 3 of Microbiology), allowing indefinite operation at a chosen, fixed growth rate set by the feed (dilution) rate.

Kinetics and Bioreactor Choice

The choice among these cultivation modes, and the corresponding bioreactor design (Chapter 4), depends substantially on the specific growth kinetics of the organism and process - for example, whether the desired product is produced during active growth (favouring continuous operation near $\mu_{max}$) or predominantly during a later, non-growth phase (often better suited to batch or fed-batch operation).
