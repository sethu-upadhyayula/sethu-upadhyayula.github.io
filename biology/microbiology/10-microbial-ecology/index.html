10. Microbial Ecology

This closing chapter zooms out from individual microorganisms to the communities they form and the planetary-scale processes those communities drive - arguably microbiology's largest-scale and most consequential contribution to biology as a whole.

Microbial Interactions

Microorganisms rarely live in isolation - they compete for resources, engage in mutualistic and symbiotic relationships (such as the legume-Rhizobium nitrogen-fixing symbiosis, Chapter 4 of Plant Biology, or the gut microbiome's relationship with its animal host, Chapter 9 of Animal Physiology), and, through quorum sensing (Chapter 4 of Cell Signaling), coordinate collective behaviours across an entire local population.

Biogeochemical Cycling

Microbial Roles in Nutrient Cycles
  • Carbon cycle - microbial decomposition returns carbon locked in organic matter back to the atmosphere as CO₂, while photosynthetic and chemolithotrophic microbes (Chapter 5) fix carbon in the first place.
  • Nitrogen cycle - nitrogen-fixing, nitrifying, and denitrifying bacteria and archaea (Chapter 2 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics) collectively interconvert the various chemical forms of nitrogen available to the rest of the biosphere.
  • Sulfur cycle - analogous to the nitrogen cycle, driven substantially by sulfur-oxidizing and sulfate-reducing microorganisms exploiting the chemolithotrophic and anaerobic respiration strategies of Chapter 5.

Soil Microorganisms and Plant Associations

Soil harbours an extraordinarily dense and diverse microbial community, much of it in direct association with plant roots - including both symbiotic partnerships (mycorrhizal fungi, Chapter 5 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics; nitrogen-fixing bacteria) and pathogenic relationships (Chapter 8 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding) - collectively exerting major influence over plant health and nutrient availability.

Bioremediation

Microorganisms capable of metabolizing pollutants - hydrocarbons, heavy metals, and various industrial contaminants - are exploited directly in bioremediation, an application covered further in Environmental & Marine Biotechnology.

Uncultivable Microorganisms and Metagenomics

Remark - The Great Plate Count Anomaly

The overwhelming majority of microbial species detected in environmental samples cannot currently be grown in pure culture (Chapter 1) under standard laboratory conditions - a longstanding puzzle known as the "great plate count anomaly." Metagenomics and metatranscriptomics (Chapter 6 of Genomics) sidestep this limitation entirely by sequencing DNA and RNA directly from the environment, and have consequently transformed microbial ecology from a field constrained by what could be cultured to one capable of surveying the full extent of microbial diversity and activity in a given environment.
