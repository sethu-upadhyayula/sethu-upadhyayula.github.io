1. Historical Perspective and Methods in Microbiology

Microbiology as a discipline emerged from a specific historical controversy - whether life could arise spontaneously from non-living matter - and the methodological innovations developed to resolve it remain foundational to the field today.

Discovery of the Microbial World

The invention of the microscope revealed an entire invisible world of microorganisms, but it took considerably longer to establish that these organisms were responsible for fermentation and disease, rather than being incidental to it. Resolving the spontaneous generation controversy - whether microorganisms arise spontaneously from non-living matter, or always from pre-existing microorganisms - required carefully controlled experiments (most famously Pasteur's swan-neck flask experiments) that definitively supported the latter.

The Role of Microorganisms in Disease and Decomposition

Establishing that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases (formalized in Koch's postulates) transformed medicine, while the recognition that microorganisms drive the decomposition and transformation of organic matter established microbiology's central role in ecology and biogeochemistry (Chapter 10).

Pure Culture Techniques

Definition - Pure Culture

A pure culture contains only a single microbial species or strain, essential for reliably studying any one organism's specific properties. Pure cultures are typically obtained by streaking a diluted sample across solid growth medium so that individual cells are physically separated and grow into isolated colonies, each presumed to be a clone of a single founding cell.

Microbial Nutrition and Enrichment Culture

Different microorganisms require different nutrients and growth conditions; enrichment culture exploits this by using a growth medium and conditions favouring a particular organism or metabolic type of interest, allowing that organism to be selectively isolated even from a complex natural sample containing many other species.

Detection and Diagnostic Methods

Beyond culturing, microorganisms are identified using antigen/antibody-based detection (Chapter 8 of Immunology), light/phase-contrast/fluorescence/electron microscopy, and molecular methods such as PCR and real-time PCR for direct genetic detection and quantification - the latter increasingly supplemented by the next-generation sequencing approaches covered in Chapters 3 and 6 of Genomics.
