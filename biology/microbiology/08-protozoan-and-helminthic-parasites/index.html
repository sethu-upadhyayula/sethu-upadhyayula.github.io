8. Protozoan and Helminthic Parasites

Beyond bacteria and viruses, a range of eukaryotic organisms - protozoa and helminths - have evolved parasitic lifestyles, presenting distinct challenges for both the host immune system and for medical treatment, given their closer evolutionary relationship to their animal hosts than bacteria or viruses.

The Nature of Parasitism

Definition - Host-Parasite Relationship

A parasite lives in or on a host organism and derives nutrients at the host's expense, typically without immediately killing it - a relationship that, over evolutionary time, often trends toward reduced virulence, since a parasite that kills its host too quickly or too reliably may limit its own opportunities for further transmission (Chapter 5 of Evolution's discussion of coevolution).

Protozoan Parasites

Several protozoan lineages (Chapter 4 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics) include medically important parasites - the causative agents of malaria (transmitted by mosquitoes, and undergoing a complex life cycle alternating between human and insect hosts), African sleeping sickness, and amoebic dysentery among them, each requiring immune evasion strategies (such as antigenic variation, in the case of the malaria parasite) sophisticated enough to persist despite an adaptive immune response (Immunology).

Helminthic Parasites

Example - Parasitic Worms

Helminths (parasitic worms, including flatworms and roundworms) are typically far larger than protozoan or microbial parasites, and often have complex life cycles involving one or more intermediate hosts before reaching their definitive host. Rather than evading the immune system entirely, many helminths persist by dampening or redirecting the host immune response - notably skewing it toward a distinct type of adaptive response less effective at directly eliminating the worm, but which also happens to underlie the allergic-type hypersensitivity reactions covered in Chapter 6 of Immunology.

Host-Parasite Immune Interactions

Chronic parasitic infections illustrate a distinct pattern from acute bacterial or viral infection - a prolonged standoff in which neither host clearance nor parasite success is complete, often for years, reflecting the coevolutionary "arms race" dynamic (Chapter 5 of Evolution) between host immune defenses and parasite immune evasion mechanisms.
