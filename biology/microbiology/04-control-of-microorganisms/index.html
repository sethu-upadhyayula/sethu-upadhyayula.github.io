4. Control of Microorganisms

Controlling microbial growth - whether to prevent food spoilage, sterilize medical equipment, or treat infection - depends on understanding exactly what different control methods do, and their limits.

Disinfection versus Sterilization

Definition - Disinfection and Sterilization

Disinfection reduces the number of viable microorganisms (particularly pathogens) to a level considered safe, but does not necessarily eliminate all microbial life, including resistant endospores (Chapter 2). Sterilization is the complete elimination or destruction of all forms of microbial life, including endospores - a more stringent standard required for surgical instruments and injectable pharmaceuticals.

Physical Methods of Control

Heat is the most widely used physical control method - moist heat (e.g., autoclaving under pressurized steam) is more effective than dry heat at a given temperature, since water facilitates the denaturation of proteins (Chapter 2 of Biochemistry) at lower temperatures. Other physical methods include filtration (physically removing microorganisms from a liquid or gas, essential for heat-sensitive solutions), and ionizing or UV radiation (damaging nucleic acids and proteins directly).

Chemical Methods of Control

Chemical disinfectants and antiseptics (the latter safe for use on living tissue) act through a variety of mechanisms - denaturing proteins, disrupting membranes, or oxidizing cellular components - with effectiveness depending on concentration, contact time, and the presence of organic material that can inactivate some disinfectants before they reach target microorganisms.

Assessing Efficacy

Remark - Validating Sterilization

Because sterilization failure can have severe consequences, its efficacy is routinely validated using biological indicators - standardized preparations of unusually resistant endospores that are exposed alongside the actual items being sterilized; if the endospores are killed, the process is validated as sufficient to have also killed any more vulnerable organism present.
