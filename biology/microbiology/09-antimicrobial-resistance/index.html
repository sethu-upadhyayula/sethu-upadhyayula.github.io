9. Antimicrobial Resistance

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) - a microorganism's acquired or inherent ability to survive exposure to a drug that would normally kill or inhibit it - has become one of the most pressing challenges in modern medicine, and is fundamentally an evolutionary problem.

The Evolutionary Basis of Resistance

Resistance as Natural Selection

Antibiotic exposure imposes intense natural selection (Chapter 3 of Population & Quantitative Genetics) on a microbial population - cells carrying a pre-existing resistance mutation or gene survive and reproduce, while susceptible cells die, rapidly shifting the population's genetic composition toward resistance, especially given bacteria's typically very short generation times (Chapter 3 of this subject) and, therefore, rapid rate of evolutionary response.

Sources of Resistance Genes

Resistance can arise by spontaneous mutation (Chapter 6 of Molecular Biology) altering a drug's target or increasing drug efflux, or be acquired ready-made from another organism via horizontal gene transfer - transformation, conjugation, or transduction (Chapter 6 of Genetics) - allowing resistance genes to spread not just vertically within a species but laterally across different, even distantly related, bacterial species.

Spread of Resistance

Remark - Resistance Genes on Plasmids

Resistance genes are frequently carried on plasmids or other mobile genetic elements (Chapter 6 of Cell Biology), which can transfer between bacterial cells independent of chromosomal inheritance - a single conjugation event (Chapter 6 of Genetics) can transfer resistance to several different antibiotics simultaneously if those genes are clustered on the same mobile element.

Drivers and Consequences of AMR

Widespread and often unnecessary antibiotic use - in medicine, agriculture, and animal husbandry - intensifies selection pressure for resistance across microbial populations far beyond any single treated patient, a central concern connecting antimicrobial resistance to the broader disease ecology and public health themes covered in Epidemiology & Public Health Biology.

Combating Resistance

Strategies to slow the spread of resistance include antibiotic stewardship (restricting use to situations where it is genuinely necessary), combination therapy (using multiple drugs simultaneously, making it statistically far less likely that a pathogen carries resistance to all of them at once), and ongoing surveillance to detect emerging resistance patterns before they become widespread.
