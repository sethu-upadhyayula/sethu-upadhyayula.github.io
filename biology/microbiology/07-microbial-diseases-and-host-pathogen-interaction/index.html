7. Microbial Diseases and Host-Pathogen Interaction

Infection is fundamentally a contest between a pathogen's strategies for invading and exploiting a host, and the host's defenses (Immunology) against it - this chapter covers the pathogen's side of that contest.

Classifying Infectious Disease

Key Epidemiological Terms
  • Normal microbiota - the resident microbial community of a healthy host (Chapter 9 of Animal Physiology), which can itself become pathogenic under altered conditions.
  • Reservoir - the natural habitat (human, animal, or environmental) in which a pathogen persists between episodes of disease.
  • Nosocomial infection - infection acquired within a healthcare setting.
  • Opportunistic infection - caused by an organism (often part of the normal microbiota) that only causes disease when host defenses are compromised.
  • Emerging infectious disease - a disease newly recognized or rapidly increasing in incidence or geographic range.

Recognition and Entry: How Pathogens Invade Host Cells

Recognition and entry into a host cell typically requires a specific interaction between a pathogen surface molecule and a host cell surface receptor - conceptually similar to a hormone-receptor interaction (Chapter 1 of Cell Signaling), but exploited by the pathogen for its own entry rather than for host signaling purposes. This recognition step often substantially determines a pathogen's host range and which host tissues it can infect.

Alteration of Host Cell Behaviour

Example - Virus-Induced Cell Transformation

Some pathogens actively reprogram host cell behaviour beyond simple invasion - certain viruses, for instance, can trigger cell transformation, disrupting normal cell-cycle control (Chapter 7 of Cell Biology) and contributing to cancer development (Chapter 5 of Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology's discussion of virus-induced cancer). Other pathogens trigger cell-cell fusion, either as part of their own entry mechanism or as an incidental effect of infection.

Pathogen-Induced Disease in Animals and Plants

The same basic principles of recognition, entry, and host manipulation apply across kingdoms - bacterial and viral pathogens of plants (Chapter 8 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding) face a fundamentally different host cell wall barrier (Chapter 1 of Cell Biology) than animal pathogens do, and have evolved correspondingly different entry strategies, despite facing conceptually parallel challenges.

Mechanisms of Microbial Pathogenicity

Beyond simple invasion, pathogenicity often depends on specific virulence factors - toxins, capsules (Chapter 2) that resist phagocytosis, enzymes that degrade host tissue barriers, and mechanisms for evading or actively suppressing the host immune response (Immunology) - the specific combination of which determines a given pathogen's characteristic disease presentation.
