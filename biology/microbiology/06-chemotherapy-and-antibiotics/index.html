6. Chemotherapy and Antibiotics

Antimicrobial chemotherapy exploits biochemical differences between pathogen and host to selectively harm the pathogen while sparing the patient - a principle called selective toxicity, central to every drug covered in this chapter.

General Characteristics of Antimicrobial Drugs

Definition - Selective Toxicity

An ideal antimicrobial drug exhibits selective toxicity - harming the pathogen far more than the host - typically by targeting a structure or process absent from host cells entirely (e.g., the bacterial cell wall, Chapter 1 of Cell Biology) or sufficiently different in the pathogen to allow selective drug binding (e.g., prokaryotic versus eukaryotic ribosomes, Chapter 4 of Molecular Biology).

Classes of Antibiotic and Mechanism of Action

Major Antibiotic Mechanisms
  • Cell wall synthesis inhibitors (e.g., penicillins) - disrupt peptidoglycan synthesis (Chapter 1 of Cell Biology), effective only against actively dividing cells and inherently selective, since animal cells lack a cell wall entirely.
  • Protein synthesis inhibitors (e.g., tetracyclines, chloramphenicol) - exploit structural differences between bacterial and eukaryotic ribosomes (Chapter 4 of Molecular Biology).
  • Nucleic acid synthesis inhibitors (e.g., fluoroquinolones) - target bacterial DNA replication enzymes not shared with the host.
  • Membrane-disrupting agents - exploit differences in membrane composition between microorganisms and host cells.

Molecular Mechanisms of Resistance

Bacteria evade antibiotics through several molecular strategies: enzymatic drug inactivation (e.g., beta-lactamase enzymes that destroy penicillin-class antibiotics before they can act), target modification (altering the drug's binding site so it no longer binds effectively), reduced membrane permeability, and active efflux pumps that export the drug from the cell before it can accumulate to an effective concentration.

Antifungal and Antiviral Drugs

Antifungal drugs face an inherently harder selectivity problem than antibacterials, since fungi are eukaryotic like the host (Chapter 5 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics), leaving fewer uniquely fungal targets (e.g., the fungal-specific membrane sterol ergosterol, or the fungal cell wall's chitin). Antiviral drugs similarly must exploit the comparatively few viral-specific enzymes (e.g., viral polymerases or proteases) given how completely viruses depend on host cell machinery for most of their replication (Chapter 3 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics).
