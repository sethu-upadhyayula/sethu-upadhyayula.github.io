7. The Digestive System and Energy Balance

The digestive system breaks down ingested food into absorbable components, supplying the raw material and energy for every other physiological process covered in this subject.

Digestion

Definition - Digestion

The mechanical and enzymatic breakdown of complex food macromolecules (carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids, Chapter 1 of Biochemistry) into simpler absorbable units, carried out by digestive enzymes secreted along the gastrointestinal tract and, in many herbivores, assisted substantially by symbiotic gut microorganisms capable of breaking down cellulose (Chapter 1 of Biochemistry) that host enzymes alone cannot digest.

Absorption

Digested nutrients are absorbed predominantly across the small intestine's highly folded, villus-covered surface - a structural adaptation that dramatically increases absorptive surface area - using both passive diffusion and active transport mechanisms (Chapter 2 of Cell Biology) depending on the specific nutrient.

Energy Balance and Basal Metabolic Rate

Definition - Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the minimal rate of energy expenditure required to sustain essential physiological function at rest, and scales with body size following well-established allometric relationships across animal groups. Energy balance - intake versus total expenditure (BMR plus activity plus, in endotherms, thermoregulation, Chapter 6) - determines whether an animal gains, loses, or maintains body mass over time.

Hormonal Regulation of Feeding and Energy Balance

Feeding behaviour and energy balance are regulated by a network of hormonal signals (Chapter 8) originating from the digestive tract, adipose tissue, and pancreas, feeding back to hypothalamic centres that adjust hunger, satiety, and metabolic rate - a system covered further, in the context of metabolic disease, in Chapter 9's discussion of interorgan communication.
