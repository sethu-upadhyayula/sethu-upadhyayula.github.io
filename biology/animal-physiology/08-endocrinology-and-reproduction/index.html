8. Endocrinology and Reproduction

The endocrine system provides the body's slower, longer-lasting complement to fast neural signaling, using hormones distributed through the bloodstream to coordinate physiology across distant organs - including, as this chapter covers, the reproductive system.

Endocrine Glands and Hormone Action

The major endocrine glands - pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas, and gonads among them - secrete hormones that act on distant target tissues bearing the appropriate receptor, using the same basic receptor and signal transduction logic covered in Chapters 1–2 of Cell Signaling, whether the hormone is water-soluble (acting via a surface receptor) or lipid-soluble (often acting via an intracellular receptor directly regulating gene expression).

Hormones and Disease

Example - Endocrine Disorders

Endocrine disease typically arises from a gland producing too much or too little hormone (e.g., hyperthyroidism versus hypothyroidism), or from target-tissue resistance to a hormone that is present at normal or even elevated levels (as in type 2 diabetes, where tissues become resistant to insulin's normal signaling effects, Chapter 8 of Biochemistry).

Gametogenesis and Ovulation

Gametogenesis itself was introduced in Chapter 2 of Developmental Biology; this chapter focuses on its whole-body hormonal control. In females, a cyclic interplay of hypothalamic, pituitary, and ovarian hormones drives the maturation and release of an egg (ovulation) on a regular cycle, while in males, a comparable hormonal axis supports continuous sperm production.

Neuroendocrine Regulation

Definition - The Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

Reproductive (and many other endocrine) hormones are ultimately governed by the hypothalamic-pituitary axis - the hypothalamus, part of the brain, secretes releasing hormones that control pituitary hormone secretion, which in turn controls hormone output from peripheral glands such as the gonads. This arrangement, a direct interface between the nervous system (Chapter 4) and endocrine system, allows reproductive physiology to be modulated by external and internal cues, such as day length, stress, and nutritional status.
