3. The Respiratory System

Every animal must exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide with its environment, but the anatomical solution varies enormously depending on whether that environment is water or air.

Comparative Respiratory Anatomy

Gas Exchange Structures Across Animal Groups
  • Gills - used by most aquatic animals, maximizing surface area for gas exchange with water, often arranged for countercurrent exchange to maximize oxygen extraction from water's relatively low oxygen content.
  • Tracheal systems - used by insects, a network of air-filled tubes delivering oxygen directly to tissues without relying on the circulatory system for gas transport.
  • Lungs - used by most terrestrial vertebrates, providing an internal, moisture-protected respiratory surface; bird lungs are unusual in supporting unidirectional, rather than tidal, airflow, aided by a system of air sacs, achieving unusually efficient gas exchange that supports the high metabolic demands of powered flight.

Transport and Exchange of Gases

Oxygen is transported in blood predominantly bound to haemoglobin (Chapter 1), while carbon dioxide is transported partly dissolved in plasma, partly bound to haemoglobin, and largely as bicarbonate ion following its conversion by the enzyme carbonic anhydrase - a reaction that also contributes significantly to blood pH buffering (Chapter 1 of Biochemistry).

Neural and Chemical Regulation of Respiration

Definition - Chemoreceptor Control of Breathing

Breathing rate and depth are regulated by a respiratory control centre in the brainstem, adjusted primarily in response to blood CO₂ levels (detected via pH-sensitive central chemoreceptors) and, to a lesser degree under normal conditions, blood oxygen levels (detected via peripheral chemoreceptors) - meaning the drive to breathe is normally governed more by the need to expel CO₂ than by the need to acquire O₂.

Waste Elimination

The respiratory system serves as a major route of waste elimination for the volatile end-product CO₂, complementing the excretory system's role (Chapter 5) in eliminating non-volatile metabolic wastes such as nitrogenous compounds.
