6. Thermoregulation and Stress Adaptation

Maintaining an appropriate internal body temperature, and coping with physiological stress more generally, both draw on many of the same regulatory systems already introduced elsewhere in this subject.

Endotherms and Ectotherms

Definition - Endothermy and Ectothermy

Endotherms (birds and mammals) generate substantial metabolic heat internally and actively regulate body temperature within a narrow range, at high energetic cost. Ectotherms (most other animals) rely predominantly on external heat sources and behavioural strategies (e.g., basking, seeking shade) to regulate body temperature, at much lower energetic cost but with body temperature - and metabolic rate - more directly tied to ambient conditions.

Mechanisms of Thermoregulation

Within a "comfort zone," an endotherm's metabolic heat production alone maintains body temperature; outside this zone, physical mechanisms (adjusting blood flow to the skin, altering insulation), chemical mechanisms (adjusting metabolic heat production, including via uncoupling proteins, Chapter 5 of Biochemistry), and behavioural mechanisms all contribute, under neural control from a hypothalamic thermoregulatory centre.

Acclimatization

Example - Physiological Acclimatization

Acclimatization refers to reversible physiological adjustments made over days to weeks of exposure to a new environment - for example, increased red blood cell production and altered breathing patterns following relocation to high altitude, improving oxygen delivery despite lower ambient oxygen availability, without requiring any genetic change.

Physiological Stress Response

Beyond temperature specifically, animals mount a broader physiological stress response to a wide range of challenges (injury, predation risk, extreme exertion), coordinated substantially through the endocrine system (Chapter 8) - most notably the release of stress hormones that mobilize energy reserves and temporarily prioritize functions needed for immediate survival over longer-term processes such as digestion and growth.
