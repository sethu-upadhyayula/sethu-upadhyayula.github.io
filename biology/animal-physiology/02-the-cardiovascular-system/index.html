2. The Cardiovascular System

The cardiovascular system pairs a muscular pump - the heart - with a closed network of vessels, driving the continuous circulation of blood throughout the body.

Comparative Heart Structure

Heart structure varies systematically across vertebrate groups, tracking increasing separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood - from the two-chambered fish heart, through the incompletely separated three- or four-chambered hearts of amphibians and most reptiles, to the fully separated four-chambered hearts of birds and mammals, which allow fully independent pulmonary and systemic circuits.

The Myogenic Heart

Definition - Myogenic Heart

Unlike skeletal muscle, the vertebrate heart is myogenic - it generates its own rhythmic contraction intrinsically, from specialized pacemaker tissue (the sinoatrial node in mammals), rather than requiring an external nervous stimulus to initiate each beat. Specialized conducting tissue (e.g., the bundle of His and Purkinje fibres) then coordinates the spread of this signal to produce an efficient, sequential contraction.

The Cardiac Cycle and the Heart as a Pump

The cardiac cycle - alternating systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation) - moves blood through the heart's chambers and out into circulation, with one-way valves ensuring blood flows in a single direction. Cardiac output, the volume pumped per minute, is the product of heart rate and stroke volume, and is adjusted continuously to meet the body's changing metabolic demands.

The Electrocardiogram (ECG)

Definition - ECG

The ECG records the heart's electrical activity from the body surface, its characteristic waveform (P, QRS, and T waves) corresponding to sequential depolarization and repolarization of the atria and ventricles - a widely used, non-invasive diagnostic window onto both cardiac rhythm and structural abnormalities.

Regulation of Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is regulated by both fast-acting neural reflexes (via baroreceptors detecting pressure changes and adjusting heart rate and vessel diameter) and slower-acting chemical/hormonal mechanisms (Chapter 8's discussion of endocrine regulation), working together to maintain adequate perfusion of all tissues despite changes in posture, activity, and blood volume.
