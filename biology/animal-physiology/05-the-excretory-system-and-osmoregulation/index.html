5. The Excretory System and Osmoregulation

Every animal must eliminate metabolic waste while carefully regulating its internal water and ion balance - a set of tasks centred, in vertebrates, on the kidney.

Comparative Physiology of Excretion

Excretory strategies vary with an animal's environment and evolutionary history - freshwater animals must actively excrete excess water while retaining ions, marine animals often face the opposite challenge, and terrestrial animals must conserve water above all else, reflected in the differing nitrogenous waste products favoured by each group (ammonia, easily excreted but toxic and requiring abundant water to dilute, versus urea or uric acid, more energetically costly to produce but far less water-intensive to excrete).

The Kidney and Urine Formation

Definition - The Nephron

The functional unit of the vertebrate kidney, the nephron, forms urine through three sequential processes: filtration (blood plasma is filtered under pressure into the nephron, excluding cells and large proteins), reabsorption (most filtered water, ions, and nutrients are recovered back into the blood), and secretion (additional waste products are actively added to the forming urine) - together allowing highly selective control over what is ultimately excreted.

Urine Concentration

The mammalian kidney can produce urine considerably more concentrated than blood plasma, achieved through a countercurrent multiplier mechanism in the loop of Henle that establishes a strong osmotic gradient in the surrounding kidney tissue, against which water can then be drawn out of the collecting duct under hormonal control.

Regulation of Water and Electrolyte Balance

Remark - Hormonal Control of Kidney Function

Kidney function is under tight hormonal control (Chapter 8) - antidiuretic hormone increases water reabsorption in response to dehydration or low blood volume, while the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system responds to low blood pressure by promoting sodium (and, osmotically, water) reabsorption - together maintaining stable blood volume, blood pressure, and electrolyte balance despite highly variable fluid and salt intake.

Acid-Base Balance and Micturition

The kidney also contributes, alongside the respiratory system (Chapter 3), to regulating blood pH by selectively excreting or retaining acidic and basic substances, while micturition (urination) itself is coordinated by both autonomic reflexes and voluntary neural control over the bladder and urethral sphincters.
