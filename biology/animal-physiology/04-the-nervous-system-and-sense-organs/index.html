4. The Nervous System and Sense Organs

The nervous system provides an animal's fastest system of internal communication and its interface to the external world, built from the neurons and synaptic mechanisms already introduced in earlier subjects.

Gross Neuroanatomy

The vertebrate nervous system is divided into the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and the peripheral nervous system (the nerves connecting the CNS to the rest of the body, further divided into somatic and autonomic divisions). The brain itself is organized into regions with broadly conserved functions across vertebrates - a hindbrain governing basic autonomic functions, a midbrain relaying sensory information, and a forebrain (including, in mammals, an especially expanded cortex) responsible for higher-order processing.

Neurons and Action Potentials

The neuron's action potential (Chapter 2 of Cell Biology) and synaptic transmission (Chapter 3 of Cell Signaling) provide the electrical and chemical basis for all nervous system function covered in this chapter - from simple reflex arcs to the integration of complex sensory information.

Neural Control of Muscle Tone and Posture

Maintaining posture and muscle tone requires continuous, largely unconscious neural feedback - stretch receptors within muscles report their length to the spinal cord and brain, which adjust motor output to resist unwanted stretching or maintain a desired position, a foundational example of a neural feedback control loop.

Sense Organs

Vision, Hearing, and Tactile Senses
  • Vision - the vertebrate eye focuses light onto the retina, where photoreceptor cells (rods for low-light, achromatic vision; cones for color vision in brighter light) convert light into neural signals via a light-sensitive pigment.
  • Hearing - the cochlea of the inner ear converts sound-induced mechanical vibrations into neural signals via specialized hair cells, with different regions of the cochlea tuned to different sound frequencies.
  • Tactile responses - a variety of mechanoreceptors in the skin detect touch, pressure, vibration, and temperature, each tuned to a particular type and timescale of mechanical or thermal stimulus.
