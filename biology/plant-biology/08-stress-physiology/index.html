8. Stress Physiology

As organisms that cannot flee unfavorable conditions, plants have evolved an extensive physiological and molecular toolkit for tolerating environmental stress - the subject of this closing chapter.

Abiotic Stress

Major Categories of Abiotic Stress
  • Drought stress - triggers stomatal closure (Chapter 6) via abscisic acid signaling (Chapter 5) and osmotic adjustment to maintain cell turgor under water deficit.
  • Salinity stress - requires managing both osmotic imbalance and the direct ionic toxicity of excess salt, often through compartmentalizing ions away from metabolically sensitive parts of the cell.
  • Freezing and heat stress - require distinct protective responses, including production of protective solutes and heat-shock/cold-shock proteins that stabilize cellular macromolecules (Chapter 2 of Biochemistry) against temperature-induced damage.
  • Metal toxicity - plants deploy chelating compounds and specialized transporters to sequester toxic metal ions, often into the vacuole (Chapter 4 of Cell Biology).

The Central Role of Abscisic Acid

Abscisic acid (Chapter 5) acts as the central integrating hormone across most abiotic stress responses, triggering stomatal closure, activating stress-responsive gene expression, and promoting the accumulation of protective osmolytes - making it the plant physiological analogue of a generalized "stress hormone."

Plant Innate Immunity

Remark - Plant Innate Immunity

Lacking an adaptive immune system comparable to animals' (Immunology), plants rely entirely on innate immune mechanisms - cell-surface and intracellular receptors that detect conserved microbial molecules or pathogen-derived effector proteins, triggering localized defense responses mediated substantially through the salicylic acid and jasmonic acid signaling pathways introduced in Chapter 5, and covered further alongside specific plant diseases in Chapter 8 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding.

Molecular Responses to Stress

At the molecular level, both abiotic and biotic stress responses converge on large-scale, coordinated shifts in gene expression (Chapter 5 of Molecular Biology), often orchestrated by stress-specific transcription factors - allowing a plant to redeploy much of its metabolism toward tolerance and survival for as long as the stress persists.
