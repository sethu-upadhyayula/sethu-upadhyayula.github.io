3. Water and Solute Transport

Moving water from soil to the top of a tall tree, against gravity and without a pump, is one of plant physiology's most elegant engineering solutions - this chapter covers how it, and the related transport of nutrients and sugars, actually works.

Water Uptake and the Cohesion-Tension Mechanism

The Cohesion-Tension Theory

Water evaporating from leaf mesophyll cells during transpiration creates negative pressure (tension) that pulls water upward through the continuous xylem column, held together by the strong cohesive hydrogen bonding between water molecules (Chapter 1 of Biochemistry) - allowing water transport purely through physical forces, without any cellular energy expenditure along the xylem pathway itself.

Apoplastic and Symplastic Transport

Definition - Apoplast and Symplast

Water and solutes can move through a root via the apoplast (the continuous space of cell walls and intercellular spaces, without crossing any membrane) or the symplast (from cell to cell through cytoplasm, connected via plasmodesmata). Apoplastic movement is interrupted at the endodermis by the Casparian strip (Chapter 1), forcing water to cross into the symplast - and through a living, selectively permeable membrane - before entering the vascular cylinder.

Ion and Macromolecule Transport

Mineral ion uptake from soil often requires active transport against a concentration gradient (Chapter 2 of Cell Biology), using membrane transporters embedded in root epidermal and cortical cells, while some larger molecules move through the plant via the symplastic route through plasmodesmata rather than by crossing membranes individually.

Phloem Loading and Unloading of Photoassimilates

Definition - Pressure-Flow Hypothesis

Sugars produced by photosynthesis (Chapter 8 of Biochemistry) are actively loaded into phloem sieve elements at a "source" (e.g., a mature leaf), raising the local solute concentration and drawing water in osmotically; the resulting pressure drives bulk flow through the phloem toward a "sink" (e.g., a root or developing fruit), where sugars are unloaded and osmotic pressure correspondingly drops - a pressure-driven mechanism, distinct from the tension-driven mechanism of xylem transport.
