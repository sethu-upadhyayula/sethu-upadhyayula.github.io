5. Plant Hormones

Plant growth and development are coordinated by a small set of chemical signals - plant hormones - that act at very low concentrations across the whole plant body, referenced throughout Plant Biology and Developmental Biology and gathered together here.

The Major Plant Hormone Classes

Overview of Plant Hormones
  • Auxin - promotes cell elongation, and directs phyllotaxy (Chapter 6 of Developmental Biology) and tropic growth responses toward light and gravity.
  • Cytokinins - promote cell division, typically acting antagonistically with auxin to regulate the balance of root versus shoot growth.
  • Gibberellins - promote stem elongation and are central to breaking seed and bud dormancy.
  • Abscisic acid (ABA) - the primary stress hormone, promoting stomatal closure (Chapter 3) and seed dormancy, and mediating responses to drought and other abiotic stresses (Chapter 8).
  • Ethylene - a gaseous hormone that promotes fruit ripening, leaf and flower senescence, and the "triple response" seedling growth pattern.
  • Brassinosteroids - steroid hormones promoting cell elongation and division, with roles overlapping and interacting with auxin signaling.
  • Salicylic acid and jasmonic acid - primarily mediate biotic stress and defense responses (Chapter 8 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding's discussion of plant pathology).
  • Strigolactones - regulate shoot branching and also function as signaling molecules in below-ground interactions with mycorrhizal fungi and, less beneficially, parasitic plants.

Mechanisms of Hormone Action

Plant hormones act through cell-surface or intracellular receptors that trigger downstream signal transduction cascades (Chapters 1–2 of Cell Signaling), frequently converging on changes in gene expression - for example, auxin signaling operates substantially by triggering the targeted degradation of transcriptional repressors, de-repressing auxin-responsive genes.

Hormone Transport and Crosstalk

Several hormones, most notably auxin, are actively and directionally transported cell-to-cell by dedicated efflux and influx carriers, creating the local concentration gradients (Chapter 1 of Developmental Biology) that pattern processes such as phyllotaxy and root branching. Hormone pathways also extensively "crosstalk" with one another, such that a plant's ultimate developmental response to any single signal typically reflects the combined output of several interacting hormone pathways rather than one hormone acting in isolation.
