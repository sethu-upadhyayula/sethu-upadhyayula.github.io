4. Nitrogen Metabolism in Plants

Nitrogen is frequently the limiting nutrient for plant growth. This chapter covers how plants take up inorganic nitrogen from the soil and assimilate it into organic molecules - complementing the broader biosynthetic pathways covered in Chapter 7 of Biochemistry.

Nitrate and Ammonium Assimilation

Definition - Nitrate Assimilation

Plants take up nitrogen from soil predominantly as nitrate ($NO_3^-$) or ammonium ($NH_4^+$). Nitrate must first be reduced - via nitrate reductase and then nitrite reductase - to ammonium before it can be incorporated into organic compounds, an energetically costly process typically occurring in roots or leaves depending on species and nitrate supply.

Amino Acid Biosynthesis from Assimilated Nitrogen

Once available as ammonium, nitrogen is incorporated into glutamate and glutamine via the same glutamine synthetase/glutamate synthase (GS-GOGAT) system used across the tree of life (Chapter 7 of Biochemistry), from which it is subsequently distributed to other amino acids by transamination.

Biological Nitrogen Fixation and Plant Symbioses

Example - Legume-Rhizobium Symbiosis

Legumes form a specialized symbiosis with nitrogen-fixing Rhizobium bacteria (Chapter 7 of Biochemistry), housing them within specialized root nodules where the plant supplies carbon and a protective, low-oxygen environment (via leghemoglobin), and the bacteria supply fixed nitrogen in return - a mutualism of major agricultural importance, since it substantially reduces a legume crop's need for external nitrogen fertilizer.

Nitrogen Remobilization

Plants can remobilize nitrogen from older, senescing tissues (Chapter 8 of Cell Biology) to actively growing tissues or developing seeds, a process of particular agricultural significance for determining how efficiently a crop converts absorbed soil nitrogen into harvestable grain protein.
