2. Vascular Tissue and Secondary Growth

Vascular plants owe their name to a specialized long-distance transport tissue system - xylem and phloem - whose structure this chapter covers, along with the process by which some plants add substantial girth year after year.

Xylem Structure

Definition - Xylem

Xylem transports water and dissolved minerals from root to shoot, built from dead, hollow, lignin-reinforced cells (tracheids and, in flowering plants, the wider and more efficient vessel elements) that form continuous, pressure-tolerant conduits - their vascular function possible precisely because the cells are dead at maturity, offering no resistance from living cytoplasm.

Phloem Structure

Phloem transports sugars and other organic molecules (photoassimilates, Chapter 3) from sites of production or storage to sites of use, through living sieve-tube elements connected end to end via perforated sieve plates, assisted by adjacent companion cells that support the sieve elements' metabolism (which lack their own nucleus at maturity).

Ontogeny of the Vascular System

Both xylem and phloem originate from procambium during primary growth, and - in plants capable of secondary growth - from vascular cambium thereafter, tracing a developmental lineage from the shoot and root apical meristems covered in Chapter 6 of Developmental Biology.

Secondary Growth and Wood Anatomy

Definition - Secondary Growth

Secondary growth is growth in stem or root diameter, driven by two lateral meristems: the vascular cambium, which produces secondary xylem (wood) inward and secondary phloem outward, and the cork cambium, which produces the protective outer bark. Seasonal variation in cell size and wall thickness produces the annual growth rings visible in temperate woody plants, a record of a tree's yearly growth history.
