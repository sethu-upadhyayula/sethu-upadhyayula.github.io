7. Secondary Metabolites

Beyond the primary metabolic pathways shared broadly across life (Chapters 6–7 of Biochemistry), plants produce an enormous diversity of "secondary" metabolites - compounds not directly required for basic growth and reproduction, but often crucial for ecological interactions and defense.

Major Classes of Secondary Metabolite

Overview of Secondary Metabolite Classes
  • Terpenes - built from repeating five-carbon isoprene units; include essential oils, resins, and many plant defense and signaling compounds (including some plant hormones, Chapter 5).
  • Phenolics and phenylpropanoids - a large class including flavonoids (pigments and UV protectants), lignin (structural strengthening, Chapter 2), and tannins (deterring herbivory).
  • Alkaloids - nitrogen-containing compounds (derived from amino acid metabolism, Chapter 4) often with potent biological activity in animals, including many medicinally and economically important compounds.
  • Other nitrogenous compounds - including cyanogenic glycosides and glucosinolates, which release toxic compounds upon tissue damage as a defense mechanism.

Ecological Roles

Secondary metabolites mediate many of a plant's interactions with other organisms - deterring or poisoning herbivores and pathogens, attracting pollinators and seed dispersers through scent, color, and reward, and, in some cases, suppressing the growth of competing plants (allelopathy) - extending the coevolutionary dynamics discussed in Chapter 5 of Evolution into plant chemistry specifically.

Metabolic Engineering

Remark - Engineering Secondary Metabolism

Because many secondary metabolites have direct pharmaceutical, flavor, or fragrance value, metabolic engineering - introducing or modifying the enzymes of a secondary metabolite pathway, often using the recombinant DNA techniques of Recombinant DNA Technology - is an active area of plant biotechnology, aiming to increase yield of a valuable compound or produce it in an easier-to-cultivate host organism entirely.
