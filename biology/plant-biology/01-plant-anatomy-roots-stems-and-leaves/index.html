1. Plant Anatomy: Roots, Stems and Leaves

Vascular plants are built from three basic vegetative organs - root, stem, and leaf - each with a distinctive internal tissue organization suited to its specific role.

Plant Tissue Systems

The Three Tissue Systems
  • Dermal tissue - the outer epidermis, often covered by a waxy cuticle that limits water loss, and studded with stomata (Chapter 3) in leaves and green stems.
  • Ground tissue - parenchyma (metabolically active, thin-walled), collenchyma (flexible structural support), and sclerenchyma (rigid, often dead at maturity, providing structural strength).
  • Vascular tissue - xylem and phloem, covered in Chapter 2, transporting water and photosynthate respectively.

Root Anatomy

In cross-section, a typical root shows an outer epidermis (often bearing root hairs that greatly increase surface area for absorption), a cortex of ground tissue, an endodermis with a specialized waxy band (the Casparian strip) that forces water to cross a selectively permeable membrane before entering the vascular tissue, and a central vascular cylinder (stele).

Stem Anatomy

Stem vascular tissue is arranged differently between the two major flowering plant lineages: scattered vascular bundles throughout the ground tissue in monocots, versus a single ring of vascular bundles in dicots - an anatomical distinction with direct consequences for whether secondary growth (Chapter 2) is possible.

Leaf Anatomy

Definition - Mesophyll

The photosynthetic ground tissue of a leaf, the mesophyll, is often differentiated into an upper palisade layer (tightly packed, chloroplast-rich cells oriented for efficient light capture) and a lower spongy layer (loosely packed, with large air spaces facilitating gas exchange, Chapter 8 of Biochemistry) leading to the stomata on the leaf's lower surface.

Floral Organs, Embryo, and Seedling Anatomy

The same three tissue systems extend into floral organs and the embryo itself (Chapter 4 of Developmental Biology), and reorganize substantially during germination as the young seedling establishes its first true root and shoot systems from the tissues laid down within the seed.
