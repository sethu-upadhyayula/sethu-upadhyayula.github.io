6. Cancer Cell Biology and Therapeutic Interventions

Beyond the initiating genetic mutations, cancer's clinical danger comes from how transformed cells behave - evading normal growth controls, invading surrounding tissue, and eventually spreading throughout the body.

Cancer and the Cell Cycle

Cancer cells characteristically bypass the cell-cycle checkpoints described in Chapter 7 of Cell Biology, continuing to divide despite DNA damage or absent growth signals. Many cancers also achieve unlimited replicative potential by reactivating telomerase, avoiding the progressive telomere shortening that normally limits a healthy cell's number of divisions.

Interaction with Normal Cells

Tumors are not purely autonomous - they actively recruit blood vessel growth (angiogenesis) to sustain their expanding metabolic needs, and interact with surrounding normal and immune cells within the tumor microenvironment, often suppressing local immune responses that would otherwise target transformed cells for destruction.

Metastasis

Definition - Metastasis

The process by which cancer cells detach from the primary tumor, invade surrounding tissue and blood or lymphatic vessels, survive circulation, and establish secondary tumors at distant sites. This requires loss of normal cell-adhesion controls (Chapter 3) and degradation of the extracellular matrix, and is responsible for the great majority of cancer deaths.

Therapeutic Interventions

Strategies for Targeting Uncontrolled Growth
  • Cytotoxic chemotherapy - broadly targets rapidly dividing cells (e.g., by damaging DNA or blocking mitosis), affecting normal proliferating tissue alongside tumor cells.
  • Targeted therapy - inhibits a specific oncogenic protein (e.g., small-molecule kinase inhibitors against constitutively active receptor tyrosine kinases).
  • Immunotherapy - restores or enhances the immune system's own ability to recognize and destroy tumor cells (e.g., checkpoint inhibitors, engineered CAR-T cells), connecting directly to the adaptive immune mechanisms covered in Immunology.
