2. G-Protein Coupled Receptors and Second Messengers

G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs) are the most common receptor family in animal genomes, mediating responses to signals as varied as hormones, neurotransmitters, odorants, and light.

The GPCR Signaling Cycle

Definition - Heterotrimeric G-Protein

A GPCR is a seven-transmembrane-helix protein coupled to an intracellular heterotrimeric G-protein (Gα, Gβ, Gγ subunits). Ligand binding causes the receptor to act as a guanine-nucleotide exchange factor, prompting Gα to exchange GDP for GTP and dissociate from Gβγ; both the GTP-bound Gα and the freed Gβγ can then regulate downstream effector enzymes, until Gα's intrinsic GTPase activity hydrolyzes GTP back to GDP, terminating the signal.

The cAMP Pathway

Stimulatory Gα subunits (Gαs) activate adenylyl cyclase, raising intracellular cAMP, which activates protein kinase A (PKA) to phosphorylate downstream targets; inhibitory Gα subunits (Gαi) instead suppress adenylyl cyclase. This pathway mediates responses to hormones such as adrenaline and glucagon, including the glycogen-mobilizing cascade described in Chapter 6 of Biochemistry.

The IP₃/DAG/Calcium Pathway

Definition - Phospholipase C Pathway

Other Gα subunits (Gαq) activate phospholipase C, which cleaves a membrane phospholipid into two second messengers: IP₃, which diffuses to the endoplasmic reticulum and triggers release of stored Ca²⁺, and DAG, which remains in the membrane and activates protein kinase C (PKC). Released Ca²⁺ itself acts as a versatile second messenger, often via the calcium-binding protein calmodulin.

Remark - Receptor-Ligand Interactions

The strength and specificity of receptor-ligand binding, described by an equilibrium dissociation constant analogous to the enzyme-substrate $K_m$ (Chapter 4 of Biochemistry), determines a signaling system's sensitivity - receptors with very high affinity can detect vanishingly low ligand concentrations.
