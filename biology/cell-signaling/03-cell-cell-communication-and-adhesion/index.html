3. Cell-Cell Communication and Adhesion

Beyond diffusible signals, cells also communicate through direct physical contact and connection - mechanisms that simultaneously provide structural cohesion to tissues and pathways for information exchange.

Cell Adhesion Molecules

Major Classes of Adhesion Molecule
  • Cadherins - calcium-dependent, mediate adhesion between similar cell types, and anchor to the actin cytoskeleton at adherens junctions.
  • Integrins - link the cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix, and also transmit signals bidirectionally across the membrane ("inside-out" and "outside-in" signaling).
  • Selectins and Ig-superfamily CAMs - mediate transient adhesion, notably in immune cell trafficking (Chapter 4 of Immunology).

Gap Junctions

Gap junctions, built from connexin proteins arranged into channels called connexons, directly connect the cytoplasm of adjacent cells, allowing ions and small molecules (including some second messengers) to pass between cells without crossing the extracellular space - electrically coupling cardiac muscle cells, for instance, to synchronize heartbeat.

The Extracellular Matrix

The extracellular matrix (ECM) is a structural meshwork of secreted proteins and polysaccharides - collagen, fibronectin, laminin, and proteoglycans - that provides mechanical support to tissues, and is far from inert: it presents binding sites that integrins detect, actively regulating cell shape, migration, and gene expression.

Neurotransmission

Definition - Chemical Synapse

At a chemical synapse, an arriving action potential (Chapter 2 of Cell Biology) triggers calcium influx and fusion of synaptic vesicles, releasing neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft. The neurotransmitter binds postsynaptic receptors - either ligand-gated ion channels for fast signaling, or GPCRs for slower, modulatory signaling - before being cleared by reuptake or enzymatic degradation to terminate the signal.
