4. Signaling in Bacteria and Plants

Signal transduction is not exclusive to animal cells. Bacteria and plants have evolved their own distinctive signaling architectures, adapted to their particular sensory needs.

Bacterial Two-Component Systems

Definition - Two-Component System

The dominant bacterial signaling mechanism, consisting of a membrane-bound sensor histidine kinase, which autophosphorylates in response to an environmental stimulus, and a cytoplasmic response regulator, to which the phosphate group is transferred, activating it (often as a transcription factor). Plants also use two-component-like systems, for example in some hormone signaling pathways.

Bacterial Chemotaxis

Bacteria such as E. coli bias their otherwise random swimming toward favorable chemical gradients (chemotaxis) by using methyl-accepting chemoreceptor proteins coupled to a two-component-like signaling pathway that controls the direction of flagellar rotation - smooth swimming ("running") when conditions are improving, and reorientation ("tumbling") when they are not.

Quorum Sensing

Definition - Quorum Sensing

Bacteria continuously secrete and detect small signaling molecules (autoinducers); once population density - and hence local autoinducer concentration - crosses a threshold, cells collectively switch on density-dependent behaviours such as bioluminescence, virulence factor production, or biofilm formation, effectively allowing a bacterial population to coordinate gene expression as if it were a single multicellular organism.

Light Signaling in Plants

Plants use dedicated photoreceptors - phytochromes (red/far-red light), cryptochromes, and phototropins (both blue light) - to sense light quality, direction, and duration, triggering responses from seed germination and stem elongation to stomatal opening and the timing of flowering (Chapter 6 of Plant Biology covers this in more depth).
