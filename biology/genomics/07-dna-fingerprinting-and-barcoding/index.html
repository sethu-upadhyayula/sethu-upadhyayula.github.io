7. DNA Fingerprinting and Barcoding

This closing chapter covers two related, highly practical applications of genomic variation: identifying individuals from their DNA, and identifying species from a short, standardized DNA sequence.

DNA Fingerprinting

Definition - DNA Fingerprinting

DNA fingerprinting exploits highly variable regions of the genome - classically, variable-number tandem repeats, and today most often short tandem repeats (STRs) - that differ enough between unrelated individuals to produce an essentially unique genetic profile, while still following predictable Mendelian inheritance (Chapter 1 of Genetics) useful for confirming biological relationships.

Example - Applications of DNA Fingerprinting

DNA fingerprinting is used in forensic identification (matching biological evidence to a suspect or victim), paternity and other kinship testing, and - in agriculture and plant breeding - in verifying the genetic identity and purity of crop varieties and germplasm collections (Chapter 1 of Plant Biotechnology & Breeding).

DNA Barcoding

Definition - DNA Barcoding

DNA barcoding uses a short, standardized gene region - chosen because it varies enough between species while remaining easy to amplify and sequence across a broad taxonomic range (e.g., a mitochondrial gene region in animals, a chloroplast region in plants) - to rapidly identify the species of a biological sample, without requiring the specialized taxonomic expertise that morphological identification demands.

Barcoding is especially valuable for identifying samples that are damaged, processed, or morphologically ambiguous (larvae, fragments, processed food products), and complements the classical and molecular taxonomic methods covered in Chapter 1 of Diversity, Taxonomy & Phylogenetics, extending species identification to contexts where full genome sequencing would be unnecessary or impractical.
