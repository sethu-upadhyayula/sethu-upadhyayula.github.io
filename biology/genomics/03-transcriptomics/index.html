3. Transcriptomics

Rather than studying the expression of one gene at a time, transcriptomics measures the abundance of every transcript in a sample simultaneously - a shift in scale that has transformed how gene regulation (Chapter 5 of Molecular Biology) is studied.

Microarrays

Early transcriptome profiling used microarrays - a solid surface spotted with thousands of known DNA probes, each corresponding to a specific gene, to which fluorescently labelled sample cDNA is hybridized. The intensity of fluorescence at each spot estimates the corresponding transcript's abundance, limited to genes the array was designed to detect.

RNA Sequencing

Definition - RNA-Seq

RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) instead directly sequences cDNA derived from a sample's RNA, using the same next-generation sequencing technology as genome sequencing. Because it does not require pre-designed probes, RNA-seq can detect novel transcripts and splice variants (Chapter 3 of Molecular Biology) invisible to microarrays, and has largely replaced microarrays for new studies as sequencing costs have fallen.

Differential Gene Expression Analysis

Comparing transcript abundance between conditions (e.g., diseased versus healthy tissue, or treated versus untreated cells) identifies genes that are significantly up- or down-regulated - differential gene expression - using statistical methods that account for the count-based nature of sequencing data and the need to control for testing thousands of genes simultaneously.

Comparative Transcriptomics

Remark - Comparing Expression Across Species

Comparative transcriptomics compares gene expression patterns across species, or across tissues within a species, revealing that regulatory changes - when and where a gene is expressed - are often as evolutionarily important as changes to the coding sequence itself (Chapter 6 of Evolution), a theme central to the study of evolutionary developmental biology.
