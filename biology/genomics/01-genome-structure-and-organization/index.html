1. Genome Structure and Organization Across Life

Before comparing genomes in detail, it helps to see just how differently genomes are organized across the tree of life - in size, gene density, and how much of the genome actually codes for protein.

Prokaryotic versus Eukaryotic Genome Architecture

Prokaryotic genomes are typically small, circular, and gene-dense, with genes frequently organized into operons (Chapter 6 of Cell Biology) and comparatively little non-coding DNA between genes. Eukaryotic genomes are generally much larger, linear, and organized across multiple chromosomes packaged into chromatin (also Chapter 6 of Cell Biology), with genes interrupted by introns (Chapter 3 of Molecular Biology) and separated by large stretches of non-coding, often repetitive, DNA.

The C-Value Paradox

Definition - C-Value Paradox

The C-value is the total amount of DNA in a haploid genome. The C-value paradox refers to the observation that genome size correlates poorly with organismal complexity or gene number - some amoebae and lungfish, for instance, have genomes many times larger than the human genome, almost entirely due to differences in repetitive, non-coding DNA content rather than gene count.

Repetitive DNA and Genome Content

A large fraction of many eukaryotic genomes consists of transposable elements and other repetitive sequences (Chapter 6 of Cell Biology) rather than protein-coding genes - in humans, protein-coding sequence makes up only a small percentage of total genome size, with the remainder including introns, regulatory elements, and various classes of repetitive and currently function-unassigned DNA.

Organelle Genomes at Genome Scale

Remark - Reduced Organelle Genomes

Mitochondrial and chloroplast genomes (Chapter 3 of Genetics covers their inheritance pattern) are dramatically reduced compared to their free-living bacterial ancestors, having transferred most of their original genes to the nuclear genome over evolutionary time - a striking illustration that genome size and gene content can be reshaped substantially even long after a genome's basic architecture is established.
