6. Single-Cell Genomics and Metagenomics

Two related developments have pushed genome-scale sequencing in opposite directions of scale - down to the resolution of a single cell, and outward to entire microbial communities sampled directly from their environment.

Single-Cell Sequencing

Definition - Single-Cell Omics

Conventional genomic and transcriptomic profiling measures an average signal across millions of pooled cells, obscuring differences between individual cells. Single-cell sequencing instead isolates and sequences the genome or transcriptome of individual cells one at a time, revealing cell-to-cell heterogeneity - for example, distinguishing genuinely distinct cell types and states within what was previously treated as a single, uniform tissue sample (Chapter 1 of Developmental Biology's discussion of cell fate).

Remark - Applications of Single-Cell Data

Single-cell transcriptomics has been particularly transformative for developmental biology and cancer research, allowing researchers to reconstruct differentiation trajectories directly from data and to characterize the diverse cell populations - including rare, therapeutically important subpopulations - within a tumor (Chapter 6 of Cell Signaling & Cancer Biology).

Metagenomics

Definition - Metagenomics

Metagenomics sequences DNA extracted directly from an environmental sample (soil, water, or a host-associated microbiome) without first isolating and culturing individual organisms - essential given that the great majority of microbial diversity consists of uncultivable microorganisms that cannot be grown under standard laboratory conditions (Chapter 2 of Microbiology).

Metatranscriptomics

Metatranscriptomics extends the same logic to RNA, sequencing the collective transcriptome of an environmental or host-associated microbial community to reveal which genes are actively being expressed, not merely which organisms and genes are present - a crucial distinction, since a community's genomic potential and its actual, currently active metabolism can differ substantially depending on environmental conditions (Chapter 11 of Microbiology's discussion of microbial ecology).
