2. Comparative and Functional Genomics

Comparing genomes across species - an increasingly routine analysis given how many genomes are now sequenced - reveals which genomic features are ancient and conserved, and which are recent and lineage-specific.

Synteny and Genome Rearrangement

Definition - Synteny

Synteny describes the conservation of gene order along chromosomes between related species. Comparing syntenic blocks across genomes reveals the history of large-scale genome rearrangements - inversions, translocations, and fusions/fissions of chromosomes (Chapter 4 of Genetics) - that have occurred since two species diverged from a common ancestor.

Gene Family Evolution

Comparative genomics traces the expansion and contraction of gene families through duplication and loss over evolutionary time - for example, immune-related and olfactory receptor gene families have expanded dramatically in some lineages while shrinking in others, reflecting differing ecological pressures on those functions.

Identifying Functional Elements Through Conservation

Remark - Conservation as Evidence of Function

A genomic region conserved across distantly related species, despite ample opportunity for neutral drift (Chapter 6 of Evolution) to erase any unconstrained sequence, is strong indirect evidence that the region is functionally important - a principle used extensively to identify regulatory elements and functional non-coding RNAs that annotation methods based on protein-coding similarity (Chapter 6 of Structural & Computational Biology) would otherwise miss.

Model Organism Genomics

Much of functional genomics depends on a small set of well-studied model organisms - E. coli, yeast, Drosophila, C. elegans, mouse - whose genomes are richly annotated and experimentally tractable, allowing genes discovered in humans or crops to be functionally tested by comparison to (or direct experimentation in) a model system.
