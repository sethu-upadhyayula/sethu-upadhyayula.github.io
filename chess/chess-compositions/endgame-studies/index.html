Endgame Studies

Endgame studies occupy a unique position in chess art: composed positions that look like they could arise in real play, where White wins or draws by force through a sequence of moves that surprises and instructs. The greatest studies reveal truths about chess that no practical game could teach as cleanly.

1. Definition

An endgame study is a composed chess position — typically with few pieces — carrying one of two stipulations:

  • White to play and win (+) — White must achieve a theoretically won position against Black's best resistance.
  • White to play and draw (=) — White, seemingly in a losing or desperate position, finds the only path to a draw.

There is no specified move count. White wins (or draws) "by force," meaning the result holds against every Black defence, however many moves it takes. This distinguishes studies from moremovers: the freedom from a fixed move count allows studies to feel like genuine chess — with decisions made on principle and intuition rather than precise counting.

The aesthetic criterion for studies is surprise: the winning or drawing mechanism should be unexpected, beautiful, and non-obvious. A study whose solution is simply "play the best moves and the advantage converts naturally" has no artistic merit. The key move must astonish.

2. Studies vs. Problems

FeatureEndgame StudyChess Problem
StipulationWin or draw (no move count)Mate in exactly n moves
AppearanceNatural, game-like positionsOften artificial arrangements
Key criterionSurprise, instruction, beautyTheme, economy, key quality

Many grandmasters study endgame studies specifically for practical benefit. The patterns they reveal — king geometry, piece coordination, pawn breakthrough timing — recur in real games. This dual nature (art and instruction) is unique to the study form.

3. Major Composers

Henri Rinck (1870–1952)

The most prolific study composer in history, with over 1,700 studies published. Rinck was a Spanish-based French chemist who composed prolifically throughout the early 20th century. His specialty was rook and bishop endgames, and he systematically catalogued winning and drawing techniques that remain standard reference material. His style was direct and instructive rather than spectacular.

Alexei Troitzky (1866–1942)

The Russian master produced studies of extraordinary depth, including his famous analysis of the K+2N vs K+P endgame — a theoretically drawn endgame that is winning in practice when the pawn has not advanced too far. Troitzky's "Troitzky Line" (the set of pawn positions where White wins with two knights) remains one of the most celebrated contributions to both composition and theory.

Richard Réti (1889–1929)

The Czech hypermodern grandmaster composed one of the most famous studies in history (see below), demonstrating a profound geometric insight about king movement that permanently changed how players understand king activity in the endgame. Réti's few studies are models of economy — typically just a handful of pieces, with the entire universe of the position contained in those sparse forces.

Josef Kling and Bernhard Horwitz (19th century)

The earliest serious study composers, whose 1851 collection Chess Studies established the endgame study as an independent art form. Many of their compositions are still cited in endgame theory books, particularly their K+R vs K+B and K+Q vs K+R studies.

Mark Dvoretsky (1947–2016)

The legendary trainer used endgame studies as training tools, and his books introduced countless players to the study tradition. Dvoretsky argued — and demonstrated — that mastering studies is essential for anyone who wants to develop a truly deep understanding of chess endings.

4. The Réti Study (1921)

This is perhaps the most famous endgame study ever composed. The position appears totally lost for White: Black has a passed pawn close to queening, and White's king seems far too distant to catch it. But Réti found a miraculous saving idea.

Position: White: ♔h8, ♙c6  |  Black: ♚a6, ♟h5

White to play and draw

Réti's famous drawing study, 1921 — White king on h8, Black pawn on h5

Solution

1. Kg7! (the key — not Kh7, which only chases the pawn)

The genius: the White king heads toward the centre diagonally,
simultaneously threatening to support its own pawn (c6-c7-c8) AND
to catch the Black pawn if it advances.

1...h4 (Black advances the pawn)
2. Kf6! (diagonal approach continues — heading for both c-pawn and h-pawn)
2...h3
3. Ke5! (threatening 4.Kd6 supporting the c-pawn to queen)
3...h2 (Black must advance)
4. Kd6 (now threatening c7 and c8=Q)
4...h1=Q (Black queens, but now White queens too)
5. c7 and White promotes with a drawn position (queens trade or perpetual).

If instead Black tries to stop the White pawn:
3...Kb6 (attacking c6)
4. Kd4! (king catches the h-pawn) → drawn.

The king's diagonal path simultaneously threatens two objectives —
this geometric duality is the eternal insight of the Réti study.

The Réti study taught every chess player that a king can threaten two distant objectives at once by travelling diagonally between them. This insight — now called "the Réti idea" — appears regularly in practical endgames and is essential knowledge for any serious player.

5. Troitzky's K+2N vs K+P

It is well-known that K+2N cannot force checkmate against a lone king — the defending king can always reach a corner and the attacking side cannot force the final position without stalemating. However, Troitzky proved that if Black has a pawn, the situation changes dramatically: the pawn's advance can be used to avoid the stalemate that would otherwise save Black. In other words, the extra Black pawn is a liability, not an asset.

Troitzky spent decades analysing every pawn-on-every-file scenario and constructed the "Troitzky Line" — for each file, the furthest rank to which the pawn can have advanced for White to still win with best play. This was an enormous computational and compositional achievement for the pre-computer era, and Troitzky's specific composed studies illustrating this theme remain among the most instructive endgame compositions ever created.

6. Solving and Appreciating Studies

Solving studies requires different mental habits from solving problems:

  1. Ask: what is the most natural move? Then ask: why doesn't it work? Studies are designed so the natural move fails, and the surprise lies in why.
  2. Consider all checks, captures, and promotions first. These are forcing moves. The study's key move often appears only after these forcing lines are exhausted.
  3. Look for geometric ideas. King triangulation, diagonal threats toward two objectives, zugzwang setups — the solution often hinges on a geometric relationship between pieces and pawns.
  4. Think about stalemate. Drawing studies often exploit stalemate or perpetual check as the saving resource. Winning studies often must navigate around stalemate traps.
  5. Appreciate the try lines. The best studies include "tries" — lines that appear to win but fail — just like the best problems. Understanding why the try fails is as instructive as finding the main line.

Studies are deeply instructive for practical play in a way that pure problems are not. The endgame positions that arise — lone kings chasing pawns, rooks versus bishops in open positions, knight coordination — recur in real games. A player who has absorbed fifty great studies has broadened their pattern library in a way that hours of opening preparation cannot match.
