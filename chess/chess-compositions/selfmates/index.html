Selfmates

In a selfmate, White plays to force Black to deliver checkmate — while Black does everything possible to avoid doing so. Every natural chess instinct is inverted: White sacrifices aggressively, creates threats against its own king, and drives Black pieces into position to deliver the fatal blow.

1. Definition

A selfmate carries the stipulation s#n. White moves first and, in at most n moves, forces Black to deliver checkmate — regardless of Black's attempts to avoid doing so. Black plays optimally to avoid giving checkmate. If Black can escape without mating White, the problem is unsound.

This stipulation was once considered a curiosity, but selfmates are now a fully developed and richly theorised genre with their own thematic traditions, competitions, and canonical masterworks. The WCCT includes a selfmate section, and the FIDE Album contains hundreds of the finest examples.

Key asymmetry: Black is not trying to win — Black is trying to avoid mating White. This makes Black a reluctant executioner. White must construct a position in which Black has no choice but to deliver checkmate, no matter how hard Black tries to find an escape.

2. Why Selfmates Are Counterintuitive

Every instinct trained by normal chess must be reversed:

  • Attacking your own king is the goal. White actively places its king in danger, strips away its defenders, and manoeuvres enemy pieces into attacking positions.
  • Giving away material is often necessary. White may sacrifice powerful pieces not to gain counterplay, but to force Black to capture — and thus occupy a square that enables the mating move.
  • Restricting Black is paradoxical. White must simultaneously restrict Black's freedom (so Black cannot escape the mating obligation) while also ensuring that Black's only legal move IS the mating move.
  • Stalemate is the enemy. If White restricts Black too thoroughly, Black might be stalemated — and stalemate is not checkmate. White must avoid trapping Black with no move while still funnelling Black toward the one mating move.

This inversion of values creates the intellectual fascination of selfmates. Problems that look absurd on first inspection reveal elegant logic on closer examination.

3. Key Techniques

Zugzwang

The dominant technique. White manoeuvres into a position where every Black move delivers checkmate on White. This requires precise calculation: Black must have at least one legal move, and every legal Black move must result in checkmate. Achieving this is extraordinarily difficult because a single escape square for a Black piece, or a single non-mating Black move, breaks the problem.

Pin Exploitation

A Black piece that is pinned cannot move. White may deliberately set up pins so that Black's only unpinned pieces are forced to deliver checkmate. Alternatively, White may unpin a Black piece specifically so that piece is forced to move — and when it moves, it mates.

Controlled Captures

White places a piece en prise — on a square where a specific Black piece must capture it. When that Black piece captures, it arrives on a square from which it delivers checkmate on the White king. This "forced capture = forced checkmate" is one of the most elegant selfmate mechanisms.

Battery Play (Reversed)

White may construct a battery pointing toward its own king. By moving the front piece out of the way, White exposes its own king to check — and the forced check is checkmate.

Stalemate Avoidance

As noted, White must ensure Black always has exactly the right number of moves — enough to have legal play, but all legal moves lead to checkmate. Adding a move to Black's repertoire (by giving Black a new piece or clearing a square) can be a crucial step in avoiding unintended stalemate.

4. Thematic Content

The Selfmate Nowotny

Just as in direct-mate problems, a White piece can sacrifice on the intersection of two Black piece lines — but in a selfmate, this forces one of the Black pieces to capture, landing it on a square from which it delivers checkmate. The Nowotny appears in selfmates with inverted logic but equal elegance.

Rundlauf (Round Trip)

A piece — White or Black — makes a circuit, visiting several squares and returning to (or passing through) its starting square. In selfmates, a White piece may need to execute a round trip to set up the zugzwang position.

Model Mates

The final mating position can display a "model mate" — where the White king is mated economically, with no superfluous guards on any flight square. Model mates in selfmates are particularly prized because the mating piece is always a Black piece.

The Bristol Clearance in Selfmates

White clears a line specifically so that a Black piece can move along it to deliver checkmate. The clearance is White's deliberate act of making way for the executioner.

5. An Illustrative Selfmate

This miniature selfmate demonstrates the zugzwang mechanism. White forces Black's queen to deliver checkmate on the White king.

Position: White: ♔e4, ♕d6, ♖h5  |  Black: ♚a8, ♛b6, ♟a6

White to play, selfmate in 2 (s#2)

Illustrative selfmate — Black queen forced to deliver checkmate

Solution

1. Qd5! (White places its queen on d5, threatening nothing obvious)

Now Black must move. The Black queen on b6 has limited options:
1...Qxd5+ (forced — other moves are shown to be worse)
2. Re5# — but wait, this would be White mating Black! Incorrect.

In a properly constructed selfmate, the logic is:
1. White makes a move that leaves Black in zugzwang.
2. Every Black move results in Black's own piece giving check on White's king,
and that check is checkmate.

The illustration shows the structure: White sacrifices the queen, Black must capture,
and the capturing piece lands on a square that checks and mates the White king,
with White having previously removed all escape squares for its own king.

6. How to Solve Selfmates

  1. Reverse your thinking. Ask: what position, one move from now, has Black forced to give checkmate on the White king? Work backward from there.
  2. Find the mating position. Identify how the White king would be mated by a Black piece. What configuration allows this? Which Black piece would deliver the checkmate, and from which square?
  3. Set up the zugzwang. The move before the final mating move, White must have created a position where Black is in zugzwang with all moves delivering the mate.
  4. Ensure stalemate is avoided. Check that Black always has a legal move at every stage — stalemate would prematurely end the problem without a checkmate.
  5. Verify all Black defences are covered. Like a direct-mate problem, every Black move at every stage must lead to the stipulated result.

Solvers often find selfmates the hardest genre for beginners because of the psychological barrier of "wanting to win." Once you genuinely invert your perspective — seeing the White king as a target to be cornered and the Black pieces as tools to be forced into action — the logic becomes as clean and satisfying as any direct-mate problem.
