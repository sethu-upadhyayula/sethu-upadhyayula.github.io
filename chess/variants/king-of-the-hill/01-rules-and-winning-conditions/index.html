1. Rules and Winning Conditions

King of the Hill (often abbreviated KOTH) is played with exactly the same pieces, board, starting position, and move rules as standard chess. Every familiar rule still applies: castling, en passant, pawn promotion, the fifty-move rule, threefold repetition, and stalemate all work exactly as they do in classical chess. The only change is the addition of a second way to win the game outright.

The Two Winning Conditions

  • Standard win: Checkmate the opponent's king, exactly as in regular chess.
  • King of the Hill win: Move your king to one of the four central squares — e4, d4, e5, or d5 — and the position is not immediately lost for you as a result. The instant your king legally lands on one of these squares (and the game has not simultaneously ended in your opponent's favour), you win.

This is a genuinely radical change. In classical chess, the king's job is almost entirely defensive: stay safe, stay out of the way, and let the other pieces do the fighting. In King of the Hill, the king has an offensive mission from the very start of the game — reach the centre, by any safe route, before your opponent does.

The Hill Squares

The four "hill" squares are exactly the same squares that classical opening theory already identifies as the most valuable real estate on the board — the centre. Controlling e4, d4, e5, and d5 has always been a cornerstone of good chess strategy because pieces placed there have maximum mobility and influence over the largest number of other squares. King of the Hill simply makes this central importance literal and absolute: occupying one of these squares with your king is not merely advantageous, it ends the game.

The four green "hill" squares: d4, e4, d5, e5. Move your king onto any of these to win instantly (subject to the simultaneous-threat rule below).

Reaching the Hill from the Starting Position

From their home squares (e1 for White, e8 for Black), each king is theoretically only a handful of moves from a hill square. The shortest geometric path for White's king from e1 to e4 is three moves (e1–e2–e3–e4), and to d4 is also three moves via a diagonal route (e1–e2–d3–d4 or similar). In practice, however, the king cannot simply walk forward on move one — the squares in front of it are occupied by pawns and pieces, and walking an undeveloped king into the open early in the game is almost always fatally unsafe. The real skill of KOTH lies in clearing a safe path and timing the march correctly, which is the subject of Chapters 2 and 3.

A possible diagonal path for White's king from e1 toward d4: e1–e2–d3–d4. Every intermediate square must be safe and unoccupied.

The Simultaneous Threat Rule

There is one important caveat that every KOTH player must understand precisely: a king move to a hill square only wins if, immediately after the move, your opponent cannot legally and immediately end the game in their own favour. In practice this means:

  • If moving your king to the hill square would place your own king in a position where it is simultaneously in check from a discovered attack and that check happens to also be checkmate, the move is simply illegal (you cannot move into checkmate, just as in regular chess) — so this scenario cannot actually occur.
  • The more practically relevant case is move ordering at the board level: if your king move to the hill is itself check or checkmate against the opponent, both conditions are satisfied simultaneously and you win — there is no ambiguity, since your move is what just happened.
  • The caveat mainly exists to clarify that the hill win is evaluated after your move is complete and fully legal. You cannot "almost" reach the hill or pass through a hill square en route to somewhere else and claim a win — your king must come to rest on d4, e4, d5, or e5 at the end of your turn.

In almost all practical games, the simultaneous-threat subtlety boils down to a simple rule of thumb: if it is legal for your king to move to a hill square, and your king is not currently in check, making that move wins the game immediately. The exotic edge cases rarely arise outside of constructed puzzle positions.

Rule summary: Win by (1) checkmate, exactly as in standard chess, or (2) a legal king move to d4, e4, d5, or e5. All other rules of chess (castling, en passant, promotion, draws by repetition/50-move/insufficient material, stalemate) are unchanged.

What Does NOT Count

It is worth being explicit about a few things that do not trigger a hill win, since new players sometimes misremember the rule:

  • Other pieces on the hill squares don't win the game. A rook, knight, bishop, queen, or pawn sitting on d4/e4/d5/e5 has no special winning status — only the king triggers the win, and only by moving there itself.
  • Castling does not count, even though castling is technically a king move — the king's destination squares after castling (c1/g1 for White, c8/g8 for Black) are never hill squares anyway, so this is moot in practice, but it underlines that the win condition is about the king's final resting square after a normal king move.
  • Passing through a hill square is not enough. The king's move must terminate on a hill square; a king cannot "pass through" squares in the way a rook or queen slides, so this point is mostly conceptual — but it reinforces that only the final square matters.

Comparison with Standard Chess

It can be useful to think of King of the Hill as standard chess with an additional, parallel "objective" layered on top — similar to how some video games have multiple win conditions (eliminate all enemies, or capture the flag). A game of KOTH can be won in the classical way (a full mating attack, with the king staying safely tucked away the whole game) just as easily as it can be won by a king march. Many games are decided by the threat of one method forcing a response that allows the other — for example, a defender forced to give up central control while parrying a mating attack may inadvertently open the door for the attacker's king to walk to the centre.
