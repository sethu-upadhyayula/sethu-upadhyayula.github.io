1. Rules and Starting Position

Horde Chess is one of the most visually striking chess variants: one side fields a wall of pawns nearly five times the size of a normal pawn force, while the other side keeps its full conventional army. The asymmetry is total — different material, different winning conditions, and a completely different starting position.

The Setup

  • White (the Horde): 36 pawns, no other pieces. White has no king at all — White cannot be checkmated, cannot castle, and has no royal piece to protect. White wins by checkmating Black's king.
  • Black (the Kingdom): The standard chess set — king, queen, two rooks, two bishops, two knights, and eight pawns, arranged exactly as in a normal game.
  • White moves first, as in standard chess.
  • Winning conditions are asymmetric: White wins only by delivering checkmate to Black's king. Black wins either by checkmating White (impossible, since White has no king) or — far more realistically — by capturing every single one of White's 36 pawns. If White is reduced to zero pawns, Black wins immediately, even if it isn't Black's move to deliver "mate."

Starting Position

Black's pieces occupy ranks 7 and 8 exactly as in a standard game. White's 36 pawns occupy a large block at the bottom of the board:

  • Ranks 1 through 4 are completely filled with White pawns — that's 8 pawns × 4 ranks = 32 pawns.
  • Four additional pawns sit on rank 5, on the squares b5, c5, f5, and g5 — directly in front of the gaps on the queenside and kingside, forming two small "towers" that poke into the centre.
  • That gives White exactly 32 + 4 = 36 pawns, the maximum number of pawns that can exist on a board for one side under normal chess piece-count constraints (since 8 pieces per side are normally non-pawns, the Horde's missing pieces become extra pawns instead).

Horde starting position: White's 36 pawns (ranks 1-4 plus b5, c5, f5, g5) vs Black's full army on ranks 7-8.

Why b5, c5, f5, and g5?

The choice of squares for the four "extra" pawns is not arbitrary. The d-file and e-file — the two central files — are deliberately left clear at rank 5, so that Black's central pawn pushes (1...d5 or 1...e5) and central pieces have an immediate route into the heart of the Horde. If the centre were also packed with pawns up to rank 5, the position would be far more lopsided in White's favour, since Black would have no quick way to attack the mass. Placing the salients on the b/c and f/g files instead gives the Horde some forward presence on both wings while keeping the centre relatively open for Black's pieces to operate.

Special Pawn Rules for the Horde

  • First-move double steps everywhere: Any White pawn that has not yet moved may advance one or two squares, exactly as a pawn on its home rank does in standard chess. Because the Horde occupies ranks 1-5, this means a pawn starting on rank 4 can jump to rank 6 in one move, and even a rank-1 pawn can leap to rank 3. This gives the Horde considerable reach right from the opening.
  • Standard captures: White pawns capture diagonally forward exactly as usual. With pawns packed shoulder-to-shoulder across four-plus ranks, almost every pawn has at least one diagonal neighbour defending it.
  • En passant applies normally for both sides, including for Horde pawns making their initial double-step.
  • Promotion is possible but rare. If a White pawn survives the gauntlet of Black's pieces and reaches rank 8, it promotes to a queen, rook, bishop, or knight just like in standard chess. In practice this almost never happens in balanced games, because by the time a single pawn could fight through, the bulk of the Horde has usually already been captured or has already delivered (or failed to deliver) mate.
  • White has no king, so there is no castling for White and no concept of "check" against White. Black, however, still castles and is checked normally — Black's king remains the sole mating target in the game.

Counting the Material

It's worth pausing on just how lopsided the raw material count looks on paper:

White: 36 pawns  =  36 points (by the standard pawn=1 valuation)
Black: 1 queen (9) + 2 rooks (10) + 2 bishops (6) + 2 knights (6) + 8 pawns (8) = 39 points

Despite appearances, the material counts are actually close to balanced under simple point-counting — White's 36 "pawn points" are roughly comparable to Black's 39 points of mixed material. The real imbalance is not in total value but in concentration and mobility: Black's 39 points are distributed across 15 highly mobile units that can coordinate complex tactics, while White's 36 points are spread across 36 units that can each only shuffle forward or capture one square diagonally.

What Makes the Position Unusual

A few consequences of this setup are worth internalising before looking at strategy in the following chapters:

  • White's "king safety" is a non-issue — there is nothing to protect. All of White's effort goes into offense.
  • Black's normal opening principles (develop pieces, control the centre, castle early) still apply, but with a twist: Black's pieces must very quickly start attacking the Horde, because every quiet developing move gives White another free push forward.
  • The b5/c5/f5/g5 salients are often the first targets — capturing them removes the most advanced (and most dangerous) Horde pawns and can disrupt the connectivity of the mass behind them.
  • Because the Horde has no king, there is no "endgame technique" in the traditional sense for White — there is only "how many pawns are left, and can they still mate the Black king before running out."
