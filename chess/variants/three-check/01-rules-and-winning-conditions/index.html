1. Rules and Winning Conditions

Three Check is played exactly like standard chess — same board, same pieces, same legal moves — with one additional winning condition layered on top. This single addition is enough to transform the entire character of the game.

Two Paths to Victory

  • Standard win: Checkmate the opponent's king, exactly as in classical chess.
  • Three Check win: Deliver check to the opponent's king three times over the course of the game. The game ends immediately when the third check is given — even if that check could be trivially escaped on the next move, it does not matter. The moment the king is in check for the third time, the game is over.

Either condition ends the game instantly. A player can be completely lost on the board — down a queen, facing an unstoppable mating attack — and still win on the spot by landing their third check first. This is the defining tension of the variant: the position on the board and the "score" on the check counter are two separate races, and only one of them has to finish first.

The Check Counter

Both players accumulate a check counter that is displayed throughout the game, usually as +1, +2, and finally +3 (the winning total) for each side. Here is what the counter looks like mid-game:

White: +2 Black: +1
White has given 2 checks — one more wins outright. Black has given 1 and must now play extremely carefully.

Because the counter only ever increases — it can never go back down — the game has a built-in sense of urgency that classical chess lacks. A player sitting on +2 is one accurate check away from victory regardless of what else is happening on the board, and their opponent knows it.

Checks That Count

One of the most important things to internalise about Three Check is that every check counts, regardless of context:

  • A check that immediately loses the checking piece (e.g., a queen sacrifice that gives check and is captured next move) still counts toward the total. The counter increments the instant the king is placed in check — what happens afterward is irrelevant to the score.
  • A discovered check — where moving one piece reveals an attack from another piece behind it — counts as a check like any other.
  • A double check, where two pieces attack the king simultaneously, counts as one check, not two. The counter measures instances of "the king is in check," not the number of attacking pieces.
  • Checks delivered from a completely lost position still count. A player down a queen and two rooks, facing mate in one, can still win the game outright by landing their third check on the move before they would have been mated.
  • Perpetual check sequences — repeatedly checking the king with the same piece via a repeating pattern — also count each time, which means a perpetual check that would be a draw by repetition in classical chess can instead be an outright win in Three Check if it reaches three checks before three-fold repetition is claimed.

What Does Not Count

A few clarifications are worth stating explicitly:

  • Only checks against the king count. Threatening to check, or a move that merely attacks a square the king might move to, does nothing for the counter.
  • If a king is already in check and a move is made that blocks or escapes the check without itself delivering a new check, the counter does not change.
  • Stalemate, draw by repetition, the 50-move rule, and insufficient material all function exactly as in classical chess — Three Check does not change any of the classical draw conditions. The check counter is purely an additional win condition, not a replacement for the existing rules.

White's knight on g6 and bishop on h5 both attack the king on e8 — a double check. Even though two pieces are checking the king at once, this counts as a single increment to White's check counter.

Time Controls and Practical Play

Three Check is most commonly played online as a fast variant — bullet and blitz time controls dominate, since the check-counter race rewards rapid, forcing play and punishes slow manoeuvring. The short time controls amplify the variant's tactical nature: players who can spot forcing checking sequences quickly have a significant edge over players calculating deep positional plans.
