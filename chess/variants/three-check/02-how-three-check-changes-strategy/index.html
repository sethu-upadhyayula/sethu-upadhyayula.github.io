2. How Three Check Changes Strategy

The check-counter rule sounds like a small addition, but it touches almost every strategic principle in classical chess. Pieces, pawn structures, and even the value of the queen all need to be re-evaluated through the lens of "does this help me give checks, and does it help me avoid being checked?"

Every Check Has New Value

In classical chess, a check is only "good" if it accomplishes something concrete - it gains a tempo, forces the king to an inferior square, wins material, or is part of a forced mating sequence. A check that achieves nothing and simply allows the opponent to escape is normally considered a wasted move, sometimes even a mistake if it helps the opponent develop (e.g., a check that forces a king to a square where it is safer).

In Three Check, this calculus is completely overturned. Every check has intrinsic value: it advances your counter by one, regardless of what else happens. A check that forces you to give up a bishop in the process might still be an excellent practical decision if it brings your counter to 2 of 3 - because from that point on, your opponent must play every single move under the threat that any check, however small, ends the game instantly.

King Safety Is Completely Redefined

In classical chess, "king safety" means avoiding checkmate and avoiding dangerous attacks against the king's position - a king that is merely checked and moves to a perfectly fine square has lost nothing tangible. In Three Check, king safety means avoiding being checked at all, full stop. Even a completely harmless check that the king escapes for free still adds a permanent mark to the opponent's counter. There is no way to "undo" a check once it has been given.

This reframing changes priorities from the very first moves:

  • Pawn shelters matter enormously. Keeping the king behind an intact pawn wall - particularly on the f, g, and h files after castling - closes off entire families of checking patterns (queen and bishop checks along diagonals, rook checks along open files).
  • Trading pieces to prevent a check is often correct, even when it is materially unfavourable in isolation. Giving up a knight to remove a piece that would otherwise deliver an unavoidable check next move can be the difference between staying at 1/3 and dropping to 2/3.
  • Blocking with a piece can be better than moving the king. If a check can be intercepted by interposing a piece on the checking line, that is often preferable to moving the king to an open square - both responses get you out of check on the board, but interposing keeps your own future king movements more flexible and doesn't commit the king to a potentially exposed square.
  • Avoid weakening pawn moves near your own king. A move like ...g6 or ...h6 that looks purely defensive in classical chess can open a long diagonal that a bishop or queen exploits for a check several moves later.

Both kings are tucked behind intact pawn shelters after castling. In Three Check, an intact f-g-h (or a-b-c) pawn structure is one of the most valuable defensive assets on the board - it removes almost every diagonal and file a queen or bishop could use to check the king.

Attack-Minded, Forcing Play

Three Check rewards aggressive, forcing play far more heavily than classical chess. Since checks are valuable in their own right, both players are pushed toward:

  • Opening lines toward the enemy king as rapidly as possible - even at the cost of long-term structural weaknesses, because the structural weaknesses may never matter if the game ends first on the counter.
  • Sacrificing material for checking opportunities. A piece sacrifice that "only" wins a tempo in classical chess might be a strong move in Three Check if that tempo is itself a check.
  • Prioritising piece activity over pawn structure. An actively placed knight or bishop that can hop to a checking square is often worth more than a "better" pawn structure that leaves pieces passive.
  • Keeping queens on the board. The queen's combination of long-range diagonal, file, and rank movement makes it the single most prolific checking piece. Trading queens early - often a simplifying, safe choice in classical chess - removes a major source of checking power for both sides and tends to slow the game down toward a more classical struggle.

White's queen lands on f7, giving check to the king on e8. Even if Black's rook captures the queen on the very next move, White has already banked check number one - the trade of a queen for a rook may be "bad" materially but excellent on the counter.

Re-evaluating Piece Values

Because checking potential matters so much, the relative values of the pieces shift slightly from their classical baselines:

  • Queens become even more valuable as the primary checking weapon, but also become bigger liabilities - losing your queen removes your best checking tool while a queen left on an exposed square is itself a target for tactics that combine threats with checks.
  • Knights gain relative value. Their unusual jumping movement makes them excellent at delivering surprise checks that cannot be blocked (a knight check can never be interposed against), and they are difficult for the opposing king to predict and avoid.
  • Bishops on open diagonals toward the enemy king become extremely valuable "sniper" pieces - a fianchettoed bishop bearing down on a long diagonal is a constant latent checking threat.
  • Rooks gain value on open files leading to the enemy king, especially in the middlegame once some pawns have traded off.

Tempo and the "Race" Mentality

Perhaps the single biggest mental shift for players coming from classical chess is learning to think of Three Check as two simultaneous races: the race to checkmate, and the race to three checks. A move's quality must be judged against both races at once. A move that is "only" the third-best option on the board in classical terms might be the clear best move in Three Check if it is the only one of the candidates that also delivers a check - because it makes progress in the second race "for free."
