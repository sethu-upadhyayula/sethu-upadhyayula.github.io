4. Openings and Endgames in Three Check

The opening and endgame phases of Three Check look superficially similar to classical chess — the same opening moves are legal, the same endgame material configurations occur — but the underlying priorities are reshaped by the check counter from the very first move all the way to the final one.

Opening Principles

Classical opening principles still apply — develop your pieces, fight for the centre, get your king to safety — but the relative priority of each principle shifts. Development is still essential, since you need active pieces to deliver checks, but the kind of development matters more than in classical chess:

  • Knights and bishops first. Minor pieces can generate early checking threats (especially knight checks, which cannot be blocked) without overextending the queen the way an early queen sortie would.
  • King safety is paramount, and urgent. Castle as quickly as soundly possible to tuck the king behind its pawn shelter. Leaving the king in the centre for even a few extra moves against a Three-Check-aware opponent is far more dangerous than in classical chess, where a centralised king in the opening is merely "risky" — here it is an open invitation to rack up checks.
  • Avoid pawn moves that open lines toward your own king. Moves like an early ...g6 (weakening the long dark diagonal toward a fianchettoed king) or h3/...h6 (creating a hook for sacrificial attacks) should be considered not just for their positional merit but for what diagonals and files they open.
  • Keep an eye on the long diagonals from move one. Openings that fianchetto a bishop (e.g., 1.Nf3 followed by g3 and Bg2) place a piece that can deliver a check along the long diagonal from the very start of the game, and similarly should be wary of the opponent doing the same against their own king.
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 (threatening ...Bxc6 followed by tactics, but also simply developing toward the king) Nf6 4.Nc3 Bb4 5.O-O O-O ...
Both sides castle quickly — in Three Check, getting the king to safety behind its pawns in the first five or six moves is close to mandatory against a competent opponent.

Opening Lines That Prioritise Checks

Some opening choices that are considered slightly inferior in classical chess gain a new lease of life in Three Check because they generate early checking chances. For example, lines featuring an early bishop check such as 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 (the Ruy Lopez) or 1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.Bb5+ (a check against the Sicilian) take on extra significance: the bishop check, even though Black can simply block it with ...Bd7, ...Nd7, or ...Nc6, is "check number one" banked for free as part of normal development.

A Ruy Lopez-style position: White's bishop on b5 gives check (illustrative — the knight on c6 blocks the check from being delivered to a king on e8 in this exact setup, but the idea is the bishop sortie to b5 develops with a check in many similar lines). Even a check that is immediately blocked is "check number one" achieved as part of completely normal development.

Avoiding Symmetrical Danger

Because both players can pursue the same checking ideas, openings in Three Check often turn into races where each side tries to generate checks against the other's king while shoring up their own defences. A useful practical guideline: before playing any developing move, ask not only "does this develop my pieces and fight for the centre?" but also "does this open any new line toward my own king, and does it set up a check against the opponent's king?" A move that answers "yes" to the second half of that second question while "no" to the first is often the best move on the board, even if it looks slightly unusual.

Endgame Peculiarities

Three Check endgames are unlike any classical endgame, because the check counter persists from the opening all the way through — a player who banked two checks in the first ten moves carries that advantage into an endgame fifty moves later. This produces some genuinely strange practical situations:

  • A player who is two checks ahead (2/3) might have an objectively losing classical position — down significant material, facing a mating attack — but still be winning the game, because they need only land one more check, however desperate, to end it immediately.
  • Conversely, a player with an overwhelming material advantage (say, an extra rook and bishop in a simplified endgame) must remain extremely cautious if their opponent is at 2/3. Normal endgame technique — bringing the king forward, advancing passed pawns, even giving "harmless" checks of their own — must all be filtered through the question "could this allow my opponent a checking resource?"
  • King and pawn endgames are particularly treacherous. Advancing a passed pawn often requires the king to step out from behind its shelter to support it, and the very same king march that wins a classical king-and-pawn endgame can walk directly into a checking square from a stray enemy piece that was thought to be irrelevant.

A simplified king-and-pawn ending. In classical chess, White's plan is simple: walk the king up to support the d-pawn's promotion. In Three Check, if Black is sitting at 2/3 checks, White must first calculate whether any king march allows the bishop on h4 to deliver a check along a newly-opened diagonal — a single careless king move could lose instantly regardless of the pawn's promotion prospects.

The "Race to Mate vs. Race to Checks" Endgame Calculus

In a Three Check endgame, the side that is behind on the check counter but ahead on the board (material or positional advantage) faces a constant decision: push for the classical win (checkmate or material conversion) at the cost of accepting some risk of being checked, or play with maximum safety and accept a slower classical winning process. The correct balance depends heavily on the exact counter score:

  • At 0/3 vs 0/3 or 1/3 vs 1/3, the check counter is a secondary consideration — play normal, sound chess, but stay alert to any free checks along the way.
  • At 2/3 vs 0/3 or 1/3, the player ahead on checks should actively look for any checking resource, even a "useless" one, since landing it ends the game instantly regardless of the board position. The player behind must play with extreme caution and treat every move as a potential loss.
  • At 2/3 vs 2/3, the game has effectively become sudden death for both sides — whoever lands the next check, by any means, wins immediately. In this situation, forcing moves, perpetual-check attempts, and even objectively "losing" sacrifices that create checking threats are all worth serious consideration.

Mastering Three Check ultimately means learning to hold these two parallel evaluations — the classical evaluation of the board, and the simple integer count of checks remaining for each side — in your head simultaneously, and recognising the (frequent) moments when the second overrides the first.
