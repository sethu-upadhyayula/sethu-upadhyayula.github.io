1. The Rules of Antichess

Antichess (also called Losing Chess, Suicide Chess, or Giveaway Chess depending on the platform) starts from the standard chess setup and standard piece movement rules, but flips the objective and adds a handful of crucial modifications. Once you internalise these rule changes, the entire game feels different — every move you consider has to be evaluated through the lens of "does this help me get rid of pieces, or get stuck with them?"

The Goal: Lose Everything

The objective of Antichess is the exact opposite of regular chess:

  • Win condition #1 — No pieces left: Be the first player to have zero pieces remaining on the board (including no king — the king can be captured or promoted away just like any other piece).
  • Win condition #2 — Stalemate: Be the player with no legal moves available on your turn. In regular chess this is a draw; in Antichess it is an outright win for the stalemated player.

Both conditions reward the same underlying skill: efficiently disposing of your own army while leaving your opponent stuck holding pieces they cannot get rid of.

Captures Are Mandatory

This is the single rule that defines Antichess. If you have one or more legal captures available on your turn, you must play a capture — you are not allowed to make any other kind of move. If multiple different captures are available (with the same piece or different pieces), you are free to choose which one to make, but you cannot decline to capture altogether.

This rule cascades through the whole game. A single piece placed "en prise" (where it could be taken) doesn't just offer a capture — it forces one, removing all other options from your opponent's position on that move. Skilled players use this to steer their opponent's moves almost as if they were making the moves themselves.

Black's pawn on d5 can capture White's pawns on c4 or e4. Since at least one capture is available, Black must play one of dxc4 or dxe4 — no other move is legal this turn.

The King Has No Special Status

One of the biggest adjustments for players coming from regular chess is that the king is just another piece. Specifically:

  • There is no concept of check or checkmate.
  • The king can be captured exactly like a pawn, knight, or queen — and if a capture of the king is available and is the only capture (or one of several), it can be forced just like any other capture.
  • The king may move into a square attacked by an enemy piece. There is no rule preventing a player from "putting themselves in check," because check doesn't exist.
  • Losing your king does not end the game by itself — the game only ends when a player has no pieces at all or is stalemated.

This single change removes an enormous amount of the safety logic that governs regular chess. In regular chess, you can never leave your king on an attacked square; in Antichess, you might want to, because getting your king captured is progress toward your goal of having zero pieces.

No Castling

Castling is not available in Antichess under standard rules. Since the king has no special role to protect and kingside/queenside safety is not a meaningful concept, castling would serve no purpose — and most implementations simply disable it.

Promotion: Any Piece, Including a King

Pawn promotion still occurs when a pawn reaches the back rank, and it is still mandatory (a pawn cannot remain a pawn on the eighth/first rank). However, the choice of promotion piece is much wider than in regular chess:

  • A pawn may promote to a queen, rook, bishop, knight, or king.
  • Promoting to a king is legal and, in many positions, strategically better than promoting to a queen — because a king has the same "must be captured if forced" status as any other piece, but its movement (one square in any direction) makes it easier to manoeuvre into a forced-capture square without overextending.
  • There is no limit on how many kings a player can have. A position with three or four kings on one side is completely legal.
Example promotion choices in the same position:
1. ...a1=Q — creates a powerful piece that is hard to force the opponent to capture (often a liability).
2. ...a1=N — creates a piece with awkward, jumping moves that can land on squares the opponent is forced to take from unexpected angles.
3. ...a1=K — creates a slow, one-square-at-a-time piece that is easy to walk into a forced capture.

Winning, Losing, and Draws

  • You win if you have zero pieces on the board, or if it is your turn and you have no legal move (stalemate).
  • You lose if your opponent achieves either of the above conditions first.
  • Draws still exist via the standard mechanisms: threefold repetition of a position, and the 50-move rule (no capture or pawn move in 50 moves by each side). In practice, draws are rare in Antichess — the mandatory capture rule is so forcing that material is shed quickly and games tend to resolve well before move-count or repetition limits are reached.
