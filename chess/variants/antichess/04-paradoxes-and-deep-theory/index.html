4. Paradoxes and Deep Theory

Antichess is small enough, and forcing enough, that it has been pushed further into "solved" territory than almost any other chess variant. This chapter looks at what that means, the specific result that has been proven, and the deeper reasons why Antichess feels so persistently counter-intuitive even after you've absorbed its strategy and tactics.

Antichess Is a Solved Game

In game theory, a game is "solved" when the outcome under perfect play by both sides is known with certainty. Antichess has been solved for the starting position: with perfect play by both sides, White wins. This result was established in 2016 by Mark Watkins using extensive computer analysis (retrograde analysis and endgame tablebases combined with deep search from the opening), building on earlier partial results and community analysis on Antichess servers.

The proven winning first move for White is:

1. e3 — the proven winning first move for White under perfect play.

This makes Antichess one of the very few chess variants played at a serious competitive level (it has an active community and rating servers) where the theoretical result of the starting position is fully known — comparable in spirit to how Connect Four or checkers (in some variants) have been solved, but unusual for anything resembling chess, where the standard 8x8 game remains far beyond solvability with current technology.

Why Antichess Is More Tractable Than Regular Chess

Several features of Antichess combine to make it dramatically more amenable to exhaustive computer analysis than standard chess:

  • The mandatory capture rule prunes the game tree enormously. In any position where a capture is available, the number of legal moves collapses to just the capture options — often just one or two moves instead of dozens.
  • Games tend to be short. Because material is shed quickly under mandatory captures, the average Antichess game has far fewer total moves than a typical chess game, meaning the search depth required to "see to the end" is smaller.
  • Endgames with very few pieces are common and well-suited to tablebase construction. Much of the solving effort relied on building tablebases for positions with small numbers of pieces and working backward.

None of this means Antichess is a simple game — the search space is still vast by everyday standards — but it sits in a sweet spot where "vast" meets "computationally feasible," unlike full chess.

Intuition Breaks Down Completely

Even strong regular-chess players tend to struggle with Antichess initially, because almost every piece of trained intuition points in the wrong direction:

  • Promoting to a weaker piece (or a king) is often better than queening. A queen's mobility, which is an asset in regular chess, makes it harder to force onto a capturable square in Antichess — so a "downgrade" promotion can be the stronger choice.
  • Moving a piece to a square where it can be captured is often the best move, not a blunder. The entire vocabulary of "hanging a piece" and "blundering material" from regular chess needs to be relabelled as "offering a sacrifice" — a good thing, when timed correctly.
  • Protecting your own pieces is actively harmful. In regular chess, defending a piece that's under attack is almost always correct. In Antichess, defending a piece just means your opponent's capturing piece would also be captured afterward — sometimes good, but often it simply means the piece you wanted gone stays on the board longer.
  • An extra queen is a massive disadvantage. Having more material, of any kind, moves you further from the "zero pieces" goal. A queen up is not an advantage to be nursed — it's a problem to be solved.
  • Stalemate, the most anticlimactic result in regular chess, is the best possible outcome you can engineer for yourself.

A Worked Paradox: The "Useless" King

Consider a king that has been promoted from a pawn late in the game, sitting in the corner of the board with no other pieces nearby. In regular chess, a lone king wandering the board with no support would be an enormous liability — it has no defenders and can be mated. In Antichess:

  • There's no checkmate, so "no defenders" is meaningless.
  • If this king is the last piece on the board for its side, getting it captured (or stalemating it) wins the game immediately.
  • Its slow, one-square movement actually makes it easier to walk deliberately into a forced-capture square than a long-range piece would be.

What looks like the weakest possible piece in the weakest possible position in regular chess is, in Antichess, often exactly where you want your last piece to be.

White's king on a1, alone on the board for White, sits on the long diagonal from Black's bishop on c3. If Black's bishop can reach a1 and must capture there, White reaches zero pieces and wins — the "weakest" piece in the "worst" square is exactly what White wants.

Why It Still Tests Real Chess Understanding

Despite being a near-total inversion of regular chess, Antichess rewards many of the same underlying cognitive skills:

  • Geometric visualisation — understanding which squares a piece attacks, and how that changes after it moves or captures, is just as important as in regular chess.
  • Pattern recognition — recognising forced capture chains, reverse zugzwang setups, and stalemate configurations is analogous to recognising mating patterns and tactical motifs in regular chess.
  • Calculation depth — the ability to look several moves ahead and track exactly how the position evolves is essential, arguably even more so than in regular chess, because forced sequences in Antichess are often longer and more deterministic.

In a sense, Antichess isolates and amplifies the pure calculation and visualisation components of chess skill while stripping away the strategic framework (king safety, piece values, positional play) that regular chess players rely on as shortcuts. This is part of why strong regular-chess players are not automatically strong Antichess players — and why dedicated Antichess specialists exist on variant servers, often with very different rating profiles from their standard chess ratings.

Where to Go From Here

If you want to put this theory into practice, the best way is to play games on a server that supports Antichess (most major online chess platforms offer it as a variant) and consciously apply the framework from this guide: identify forced captures first, look for chains, count pieces rather than material value, and always keep stalemate in mind as a winning resource in the endgame. Revisit the strategic ideas and tactical patterns chapters as you play — the patterns described there become much more intuitive once you've seen them occur in your own games.
