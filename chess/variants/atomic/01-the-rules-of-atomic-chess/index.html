1. The Rules of Atomic Chess

Atomic Chess is played exactly like standard chess — same board, same starting position, same six piece types, same basic moves — with one dramatic exception: every capture causes an explosion. That single rule change ripples through the entire game, turning quiet positional manoeuvres into minefields and making tactical awareness the single most important skill in the variant.

Explosion Mechanics

  • When a piece captures, both the capturing piece and the captured piece are removed from the board immediately.
  • Additionally, all pieces (of either colour) on the 8 squares adjacent to the capture square are also destroyed — except pawns. Pawns are immune to the blast radius, although they are never immune to being captured directly.
  • The explosion happens instantaneously, as part of the same move. A single capture can obliterate queens, rooks, bishops, and knights simultaneously if they happen to be clustered around the capture square.
  • Pawns can capture (diagonally, as usual) and their captures trigger the full explosion in the surrounding squares — but the pawn making the capture itself, like all pawns, can never be destroyed by someone else's nearby explosion. It can only be removed by being captured directly.
  • Castling, en passant, and promotion all work exactly as in standard chess. En passant captures and promotions-by-capture also trigger explosions.

Visualising an Explosion

If White's queen on d4 captures the pawn on d5, the queen and the pawn both vanish, and every highlighted square adjacent to d5 (c4, c5, c6, d6, e4, e5, e6) loses any non-pawn piece sitting there — including the Black king on c6. Note that d4 itself, the queen's origin square, is unaffected; only squares adjacent to the capture square explode.

Notice how much material can disappear from a single move. In the position above, one queen capture removes a queen, a pawn, a rook, a bishop, a knight, and a king from the board. This is why Atomic Chess games tend to be much shorter than classical games — the average decisive game lasts only a fraction of the moves of a standard chess game.

The King Exception

The king has a critical special property that shapes the entire variant: a king can never be captured directly. If a piece attempts to move onto the square occupied by the enemy king (the only way a "capture" of the king could normally happen), that move is illegal — full stop, regardless of whether an explosion would also occur.

The only way to remove the enemy king from the board is therefore to make a different capture somewhere on the board such that the resulting explosion's blast radius happens to include the square the king is standing on. The capturing piece never touches the king directly; the king is destroyed as "collateral damage" of an explosion centred one square away.

Winning Conditions

  • Explode the opponent's king: Make any legal capture adjacent to the enemy king. The resulting explosion destroys the king (along with anything else non-pawn in the blast radius), and the game ends immediately — this is sometimes informally called "atomic checkmate" even though no check needs to have existed beforehand.
  • Checkmate: The standard checkmate rules still apply if a king is in check with no legal way to escape, block, or capture the checking piece. In practice, explosions end most games long before a classical checkmate occurs, but checkmate remains a fully valid way to win.
  • Stalemate, draws, and the 50-move/threefold-repetition rules: These carry over unchanged from standard chess.
  • Note: A player who loses their king — by any means, including their own move — loses the game instantly, regardless of the overall material situation. A side can be completely dominating on the board and still lose in one move if their king is left next to an explodable piece.

Two Kings Can Never Touch

A direct consequence of the explosion rule is that kings can never occupy adjacent squares. If a king were to move next to the opposing king, that "capture" (of nothing, since kings can't be captured directly — but the move would still trigger an explosion centred on the destination square) would blow up both kings at once, which the rules forbid as an illegal move for either side. In practice this means kings repel each other like magnets: they can approach to a "knight's move" distance or stand on the same rank/file/diagonal a square or two apart, but the squares immediately bordering the enemy king are permanently off-limits to your own king.

What Doesn't Change

It's worth being explicit about what is identical to standard chess, since the explosion rule is so dramatic that newcomers sometimes assume everything is different:

  • Piece movement (how a rook, bishop, knight, queen, king, and pawn move) is completely unchanged.
  • Check still exists and still restricts which moves are legal — you cannot make a move that leaves your own king under attack unless that move would simultaneously explode the attacking piece (a unique atomic wrinkle covered in the next chapter).
  • Castling rights, en passant eligibility, and pawn promotion follow the same conditions as standard chess.
  • The game is drawn by stalemate, insufficient material (though "insufficient material" looks very different given how explosions remove pieces), the 50-move rule, and threefold repetition, exactly as in classical chess.
Atomic Chess was popularised by the FICS (Free Internet Chess Server) community and is now one of the official variants on Lichess, alongside Chess960, Crazyhouse, Horde, and others.
