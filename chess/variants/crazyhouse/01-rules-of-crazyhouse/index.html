1. Rules of Crazyhouse

Crazyhouse (sometimes called "Zh" after its ICS code, or "Loop Chess" in some clubs) is one of the most popular chess variants on the major online servers. It was reportedly inspired by Bughouse — the team variant where captured pieces are passed to a partner — but reimagined for a single board and a single pair of players. The result follows all of the standard rules of chess (the same pieces, the same legal moves, the same way of winning by checkmate) with one additional rule that changes everything.

The Drop Rule

Crazyhouse follows all standard chess rules with one fundamental addition: captured pieces join the capturing player's "pocket" and can be dropped onto any empty square as a move.

  • When you capture an opponent's piece, it converts to your colour and goes into your pocket.
  • On any future move, instead of moving a piece on the board, you may drop a piece from your pocket onto any empty square.
  • A dropped piece is immediately active — it can give check or threaten captures on the very move it lands.
  • A drop counts as a complete move — it uses your turn just like moving a piece already on the board, and it is recorded in the move list.

Notation for Drops

Drops are recorded using the @ symbol in algebraic notation. The piece letter is followed by @ and the destination square — for example, N@f7 means "drop a knight on f7", and @e6 (with no letter) means "drop a pawn on e6". A drop that delivers check is written with a + as usual, e.g. Q@h7+.

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6 4.Bxc6 dxc6 — on move 4, White's bishop captures Black's knight on c6, so White's pocket gains a knight (N). On move 4...dxc6, Black's pawn recaptures White's bishop, so Black's pocket gains a bishop (B). Both pieces are now available for drops — for instance, White could later play N@f7 or Black could play B@g4.

Drop Restrictions

Not every drop is legal. The restrictions exist to prevent absurd or game-breaking positions, and every Crazyhouse player must memorise them:

  • Pawns cannot be dropped on rank 1 or rank 8 — only ranks 2 through 7 are valid pawn drop squares. This mirrors the fact that a pawn reaching the back rank in normal play is immediately promoted, so a "pawn" can never legally exist there.
  • A drop cannot give check unless it is checkmate on some rule sets — historically, several Crazyhouse implementations forbade dropping a piece to give check unless that check was immediately checkmate (i.e., there was no legal escape). However, on Lichess (the most popular platform today) and on chess.com, drops that give check are fully allowed, just like any other check. Always confirm the house rules of the server or club you are playing on, since older engines and some correspondence formats still use the stricter rule.
  • You cannot drop a piece onto an occupied square — drops, like all moves, can only target empty squares.
  • Promoted pawns that are captured return to the capturer's pocket as pawns, not as the promoted piece. If a pawn was promoted to a queen and that queen is later captured, the capturing player's pocket receives a pawn, not a queen. This prevents an endless cycle of pawns being promoted, captured, and re-dropped as queens.
  • You may not drop a piece if you have none of that type in your pocket — the pocket is a strict inventory and nothing can be conjured from nothing.

The Pocket

Each player's pocket is displayed alongside the board on every Crazyhouse interface, showing all captured pieces currently available for dropping. The pocket is just as much a part of the position as the pieces on the board — a strong player reads the pocket every move, the same way they read the board.

Checkmate, Stalemate, and the End of the Game

Aside from the drop rule, Crazyhouse ends exactly like standard chess: checkmate, resignation, time forfeit, or the usual draw conditions (stalemate, insufficient material is essentially impossible once drops are considered, threefold repetition, and the 50-move rule). One subtlety: because pieces can be dropped back onto the board indefinitely, games rarely end by "insufficient material" — if a player has any piece in their pocket, they always have mating material available somewhere.

Setup and Time Controls

Crazyhouse games begin from the standard chess starting position — there is no special setup. The variant is most commonly played at fast time controls (bullet and blitz), because the drop rule produces sharp tactical complications that reward quick calculation and pattern recognition over deep, slow strategic planning. That said, Crazyhouse is also played at classical time controls in some online events, where the additional thinking time allows for much deeper pocket-based calculation.
