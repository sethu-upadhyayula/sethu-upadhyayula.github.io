4. How to Approach Chess 960 Strategically

Once you understand how the starting positions are generated (Chapter 2) and how castling works from any square (Chapter 3), the remaining question is purely practical: when the arbiter or the website shows you a brand-new, never-seen-before arrangement of pieces, how should you actually think about the position?

Principles Over Memory

The single most important adjustment when playing Chess960 is to abandon any instinct to "remember" an opening and instead rely entirely on the timeless first principles that govern every chess position:

  • Develop your pieces toward active squares - knights toward the centre (where they control the most squares), bishops onto open diagonals where they are not blocked by your own pawns.
  • Castle early to connect your rooks and tuck your king away safely - though note that in some starting positions the king may already sit on or near g1/c1, or may already be relatively safe, so castling can be less urgent than in standard chess. Re-read Chapter 3 if you're unsure how many moves castling will take in your specific position.
  • Control the centre with pawns and pieces - this principle is completely universal and does not depend on the starting arrangement at all. The squares d4, d5, e4, and e5 are exactly as important in Chess960 as in standard chess.
  • Identify your worst-placed piece and prioritise improving it. With randomised starting setups, some pieces begin awkwardly placed - a rook trapped in a corner behind a knight, a bishop staring into its own pawn chain, or a queen on a square where it blocks its own bishop's development - and these pieces need attention before you can play a "normal" middlegame.

Reading the Starting Position: A Checklist

Spend the first 30-60 seconds of a Chess960 game doing a careful visual audit of the position before making any move. A useful checklist:

  • Where are your bishops pointing? Trace each bishop's diagonal. Is it immediately blocked by one of your own pawns? If so, that pawn (or a different developing move) may need to move first to free the bishop.
  • Which rook is more active? One rook is often closer to an open file or already partially connected to the centre. Identify which rook you'll want to activate first.
  • Is castling immediately possible? Check whether the squares between the king and the relevant rook's destination are already clear, or whether several pieces need to move out of the way first (refer back to Chapter 3's "union of paths must be clear" rule).
  • Are your knights well-placed? A knight already eyeing c3/f3-type squares (or their equivalents) is a head start; a knight in a corner may need two or three moves to get into the game.
  • Where is your queen, and does it block anything? In some positions the queen starts on a square that a bishop or knight wants to pass through - plan the order of development accordingly.
  • What does your opponent's position look like? Since the position is symmetric, anything awkward about your own setup is mirrored in theirs - look for ways to exploit their slow piece before they exploit yours.

Example: Planning From an Unusual Setup

Take the position from Chapter 2 with the arrangement B N R K R B N Q - bishop, knight, rook, king, rook, bishop, knight, queen.

B N R K R B N Q. Both bishops (a1 and f1) are completely blocked by their own pawns on b2 and... actually a1's diagonal runs through b2, and f1's diagonal runs through g2 - both need a pawn to move before the bishop can develop.

A reasonable plan in this position: both bishops are boxed in by pawns on the long diagonals, so an early pawn push (such as the b- or g-pawn one square) frees a bishop immediately. The queen on h1 is in the corner and will need the g- and f-files to clear before it can enter the game - but this also means it is very safe for the time being. The king on d1 is already directly flanked by both rooks, so castling in either direction requires comparatively few preparatory moves once the adjacent knight and bishop step aside - referring back to the castling examples in Chapter 3, this position reaches a castled state quickly compared to setups where the king starts in a corner.

Example: A Position Requiring More Care

Now compare with the position R N B K Q B N R from Chapter 2, where the king starts on d1 and the queen on e1.

R N B K Q B N R. The king on d1 must travel three squares to reach c1 (queenside castling) or all the way past the queen and bishop to reach g1 (kingside castling) - in this position, kingside castling will take noticeably longer to arrange than queenside, since the e1-queen and f1/g1 minor pieces must all develop first.

Here, queenside castling (king d1 → c1, a1-rook → d1) requires only that the b1-knight and c1-bishop step aside - typically two developing moves. Kingside castling (king d1 → g1, h1-rook → f1) requires the queen on e1 and both minor pieces on f1/g1 to move first - often three or four moves. A player who recognises this early can plan to castle queenside more quickly if king safety is a priority, or accept the slower kingside castling in exchange for placing the queen actively on the e-file first.

The "Worst-Placed Piece" Heuristic in Practice

A simple but powerful technique: after your first quick scan of the position, ask "if I could only make one developing move this turn, which piece most needs it?" Often this is a piece in a corner, a piece blocked by its own pawns on both available squares, or a piece that is currently undefended and could become a target. Addressing this piece first - even before grabbing the centre with a pawn - frequently pays off, because a poorly-placed piece can become a long-term liability if neglected for too many moves, exactly as it can in a standard chess opening where a player forgets to develop a particular piece.

The Equalising Effect

Chess960 is widely considered to be more equal between players of very different preparation levels than standard chess. A club player facing a grandmaster in standard chess faces a disadvantage from move one, simply because the grandmaster has spent years studying specific opening lines designed to lead into favourable, well-understood positions. In Chess960, that preparation advantage evaporates entirely - both players are confronting the position for the first time, at the same moment, with the same amount of time to think.

This does not mean skill stops mattering - quite the opposite. It means that the skills which matter are exactly the ones outlined in this chapter: rapid, accurate assessment of an unfamiliar position, sound application of development principles, and tactical alertness from the very first moves. This makes Chess960 an excellent training format for improvement: every single game, from move one, forces you to actually think rather than recall.

Practical Tips for Online and Over-the-Board Play

  • Don't rush the first move. Even in faster time controls, the 30-60 seconds spent on the initial assessment checklist above is rarely wasted - a poor first move based on a "default" habit (like automatically pushing the e-pawn) can leave you worse off if it doesn't fit the specific position.
  • Watch for tactics earlier than usual. Because pieces sometimes start on squares that already eye long diagonals or open files, simple tactical shots (forks, pins, or even short mating nets) can occasionally appear within the first handful of moves in Chess960 - far earlier than would ever happen from the standard starting position.
  • Use your opponent's symmetry against them. Since both sides start with an identical (mirrored) setup, any structural weakness you identify in your own position is mirrored in your opponent's. If you find a plan that exploits "the weakness on f1," your opponent has the same weakness on f8 - consider whether you can create threats against it before they neutralise theirs.
  • Review your games afterward. Because there is no opening book to consult, the most valuable post-game study in Chess960 is simply re-examining your own early-game decisions: did your development plan make sense for that specific position? Could the worst-placed piece have been improved sooner?
The core idea: Chess960 removes memorised theory but changes nothing about chess itself. Development, king safety, centre control, and piece activity remain exactly as important as ever - you simply have to work them out yourself, every single game.
