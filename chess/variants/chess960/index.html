Chess 960 (Fischer Random Chess)

Chess 960 randomises the starting position of the back-rank pieces across 960 legal configurations, making opening preparation obsolete and forcing players to rely on pure understanding rather than memorised lines. Created by Bobby Fischer, it is the most widely played chess variant in the world.

The four chapters below cover the history and motivation behind the variant, the rules that generate the 960 starting positions, the (initially confusing but elegant) rules for castling from any starting square, and practical strategic advice for approaching a position you have never seen before.

1. Why Fischer Created It

Fischer's disillusionment with opening memorisation, the announcement of the rules in 1996, and the philosophical case for randomised starting positions.

2. Starting Position Rules

The three constraints that generate all 960 legal setups, with example diagrams and a look at how the position number is calculated.

3. Castling in Chess 960

Why castling always lands the king and rook on the same squares as standard chess, illustrated with diagrams from unusual starting setups.

4. How to Approach Chess 960 Strategically

Practical first-principles guidance for the opening: development, the worst-placed piece, reading a new position, and the equalising effect.
