Endgame

The endgame is the phase of chess when most of the pieces have been exchanged and only kings, pawns, and perhaps a few pieces remain. Endgame technique is often the differentiating factor between club players and strong players — knowing precisely how to convert a won position or how to save a lost one requires deep knowledge. Grandmaster Reuben Fine wrote: "The endgame is the proving ground of the chess mind."

1. Endgame Principles

The following principles apply broadly across all endgame types. Mastering them reduces the frequency of drawing won positions or losing drawn ones.

Activate the King

In the middlegame, the king is a liability that needs protection. In the endgame, with fewer pieces to attack it, the king becomes a powerful active piece. The player who centralises their king first often wins. The king should march toward the centre (e4, d4, e5, d5) or toward the critical pawn battles as quickly as possible.

Practical tip: As soon as queens come off the board, start centralising your king immediately — every tempo counts.

Push Passed Pawns

A passed pawn — one with no enemy pawns blocking its path or on adjacent files — must be advanced. The further it advances, the more pieces the opponent must dedicate to stopping it. A passed pawn on the 7th rank is a queen waiting to be born.

Rooks Belong Behind Passed Pawns (Tarrasch's Rule)

Whether you own the passed pawn or your opponent does, your rook belongs behind it. If you own it, the rook behind it covers the maximum number of squares as the pawn advances. If your opponent owns it, your rook behind it attacks the maximum number of squares as the pawn advances (applying maximum pressure from behind).

Trade Pieces When Ahead, Keep Them When Behind

  • When ahead in material: Exchange pieces (not pawns). Fewer pieces make your material advantage more decisive — a rook vs nothing is a winning endgame; a rook+queen vs queen+rook is harder to convert.
  • When behind in material: Keep pieces on the board. More pieces create more tactical possibilities and chances for counterplay. Avoid pawn trades (which can create a technically lost pawn endgame).

Create Complications When Behind

In a lost position, don't resign passively — create complications. Stalemate traps, perpetual check threats, and fortress positions can save games from losing positions. Great defensive players like Anatoly Karpov and Vasily Smyslov saved countless games from technically lost positions through resourceful endgame defence.

2. King and Pawn Endgames

King and pawn endgames are the foundation of all endgame study. They are deceptively simple — just kings and pawns — yet they contain profound strategic and tactical depth. Every pawn endgame is essentially a race, and knowing precisely who wins requires understanding opposition, key squares, and the rule of the square.

Opposition

Opposition is the relationship between two kings facing each other on the same file, rank, or diagonal with an odd number of squares between them. The player who does not have to move is said to "have the opposition." Having the opposition is a decisive advantage in king and pawn endgames because the opposing king must give way.

Direct Opposition

Kings face each other on the same file or rank with exactly one square between them. The player to move must step aside, allowing the other king to advance.

Distant Opposition

Kings face each other with 3 or 5 squares between them (always an odd number). The player to move must eventually concede direct opposition. Understanding when distant opposition transitions to direct opposition is crucial in complex king and pawn endings.

Key Squares

Key squares are the specific squares the attacking king must reach to guarantee pawn promotion, regardless of where the defending king is. For a central pawn (e-pawn, d-pawn), the key squares are two ranks ahead of the pawn on the same file and adjacent files. For edge pawns, the key squares are different (and there are fewer of them — which is why edge pawns are often drawn even with an extra pawn).

Rule of the Square

The rule of the square (or "the square rule") is a quick method to determine whether a defending king can catch a passed pawn without using the king. Draw a diagonal from the pawn to the promotion square, then extend the same distance sideways — if the defending king is inside this square when it is their turn to move, they can catch the pawn. If outside, the pawn queens.

K+P endgame: White king e5, pawn e4, Black king e7. Direct opposition — Black to move must give way, White wins. White to move is a draw.

a/h-Pawn Exception

Rook-file pawns (a-pawn and h-pawn) are special cases. Even with a king and pawn vs lone king, the game is a draw if the defending king can reach the corner square (a8 or h8 for the a-pawn or h-pawn). This is because the attacking king cannot drive the defending king out of the corner without stalemating it. For example: White has Ka6 and a-pawn on a5 vs Black Ka8 — Black plays Ka8 and cannot be driven away; Stalemate is inevitable if White pushes the pawn.

Zugzwang

Zugzwang (German: "compulsion to move") occurs when every legal move for the player to move makes their position worse, but they are forced to move. In king and pawn endgames, zugzwang is a constant tactical theme — the side in zugzwang must move their king to a worse square or advance a pawn into a losing position.

3. Rook Endgames

Rook endgames are the most common type of endgame in practice, appearing in roughly 20–30% of all tournament games. They are also technically demanding — countless games that were theoretically drawn have been lost, and positions that looked drawn have been won, due to inaccurate rook endgame technique.

The Lucena Position — The Winning Method

The Lucena position (named after the 15th-century author, though actually worked out later) is the most important winning position in rook endgames: the attacking side has a pawn on the 7th rank, the attacking king is on the 8th rank in front of the pawn, and the attacking rook is somewhere flexible.

The "bridge-building" technique:

  1. The attacking rook moves to a file far away from the pawn (e.g., to the a-file from the g-file)
  2. The attacking king comes down toward the 4th rank, step by step
  3. When the defending rook checks from behind, the attacking rook interposes, blocking the checks
  4. The king is sheltered from checks — the pawn queens

This "rook cutting off the king" method (building a bridge) is essential knowledge for every serious player.

The Philidor Position — The Drawing Method

The Philidor position (named after the legendary 18th-century player François-André Philidor) is the fundamental drawing method when defending a rook endgame with one extra pawn for the opponent.

The technique:

  1. Place the defending rook on the 3rd rank (or 6th rank if Black is defending), cutting off the attacking king
  2. Wait for the attacking side to advance the pawn to the 6th rank
  3. Once the pawn advances to the 6th rank, switch the rook to the back rank and give perpetual checks from behind

The key insight: the defending rook on the 3rd rank is a waiting move that prevents the attacking king from advancing. When the pawn advances, the rook switches to the back rank for eternal harassment from behind. The attacker cannot shelter the king from the checks without abandoning the pawn.

Rook endgame: White rook on a3 cuts off the enemy king — Philidor defensive technique

Active vs Passive Rook

The most important practical principle in rook endgames: keep your rook active. A passive rook that does nothing but defend a pawn is a disaster — your opponent's active rook will pick off your pawns while your rook sits idle. Even in defending positions, look for active counterplay: attack the opponent's pawns, get your rook behind the passed pawn, or harass the enemy king with checks.

Capablanca's rule: "In rook endgames, rooks belong on the 7th rank." If you can get your rook to the 7th rank, do so. Two rooks on the 7th rank are almost always decisive.

4. Minor Piece Endgames

Minor piece endgames — those with bishops and knights alongside pawns — require understanding how the unique movement patterns of these pieces interact with pawn structure.

Bishop vs Knight

The classic positional battle:

  • Bishops prefer open positions with pawns on both sides of the board and open diagonals. In an open endgame, a bishop can control both flanks from a single position — something a knight cannot do.
  • Knights prefer closed positions with fixed pawn chains. A knight on a strong outpost square in a locked position can be worth as much as a rook. Knights can also access squares of both colours, unlike a single bishop.
  • Practical rule: If you have a bishop and your opponent has a knight, try to open the position with pawn breaks. If you have the knight, try to lock the position and create an outpost for your knight.

Opposite-Colour Bishops

When each player has one bishop and they control different colours, the position has a strong drawing tendency. Even with two extra pawns in some positions, the game may be drawn because the defending bishop simply cannot be driven off. Classic rule: "Opposite-colour bishop endgames are drawn."

However, this is an overstatement. With rooks still on the board, the side with extra material often wins because the rooks can attack regardless of square colour. In pure OPP bishop endgames, the drawing tendencies are very real.

Same-Colour Bishops

When both bishops cover the same colour, the stronger player can often win by advancing pawns and using the bishop to support them. The key concepts are the bishop's reach (can it attack the opponent's pawns from afar?) and whether the king can support the pawns' advance.

Knight Endgames Resemble Pawn Endgames

A key practical insight: knight endgames are similar to king-and-pawn endgames. Knights cannot control from afar — they must be close to the action. Zugzwang is common, and the knight's inability to "waste" a move (as a bishop can by moving along a diagonal and back) makes the positions sharper and more decisive than they might appear.

5. Checkmate Techniques

Knowing how to force checkmate with limited material is essential. These are the basic mates every serious player must know cold — in timed games, losing on time from a winning position because you don't know the technique is heartbreaking and avoidable.

K+Q vs K — Staircase and Fence

The king and queen easily mate the lone king, but you must avoid accidental stalemate. The technique:

  1. Drive the king to the edge: Use the queen to fence in the king step by step. After each queen move, the king's territory shrinks.
  2. Bring your king in: Use the king to assist in the final mating pattern.
  3. Deliver mate on the edge/corner: Standard mating patterns include Qd7+ Ke1 Kc2# or similar queen-and-king coordination.
  4. Watch for stalemate! If the enemy king has no moves and is not in check, it is stalemate. Always check before moving.

The "staircase method": use the queen to progressively reduce the king's territory, moving from the centre toward a corner. Maximum 10 moves required with correct play.

K+Q vs K: White queen on d5 restricts the Black king; White king advances to finish the mating net

K+R vs K — The Lawnmower / Box Method

King and rook vs lone king is also a forced win, but it takes more moves (up to 16) and more technique than K+Q. Stalemate avoidance is again essential.

The box method:

  1. Use the rook to cut off the enemy king along a rank or file — place the rook to reduce the king's available territory to a "box."
  2. Move the rook to shrink the box one rank (or file) at a time — hence "lawnmower" or box reduction.
  3. Use your king to assist, driving the enemy king to the edge.
  4. Deliver checkmate on the back rank once the enemy king reaches the edge.

The key: always keep the defending king from approaching your rook. If the defending king gets close to your rook, reposition the rook further away before continuing the box reduction.

K+R vs K: White rook on h4 cuts off the Black king below the 4th rank; White king advances to escort

K+2B vs K — Drive to Any Corner

Two bishops mate the lone king within 19 moves. The key: drive the king to any corner of the board using the combined power of both bishops and the king. The two bishops together can cover all squares and cooperate to progressively restrict the lone king. Standard method: create a "triangle" with the bishops that gradually shrinks the king's territory.

Unlike K+B+N vs K, any corner will do — you do not need to worry about which colour corner to use.

K+B+N vs K — The Hardest Basic Mate

King, bishop, and knight vs lone king is the most difficult of the basic mates. It requires up to 33 moves with correct play and demands precise technique. The key principle: the king must be mated in a corner of the same colour as the bishop. Mating in the wrong-colour corner is much harder and typically requires manoeuvring the king from one corner to another.

The KBNK technique (simplified):

  1. Use the bishop and knight to cut off the enemy king and drive it toward the edge.
  2. Drive the king toward a corner of the bishop's colour.
  3. Use the knight to cover the escape squares; the king and bishop assist.
  4. The mating position typically has the lone king in a corner with knight, bishop, and king cooperating to give checkmate.

Many grandmasters cannot execute this technique perfectly under time pressure. It is worth studying even if you never encounter it in practice, as the coordination of king, bishop, and knight teaches profound lessons about piece harmony.

K+P vs K — All Key Positions

Beyond what was covered in the King and Pawn Endgames section, the main positions to know are:

  • King in front of pawn by two squares: Win if it is not the a/h-pawn
  • King directly in front of pawn: Win with opposition, draw without
  • King one square in front of pawn, side by side: Depends on whose move — use the concept of key squares to determine
  • a/h-pawn with defending king in the corner: Always draw
  • Pawn races: Use the rule of the square to determine who promotes first; if both promote simultaneously, determine who gives check first

The study of king and pawn endings is the most important foundation in endgame training. Every endgame ultimately reduces to a pawn endgame when pieces are exchanged.

Endgame study resources: Dvoretsky's Endgame Manual (advanced), Silman's Complete Endgame Course (all levels), and Capablanca's Chess Fundamentals (classical introduction). Solving endgame studies also develops calculation and imagination even when the positions are beyond practical relevance.
