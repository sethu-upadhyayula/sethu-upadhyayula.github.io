6. The Principle of Two Weaknesses

One of the most important strategic ideas in chess: a single weakness can often be defended indefinitely, but two weaknesses on opposite sides of the board cannot both be defended at once. This principle, central to converting small advantages, explains why so many winning plans involve creating a second front.

How It Works in Practice

Suppose your opponent has one weak pawn that your pieces attack. A good defender can often hold this single weakness with careful piece placement - rooks doubled on the file, a knight blockading, and so on. But if you can create a second weakness elsewhere - for example, by advancing a pawn majority on the opposite wing - the defender's pieces become overextended. Eventually a single move cannot address both weaknesses simultaneously, and material falls.

Practical application: If you have a small, stable advantage (e.g., a slightly better minor piece, or an extra pawn that is currently well-blockaded), do not rush. Improve your position on one side of the board, force the opponent to commit pieces to defence there, and then switch your attention to the other side. The opponent's pieces, tied down, cannot follow.

Minority Attacks Revisited

The minority attack (mentioned briefly under planning templates) is a textbook implementation of the two-weaknesses principle. In the Queen's Gambit Declined Exchange Variation, White often advances the queenside pawn majority - even though it is a minority of pawns (a and b pawns vs Black's a, b, and c) - specifically to provoke a weakness (an isolated or backward pawn on c6 or c-file) that becomes the long-term target, while play later shifts to the kingside.
