3. Piece Activity

In chess, an active piece is worth more than its nominal material value. A rook on an open file, a knight on an outpost, and a bishop with a long open diagonal are all significantly more powerful than their piece equivalents that are passive or blocked.

Good vs Bad Bishop

Beyond the good/bad bishop distinction discussed above, consider:

  • Bishop vs opposite-coloured bishop: When both players have a bishop and they are on different colours, the bishops control entirely separate territory. Such positions are notoriously drawish even with extra pawns.
  • Two bishops (the bishop pair): In open positions, two bishops together are a significant long-term advantage. They cover both colours and dominate long diagonals. Against two knights or a bishop and knight in open play, the bishop pair typically wins the strategic battle.
  • Bishop vs knight: Bishops prefer open, dynamic positions; knights prefer closed, blocked positions where they can hop to stable outpost squares.

Rooks: Open Files and the 7th Rank

Rooks come alive on open files (no pawns of either colour) and half-open files (no friendly pawns, but an enemy pawn). A rook on the 7th rank is especially powerful - it attacks the opponent's pawns along the 7th rank and often traps the enemy king on the back rank. Two rooks on the 7th rank (the "Pig on the 7th") are almost always devastating.

Tarrasch's Rule: "Rooks belong behind passed pawns." Whether you have the passed pawn (to support its advance) or your opponent has it (to stop it), the rook behind the pawn controls the maximum number of squares as the pawn advances.

Knight Centralisation

A knight's power depends dramatically on its position. From d4 or e5 (the ideal central squares), a knight controls 8 squares and participates in both kingside and queenside play. From the edge of the board (a-file or h-file), a knight controls only 2-4 squares and is essentially passive. From a corner (a1, h1, a8, h8), a knight controls only 2 squares. Never place a knight on the rim without excellent reasons.

The Principle of the Worst-Placed Piece

When you have no immediate plan, identify your worst-placed piece and improve it. This simple principle, advocated by Grandmaster Silman, consistently leads to improvements: find the piece doing the least work and reroute it to a more active square. Over many moves, this builds up positional pressure even without a specific plan.
