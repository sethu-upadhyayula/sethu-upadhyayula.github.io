5. Imbalances

Grandmaster Jeremy Silman famously categorised strategic chess around imbalances — the differences between the two positions. Every position has imbalances, and understanding them is the key to creating a plan.

The Seven Imbalances (Silman)

Superior minor piece Pawn structure Space Material Control of key files/squares Lead in development King safety
  • Superior minor piece: Your bishop is better than their knight (or vice versa) due to the pawn structure.
  • Pawn structure: You have fewer weaknesses, a passed pawn, or a better pawn break available.
  • Space: Your pawns control more of the board, restricting the opponent's pieces.
  • Material: You are ahead or behind in material count — including non-standard trades (rook vs two minor pieces, etc.).
  • Control of key files/squares: Your rooks dominate the open file; you have outposts the opponent lacks.
  • Lead in development: Especially in the opening/early middlegame; a lead in development is a temporary advantage that must be exploited quickly before it evaporates.
  • King safety: Your king is better sheltered or the opponent's king is exposed.

Using Imbalances to Create Plans

After identifying the imbalances in a position, create plans that maximise your advantages and minimise your weaknesses. For example: if you have a space advantage but a weak pawn, avoid piece trades (which would give your opponent breathing room) and keep the position closed. If you have the bishop pair in an open position, open more diagonals with pawn breaks.
