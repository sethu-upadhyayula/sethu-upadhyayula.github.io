8. Good vs Bad Bishops, and Strategic Exchange Sacrifices

Revisiting Good and Bad Bishops

A bishop's quality is determined primarily by its own pawns. A "good" bishop has freedom of movement because its own pawns sit on the opposite colour of squares; a "bad" bishop is hemmed in by pawns on its own colour. But there is a further distinction:

  • "Bad" bishop that is actually good: Sometimes a bishop blocked by its own pawns is still valuable as a defender — for example, a dark-squared bishop on g7 behind a pawn chain on f6-g6-h6 (a fianchetto structure) is "bad" by the strict definition but extremely effective at guarding the king.
  • "Good" bishop that is actually bad: A bishop with open diagonals but no targets and no safe outpost can simply have nothing to do — activity requires both mobility and purpose.

The practical lesson: evaluate a bishop by its function in the position, not just by counting pawns on light/dark squares.

Strategic Exchange Sacrifices

An exchange sacrifice — giving up a rook for a bishop or knight (the "exchange") — is one of the most important tools for converting structural and positional advantages into something concrete. Unlike a tactical sacrifice that leads to forced material gain or mate, a strategic exchange sacrifice trades material for long-term factors:

  • Eliminating a key defensive minor piece — e.g., sacrificing Rxc3 to remove a knight that was the only piece controlling a critical central square or defending the king.
  • Securing a dominant outpost for a remaining knight that the opponent's rook cannot challenge.
  • Wrecking the opponent's pawn structure — the recapturing pawn often creates permanent weaknesses (doubled or isolated pawns).
  • Closing the position so that the remaining minor pieces outweigh the material deficit — two well-placed minor pieces can be worth more than a rook and pawn in a closed position.
Tigran Petrosian, World Champion 1963-1969, was famous for prophylactic exchange sacrifices — giving up the exchange not to win material back quickly, but to permanently neutralise an opponent's plan or secure an unassailable outpost. Many of his games feature a rook traded for a knight that, ten or twenty moves later, still has not been "justified" by any direct tactical gain — the gain was entirely positional and long-term.

Evaluating an Exchange Sacrifice

Before sacrificing the exchange, ask:

  1. What concrete weakness or piece am I eliminating?
  2. Do I have at least one minor piece that becomes dominant as a result?
  3. Is the position closing (favouring minor pieces) or opening (favouring rooks)?
  4. Can my opponent's rooks become active quickly enough to outweigh my compensation?
