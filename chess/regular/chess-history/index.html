Chess History

Chess has evolved over roughly fifteen centuries from a slow-moving Indian war game into the rapid, globally-played, computer-saturated game of today. Understanding this history — how the rules changed, how theory developed, and who shaped the game at each stage — gives valuable context to everything else in this Regular Chess section, from Opening Theory to Master Games.

The six chapters below trace chess from its Indian and Persian origins, through its arrival in Europe and the rule changes that created the modern game, the Romantic era of sacrificial attacks, the scientific and Soviet eras, and finally the computer era that continues to reshape the game today.

1. Origins: Chaturanga and Shatranj

Chess's roots in chaturanga (India) and shatranj (Persia).

2. Arrival in Europe and the Birth of Modern Chess

How chess reached Europe and the 'Mad Queen' reform that created the modern rules.

3. The Romantic Era (18th – mid-19th Century)

Philidor, gambit-heavy attacking chess, and the Immortal Game.

4. The Scientific Era and the First World Champions

Steinitz, Lasker, Capablanca, Alekhine, and the rise of hypermodernism.

5. The Soviet School and the Cold War Era

The Soviet chess school, Fischer vs Spassky, and Karpov vs Kasparov.

6. The Computer Era

Deep Blue, AlphaZero, and the modern World Champions.
