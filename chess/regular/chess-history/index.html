Chess History

Chess has evolved over roughly fifteen centuries from a slow-moving Indian war game into the rapid, globally-played, computer-saturated game of today. Understanding this history — how the rules changed, how theory developed, and who shaped the game at each stage — gives valuable context to everything else in this Regular Chess section, from Opening Theory to Master Games.

1. Origins: Chaturanga and Shatranj

Chaturanga (India, c. 6th century)

The earliest known ancestor of chess is chaturanga, played in India around the 6th century CE. The name refers to the four divisions of an ancient Indian army — infantry, cavalry, elephantry, and chariotry — which evolved into the pawn, knight, bishop, and rook of the modern game. The king and a minister or counsellor (the precursor to the queen) completed the army. Movement was much more limited than today: the piece that became the bishop, for example, could only leap two squares diagonally.

Shatranj (Persia, 7th–10th centuries)

Chaturanga spread to Persia, where it became known as shatranj after the Arab conquest of Persia in the 7th century. Shatranj retained much of chaturanga's structure but standardised the rules, and Arabic chess masters began to develop opening theory and compile collections of mansubat (composed problem positions) — the ancestors of the chess compositions covered elsewhere on this site. The terms "check" and "checkmate" derive from the Persian shah ("king") and shah mat ("the king is helpless" or "the king is dead").

2. Arrival in Europe and the Birth of Modern Chess

Spread via the Islamic World (c. 1000)

Chess spread from the Islamic world into Southern Europe — via Moorish Spain and Sicily, and along trade routes — reaching the rest of Europe by around the 10th–11th centuries. Throughout the medieval period, the game remained close to shatranj: the queen (descended from the minister) could move only one square diagonally, and the bishop only two squares diagonally, jumping over any piece in between. Games were correspondingly slow, and openings often took many moves to develop any real activity.

The "Mad Queen" Reform (c. 1475–1500, Spain and Italy)

The single most important transformation in chess history occurred in the late 15th century, primarily in Spain and Italy: the queen's move was changed from one square diagonally to the modern queen's move — any number of squares in any direction. The bishop's move was similarly extended to the modern long-range diagonal move. This dramatically increased the pace and tactical sharpness of the game, to the point that the new game was sometimes called "Queen's Chess" or, less flatteringly, "Mad Queen Chess" (scacchi alla rabiosa) due to how much more powerful and mobile the queen suddenly was. Castling, en passant, and the pawn's initial two-square advance were also introduced or standardised around this period, giving rise to essentially the rules used today.

The First Chess Books

The new, faster game prompted the first printed chess manuals. Luis Ramírez de Lucena's Repetición de Amores y Arte de Ajedrez (1497) is among the earliest printed chess books and contains analysis of openings and endgames under the new rules — including the rook endgame technique that bears his name, the Lucena position, covered in Theoretical Endgames. Later, Ruy López de Segura, a Spanish priest, published an influential treatise in 1561 analysing openings in depth; the Ruy Lopez (Spanish Opening) is named after him and remains one of the most important openings to this day.

3. The Romantic Era (18th – mid-19th Century)

Philidor and the Importance of Pawns

François-André Danican Philidor (1726–1795), the strongest player of his era, was also a composer of music. His 1749 book L'analyse du jeu des Échecs contained the famous observation that "pawns are the soul of chess" — a remarkably modern strategic insight that anticipated much of what is now covered under Middlegame Strategy (pawn structure, passed pawns, and the like). The defensive technique for drawing certain rook endgames, the Philidor position, also bears his name.

The Romantic Style

The late 18th and early-to-mid 19th centuries are known as the Romantic era of chess, characterised by aggressive, gambit-heavy play in which material was routinely sacrificed for rapid development and king attacks. Cafés such as the Café de la Régence in Paris were centres of chess activity, and players competed informally for stakes and prestige rather than in formal tournaments.

The Immortal Game (1851)

The most famous example of Romantic chess is Anderssen vs Kieseritzky, London 1851, known as the Immortal Game. Adolf Anderssen sacrificed both rooks, a bishop, and his queen to deliver checkmate with his three remaining minor pieces — a game still studied today as the archetype of the all-out attacking style of the period, and discussed further in Master Games.

The First International Tournament (London 1851)

The 1851 London tournament, organised by Howard Staunton, was the first international chess tournament and was won by Adolf Anderssen, establishing him as the world's leading player. Staunton himself gave his name to the Staunton chess set design (1849), which remains the standard tournament set design to this day.

4. The Scientific Era and the First World Champions

Wilhelm Steinitz and Positional Chess

Wilhelm Steinitz (1836–1900), the first officially recognised World Chess Champion (from 1886), is regarded as the father of modern positional play. Steinitz argued — against the prevailing Romantic ethos — that material and structural advantages should be accumulated gradually, that attacks are only justified when a position contains the right ingredients (a "right to attack"), and that a sound defensive structure could refute unsound sacrifices. His ideas laid the theoretical groundwork for everything covered in Middlegame Strategy.

Emanuel Lasker (World Champion 1894–1921)

Emanuel Lasker held the World Championship for 27 years — the longest reign in history — combining deep positional understanding with a famously pragmatic, fighting style and strong psychological insight into his opponents.

Capablanca and the "Chess Machine"

José Raúl Capablanca (World Champion 1921–1927) was renowned for his effortless, almost intuitive technique, particularly in the endgame, and was rarely defeated for long stretches of his career. His clarity of style made him, in the eyes of many, the most "natural" player in chess history.

Alekhine and the Hypermodern Movement

In the 1920s, a group of players including Aron Nimzowitsch, Richard Réti, and Savielly Tartakower developed hypermodernism — the idea that the centre could be controlled from a distance with pieces and flank pawns (fianchettoed bishops, openings like the Réti and the King's Indian) rather than occupied directly with pawns, challenging the classical doctrine of immediate central pawn occupation. Alexander Alekhine (World Champion 1927–1935, 1937–1946), a player of immense calculating power and combinative vision, defeated Capablanca in 1927 and remained champion (with one interruption) until his death in 1946 — the only champion to die while holding the title.

5. The Soviet School and the Cold War Era

The Soviet Chess School

From the 1930s onward, the Soviet Union invested heavily in chess as a matter of national prestige, creating an extensive system of coaching, training camps, and competitions. This produced an unbroken run of Soviet (or Soviet-trained) World Champions from 1948 to 1972 and again from 1975 to 2000 (with the brief exception of Bobby Fischer): Mikhail Botvinnik, Vasily Smyslov, Mikhail Tal (the "Magician from Riga," famous for breathtaking sacrificial attacks), Tigran Petrosian (renowned for prophylaxis and defensive mastery), Boris Spassky, Anatoly Karpov, and Garry Kasparov.

The "Match of the Century": Fischer vs Spassky (1972)

In 1972, the American Bobby Fischer defeated the reigning Soviet champion Boris Spassky in Reykjavík, Iceland, in a match that became a global media sensation amid Cold War tensions. Fischer's near-flawless preparation and play — and his subsequent withdrawal from competitive chess — made the match one of the most famous events in the history of the game.

Karpov vs Kasparov

The 1980s and early 1990s were dominated by an intense rivalry between Anatoly Karpov, a positional player in the Capablanca-Petrosian mould, and Garry Kasparov, an aggressive and deeply prepared attacking player. Their five World Championship matches (1984–1990) — including the controversial 1984 match, abandoned after 48 games without a winner — produced some of the deepest opening theory and most analysed games of the 20th century.

6. The Computer Era

Deep Blue vs Kasparov (1997)

In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue defeated World Champion Garry Kasparov in a six-game match — the first time a reigning World Champion had lost a match to a computer under standard time controls. This was a watershed moment, signalling the point at which computer chess engines began to surpass human ability at the highest level.

From Brute Force to Neural Networks

Early strong engines (Deep Blue, and later Stockfish) relied on hand-crafted evaluation functions combined with very deep brute-force search ("alpha-beta" search with extensive pruning). In 2017, DeepMind's AlphaZero demonstrated that a neural network trained purely through self-play (reinforcement learning), combined with Monte Carlo tree search, could reach — and exceed — the strength of the best traditional engines, playing in a strikingly more "human," dynamic style. Modern top engines (including current versions of Stockfish) now incorporate neural network evaluation (NNUE) themselves, blurring the line between the two approaches.

Magnus Carlsen and the Modern Champions

Magnus Carlsen (World Champion 2013–2023) became the highest-rated player in history, known for his exceptional endgame technique and ability to grind out advantages from seemingly equal positions — a style often described as bringing the "engine era" understanding of small advantages into practical play. Carlsen voluntarily relinquished his title in 2023, after which Ding Liren and then Gukesh Dommaraju (who became the youngest undisputed World Champion in history in 2024) have held the title.

Why history matters for study: Each era's dominant ideas remain embedded in modern theory. The Romantic era's tactical motifs underpin Middlegame Tactics; Steinitz's positional principles underpin Middlegame Strategy; the hypermodern openings remain mainstream choices in Opening Theory; and engine-era analysis has reshaped both opening preparation and the precise theoretical positions in Theoretical Endgames. Studying the history of chess is, in many ways, studying the history of how these ideas were discovered.
