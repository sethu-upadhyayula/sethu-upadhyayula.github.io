7. The Modern Era: Engines, the Internet, and the Streaming Boom

Engines Beyond Human Reach

In the years after Deep Blue's 1997 victory over Kasparov, chess engines continued to improve at a rapid pace. Open-source projects such as Stockfish became dramatically stronger than even the best humans, and by the 2010s the gap had grown so wide that engine evaluation became the default lens through which serious players and commentators analyzed any position. The arrival of AlphaZero in 2017, and the open-source Leela Chess Zero project that followed the same neural-network, self-play approach, introduced a new playing style into engine chess: more willing to sacrifice material for long-term initiative, more comfortable with king walks and unconventional piece placements, and less wedded to the static material-and-space evaluations of older brute-force engines. These neural-network engines reshaped opening theory almost overnight, as lines once considered dubious were rehabilitated by engine analysis and quickly adopted by elite grandmasters. Today's strongest engines combine both traditions — Stockfish itself now uses a neural-network evaluation function (NNUE) layered on top of deep alpha-beta search — and engines are now so far ahead of human players that "engine strength" is treated less as a curiosity and more as an objective ceiling against which all human play is measured.

Chess Moves Online

Alongside the rise of engines, the practical experience of playing chess shifted decisively to the internet. Platforms such as Chess.com and Lichess grew into enormous communities offering instant matchmaking, global rating pools, puzzles, and analysis tools that were once the preserve of strong players with expensive software. Online play compressed the time control spectrum toward faster formats — blitz and bullet games measured in minutes or even seconds — and made it possible for millions of players worldwide to compete against opponents of precisely calibrated strength at any hour of the day. The same technology that made engines so strong also made them a constant background presence in online preparation: top players now study openings with engine assistance as a matter of course, and "engine-checking" a line before playing it in a tournament became standard practice. This abundance of accessible engine power also created a persistent shadow issue for online chess — the temptation, and unfortunately the practice, of using engine assistance during games one is supposed to be playing oneself.

The Streaming Boom

In the late 2010s, chess found an unexpected second life as entertainment content. Strong players and commentators began streaming their games and analysis live on platforms such as Twitch and YouTube, building large audiences who enjoyed watching top-level chess explained in real time, often with humor and personality that traditional chess broadcasts had lacked. This trend accelerated dramatically in 2020: with much of the world in pandemic lockdowns, online chess saw an enormous surge in new players, and the Netflix series The Queen's Gambit brought chess imagery and storylines to a mainstream audience that had never previously paid attention to the game. Online platforms responded with high-profile events, cash-prize tournaments, and "banter blitz" style content that blended competition with entertainment. The result was a genuinely new audience for chess — younger, more international, and engaging with the game primarily through screens and streams rather than over-the-board clubs and classical tournaments.

Carlsen, the World Championship Cycle, and New Champions

Magnus Carlsen, World Champion from 2013, dominated this period both in classical chess and across the faster online formats that grew so popular during the streaming boom, becoming the highest-rated player in history and a fixture of online chess content himself. In 2023, Carlsen chose to step back from defending his classical World Championship title, citing a lack of motivation to prepare for the grueling match cycle while remaining highly active in rapid, blitz, and online events. Ding Liren won the resulting championship match to become World Champion, and the title cycle has continued to produce new champions from a new generation of players who grew up training with neural-network engines and competing online from a young age, including Gukesh Dommaraju, who became one of the youngest world champions in the sport's history. Carlsen himself has remained the dominant figure in classical rating lists and in the faster formats even after stepping away from the traditional title defense.

Fair Play in the Engine Era

The same technology that transformed how chess is studied and played also created a new institutional challenge: detecting engine assistance ("cheating") in online games. Major platforms built dedicated fair-play teams and statistical detection systems that compare a player's moves against engine recommendations across thousands of games, flagging suspicious patterns for review. High-profile cheating controversies — both online and, in a few sensational cases, accusations involving over-the-board play — have made fair-play enforcement a permanent and prominent part of the modern chess ecosystem, alongside anti-cheating measures such as electronic device screening and broadcast delays at major over-the-board events. This arms race between detection systems and would-be cheaters is now simply part of how competitive chess operates, a fitting coda to an era defined above all by the partnership — and tension — between human players and the machines that have become inseparable from the game.

Why history matters for study: Each era's dominant ideas remain embedded in modern theory. The Romantic era's tactical motifs underpin Tactical Play; Steinitz's positional principles underpin Strategic Play; the hypermodern and engine-rehabilitated openings remain mainstream choices in Opening Theory; and engine-era analysis has reshaped both opening preparation and the precise theoretical positions in Endgame Theory. Studying the history of chess is, in many ways, studying the history of how these ideas were discovered.
