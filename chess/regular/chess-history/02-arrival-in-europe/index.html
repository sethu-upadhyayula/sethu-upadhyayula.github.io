2. Arrival in Europe and the Birth of Modern Chess

Spread via the Islamic World (c. 1000)

Chess spread from the Islamic world into Southern Europe - via Moorish Spain and Sicily, and along trade routes - reaching the rest of Europe by around the 10th-11th centuries. Throughout the medieval period, the game remained close to shatranj: the queen (descended from the minister) could move only one square diagonally, and the bishop only two squares diagonally, jumping over any piece in between. Games were correspondingly slow, and openings often took many moves to develop any real activity.

The "Mad Queen" Reform (c. 1475-1500, Spain and Italy)

The single most important transformation in chess history occurred in the late 15th century, primarily in Spain and Italy: the queen's move was changed from one square diagonally to the modern queen's move - any number of squares in any direction. The bishop's move was similarly extended to the modern long-range diagonal move. This dramatically increased the pace and tactical sharpness of the game, to the point that the new game was sometimes called "Queen's Chess" or, less flatteringly, "Mad Queen Chess" (scacchi alla rabiosa) due to how much more powerful and mobile the queen suddenly was. Castling, en passant, and the pawn's initial two-square advance were also introduced or standardised around this period, giving rise to essentially the rules used today.

The First Chess Books

The new, faster game prompted the first printed chess manuals. Luis Ramírez de Lucena's Repetición de Amores y Arte de Ajedrez (1497) is among the earliest printed chess books and contains analysis of openings and endgames under the new rules - including the rook endgame technique that bears his name, the Lucena position, covered in Endgame Theory. Later, Ruy López de Segura, a Spanish priest, published an influential treatise in 1561 analysing openings in depth; the Ruy Lopez (Spanish Opening) is named after him and remains one of the most important openings to this day.
