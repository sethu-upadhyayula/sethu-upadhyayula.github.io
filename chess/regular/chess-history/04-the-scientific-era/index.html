4. The Scientific Era and the First World Champions

Wilhelm Steinitz and Positional Chess

Wilhelm Steinitz (1836-1900), the first officially recognised World Chess Champion (from 1886), is regarded as the father of modern positional play. Steinitz argued - against the prevailing Romantic ethos - that material and structural advantages should be accumulated gradually, that attacks are only justified when a position contains the right ingredients (a "right to attack"), and that a sound defensive structure could refute unsound sacrifices. His ideas laid the theoretical groundwork for everything covered in Strategic Play.

Emanuel Lasker (World Champion 1894-1921)

Emanuel Lasker held the World Championship for 27 years - the longest reign in history - combining deep positional understanding with a famously pragmatic, fighting style and strong psychological insight into his opponents.

Capablanca and the "Chess Machine"

José Raúl Capablanca (World Champion 1921-1927) was renowned for his effortless, almost intuitive technique, particularly in the endgame, and was rarely defeated for long stretches of his career. His clarity of style made him, in the eyes of many, the most "natural" player in chess history.

Alekhine and the Hypermodern Movement

In the 1920s, a group of players including Aron Nimzowitsch, Richard Réti, and Savielly Tartakower developed hypermodernism - the idea that the centre could be controlled from a distance with pieces and flank pawns (fianchettoed bishops, openings like the Réti and the King's Indian) rather than occupied directly with pawns, challenging the classical doctrine of immediate central pawn occupation. Alexander Alekhine (World Champion 1927-1935, 1937-1946), a player of immense calculating power and combinative vision, defeated Capablanca in 1927 and remained champion (with one interruption) until his death in 1946 - the only champion to die while holding the title.
