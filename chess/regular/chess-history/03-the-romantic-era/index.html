3. The Romantic Era (18th - mid-19th Century)

Philidor and the Importance of Pawns

François-André Danican Philidor (1726-1795), the strongest player of his era, was also a composer of music. His 1749 book L'analyse du jeu des Échecs contained the famous observation that "pawns are the soul of chess" - a remarkably modern strategic insight that anticipated much of what is now covered under Strategic Play (pawn structure, passed pawns, and the like). The defensive technique for drawing certain rook endgames, the Philidor position, also bears his name.

The Romantic Style

The late 18th and early-to-mid 19th centuries are known as the Romantic era of chess, characterised by aggressive, gambit-heavy play in which material was routinely sacrificed for rapid development and king attacks. Cafés such as the Café de la Régence in Paris were centres of chess activity, and players competed informally for stakes and prestige rather than in formal tournaments.

The Immortal Game (1851)

The most famous example of Romantic chess is Anderssen vs Kieseritzky, London 1851, known as the Immortal Game. Adolf Anderssen sacrificed both rooks, a bishop, and his queen to deliver checkmate with his three remaining minor pieces - a game still studied today as the archetype of the all-out attacking style of the period, and discussed further in Master Games.

The First International Tournament (London 1851)

The 1851 London tournament, organised by Howard Staunton, was the first international chess tournament and was won by Adolf Anderssen, establishing him as the world's leading player. Staunton himself gave his name to the Staunton chess set design (1849), which remains the standard tournament set design to this day.
