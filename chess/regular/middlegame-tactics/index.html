Middlegame Tactics

Tactics are short, concrete sequences of moves that win material or force checkmate through forcing play — checks, captures, and threats. While strategy is about long-term planning, tactics are about precise calculation. At every level of chess, the player who spots tactics first wins material and games. Grandmaster Siegbert Tarrasch famously said: "Before the endgame, the gods have placed the middlegame."

Tactical proficiency is built through pattern recognition: once you have seen a fork, pin, or skewer in training, you will spot it in your own games. Regular puzzle practice (solving 10–20 tactics per day) is one of the most efficient ways to improve at chess.

1. Forks

A fork occurs when one piece attacks two or more enemy pieces simultaneously. The opponent can only respond to one threat at a time, so the attacker wins material.

Knight Fork

The knight fork is the most feared and most common type of fork. Because the knight attacks squares of both colours and moves in a unique L-shape, it can attack a king and queen (or any two valuable pieces) in positions that are difficult to spot. Many games have been decided by a knight fork winning the queen.

Classic pattern: A knight on e7 or c7 (after moving from elsewhere) can fork the king on e8 and rook on a8. The opponent must deal with the check, and the rook is lost.

Knight on d5 forks the Black queen on b6 and rook on f4

Other Forks

  • Pawn fork: A pawn advances to attack two pieces — extremely common and hard to spot because players underestimate pawns.
  • Queen fork: The queen's range makes queen forks easy, but they are often anticipated because the queen's attacks are visible from afar.
  • Rook fork: A rook on an open file or rank attacks two pieces in a line.
  • Bishop fork: A bishop on a diagonal attacks two pieces; less common but still important to know.
  • Royal fork: Any piece that simultaneously attacks the king and queen — since the king must escape check, the queen is lost.

How to Set Up Forks

Most forks do not arise naturally — they must be created through preparatory moves. Common setups include:

  • Forcing an enemy king to a specific square with a check, then forking with a knight
  • Deflecting a piece from guarding a key square, then delivering a fork
  • Sacrificing a piece to create the fork in the resulting position

2. Pins

A pin occurs when a sliding piece (queen, rook, or bishop) attacks a piece, and there is a more valuable piece directly behind it on the same line. The "pinned" piece cannot (or should not) move without exposing the more valuable piece to capture.

Absolute Pin

When the piece behind the pinned piece is the king, the pin is absolute — the pinned piece cannot legally move because doing so would put the king in check (an illegal move). Absolute pins are powerful because the pinned piece is immobilised.

Example: White bishop on b3, Black knight on f7 (pinned to Black king on h5). The knight on f7 cannot move — it is absolutely pinned. White can pile more attackers onto f7 to win material.

Relative Pin

When the piece behind the pinned piece is a queen, rook, or another valuable piece (not the king), the pin is relative — the pinned piece can move, but doing so loses material. Relative pins are still extremely powerful because the opponent is forced to choose between two evils.

White bishop on b3 pins the Black knight on f7 against Black's king on h8

Exploiting Pins

  • Pile on the pinned piece: Attack it with more pieces than your opponent can defend it. Since the pinned piece cannot move to help defend, you effectively have a piece advantage in that sector.
  • Break the pin: Sometimes you are the one pinned. Defensive ideas include moving the piece behind the pin out of the line, interposing a piece, or attacking the pinning piece to drive it away.
  • Exploiting absolute pins: If a piece is absolutely pinned, use it as a target — attack it with pawns.

3. Skewers

A skewer is the reverse of a pin. A sliding piece attacks a valuable piece in the front — that piece moves to safety, and the lesser piece behind it is captured. The most common skewers involve the king being attacked (forcing it to move) with a rook, queen, or bishop, and then capturing a piece it was in front of.

White rook on b1 skewers the Black king on b8. After the king moves, the rook on b4 is captured.

Common Skewer Patterns

  • King–Rook skewer: Most common; the king is forced off a file or rank, the rook behind is taken.
  • King–Queen skewer: If a king is ever in line with its own queen, a rook or bishop can execute a skewer.
  • Queen–Rook skewer: Even without king involvement, any time a queen and rook are lined up on a diagonal, file, or rank, a bishop or rook can execute a skewer.

Key insight: Skewers are often missed because players focus on defending the front piece without noticing the piece behind it.

4. Discovered Attacks

A discovered attack occurs when one piece moves, uncovering an attack from another piece behind it. Both pieces can simultaneously create threats, making discovered attacks extremely powerful because the opponent must deal with two threats at once.

Discovered Check

When the piece behind the moving piece is attacking the king, the result is a discovered check. The moving piece is free to go anywhere — including capturing another piece — while the opponent must deal with the discovered check. This is one of the most devastating tactics in chess.

Double Check

The most powerful tactical weapon: double check occurs when the moving piece also gives check in addition to the discovered check. The king is simultaneously attacked by two pieces. There is only one way to escape: the king must move. Blocking is impossible (would only block one check), and capturing is impossible (cannot capture two pieces in one move). Double check is notated with ++.

Moving the knight on d6 creates a discovered attack from the White rook on b1 against the Black bishop on b8

Key Principle

When you spot a piece that is lined up behind one of your own pieces along a rank, file, or diagonal, always consider moving the front piece to simultaneously attack or create a second threat. The combination is especially powerful when the front piece moves with check or captures something valuable.

5. Deflection

Deflection (also called "decoy to a bad square" in some contexts) is a tactical device where you force a key defensive piece to abandon its defensive duty. You sacrifice material (or simply make a forcing move) to drive the defender away from the square or line it needs to guard.

Classic pattern: Black has a queen on d7 defending a bishop on d4. White plays Rxd7!, forcing Black's queen to recapture Qxd7 — away from its defence of d4. White then takes the bishop with material gain.

Recognising Deflection Opportunities

Ask yourself: "Which piece is performing a critical defensive function?" If a single piece is the sole defender of a mate threat, a back-rank weakness, or a valuable piece, consider how to drive it away. Common deflection ideas:

  • Offering a sacrifice to a piece that must be captured (e.g., a rook sacrifice on the first rank to drag the king away from the defence)
  • Checks that force the king to a bad square where it is less active or exposes other weaknesses
  • Tactical sequences that force the opponent to address one threat at the cost of another

6. Decoy (Attraction)

A decoy (or attraction) tactic lures an enemy piece — most often the king — to a specific square where it can be exploited. Unlike deflection (which removes a defender), decoy positively attracts the target piece to a bad position.

Classic queen sacrifice pattern: White plays Qxh7+!, sacrificing the queen. Black must capture: Kxh7. Now a double check or a fork delivers checkmate or wins decisive material. The king has been attracted to h7, exactly where the mating net is ready.

King Decoys

Decoying the king is especially powerful because the king's movements are restricted — once lured to a bad square, it is often permanently exposed. Common decoy patterns include:

  • Sacrificing a rook on h8 to lure the Black king from g8 to h8, then delivering a knight fork or bishop check
  • Queen sacrifices that lure the king to the front of its own pawns, enabling a smothered mate
  • Sacrifices on f7 (the weakest square early in the game) that drag the king into the centre

7. Overloading and Removing the Defender

Overloading

A piece is overloaded when it has two (or more) defensive duties that it cannot simultaneously fulfil. By exploiting both duties at once, you force the piece to abandon one, winning material or delivering checkmate.

Example: Black queen on d7 is defending both a rook on d4 and preventing Qxh7#. White plays 1.Rxd4! — if Black recaptures Qxd4, then Qxh7# is checkmate. If Black doesn't take, White is up a rook. The queen was overloaded.

Removing the Defender

Removing the defender (also called "undermining") is the tactical device of eliminating the piece that guards a target. This can be done by:

  • Capturing the defender directly — even if you must exchange a more valuable piece, the resulting position may be winning.
  • Driving the defender away with a check or a threat it must address.
  • Blocking the defender with an interposing piece (less common).

Typical pattern: Black has a knight on f6 defending against a back-rank mate. White plays Bxf6, removing the defender. Even if Black recaptures, the back-rank mate follows.

8. Back-Rank Mate

The back-rank mate (or "back-rank weakness") is one of the most common tactical themes in practical chess. A king that has castled but has not advanced a pawn to create an escape square is vulnerable to checkmate along the 8th rank (for Black) or 1st rank (for White) by the opponent's rook or queen.

Back-rank pattern: White Rd8+ delivers checkmate as the Black king on g8 is trapped by its own pawns

Creating "Luft"

The defensive remedy is called luft (German for "air") — advancing one of the pawns in front of the castled king by one square (typically h3 or g3 for White). This creates an escape square for the king and eliminates the back-rank vulnerability. This small prophylactic move takes only one tempo and often saves the game.

Back-Rank Combinations

Experienced players use back-rank weakness as the backdrop for complex combinations. Common patterns:

  • Sacrificing a rook to deflect the opponent's rook from its back-rank defence, then delivering mate
  • Pinning the opponent's rook that defends the back rank
  • Creating a "back-rank threat" that forces weakening pawn moves, then switching attack

9. Finding Tactics in Your Games

Knowing the tactical patterns is not enough — you also need a system for searching for tactics during your games. Here is a practical framework used by strong players:

Tactical Alarm Bells

Be alert to tactics whenever you notice:

  • Undefended pieces — any undefended (or underdefended) piece is a potential target. Count defenders and attackers on all pieces.
  • Exposed kings — a king without pawn shelter or in the centre invites direct attack.
  • Alignment — pieces on the same rank, file, or diagonal create skewer and pin opportunities.
  • Overloaded pieces — look for pieces with two defensive duties.
  • Weak back rank — check if either king is vulnerable to a back-rank mate.

The Candidate Move Approach

When calculating tactics, use the candidate move method (introduced by Alexander Kotov in "Think Like a Grandmaster"):

  1. Look for checks first — checks are forcing and limit the opponent's responses.
  2. Then captures — every capture can lead to a tactic chain.
  3. Then threats — moves that threaten to win material on the next move.
  4. Calculate each candidate move to the end (quiescence — no more captures or checks).
  5. Evaluate the resulting position and choose the best line.

Practical Training

Tactical ability improves with consistent practice. Recommended approaches:

  • Solve 10–20 puzzles per day on Lichess, Chess.com, or a tactics book
  • Study your own game mistakes — every blunder in your games was a missed tactic by you or your opponent
  • Solve positions at a slow pace initially — speed comes with pattern recognition
  • Review classic combination games (Mikhail Tal's games are an excellent resource for spectacular tactical play)
Tactical hierarchy (force first):
1. Checks    2. Captures    3. Threats

When you see a good move — look for a better one (Lasker's rule)
