3. Zugzwang

Zugzwang (German for "compulsion to move") describes a situation where a player would prefer to pass but is forced to move, and every available move worsens their position. Zugzwang is rare in the middlegame (there are usually enough pieces to make a harmless waiting move) but is a recurring and decisive theme in endgames, especially king and pawn endings.

Recognising Zugzwang

A position is a candidate for zugzwang when one side has very few legal moves, and most of those moves concede something — a square, a pawn, or the opposition. King and pawn endgames are full of "mutual zugzwang" positions, where the side not to move would win, but having to move loses (or draws) instead.

The Opposition

The opposition is the most fundamental zugzwang-related concept: when the two kings face each other with one square between them on the same file, rank, or diagonal, the player not to move is said to "have the opposition." Having the opposition often allows a king to force its way past the opposing king to support a pawn's promotion. Key and corresponding squares, and the parity (odd/even) of the distance between the kings, determine who can win the opposition in a given position — this is the conceptual foundation underlying the King and Pawn endgame theory covered in Endgame Theory.

Triangulation

Triangulation is a technique for "passing" the move to the opponent: the king manoeuvres in a triangle (e.g., e3-e2-d2 instead of directly to d2), using one extra tempo so that the same position is reached but with the opponent to move — converting a position where you would be in zugzwang into one where your opponent is.
