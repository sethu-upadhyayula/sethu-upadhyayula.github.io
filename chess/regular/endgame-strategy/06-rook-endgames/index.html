6. Rook Endgame Strategy

Rook endgames are by far the most common type of endgame in practical play, and entire books are devoted to them. A few strategic guidelines, beyond the specific theoretical positions in Endgame Theory:

  • Activity over material: A rook endgame a pawn down with a much more active rook is often better — and sometimes winning — compared to being a pawn up with a passive rook. Rook activity is paramount.
  • Rooks belong behind passed pawns — both your own (to support its advance) and the opponent's (to attack it from behind, where it cannot be driven away as easily). This is one of the most reliable rules in all of chess endgame theory.
  • Cut off the enemy king: Using your rook to confine the opponent's king to a restricted set of files or ranks is a powerful technique, both in attack (preventing the king from helping its own pawns) and defence (the Lucena and Philidor positions both hinge on king restriction).
  • Checks from behind: A rook giving checks from behind an advancing king (rather than from the side) often allows the defender to hold draws that would otherwise be lost — the geometry of "checking distance" is a recurring theme.
