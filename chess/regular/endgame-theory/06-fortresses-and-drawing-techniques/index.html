6. Fortresses and Drawing Techniques

So far this chapter has mostly asked "who wins, and how?" But many of the most important practical endgames are about the opposite question: how does the materially worse side survive? A fortress is a defensive setup so solid that the stronger side, despite an extra piece or several extra pawns, cannot break through — every attempt to make progress either achieves nothing or actively helps the defender. Recognising a fortress means recognising that a position is a draw even though a naive material count says otherwise.

What Makes a Fortress?

A fortress typically has one or more of these features:

  • An impenetrable pawn structure that the attacker's king and pieces cannot cross or infiltrate.
  • A defending king that cannot be dislodged from a key square or zone, often because doing so would require the attacker to give up the very material advantage they have.
  • Stalemate resources lurking just beneath the surface — the attacker must constantly avoid moves that would leave the defender with no legal moves.

Fortresses are easy to walk into accidentally as the attacker (trading off the wrong piece, or pushing a pawn too far) and easy to miss as the defender (resigning a position that was actually a dead draw). Recognising the pattern is therefore valuable for both sides.

Fortress 1: The Wrong-Coloured Bishop and Rook Pawn

This is the most famous fortress in endgame theory, building directly on the a/h-pawn exceptions from Chapters 1 and 5. If the stronger side has an extra rook pawn and a bishop, but that bishop is the wrong colour for the pawn's promotion square (i.e. it cannot control that corner square), the defending king can simply sit in the corner and never be evicted.

The bishop cannot attack the corner square, so it cannot help drive the king away, and the attacking king cannot approach without being stalemated or shouldered away by the defending king. The extra pawn (and even extra material elsewhere) is simply useless if it cannot be converted in this corner.

The classic wrong-bishop fortress: White's a-pawn promotes on a8, a light square, but White's bishop on c1 is a dark-squared bishop and can never control a8. The Black king simply oscillates between a8 and b8 (or a7/b7) and can never be forced away — a dead draw despite White's extra piece and pawn.

Note that if White's bishop instead operated on the light squares (controlling a8), the position would be a comfortable win — the bishop could help the king evict its counterpart from the corner. The "wrong" vs "right" distinction is entirely about which colour of square the promotion square sits on.

Fortress 2: Blocked Pawn Chains and a "Bad" Minor Piece

When pawn chains lock together so that neither side can open the position, a minor piece that is hemmed in by its own pawns ("bad bishop," for instance) can become irrelevant — and an extra pawn elsewhere may simply be unable to make progress because there is nowhere for it to go. If every pawn break leads to exchanges that simplify into a known draw, the position is a fortress regardless of the raw material count.

A blocked centre: Black's extra pawn (the e5/d5/f6 chain) cannot advance without trading off, and Black's bishop on c8 is boxed in by its own pawns on d5 and f6, with no good diagonal to develop on. White's knight on f3 covers the key central squares (d4, e5, g5), and the position is a fortress draw — Black's "extra" structure cannot be converted into anything.

Fortress 3: Rook vs Rook and Pawn

Not every extra pawn in a rook endgame matters. If the extra pawn is doubled, isolated, and blockaded by the defender's king and rook, the defending side can sometimes build a fortress simply by keeping the rooks passively placed and the king tucked in front of the dangerous pawn, refusing all exchanges that would let the pawn run.

Black has an extra pawn (doubled h-pawns, one of which is permanently weak), but White's king and rook sit safely on the f-file with no entry points for Black's king or rook. Trading rooks would lead to a drawn king and pawn ending; avoiding the trade leaves Black with no way to make progress on the kingside. The position holds as a fortress.

General Drawing Techniques

Beyond outright fortresses, the defending side in a lost or difficult endgame has several recurring resources:

  • Perpetual check: covered in the queen endgame chapter — if your king is safe and the opponent's is not, a repeating sequence of checks can force a draw even from a materially lost position. (See Chapter 5.)
  • Stalemate tricks: deliberately reducing your own material or manoeuvring your king to a corner where the only legal "improving" moves for the attacker leave you with no moves at all. The K+Q vs K+P and wrong-bishop fortresses above are really just sophisticated, static versions of this idea.
  • Fortress construction: when material loss is unavoidable, steer the game toward one of the known fortress patterns — wrong-coloured bishop, blocked structures, or a safely blockaded extra pawn — rather than fighting on in a position with no drawing chances at all.
  • Simplification to known draws: trading down to a textbook drawn endgame (e.g. the a/h-pawn K+P endings of Chapter 1, or the Philidor position of Chapter 2) is often the cleanest defensive plan, even at the cost of further material.
Key takeaway: before resigning a materially inferior endgame, always check whether the position matches a known fortress pattern. And before committing to a plan as the stronger side, make sure your technique doesn't walk straight into one of these draws.
