2. Rook Endgames

Rook endgames are the most common type of endgame in practice, appearing in roughly 20–30% of all tournament games. They are also technically demanding — countless games that were theoretically drawn have been lost, and positions that looked drawn have been won, due to inaccurate rook endgame technique.

The Lucena Position — The Winning Method

The Lucena position (named after the 15th-century author, though actually worked out later) is the most important winning position in rook endgames: the attacking side has a pawn on the 7th rank, the attacking king is on the 8th rank in front of the pawn, and the attacking rook is somewhere flexible.

The "bridge-building" technique:

  1. The attacking rook moves to a file far away from the pawn (e.g., to the a-file from the g-file)
  2. The attacking king comes down toward the 4th rank, step by step
  3. When the defending rook checks from behind, the attacking rook interposes, blocking the checks
  4. The king is sheltered from checks — the pawn queens

This "rook cutting off the king" method (building a bridge) is essential knowledge for every serious player.

The Philidor Position — The Drawing Method

The Philidor position (named after the legendary 18th-century player François-André Philidor) is the fundamental drawing method when defending a rook endgame with one extra pawn for the opponent.

The technique:

  1. Place the defending rook on the 3rd rank (or 6th rank if Black is defending), cutting off the attacking king
  2. Wait for the attacking side to advance the pawn to the 6th rank
  3. Once the pawn advances to the 6th rank, switch the rook to the back rank and give perpetual checks from behind

The key insight: the defending rook on the 3rd rank is a waiting move that prevents the attacking king from advancing. When the pawn advances, the rook switches to the back rank for eternal harassment from behind. The attacker cannot shelter the king from the checks without abandoning the pawn.

The Philidor position: Black's rook on f6 holds the 6th rank, preventing White's king from advancing — a classic drawing technique in rook-and-pawn endgames

Active vs Passive Rook

The most important practical principle in rook endgames: keep your rook active. A passive rook that does nothing but defend a pawn is a disaster — your opponent's active rook will pick off your pawns while your rook sits idle. Even in defending positions, look for active counterplay: attack the opponent's pawns, get your rook behind the passed pawn, or harass the enemy king with checks.

Capablanca's rule: "In rook endgames, rooks belong on the 7th rank." If you can get your rook to the 7th rank, do so. Two rooks on the 7th rank are almost always decisive.

The Vancura Position

The Vancura position is a key drawing technique against a rook-pawn (a- or h-pawn) on the 7th rank, even when the defending king is far away — a situation where the Philidor technique above does not apply because the pawn is already too advanced. The defending rook goes to the 6th rank (the b6 square against an a-pawn, for example) to harass the attacking king from the side, while a second rook check from behind picks off the pawn at the right moment. The Vancura position is drawn even though the defending king may be cut off on the back rank, far from the action — a surprising and important practical resource.
