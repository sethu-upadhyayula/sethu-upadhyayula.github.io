1. King and Pawn Endgames

King and pawn endgames are the foundation of all endgame study. They are deceptively simple — just kings and pawns — yet they contain profound strategic and tactical depth. Every pawn endgame is essentially a race, and knowing precisely who wins requires understanding opposition, key squares, and the rule of the square.

Opposition

Opposition is the relationship between two kings facing each other on the same file, rank, or diagonal with an odd number of squares between them. The player who does not have to move is said to "have the opposition." Having the opposition is a decisive advantage in king and pawn endgames because the opposing king must give way.

Direct Opposition

Kings face each other on the same file or rank with exactly one square between them. The player to move must step aside, allowing the other king to advance.

Distant Opposition

Kings face each other with 3 or 5 squares between them (always an odd number). The player to move must eventually concede direct opposition. Understanding when distant opposition transitions to direct opposition is crucial in complex king and pawn endings.

Key Squares

Key squares are the specific squares the attacking king must reach to guarantee pawn promotion, regardless of where the defending king is. For a central pawn (e-pawn, d-pawn), the key squares are two ranks ahead of the pawn on the same file and adjacent files. For edge pawns, the key squares are different (and there are fewer of them — which is why edge pawns are often drawn even with an extra pawn).

Rule of the Square

The rule of the square (or "the square rule") is a quick method to determine whether a defending king can catch a passed pawn without using the king. Draw a diagonal from the pawn to the promotion square, then extend the same distance sideways — if the defending king is inside this square when it is their turn to move, they can catch the pawn. If outside, the pawn queens.

K+P endgame: White king e5, pawn e4, Black king e7. Direct opposition — Black to move must give way, White wins. White to move is a draw.

a/h-Pawn Exception

Rook-file pawns (a-pawn and h-pawn) are special cases. Even with a king and pawn vs lone king, the game is a draw if the defending king can reach the corner square (a8 or h8 for the a-pawn or h-pawn). This is because the attacking king cannot drive the defending king out of the corner without stalemating it. For example: White has Ka6 and a-pawn on a5 vs Black Ka8 — Black plays Ka8 and cannot be driven away; Stalemate is inevitable if White pushes the pawn.

Zugzwang

Zugzwang (German: "compulsion to move") occurs when every legal move for the player to move makes their position worse, but they are forced to move. In king and pawn endgames, zugzwang is a constant tactical theme — the side in zugzwang must move their king to a worse square or advance a pawn into a losing position.
