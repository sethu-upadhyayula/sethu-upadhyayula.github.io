2. Opening Principles

Before memorising any opening line, master these six principles. They apply in every game, at every level. Grandmasters follow them instinctively; when they deviate, they have a concrete reason. You should too.

1. Control the Centre

The four central squares — e4, d4, e5, d5 — are the most valuable real estate on the board. A piece in the centre controls more squares than one on the edge. A pawn in the centre restricts the opponent's pieces. The classic first moves 1.e4 and 1.d4 stake a claim immediately; hypermodern systems such as 1.Nf3 and 1.c4 control the centre with pieces rather than pawns but pursue the same goal.

Good: 1. e4  e5  2. Nf3  Nc6  3. Bc4 — all moves develop toward the centre
Bad: 1. e4  e5  2. Na3? — knight heads to the rim where it controls fewer squares

Italian Game after 3.Bc4 — every White move develops a piece toward the centre

2. Develop Your Pieces

Bring knights and bishops off the back rank before moving rooks or queens. Each undeveloped piece is a liability: it cannot participate in the fight. A useful rule: develop a new piece every move for the first six to eight moves. Prioritise knights before bishops (knights need more moves to reach good squares), and develop toward the centre.

Don't move the same piece twice unless there is a concrete reason (a forced tactical sequence or a piece that was attacked). Moving the same piece twice in the opening gives your opponent a free developing move.

Good: develop Nf3, Nc3, Bc4, Be3 — four pieces in four moves
Bad: 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4 Bc5 4.Nc3 Nf6 5.d3 — fine so far
     But: 5.Bc4-b3? — retreating the bishop loses a tempo

3. Castle Early

Castling achieves two goals simultaneously: it tucks the king away from the centre (where it will be attacked by open files and diagonals) and it connects the rooks. Try to castle by move eight to ten. A king stuck in the centre is a chronic weakness in the middlegame. Both sides are often racing to castle before launching an attack.

After 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.0-0 — White castles move five.
The rooks connect; the king is safe; White can now play d3, Re1, and prepare d4.

4. Don't Bring the Queen Out Too Early

The queen is powerful but easily harassed. An early queen excursion typically loses time as the opponent chases it with developing moves. The exception is the Scholar's Mate (2.Qh5 or similar), which only works against beginners who don't defend. At any level, after 2.Qh5 Nc6 3.Bc4 g6! the queen must retreat, and Black has developed while White wasted moves.

After 3…g6! the Queen on h5 has no good square. White’s attack has collapsed; Black has developed.

5. Connect Your Rooks

Once you have developed all minor pieces and castled, the rooks should be connected — nothing standing between them on the back rank. Connected rooks support each other and can be doubled on open or half-open files. The final step of the opening is often placing rooks on the e-file, d-file, or any file likely to open in the middlegame.

6. Every Move Should Have a Purpose

Ask yourself: what does this move do? Does it develop a piece, contest a central square, open a line, or prepare castling? If you cannot answer that question, reconsider. Passive moves and pawn-shuffling in the opening hand the initiative to your opponent for free.

When to Break the Rules

These principles are guidelines, not laws. Grandmasters break all of them, but only with a specific concrete reason. The Nimzowitsch Variation of the French (1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 b6!? 5.Qg4 Bf8!? — retreating the bishop twice) is playable because Black has calculated the compensation. Before deviating from a principle, you must have a better reason than just not knowing it applied.
