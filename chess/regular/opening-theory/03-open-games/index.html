3. The Ruy Lopez (Spanish Opening)

Named after the 16th-century Spanish priest Ruy López de Segura, who published an analysis in 1561, the Ruy Lopez is the most deeply analysed opening in chess. After 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5, White immediately pressures the knight that defends the e5 pawn. The bishop doesn’t actually win the pawn immediately — Black has resources — but White creates long-term pressure that defines the next 20 moves.

Ruy Lopez after 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 — the defining position

White’s Plan

The central strategy is straightforward: play c3 and d4 to build a strong pawn centre, castle kingside, and then pursue activity depending on Black’s response. The bishop on b5 (or later a4) keeps permanent pressure on c6. If Black is ever careless, Bxc6 dxc6 followed by Nxe5 wins the e-pawn for free. Black must always be vigilant.

Morphy Defence: 3…a6 (the main line)

The most popular response. 3…a6 asks the bishop: what are you doing on b5? White must either exchange (4.Bxc6 dxc6, the Exchange Variation) or retreat (4.Ba4). The vast majority of Ruy Lopez games continue 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.0-0 Be7, reaching the starting position of the Closed Ruy Lopez.

1. e4  e5   2. Nf3  Nc6   3. Bb5  a6   4. Ba4  Nf6   5. 0-0  Be7   6. Re1  b5   7. Bb3  d6   8. c3  0-0   9. h3

Move 9.h3 prevents ...Bg4 (pinning the f3 knight) and prepares 10.d4. This is the main tabiya of the Closed Ruy Lopez — a position reached in thousands of grandmaster games. From here White plays d4, Nbd2, and prepares kingside action; Black counterattacks with ...Na5 (chasing the bishop) or ...Nb8-d7 to reorganise.

Closed Ruy Lopez tabiya after 9.h3 — White prepares d4; Black plans ...Na5 or ...Nd7

Berlin Defence: 3…Nf6 (the drawing weapon)

Black immediately develops and counterattacks e4. After 4.0-0 Nxe4 5.d4 Nd6 6.Bxc6 dxc6 7.dxe5 Nf5 8.Qxd8+ Kxd8, Black has willingly entered an endgame with a slightly damaged pawn structure (doubled c-pawns) in exchange for the bishop pair and a very solid position. Vladimir Kramnik used the Berlin to defeat Garry Kasparov in the 2000 World Championship match without losing a single game. White has the better pawn structure; Black has active pieces and no weaknesses.

1. e4  e5   2. Nf3  Nc6   3. Bb5  Nf6   4. 0-0  Nxe4   5. d4  Nd6   6. Bxc6  dxc6   7. dxe5  Nf5   8. Qxd8+  Kxd8

The resulting endgame is subtle and rich. Black’s king heads to c7 where it is actually safe. The Berlin is the top weapon at elite level for Black whenever a draw is acceptable.

Marshall Attack: 9…d5!? (the gambit)

In the Closed Ruy Lopez tabiya, instead of the standard 9…Bb7, Frank Marshall tried 9…d5!? in 1918 against José Raúl Capablanca. Black sacrifices a pawn for enormous piece activity and kingside attacking chances. It remained a prepared surprise weapon for over a decade. Capablanca won that game but only barely.

After 9.h3: 9… d5!? 10. exd5  Nxd5   11. Nxe5  Nxe5   12. Rxe5  c6   13. d4  Bd6   14. Re1  Qh4

Black has sacrificed a pawn but has three pieces pointing at White’s king. The Marshall Attack requires concrete preparation — White has several Anti-Marshall systems (8.a4, 8.h3) to avoid it, and Black must know deep lines to justify the sacrifice.

Exchange Variation: 4.Bxc6 dxc6 5.Nxe5

White immediately takes the knight, gives Black the bishop pair, but wins the e5 pawn. The resulting position has a symmetrical pawn structure (White has a 3vs2 kingside majority; Black has doubled c-pawns but the bishop pair). Bobby Fischer famously used this variation repeatedly at the 1966 Havana Olympiad. White aims to convert the pawn majority in the endgame; Black uses the bishops for counterplay.
