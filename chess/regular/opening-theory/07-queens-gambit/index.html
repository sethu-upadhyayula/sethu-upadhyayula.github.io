7. The Queen’s Gambit

1.d4 d5 2.c4 — the most classical of all openings. White offers the c-pawn to gain control of the centre. Unlike the King’s Gambit, this is not really a gambit: if Black takes 2…dxc4, White regains the pawn easily with e4 or later Qa4+. The Queen’s Gambit and its relatives form the backbone of most top-level d4 play.

QGD Classical after 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 — the central pawn tension is maintained

Queen’s Gambit Accepted (ECO D20–D29)

2…dxc4: QGA Main Lines

Black takes the pawn. White cannot immediately recapture (3.Qa4+ or 3.e3 is met by ...b5), but after 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.e3 e6 5.Bxc4, White has a nice centre and active pieces. Black will play ...c5 to challenge White’s centre. The QGA leads to open, active positions. It was heavily analysed in the 1920s Hypermodern era and again in the 1990s when many top players used it to get winning chances as Black.

1.d4 d5 2.c4 dxc4 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.e3 e6 5.Bxc4 c5
6. 0-0 a6 7. dxc5 Bxc5 — Black is fine, full equality

Queen’s Gambit Declined (ECO D30–D69)

The mainline. Black holds the centre with ...e6, accepting a slightly cramped position in exchange for solidity. The QGD is one of the deepest and most extensively analysed openings in chess. Lasker, Capablanca, and Petrosian all relied on it heavily as Black.

Orthodox Defence: ...Nf6, ...e6, ...Be7 (ECO D60–D69)

The simplest structure: Black develops naturally to e7 and castles. White can try the Exchange Variation (cxd5) gaining space on the queenside with a minority attack, or keep the tension. The Carlsbad Structure (after Exchange) is a major strategic pattern: White advances b4-b5 to exchange the c6 pawn, creating a passed pawn or queenside weakness.

1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 5.e3 0-0 6.Nf3 Nbd7
Exchange: 7.cxd5 exd5 — Carlsbad structure
Main: 7. Rc1 c6 8.Bd3 — build pressure on d5

Tartakower Variation: 6…h6 7.Bh4 b6 (ECO D58–D59)

Black immediately fianchettoes the queen’s bishop to b7, challenging White’s control of e4. The Tartakower is known for being dynamic and allowing Black real winning chances, which is why it was Kasparov’s main weapon as Black in the 1980s–90s.

Slav and Semi-Slav (ECO D10–D19, D43–D49)

Slav Defence: 2…c6 (ECO D10–D19)

Black supports d5 with the c-pawn, keeping the c8 bishop free. After 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Nf3, Black can recapture on c4 later (4…dxc4) in the Main Slav, keeping the bishop diagonal open. The Slav is solid and popular. Viswanathan Anand used it in several World Championship matches.

1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Nf3 dxc4 5.a4 Bf5
6. e3 e6 7.Bxc4 Bb4 — solid with active bishop

Semi-Slav: ...e6 and ...c6 (ECO D43–D49)

The most complex opening in chess. Black plays both ...e6 and ...c6, creating a solid structure but delaying bishop development. The Meran Variation (5…Nbd7 6.Bd3 dxc4 7.Bxc4 b5!) leads to extremely sharp tactical battles. The Anti-Meran (5.Bg5, the Moscow Variation) is currently the most theoretical — every world championship match since 2000 has featured it.

1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.e3 e6 5.Nf3 Nbd7
Meran: 6. Bd3 dxc4 7.Bxc4 b5 8.Bd3 a6 9.e4 c5 — fireworks

Catalan Opening (ECO E00–E09)

1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.g3

White fianchettoes the king bishop, combining Queen’s Gambit with hypermodern ideas. The bishop on g2 puts long-term pressure on d5. The Open Catalan (4…dxc4) gives Black the pawn but concedes the diagonal. The Closed Catalan is more solid. Magnus Carlsen has won dozens of games with the Catalan. It is currently considered one of the best openings for White at the top level.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nf3 d5 4.g3 dxc4 5.Bg2 Bb4+ 6.Bd2
— Open Catalan, White regains pawn with long-term pressure
