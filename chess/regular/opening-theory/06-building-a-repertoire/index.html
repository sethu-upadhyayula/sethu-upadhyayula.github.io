6. French Defence & Caro-Kann

Two of the most important semi-open defences to 1.e4. Both involve blocking the centre early, accepting a space disadvantage in return for a solid position that is hard to attack. They appeal to players who prefer structure and clarity over the sharp complications of the Sicilian.

The French Defence (ECO C00–C19)

1.e4 e6 — Black prepares ...d5, forming a pawn chain. The classic French pawn structure is d5 vs e5 (after White pushes e5). Black’s problem: the c8 bishop is blocked by its own pawn on e6. Black’s long-term goal: undermine White’s pawn chain with ...c5 and possibly ...f6. Viktor Korchnoi, Tigran Petrosian, and Michael Adams built careers on the French.

French Classical after 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 — White must resolve the central tension

Winawer Variation: 3…Bb4 (ECO C15–C18)

The sharpest and most popular French. Black pins the knight and will exchange on c3, giving White doubled pawns in return for surrendering the bishop pair. After 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3, White has a powerful centre but weakened queenside. Black counterattacks with ...Qa5, ...Ne7-Nf5, and ...c4. The resulting positions are enormously complex and asymmetrical.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne7
7. Qg4 — attacking g7, asking Black to choose between ...0-0 and ...Kf8

Classical Variation: 3…Nf6 (ECO C12–C14)

Natural development. After 4.Bg5 (the Classical), White pins the knight. Black must choose: 4…Be7 (solid), 4…dxe4 5.Nxe4 Be7 (MacCutcheon with 5…Bb4+ is sharper), or 4…Bb4 transposing to Winawer territory. The Classical leads to rich, strategic battles.

Tarrasch Variation: 3.Nd2 (ECO C03–C09)

White avoids the Winawer pin with 3.Nd2. The downside: the knight on d2 blocks the c-pawn and is less active than Nc3. Black can continue 3…Nf6 (solid) or 3…c5 immediately attacking the centre. The Tarrasch is Karpov’s favourite White weapon — it leads to quieter positions while keeping an edge.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nd2 c5 4.exd5 exd5 5.Ngf3 Nc6 6.Bb5
— isolated d-pawn positions with opposite-side castling possibilities

Advance Variation: 3.e5 (ECO C02)

White immediately establishes the pawn on e5, closing the centre. Black must attack this pawn with ...c5, ...Nc6, and ...Qb6. The Milner-Barry Gambit (4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Bd3!?) is an attacking attempt. Modern engines suggest the Advance is slightly better for Black with correct play, but it’s a common choice at club level.

The Caro-Kann Defence (ECO B10–B19)

1.e4 c6 — preparing ...d5 while keeping the c8 bishop unconstrained (unlike the French). The Caro-Kann is considered one of the most solid defences to 1.e4. Anatoly Karpov played it throughout his career, and Magnus Carlsen has adopted it frequently. The resulting structures are similar to the French but without the passive c8 bishop problem.

Caro-Kann Classical after 1.e4 c6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 dxe4 4.Nxe4 Nd7 — the bishop on c8 is free

Classical Variation: 4…Bf5 (ECO B18–B19)

The main line. After 4.Nxe4, Black plays 4…Bf5, immediately developing the problem bishop before closing the pawn chain. White plays 5.Ng3 Bg6 6.h4 h6 7.Nf3 Nd7 8.h5 Bh7, and a typical Caro middlegame arises: Black is solid, White tries to generate kingside pressure. The Karpov Variation (4…Nd7) is an alternative.

Advance Variation: 3.e5 (ECO B12)

As in the French, White grabs space. After 3…Bf5, Black immediately develops the bishop — the whole point of playing ...c6 before ...d5. 4.Nf3 e6 5.Be2 Ne7 and Black will later play ...c5, challenging the centre. The Tal Variation (3.e5 Bf5 4.h4!?) is an aggressive try.

Panov Attack: 3.exd5 cxd5 4.c4 (ECO B13–B14)

White trades the e-pawn for the c-pawn and plays 4.c4, aiming to reach IQP (isolated queen pawn) positions typical of the QGA. Black must choose between accepting the IQP or playing 4…Nf6 5.Nc3 Nc6, heading for a Nimzo-like structure. The Panov is White’s most principled attempt to challenge the Caro-Kann.
