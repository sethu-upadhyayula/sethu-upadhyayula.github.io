9. English & Flank Openings

Flank openings begin with moves other than 1.e4 or 1.d4, taking a hypermodern approach: control the centre with pieces and pawns from the wings rather than immediately occupying it. The English Opening (1.c4) is the most popular flank system and leads to rich, strategic positions that transpose frequently to d4 systems.

The English Opening (ECO A10–A39)

1.c4 — controls d5 without committing the d-pawn. The English is one of the most flexible openings: it can transpose to the Queen’s Gambit (with d4), the King’s Indian (with g3), or remain in independent English territory. Mikhail Botvinnik developed many English ideas, and it is a favourite of Carlsen, Kramnik, and Karpov.

English Opening, Four Knights: 1.c4 e5 2.Nc3 Nf6 3.Nf3 Nc6 4.g3 — symmetric Botvinnik setup

Symmetrical English: 1...c5 (ECO A30–A39)

Black mirrors White’s move. After 2.Nf3 Nf6 3.d4 (transposing to a Benoni-like position) or 3.g3 d5 4.cxd5 Nxd5 5.Bg2, positions arise with both sides fighting for d4/d5. The Botvinnik System (with g3+Bg2+d3) gives White a solid position and long-term pressure. The symmetrical English is extremely popular with positional players.

Reversed Sicilian: 1...e5 (ECO A20–A29)

Black plays ...e5, and White has a Sicilian structure but with an extra tempo. After 2.Nc3 Nf6 3.g3 d5 4.cxd5 Nxd5 5.Bg2, the four-knights setup gives rich strategic play. White’s extra tempo (compared to the Black side in the Sicilian) is a slight advantage. The Hedgehog Formation (Black plays ...b6, ...e6, ...d6, all pawns on the 6th rank) is a common and interesting response.

English vs. King’s Indian: 1...Nf6 (ECO A15–A19)

After 1.c4 Nf6 2.Nc3 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 0-0, positions arise that are similar to the King’s Indian but without e4. White can play e4 later (transposing to a full KID) or maintain the English character with d3. Kramnik used this system frequently.

The Reti Opening (ECO A04–A09)

1.Nf3 — the most flexible first move. White makes no commitment, prepares to fianchetto the king bishop (g3+Bg2), and keeps all options open. The Reti can transpose to virtually any opening. Richard Reti used this idea in the 1920s to challenge the classical theories of Steinitz.

Reti Gambit: 1.Nf3 d5 2.c4 (ECO A09)

White immediately challenges the d5 pawn. After 2...dxc4 3.e3 (or 3.Na3), White will regain the pawn with good central control. This transposes to Queen’s Gambit territory. Alternatively, 2...d4 gives the Reti Gambit Accepted, where White will try to exploit the advanced d-pawn.

King’s Indian Attack: 1.Nf3 then g3+Bg2+d3 (ECO A07–A08)

White fianchettoes the king bishop and plays a reversed King’s Indian setup. The KIA is used as a system against the French (1.e4 e6 2.d3), the Sicilian, and other setups. Bobby Fischer used the KIA as his main weapon with White in the 1960s, winning many brilliant games with the kingside attack f4-f5-f6.

KIA vs French: 1.e4 e6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 Nf6 4.Ngf3 Be7 5.g3 0-0 6.Bg2 — White will play 0-0, Re1, e5, then f4-f5 attack

Other Flank Systems

Bird’s Opening: 1.f4 (ECO A02–A03)

White controls e5 and prepares a kingside attack, at the cost of slightly weakening the king. Black’s sharpest response is the From Gambit (1...e5!? 2.fxe5 d6 3.exd6 Bxd6), which gives immediate attacking chances. The Bird is rarely seen at the top level but is interesting at club level.

Larsen’s Opening: 1.b3 (ECO A01)

Bent Larsen’s hypermodern system: the queen bishop goes to b2 to control the long diagonal. After 1...e5 2.Bb2, Black has a nice centre and White must fight for counterplay. Larsen shocked the world by playing this against Spassky in the 1970 USSR vs Rest of World match.
