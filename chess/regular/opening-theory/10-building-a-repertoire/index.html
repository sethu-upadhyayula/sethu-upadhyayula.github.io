10. Building a Repertoire

Having surveyed the major openings, the key question is: how do you choose what to play? A repertoire is a personal set of openings that you have studied deeply enough to understand the ideas, not just memorise the moves. A good repertoire is consistent with your style, covers all likely opponent responses, and does not require you to memorise 20-move theory lines to survive the opening.

Principles of Repertoire Design

1. Choose Openings that Match Your Style

Tactical players who want complications should look at the Sicilian (Najdorf, Dragon), the King’s Indian, the Grunfeld, or the King’s Gambit. Positional players who prefer long-term pressure should consider the Ruy Lopez, the Queen’s Gambit, the Catalan, or the English. Solid players who want to avoid early complications can choose the Caro-Kann, the French, the Slav, or the QGD. There is no objectively best opening — the best opening is the one you understand deeply.

2. Cover All Responses

A complete repertoire as White must answer 1...e5, 1...c5, 1...e6, 1...c6, 1...d5, 1...Nf6, and various others. A complete repertoire as Black must answer 1.e4 and 1.d4 (and ideally 1.c4 and 1.Nf3). You do not need a different opening for every possibility — systems like the King’s Indian Attack or the London System can be played against almost anything.

3. Learn Ideas before Moves

In the Ruy Lopez, the idea is to pressure e5 and control the centre long-term. In the King’s Indian, the idea is to allow a large White centre and then attack it with ...e5 and ...f5. In the Caro-Kann, the idea is to free the c8 bishop before playing ...e6. If you understand these ideas, you can play sensibly even when you leave theory.

Sample Beginner Repertoire

As White: 1.e4 with the Ruy Lopez and Italian

After 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6: play 3.Bb5 (Ruy Lopez) or 3.Bc4 (Italian). Against the Sicilian (1...c5): play the Alapin (2.c3). Against the French (1...e6): play the Tarrasch (3.Nd2). Against the Caro-Kann (1...c6): play the Advance (3.e5). This gives a coherent, manageable set of systems without requiring deep knowledge of the Open Sicilian.

As Black vs. 1.e4: The Caro-Kann or French

Both are solid and do not require memorising 20-move sharp lines. The Caro-Kann is slightly more active (the c8 bishop stays free); the French is slightly more strategic (the ...e6 pawn creates a compact fortress). Choose one and study the Classical and Advance variations deeply.

As Black vs. 1.d4: The Queen’s Gambit Declined or Nimzo-Indian

The QGD (1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6) is the most classical response — solid, well-understood, few losing surprises. The Nimzo-Indian (1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4) is slightly more dynamic and has excellent statistics. Both require understanding of IQP and minority attack structures.

How to Study Openings

The Three-Step Method

Step 1 — Learn the main idea: What is each side trying to achieve? What are the typical pawn structures?
Step 2 — Learn typical plans: What middlegame plans arise from this opening? (Minority attack in the Exchange QGD, kingside attack in the KID, etc.)
Step 3 — Learn the critical variations: Only memorise lines where understanding the idea alone is not sufficient — typically sharp tactical sequences where you can go wrong quickly.

Study Master Games

The most efficient way to learn opening ideas is to study master games in your openings. Look for games by Karpov in the QGD, Kasparov in the Najdorf, Carlsen in the Italian, Fischer in the Ruy Lopez. Do not memorise the moves — understand why each move was played and what plan it serves. The Master Games section has 1240 annotated games organised by tournament and event.

Where to Go From Here

This chapter concludes the Opening Theory series. The concepts you have covered — ECO classification, opening principles, the major systems for both 1.e4 and 1.d4 — give you the foundation to study any opening in depth. Recommended next steps:

  • Use an engine to check your own games only from the point where you left theory
  • Revisit this guide after 3 months of playing — the ideas will make much more sense with practice
