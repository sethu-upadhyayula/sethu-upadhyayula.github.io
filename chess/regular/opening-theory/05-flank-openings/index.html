5. Flank Openings

Flank openings do not immediately occupy the centre with a pawn. Instead, White develops pieces and prepares to influence the centre from the flanks. These are associated with hypermodern chess philosophy, pioneered by Richard Réti, Aaron Nimzowitsch, and Savielly Tartakower in the 1920s.

English Opening: 1.c4

White establishes a flank pawn on c4 immediately, fighting for the d5 square. The English is remarkably flexible — it can transpose into almost any closed or semi-closed game. White typically follows up with Nf3, g3, Bg2 (fianchetto), and 0-0, applying long-term pressure. The English is a favourite of quiet, positional players. At the top level it has been used extensively by Magnus Carlsen and Anatoly Karpov.

Main systems: Symmetrical English (1...c5), Reversed Sicilian (1...e5), Anglo-Indian setups (1...Nf6), and transpositions to Queen's Gambit structures.

Réti Opening: 1.Nf3

The most flexible first move — White develops the knight and controls e5 and d4 without committing a central pawn. The Réti often follows with g3, Bg2 (fianchetto), d3 or d4, and eventually c4, allowing White to react to Black's setup. It can transpose into the Catalan, the King's Indian Attack, or the English.

Richard Réti famously drew with the world champion José Raúl Capablanca in 1924 using this system, when draws against Capablanca were exceptional achievements.

Choosing Your Opening

For beginners and intermediate players, the best advice is:

  • Pick one main opening for each colour and learn the ideas, not just the moves
  • Play games, make mistakes, and study why certain moves are good or bad
  • Don't worry about memorising 20 moves of theory — understand the plans that arise
  • Once comfortable, gradually expand your repertoire based on your stylistic preferences

Opening preparation becomes more important above approximately 1800–2000 Elo, where opponents begin to play precise theory. Even at the grandmaster level, novelties (new moves in known lines) are often prepared days before a game.
