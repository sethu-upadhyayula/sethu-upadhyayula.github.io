1. Classifying Openings

Every chess opening has been catalogued, analysed, and named. Understanding how openings are classified lets you navigate databases, books, and your own repertoire with confidence. Two systems dominate: the broad family tree based on first moves and pawn structure, and the ECO code system (Encyclopaedia of Chess Openings, A00–E99) used in all professional databases.

The Five Families

Open Games — 1.e4 e5 (ECO C)

Both sides immediately occupy the centre. The open e-file and active piece development characterise these positions. Tactical and direct, they reward calculation and piece activity. The Ruy Lopez, Italian Game, King's Gambit, and Giuoco Piano all belong here.

1. e4   e5   (open centre, ECO C00–C99)

Semi-Open Games — 1.e4, Black plays something other than 1…e5 (ECO B)

White occupies the centre; Black declines to mirror it. Instead Black strikes from the flank or prepares a delayed central challenge. These positions are asymmetrical from move one, giving both sides real winning chances. The Sicilian (1…c5), French (1…e6), and Caro-Kann (1…c6) are the main systems.

1. e4   c5   (Sicilian, ECO B20–B99)
1. e4   e6   (French, ECO C00–C19)
1. e4   c6   (Caro-Kann, ECO B10–B19)

Closed Games — 1.d4 d5 (ECO D)

The d-pawns are protected by queens from the outset, so early captures are less forcing. Play tends to be slower and more strategic. The Queen's Gambit (1.d4 d5 2.c4) and all its variations — Accepted, Declined, Slav — live here, along with the Colle and London systems.

1. d4   d5   2. c4   (Queen's Gambit, ECO D00–D69)

Indian Systems — 1.d4 Nf6 (ECO A & E)

Black develops the king's knight to f6 without an immediate central pawn counter, often preparing a fianchetto. Hypermodern in spirit: Black invites White to build a large centre, then attacks it. The King's Indian, Nimzo-Indian, Queen's Indian, Grünfeld, and Benoni all belong here.

1. d4   Nf6   (generic Indian, leads to E00–E99)
1. d4   Nf6   2. c4   g6   3. Nc3   Bg7   (King's Indian, E60–E99)

Flank Openings — 1.c4, 1.Nf3, 1.g3, 1.b3… (ECO A)

White does not immediately occupy the centre with a pawn. The English (1.c4) and Réti (1.Nf3) are the main flank openings. White often transposes into familiar pawn structures but on different terms, gaining flexibility at the cost of some central space. ECO A00–A39.

1. c4   (English, ECO A10–A39)
1. Nf3   (Réti, ECO A04–A09)

The ECO Code System

The ECO system divides all openings into five volumes (A–E), each subdivided into 100 codes (00–99). Every professional database and engine book uses these codes. You do not need to memorise them all — but knowing the volume letters instantly tells you the opening family.

VolumeCode RangeOpening Family
AA00–A99Flank openings, irregular, and Indian systems not in E (incl. Dutch, Benoni)
BB00–B99Semi-open games: Sicilian, Caro-Kann, Pirc, Modern, and other 1.e4 replies
CC00–C99Open games and French Defence (1.e4 e5 and 1.e4 e6)
DD00–D99Closed games: Queen's Gambit, Slav, Grunfeld (some), Catalan
EE00–E99Indian systems: Nimzo-Indian, Queen's Indian, King's Indian, Catalan (some)

Why Classification Matters

Knowing a position is "an E60" or "a B90" instantly places it in context. More practically: pawn structure determines piece placement, plans, and weaknesses. A French Defence player and a Caro-Kann player both begin with 1.e4, but the resulting structures — and what each side should aim for — are completely different. Learn the families first; the specific lines follow naturally.
