5. Italian Game & King’s Gambit

Two of the oldest openings in chess history. The Italian Game (1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4) aims for rapid development and long-term pressure on f7. The King’s Gambit (1.e4 e5 2.f4) sacrifices a pawn for immediate initiative. Both belong to the open games (1.e4 e5) family.

The Italian Game (ECO C50–C59)

After 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4, White eyes the f7 square and prepares to castle. The Italian was played by Greco in the 1600s and has seen a massive revival in modern super-GM chess thanks to players like Magnus Carlsen, who uses it to avoid Ruy Lopez theory.

Italian Game after 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4 Bc5 — Giuoco Piano

Giuoco Piano: 3…Bc5 (ECO C50–C54)

The “Quiet Game” is anything but quiet in modern theory. After 4.c3 Nf6, White prepares d4. The classical line 5.d4 exd4 6.cxd4 Bb4+ 7.Nc3 leads to the Italian Main Line, where White sacrifices the e4 pawn for central control. Carlsen and Anand have played this dozens of times. The double e-pawn endgames arising from early queen trades are a key practical challenge.

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4 Bc5 4.c3 Nf6 5.d4 exd4 6.cxd4 Bb4+
7. Bd2 Bxd2+ 8. Nbxd2 d5 — solid equalizing approach
7. Nc3 Nxe4 8. 0-0 — sacrifice for attack

Two Knights Defence: 3…Nf6 (ECO C55–C59)

Black immediately counterattacks e4 rather than developing the bishop. After 4.Ng5, White threatens Nxf7 (the “Fried Liver Attack” if Black plays 4…d5 5.exd5 Nxd5?? 6.Nxf7!). The sharper lines involve enormous complications. Black’s best is probably 4…d5 5.exd5 Na5, the Polerio-Suhle variation, which gives good counterplay for the pawn.

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4 Nf6 4.Ng5 d5 5.exd5 Na5
6. Bb5+ c6 7. dxc6 bxc6 8. Be2 h6 — Black has good comp

Evans Gambit: 4.b4 (ECO C51–C52)

A 19th-century gambit that shocked the world when Kasparov revived it against Anand in 1995 (PCA Blitz). After 3…Bc5 4.b4!?, White offers a pawn to deflect the bishop and gain time to play c3+d4 with tempo. The resulting positions are extremely sharp and White gets a powerful centre. The refutation attempts by Black are long and demanding.

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4 Bc5 4.b4!? Bxb4 5.c3 Ba5 6.d4 exd4
7. 0-0 — White sacrifices a second pawn for the attack

The King’s Gambit (ECO C30–C39)

1.e4 e5 2.f4 — offering a pawn to open the f-file and seize the centre. The King’s Gambit dominated chess in the 18th and 19th centuries. Fischer declared in 1960 “In my opinion the King’s Gambit is busted,” then proceeded to write a famous article with his refutation. Modern engines have rehabilitated the opening somewhat.

King’s Gambit Accepted after 1.e4 e5 2.f4 exf4 — Black has the pawn, White has the initiative

King’s Gambit Accepted: 2…exf4 (ECO C33–C39)

Black takes the pawn and must defend it or use it as a lever. The classical lines begin with 3.Nf3. If Black plays 3…g5 (holding the pawn), White has 4.h4!? g4 5.Ne5 (the Kieseritzky Gambit) or 4.Bc4 (the Bishop’s Gambit). Fischer’s line is 3…d6 (the Fischer Defence), aiming to return the pawn for easy equality — it is the theoretically safest response.

1.e4 e5 2.f4 exf4 3.Nf3 d6 — Fischer Defence
3...g5 4.h4 g4 5.Ne5 — Kieseritzky Gambit
3...Nf6 4.e5 Nh5 — Schallopp Defence

King’s Gambit Declined: 2…Bc5 or 2…d5 (ECO C30–C31)

Black can also decline with 2…Bc5 (Falkbeer Counter-Gambit Declined) or the sharp Falkbeer Counter-Gambit 2…d5!?. The Falkbeer gives back a pawn immediately in return for rapid development and open lines. The main line runs 3.exd5 e4 4.d3 Nf6, and Black has excellent counterplay.
