4. The Sicilian Defence

After 1.e4 c5, Black fights for the centre without occupying it symmetrically. The c5 pawn contests d4 while keeping the position asymmetrical from move one — giving both sides genuine winning chances. The Sicilian is the most popular and statistically most successful response to 1.e4, accounting for roughly 25% of all tournament games. Fischer, Kasparov, Tal, and Carlsen all played it regularly.

Open Sicilian after 1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 — Black to play

The Open Sicilian: 2.Nf3 and 3.d4

The main line. White opens the centre with d4 (2.Nf3 followed by 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4), and now Black must choose a variation. The resulting tabiya after 5.Nc3 is one of the most important branching points in chess theory.

Najdorf Variation: 5…a6 (ECO B90–B99)

The most popular variation in the world. 5…a6 seems modest — it prevents Nb5 and Bb5+ — but it is a preparation for ...e5 or ...b5-b4 queenside expansion. Bobby Fischer called the Najdorf “my favourite opening,” and Garry Kasparov played it for his entire career. The typical White responses are 6.Bg5 (the classical, ultra-sharp Poisoned Pawn Variation being the apex), 6.Be3 (the English Attack), and 6.Bc4.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6
6. Bg5 e6 7. f4 — classical with kingside attack
6. Be3 e5 7. Nb3 Be6 — English Attack vs Najdorf

Black’s pieces are active; the position is completely unbalanced. White often attacks the kingside, Black counterattacks on the queenside — this is the Sicilian at its purest.

Dragon Variation: 5…g6 (ECO B70–B79)

Black fianchettoes the bishop to g7, creating a powerful diagonal aimed at the queenside. The Dragon bishop on g7 is one of the most feared pieces on the board in the endgame. White’s most dangerous response is the Yugoslav Attack (6.Be3 Bg7 7.f3 0-0 8.Qd2 Nc6 9.Bc4 0-0-0), which leads to one of the most complex and analysed positions in chess: both sides castle on opposite wings and race to attack.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 g6
6. Be3  Bg7   7. f3  0-0   8. Qd2  Nc6   9. Bc4  Bd7   10. 0-0-0

Yugoslav Attack in Dragon — White has castled queenside and will attack h7; Black attacks c3/d4

Scheveningen Variation: 5…e6 (ECO B80–B89)

Black forms the "small centre" with pawns on d6 and e6 — solid, flexible, and leaving all options open for knight and bishop development. The Scheveningen pawn formation is so common it appears in many Najdorf lines too (when Black plays ...e6 after ...a6). White typically tries the Keres Attack (6.g4!?) or the classical 6.Be2. Tigran Petrosian and Anatoly Karpov were famous Scheveningen players.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 e6
6. g4!? — Keres Attack, immediately aggressive
6. Be2 a6 7. 0-0 Qc7 — classical development

Classical Variation: 5…Nc6 (ECO B55–B69)

Natural development. 5…Nc6 attacks the d4 knight directly, forcing White to decide how to handle it. The most popular continuation is 6.Bg5, leading to the Richter-Rauzer Attack (6…e6 7.Qd2 Be7 8.0-0-0), or 6.Bc4, the Sozin Attack. The Classical Sicilian is solid and less forcing than the Najdorf.

Anti-Sicilian Systems

Many players avoid the Open Sicilian’s enormous theory. These alternatives trade ambition for control:

Alapin Variation: 2.c3

White immediately prepares d4 without playing Nf3 first. After 2…Nf6 3.e5 Nd5 4.d4 cxd4 5.cxd4, White achieves a strong pawn centre. The downside: Black can challenge it effectively with ...d5 ideas, and the c3 square is no longer available for the knight. Solid and theory-light.

1.e4 c5 2.c3 Nf6 3.e5 Nd5 4.d4 cxd4 5.cxd4 d6 6.Nf3

Grand Prix Attack: 2.Nc3 followed by f4

White develops the knight to c3 and plays f4-f5 for a direct kingside attack. The Grand Prix is a particularly good weapon against unprepared Sicilian players. It avoids the main Sicilian theory entirely. Black’s best response is typically 2…Nc6 3.f4 g6, meeting the attack with a Dragon-style setup.

Closed Sicilian: 2.Nc3 and 3.g3

White fianchettoes the kingside bishop and builds up slowly. The resulting positions resemble the King’s Indian Attack structure. Less sharp than the Open Sicilian, but can lead to complex middlegames. Popular at club level as it avoids massive theory.
