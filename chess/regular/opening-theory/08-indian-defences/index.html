8. Indian Defences

After 1.d4 Nf6, Black delays ...d5 and prepares a counterattack in hypermodern style. Instead of occupying the centre immediately, Black allows White to build a large centre and then attacks it from the flanks. The Indian defences were pioneered in the 1920s by Nimzowitsch, Reti, and others, and remain the most popular responses to 1.d4 at all levels today.

King’s Indian Defence (ECO E60–E99)

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 0-0 6.Be2 e5 — Black allows White a large pawn centre (e4+d4) and then counterattacks with ...e5. The KID is the fighting choice for players who want to win as Black. Bobby Fischer, Kasparov, and Bronstein all used it as their main weapon.

KID Classical after 6.Be2 e5 7.0-0 Nc6 — White has space; Black has a strong centre-attack

Classical Variation: 7.0-0 Nc6 (ECO E90–E99)

The main line. White can play 8.d5 (the Mar del Plata, leading to opposite-wing attacks: White advances c4-c5, Black attacks kingside with ...f5-f4-f3), or 8.Be3 (the Gligoric). The Mar del Plata is the sharpest — positions of enormous complexity where both sides attack with maximum ferocity.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 0-0 6.Be2 e5 7. 0-0 Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 — Mar del Plata 9. Ne1 Nd7 10.Nd3 f5 11.Bd2 — White prepares c5

Samisch Variation: 5.f3 (ECO E80–E89)

White builds an imposing centre with f3+e4+d4. After 5.f3 0-0 6.Be3, the idea is to play Qd2 and castle queenside, then storm the kingside. This gives very sharp, violent positions. Black can try the Panno Variation (6...Nc6 7.Qd2 a6), aiming for queenside counterplay.

Nimzo-Indian Defence (ECO E20–E59)

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 — Black immediately pins the c3 knight, preventing e4 and pressuring the centre indirectly. Black accepts the bishop pair disadvantage in return for doubling White’s pawns. The Nimzo-Indian is considered one of the most theoretically sound openings for Black and has the highest winning percentage of all Black responses to 1.d4.

Rubinstein Variation: 4.e3 (ECO E40–E49)

White develops calmly and will recapture on c3 with the bishop (maintaining a nice pawn structure). After 4...0-0 5.Bd3, positions arise where White has the bishop pair but Black has solid structure. Karpov won many beautiful games in this line.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 0-0 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nf3 c5

Classical Variation: 4.Qc2 (ECO E30–E39)

White avoids doubled pawns by placing the queen on c2. After 4...d5 5.cxd5 Qxd5 6.Nf3, or 4...0-0 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3, White maintains pawn structure. This is the most popular Nimzo-Indian system at the top level today.

Queen’s Indian Defence (ECO E12–E19)

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nf3 b6 — Black fianchettoes the queen bishop to b7, controlling the long diagonal and pressuring e4. The QID is extremely solid. After 4.Nc3 Bb4 (Nimzo-QID hybrid) or 4.g3 Ba6 (the Petrosian Variation), Black gets comfortable play. Karpov and Petrosian were famous QID practitioners.

Petrosian Variation: 4.a3 (ECO E12)

White prevents ...Bb4 immediately. After 4...Bb7 5.Nc3 d5 6.cxd5 Nxd5, an IQP position arises. The alternative 4.g3 Ba6, where Black’s bishop eyes c4, leads to extremely complex theory.

Grunfeld Defence (ECO D70–D99)

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 d5 — Black occupies the centre with a pawn, then allows it to be exchanged: 4.cxd5 Nxd5 5.e4 Nxc3 6.bxc3 Bg7. White has an imposing centre; Black’s fianchettoed bishop on g7 attacks it from afar. The Grunfeld is extremely sharp and the ultimate test of White’s centre. Kasparov and Bobby Fischer both used it.

Russian Variation: 7.Bc4 (ECO D85)

The most popular Grunfeld main line. Black plays 7...c5, immediately counter-attacking d4. The resulting positions require deep theoretical knowledge. Key tabiya: 8.Ne2 Nc6 9.Be3 0-0 10.0-0 Bg4, with mutual attacks.
