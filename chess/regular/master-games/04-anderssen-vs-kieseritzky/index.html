4. Sacrifice Without Limit — The Immortal Game

Adolf Anderssen – Lionel Kieseritzky

Casual game, London, 1851 · Bishop's Gambit

Played during a break in the first international tournament, this game has been celebrated for over 170 years for its sheer audacity: White gives up both rooks and a bishop, and finally checkmates with only three minor pieces left on the board, his queen having been left untouched on its home square the whole game.

1. e4 e5 2. f4 exf4 3. Bc4 Qh4+ 4. Kf1 b5 5. Bxb5 Nf6 6. Nf3 Qh6 7. d3 Nh5 8. Nh4 Qg5 9. Nf5 c6 10. g4 Nf6 11. Rg1 cxb5 12. h4 Qg6 13. h5 Qg5 14. Qf3 Ng8 15. Bxf4 Qf6 16. Nc3 Bc5 17. Nd5 Qxb2 18. Bd6 Bxg1 19. e5 Qxa1+ 20. Ke2 Na6 21. Nxg7+ Kd8 22. Qf6+ Nxf6 23. Be7+ Kc8 24. Bxf6# 1–0
Key idea (18. Bd6 and 19. e5): With both his rooks already hanging and soon to be lost, Anderssen ignores material entirely and instead drives Black's queen and pieces offside while quietly building a mating net around the black king, which is still stuck on its back rank.
Key idea (22. Qf6+!! and 24. Bxf6#): The final combination is the move that gave the game its name: White's only developed major piece, the queen, is sacrificed to deflect the knight from f6, clearing the way for a smothering mate delivered by a bishop, a knight, and a pawn alone — with White's own queen and both rooks already off the board.
Takeaway: Material counts are only a proxy for what actually matters — the activity of the pieces that remain and the safety of the two kings. When the opponent's king is stuck in the centre, even a near-total material deficit can be irrelevant if your remaining pieces can force mate first.
