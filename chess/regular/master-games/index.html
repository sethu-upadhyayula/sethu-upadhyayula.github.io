Master Games

This section is a growing collection of annotated games played by grandmasters, chosen to illustrate recurring ideas in the opening, middlegame, and endgame — the kind of ideas discussed elsewhere in the openings, strategy, tactics, and endgame sections. Each game is presented with the full move list and short annotations explaining the key strategic and tactical turning points, rather than deep engine analysis. The aim is to show these ideas at work in real, high-level play.

A work in progress: more games will be added here over time, organised loosely by theme (classic attacking games, model endgames, instructive positional games, and so on).

1. The Power of the Bishop Pair and a King Hunt

Garry Kasparov – Veselin Topalov

Hoogovens (Wijk aan Zee), 1999 · Pirc Defence

Famous as "Kasparov's Immortal," this game is a model of how a temporary material sacrifice can be used to expose an enemy king that has castled into apparent safety. White's pieces work together with remarkable coordination once the position opens up.

1. e4 d6 2. d4 Nf6 3. Nc3 g6 4. Be3 Bg7 5. Qd2 c6 6. f3 b5 7. Nge2 Nbd7 8. Bh6 Bxh6 9. Qxh6 Bb7 10. a3 e5 11. O-O-O Qe7 12. Kb1 a6 13. Nc1 O-O-O 14. Nb3 exd4 15. Rxd4 c5 16. Rd1 Nb6 17. g3 Kb8 18. Na5 Ba8 19. Bh3 d5 20. Qf4+ Ka7 21. Rhe1 d4 22. Nd5 Nbxd5 23. exd5 Qd6 24. Rxd4! cxd4 25. Re7+ Kb6 26. Qxd4+ Kxa5 27. b4+ Ka4 28. Qc3 Qxd5 29. Ra7 Bb7 30. Rxb7 Qc4 31. Qxf6 Kxa3 32. Qxa6+ Kxb4 33. c3+ Kxc3 34. Qa1+ Kd2 35. Qb2+ Kd1 36. Bf1 Rd2 37. Rd7 Rxd7 38. Bxc4 bxc4 39. Qxh8 Rd3 40. Qa8 c3 41. Qa4+ Ke1 42. f4 f5 43. Kc1 Rd2 44. Qa7 1–0
Key idea (15. Rxd4!): White sacrifices the exchange to rip open the centre and the a-file, transforming a static material edge into a permanent attack on Black's king.
Key idea (24. Rxd4+): A second exchange sacrifice removes the last defender of the black king's shelter, after which White's queen and bishop pair simply overwhelm the king as it is driven across the board.
Takeaway: When the opposing king is exposed, material can often be a secondary consideration — tempo and king safety dominate the evaluation.

2. "The Game of the Century" — A Queen Sacrifice for Domination

Donald Byrne – Robert James Fischer

Rosenwald Memorial, New York, 1956 · Grünfeld Defence

Played by a 13-year-old Fischer against a strong master, this game became famous for a queen sacrifice (17...Be6!) that does not win material back immediately, but instead sets up a mating net built from minor pieces and rooks — a striking early demonstration of positional judgement overriding material count.

1. Nf3 Nf6 2. c4 g6 3. Nc3 Bg7 4. d4 O-O 5. Bf4 d5 6. Qb3 dxc4 7. Qxc4 c6 8. e4 Nbd7 9. Rd1 Nb6 10. Qc5 Bg4 11. Bg5!? Na4! 12. Qa3 Nxc3 13. bxc3 Nxe4 14. Bxe7 Qb6 15. Bc4 Nxc3 16. Bc5 Rfe8+ 17. Kf1 Be6!! 18. Bxb6 Bxc4+ 19. Kg1 Ne2+ 20. Kf1 Nxd4+ 21. Kg1 Ne2+ 22. Kf1 Nc3+ 23. Kg1 axb6 24. Qb4 Ra4 25. Qxb6 Nxd1 26. h3 Rxa2 27. Kh2 Nxf2 28. Re1 Rxe1 29. Qd8+ Bf8 30. Nxe1 Bd5 31. Nf3 Ne4 32. Qb8 b5 33. h4 h5 34. Ne5 Kg7 35. Kg1 Bc5+ 36. Kf1 Ng3+ 37. Ke1 Bb4+ 38. Kd1 Bb3+ 39. Kc1 Ne2+ 40. Kb1 Nc3+ 41. Kc1 Rc2# 0–1
Key idea (17...Be6!!): Fischer offers his queen rather than retreat it, because the resulting tangle of pins and forks lets his minor pieces and rooks dominate the board — material is regained gradually, with interest, while the attack never stops.
Takeaway: A queen is only as valuable as the activity of the pieces around it. When every other piece can be activated at the cost of the queen, the sacrifice can be objectively sound.

3. Development, Development, Development — The Opera Game

Paul Morphy – Duke of Brunswick & Count Isouard

Paris Opera House, 1858 (consultation game) · Philidor Defence

One of the most quoted games in chess literature, this miniature is the classic illustration of the principle that development and open lines matter more than small pawn gains in the opening. Morphy ignores a pawn to keep developing, then finishes with a famous queen sacrifice that forces mate.

1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 d6 3. d4 Bg4 4. dxe5 Bxf3 5. Qxf3 dxe5 6. Bc4 Nf6 7. Qb3 Qe7 8. Nc3 c6 9. Bg5 b5 10. Nxb5! cxb5 11. Bxb5+ Nbd7 12. O-O-O Rd8 13. Rxd7! Rxd7 14. Rd1 Qe6 15. Bxd7+ Nxd7 16. Qb8+! Nxb8 17. Rd8# 1–0
Key idea (10. Nxb5! and 13. Rxd7!): Morphy repeatedly trades material for time, keeping Black's king in the centre and his own pieces flowing toward it.
Key idea (16. Qb8+!): The final queen sacrifice clears b8 so that the rook on d1 can deliver mate on d8, with Black's own knight blocking its king's escape.
Takeaway: In open positions with uncastled kings, tempo gained by attacking moves is often worth far more than the pawns or pieces given up to gain it.
Suggested further viewing: pair these games with the relevant ideas in Middlegame Tactics (combinations and sacrifices), Middlegame Strategy (piece coordination and king safety), and Endgame (technique once the attack has done its work).
