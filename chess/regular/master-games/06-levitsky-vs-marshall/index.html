6. One Recapture, Three Mates — Marshall's Gold Coins Game

Stepan Levitsky – Frank Marshall

Breslau, 1912 · French Defence

Known as "Marshall's Gold Coins game" after the story that admirers in the audience showered the board with gold coins on seeing the finish, this brief but spectacular game is remembered for one of the most famous quiet queen moves in chess history — a move that creates three separate mating threats at once.

1. d4 e6 2. e4 d5 3. Nc3 c5 4. Nf3 cxd4 5. Nxd4 Nf6 6. Bg5 Be7 7. Bxf6 Bxf6 8. Nxd5 exd5 9. exd5 Qe7+ 10. Be2 Nd7 11. O-O O-O 12. Nb5 Re8 13. Re1 Nb6 14. Bf3 Bf5 15. Qd2 Bxb2 16. Rab1 Bf6 17. d6 Qxd6 18. Nxd6 Re6 19. Nxb7 Rb8 20. Nd6 Bxd6 21. Qxd6 Nc4 22. Qg3 Rxg3 23. hxg3 Nxg3 24. Bg4 Rxg4 25. fxg3 Rxg3+! 0–1
Key idea (25...Rxg3+!!): Although White can recapture in three different ways — with the f-pawn, the h-pawn, or the king — every single one of them allows mate. White resigned on the spot rather than allow the demonstration to be completed, which is why the game is remembered as ending with this one extraordinary move.
Takeaway: A position can be lost the instant every legal response to a single threat leads to mate, even when the side to move appears, on the surface, to have several reasonable-looking replies. Calculating "what happens after every recapture" is essential before committing to a combination.
Suggested further viewing: pair these games with the relevant ideas in Tactical Play (combinations and sacrifices), Strategic Play (piece coordination and king safety), and Endgame (technique once the attack has done its work).
