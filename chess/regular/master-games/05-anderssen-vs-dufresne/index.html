5. A Symphony of Sacrifices — The Evergreen Game

Adolf Anderssen – Jean Dufresne

Casual game, Berlin, 1852 · Evans Gambit

Played a year after the Immortal Game and considered by some to be even finer, this game was so admired by Wilhelm Steinitz that he included it in an anthology titled "evergreen" — giving the game its name. It is the textbook example of a king-hunt finished off by a double bishop sacrifice.

1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bc4 Bc5 4. b4 Bxb4 5. c3 Ba5 6. d4 exd4 7. O-O d3 8. Qb3 Qf6 9. e5 Qg6 10. Re1 Nge7 11. Ba3 b5 12. Qxb5 Rb8 13. Qa4 Bb6 14. Nbd2 Bb7 15. Ne4 Qf5 16. Bxd3 Qh5 17. Nf6+ gxf6 18. exf6 Rg8 19. Rad1 Qxf3 20. Rxe7+ Nxe7 21. Qxd7+! Kxd7 22. Bf5++ Ke8 23. Bd7+ Kf8 24. Bxe7# 1–0
Key idea (20. Rxe7+! and 21. Qxd7+!): Anderssen sacrifices a rook and then his queen in succession, dragging Black's king out into the open. Each sacrifice is a forcing move that leaves Black no real choice, so the material given up is repaid in tempo and king safety.
Key idea (22. Bf5++ and 24. Bxe7#): With queen and rook gone, only White's two bishops and a knight remain — yet they are enough. The double check on move 22 forces the king forward into a mating net that the bishops alone can complete, a striking demonstration that mating material is not the same as material count.
Takeaway: When a king is driven into the open with no pawn shelter, a series of "only moves" forced by checks and captures can be more valuable than the queen and rook used to create them. Calculating such forcing sequences to their end is the essence of attacking chess.
