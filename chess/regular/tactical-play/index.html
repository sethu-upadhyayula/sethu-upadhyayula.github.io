Tactical Play

Tactics are short, concrete sequences of moves that win material or force checkmate through forcing play - checks, captures, and threats. While strategy is about long-term planning, tactics are about precise calculation. At every level of chess, the player who spots tactics first wins material and games. Grandmaster Siegbert Tarrasch famously said: "Before the endgame, the gods have placed the middlegame."

Tactical proficiency is built through pattern recognition: once you have seen a fork, pin, or skewer in training, you will spot it in your own games. Regular puzzle practice (solving 10-20 tactics per day) is one of the most efficient ways to improve at chess. The eleven chapters below cover the core tactical motifs, in roughly the order you should learn to recognise them, finishing with a practical framework for finding tactics in your own games.

1. Forks

One piece attacks two enemy pieces at once - knight forks, pawn forks, and the royal fork.

2. Pins

Absolute and relative pins, and how to exploit or break them.

3. Skewers

The reverse of a pin - attack the valuable piece in front to win the piece behind it.

4. Discovered Attacks

Moving one piece unleashes an attack from another - including discovered and double check.

5. Deflection

Force a key defender away from the square or line it needs to guard.

6. Decoy (Attraction)

Lure the enemy king or another piece onto a square where it can be exploited.

7. Overloading & Removing the Defender

Exploit a piece with too many jobs, or eliminate the piece guarding a target.

8. Zwischenzug

The "in-between move" - a forcing move inserted before an expected recapture.

9. X-Ray Attacks & Defence

Attacks and defences that operate "through" another piece on the same line.

10. Back-Rank Mate

The most common practical mating pattern, and how to defend against it with luft.

11. Finding Tactics in Your Games

Tactical alarm bells, the candidate move method, and a practical training plan.
