5. Deflection

Deflection (also called "decoy to a bad square" in some contexts) is a tactical device where you force a key defensive piece to abandon its defensive duty. You sacrifice material (or simply make a forcing move) to drive the defender away from the square or line it needs to guard.

Classic pattern: Black has a queen on d7 defending a bishop on d4. White plays Rxd7!, forcing Black's queen to recapture Qxd7 — away from its defence of d4. White then takes the bishop with material gain.

Recognising Deflection Opportunities

Ask yourself: "Which piece is performing a critical defensive function?" If a single piece is the sole defender of a mate threat, a back-rank weakness, or a valuable piece, consider how to drive it away. Common deflection ideas:

  • Offering a sacrifice to a piece that must be captured (e.g., a rook sacrifice on the first rank to drag the king away from the defence)
  • Checks that force the king to a bad square where it is less active or exposes other weaknesses
  • Tactical sequences that force the opponent to address one threat at the cost of another
