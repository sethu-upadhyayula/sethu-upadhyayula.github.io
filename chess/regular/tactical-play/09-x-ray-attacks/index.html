9. X-Ray Attacks and X-Ray Defence

An x-ray attack occurs when a sliding piece (rook, bishop, or queen) attacks a square or piece through another piece — friendly or enemy — that is sitting on the same line. The "x-rayed" piece is influenced by an attacker that appears, at first glance, to be blocked.

X-ray attack example: Two rooks face each other on the same open file, separated by an enemy pawn or piece. If that blocking piece moves or is captured, both rooks suddenly "see" each other and the square behind the original blocker — a capture on that square may be met by a recapture from the rook "through" the x-ray.

X-Ray Defence

An x-ray defence happens when a piece appears to defend a square only "through" another piece. For example, a queen on d1 may appear to support a piece on d5, with a friendly piece on d3 in between — if the piece on d3 moves away, the queen's defence of d5 is suddenly real, not just apparent. Strong players account for x-ray relationships when calculating exchanges, because the "real" balance of attackers and defenders on a square can change the moment an in-between piece moves.

X-Rays and Pins/Skewers

X-ray attacks are closely related to pins and skewers — in fact, a pin is simply an x-ray attack where the front piece is more valuable to keep safe than to move. Recognising the x-ray relationship between your pieces and the opponent's — even when several pieces sit "in the way" — often reveals tactics that are otherwise invisible.
