3. Skewers

A skewer is the reverse of a pin. A sliding piece attacks a valuable piece in the front - that piece moves to safety, and the lesser piece behind it is captured. The most common skewers involve the king being attacked (forcing it to move) with a rook, queen, or bishop, and then capturing a piece it was in front of.

White rook on a1 checks the Black king on a4. After the king moves off the a-file, the rook captures the Black rook on a8.

Common Skewer Patterns

  • King-Rook skewer: Most common; the king is forced off a file or rank, the rook behind is taken.
  • King-Queen skewer: If a king is ever in line with its own queen, a rook or bishop can execute a skewer.
  • Queen-Rook skewer: Even without king involvement, any time a queen and rook are lined up on a diagonal, file, or rank, a bishop or rook can execute a skewer.

Key insight: Skewers are often missed because players focus on defending the front piece without noticing the piece behind it.
