1. Forks

A fork occurs when one piece attacks two or more enemy pieces simultaneously. The opponent can only respond to one threat at a time, so the attacker wins material.

Knight Fork

The knight fork is the most feared and most common type of fork. Because the knight attacks squares of both colours and moves in a unique L-shape, it can attack a king and queen (or any two valuable pieces) in positions that are difficult to spot. Many games have been decided by a knight fork winning the queen.

Classic pattern: A knight on c7 (after moving from elsewhere) can fork the king on e8 and rook on a8. The opponent must deal with the check, and the rook is lost.

Knight on d5 forks the Black queen on b6 and rook on f4

Other Forks

  • Pawn fork: A pawn advances to attack two pieces — extremely common and hard to spot because players underestimate pawns.
  • Queen fork: The queen's range makes queen forks easy, but they are often anticipated because the queen's attacks are visible from afar.
  • Rook fork: A rook on an open file or rank attacks two pieces in a line.
  • Bishop fork: A bishop on a diagonal attacks two pieces; less common but still important to know.
  • Royal fork: Any piece that simultaneously attacks the king and queen — since the king must escape check, the queen is lost.

How to Set Up Forks

Most forks do not arise naturally — they must be created through preparatory moves. Common setups include:

  • Forcing an enemy king to a specific square with a check, then forking with a knight
  • Deflecting a piece from guarding a key square, then delivering a fork
  • Sacrificing a piece to create the fork in the resulting position
