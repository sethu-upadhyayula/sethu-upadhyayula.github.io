Basics of Chess

Chess is a two-player strategy board game played on an 8×8 grid. Understanding the board, the pieces, and the fundamental rules is the starting point for every chess journey — from beginner to grandmaster.

1. The Board

The chessboard consists of 64 squares arranged in an 8×8 grid, alternating between light and dark colours. The board is always oriented so that each player has a light square in the bottom-right corner ("light on right").

Files, Ranks, and Squares

  • Files run vertically (columns) and are labelled a through h from left to right from White's perspective.
  • Ranks run horizontally (rows) and are numbered 1 through 8 from White's side of the board.
  • Every square is identified by its file letter followed by its rank number. For example, e4 is the square on the e-file, 4th rank — the most common first move in chess.
  • Diagonals run at 45° angles across the board and are important for bishop and queen movement.

Light and Dark Squares

There are exactly 32 light squares and 32 dark squares. Squares of the same colour share diagonals but never share ranks or files. This distinction is critically important for bishop movement and endgame fortress positions.

Empty chessboard — light square always bottom-right (h1 is light)

Key Central Squares

The four central squares — e4, d4, e5, d5 — are the most strategically important squares on the board. Controlling the centre gives your pieces maximum mobility and restricts your opponent's options. The extended centre also includes c3, d3, e3, f3 and their mirror squares.

2. The Pieces

Each player starts with 16 pieces: 1 king, 1 queen, 2 rooks, 2 bishops, 2 knights, and 8 pawns. White always moves first.

PieceSymbolValueStarting SquaresMovement
King ♔ ♚ ∞ (priceless) e1 / e8 One square in any direction (horizontally, vertically, or diagonally). Cannot move into check. Most important piece — losing it means losing the game.
Queen ♕ ♛ ~9 points d1 / d8 Any number of squares along a rank, file, or diagonal. The most powerful piece on the board. Combines rook and bishop movement.
Rook ♖ ♜ ~5 points a1, h1 / a8, h8 Any number of squares horizontally or vertically (along ranks and files). Cannot jump over pieces. Most powerful in open files and the 7th rank.
Bishop ♗ ♝ ~3 points c1, f1 / c8, f8 Any number of squares diagonally. Always remains on the same colour it started on. One bishop covers light squares, the other covers dark squares.
Knight ♘ ♞ ~3 points b1, g1 / b8, g8 L-shaped: 2 squares in one direction, then 1 square perpendicular (or vice versa). The only piece that can jump over other pieces. Always lands on the opposite colour from its starting square.
Pawn ♙ ♟ ~1 point a2–h2 / a7–h7 Moves forward one square (or two squares from its starting position on the 2nd/7th rank). Captures diagonally forward one square. Cannot move backward. The "soul of chess" (Philidor).

Material Values and Exchanges

The material values above are rough guides for evaluating exchanges. Key principles:

  • Queen (~9) ≈ Rook (~5) + Bishop (~3) + one pawn (with some positional bonus)
  • Trading a rook for a bishop or knight is called "winning/losing the exchange"
  • Two bishops together (the "bishop pair") are often worth slightly more than a bishop and knight
  • In the endgame, the king becomes an active piece and should be used aggressively

The Knight's Movement

The knight moves in an L-shape: exactly 2 squares in one orthogonal direction, then 1 square perpendicular. From the centre of the board (e.g., d4), a knight controls 8 squares. From the corner (a1), it only controls 2. This is why "A knight on the rim is dim" — always aim to centralise your knights.

3. Special Rules

Castling

Castling is a special move involving the king and a rook. The king moves two squares toward a rook, and the rook jumps over the king to the square on the king's other side. It is the only move in chess where two pieces move simultaneously.

Kingside Castling (0-0)

King moves from e1 to g1; rook moves from h1 to f1. (For Black: e8 to g8, rook h8 to f8.)

Queenside Castling (0-0-0)

King moves from e1 to c1; rook moves from a1 to d1. (For Black: e8 to c8, rook a8 to d8.)

Conditions for Castling

  • Neither the king nor the chosen rook has previously moved
  • There are no pieces between the king and the rook
  • The king is not currently in check
  • The king does not pass through a square that is attacked by an opponent's piece
  • The king does not land on a square that is in check

Note: The rook may pass through an attacked square; only the king's path matters.

White can castle both sides: king on e1, rooks on a1 and h1

En Passant

En passant (French: "in passing") is a special pawn capture. If a pawn advances two squares from its starting position and lands beside an opponent's pawn, the opponent's pawn may capture it as if it had only moved one square. This capture must be made immediately on the very next move — the right to capture en passant expires after one move.

Example: White pawn on e5, Black plays d7–d5 (landing on d5 next to the e5 pawn). White may capture: exd6, with the white pawn ending up on d6 and the black pawn on d5 removed from the board.

En passant: White pawn on e5, Black just played d7–d5. White can capture exd6.

Promotion

When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board (the 8th rank for White, 1st rank for Black), it must be promoted to another piece: queen, rook, bishop, or knight. The player chooses which piece. Promotion to a queen (called "queening") is by far the most common choice, as the queen is the most powerful piece. Occasionally, promoting to a knight ("underpromotion") is the correct move to avoid stalemate or to deliver an immediate fork.

4. Check, Checkmate, Stalemate, and Draws

Check

A king is in check when it is directly attacked by one or more of the opponent's pieces. When in check, the player must resolve the check on their next move. There are exactly three ways to escape check:

  1. Move the king to a safe square
  2. Block the check by interposing a friendly piece between the attacker and the king (only possible against sliding pieces — rooks, bishops, queens)
  3. Capture the attacking piece

A player cannot make a move that leaves or places their own king in check. Check is notated with a + symbol (e.g., Qh5+).

Checkmate

Checkmate occurs when a king is in check and there is no legal move to escape. The game ends immediately — the player who delivered checkmate wins. Checkmate is notated with # (e.g., Qh7#). The goal of chess is to checkmate the opponent's king.

Checkmate: Black king on e8 has no escape from Qf7#

Stalemate

Stalemate occurs when a player has no legal moves and their king is not in check. The game ends as a draw. This is a crucial distinction: stalemate is not a loss, it is a draw! A player on the verge of losing can sometimes force stalemate as a last resort. Conversely, a winning player must avoid accidentally stalemating their opponent.

Other Ways to Draw

  • Insufficient Material: Neither player has enough pieces to force checkmate (e.g., K vs K, K+B vs K, K+N vs K).
  • 50-Move Rule: If 50 consecutive moves pass without a pawn move or a capture, either player may claim a draw.
  • Threefold Repetition: If the same position occurs three times (with the same player to move and same castling/en passant rights), either player may claim a draw.
  • Mutual Agreement: Both players may agree to a draw at any point during the game.

5. Algebraic Notation

Algebraic notation is the standard method for recording chess moves. Every serious chess player should learn to read and write it — it allows you to study games, solve puzzles, and communicate with other players worldwide.

Piece Identifiers

K = King    Q = Queen    R = Rook    B = Bishop    N = Knight    (Pawn has no letter)

Basic Move Notation

  • Pawn moves: just the destination square — e4, d5, c6
  • Piece moves: piece letter + destination square — Nf3 (knight to f3), Bb5 (bishop to b5)
  • Captures: insert x before the destination — Nxe5 (knight captures on e5), dxe5 (d-pawn captures on e5)
  • Check: append + — Qh5+
  • Checkmate: append # — Qf7#
  • Kingside castling: 0-0
  • Queenside castling: 0-0-0
  • Pawn promotion: destination square + piece letter — e8=Q (pawn promotes to queen on e8)
  • En passant: notated as a regular capture — exd6 (en passant implied by context)

Disambiguation

When two identical pieces can move to the same square, the notation must specify which piece moves:

  • If they are on different files: add the file letter — Rab1 (rook on the a-file moves to b1)
  • If they are on the same file but different ranks: add the rank number — R1b3
  • If both are needed: add both file and rank — Qd1e2

Sample Game Notation

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.0-0 Be7 6.Re1 b5 7.Bb3 d6 8.c3 0-0
— The Ruy López (Spanish Opening), a classic sequence reaching the main line

In this notation, White's move and Black's reply share the same move number (e.g., 1.e4 = White plays e4, e5 = Black replies with e5).

6. FEN and PGN: Recording Positions and Games

Beyond move-by-move algebraic notation, two further standards let players, databases, and engines store and exchange chess positions and games.

Forsyth-Edwards Notation (FEN)

FEN is a compact way to describe a single position — the placement of every piece, whose turn it is, castling rights, en passant target square, and move counters — in one line of text. This is what chess engines and websites use to set up "puzzle of the day" positions or analyse a specific moment in a game.

rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/4P3/8/PPPP1PPP/RNBQKBNR b KQkq e3 0 1
— The starting position after 1.e4

A FEN string has six fields, separated by spaces:

  1. Piece placement, rank by rank from rank 8 to rank 1, files a to h. Uppercase letters are White pieces, lowercase are Black. Numbers represent consecutive empty squares.
  2. Active colour: w or b — whose turn it is to move.
  3. Castling availability: any combination of K, Q, k, q (kingside/queenside, White/Black), or - if neither side can castle.
  4. En passant target square: the square behind a pawn that just moved two squares, or - if none.
  5. Halfmove clock: the number of halfmoves since the last capture or pawn move (used for the 50-move rule).
  6. Fullmove number: starts at 1, increments after Black's move.

Portable Game Notation (PGN)

PGN is the standard file format for storing entire games, including metadata. A PGN file contains a "tag pair" header section followed by the move list in algebraic notation:

[Event "World Championship"]
[Site "Reykjavik"]
[Date "1972.07.23"]
[White "Fischer, Robert J."]
[Black "Spassky, Boris V."]
[Result "1-0"]

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 ... 1-0

Almost every chess database, online platform, and analysis tool can import and export PGN. Learning to read a PGN header tells you the players, event, date, and result of a game at a glance — useful when studying master games such as those covered in the Master Games section.

7. An Introduction to Mating Patterns

Beginners often struggle to convert a winning material advantage into checkmate because they have not yet internalised common mating patterns — recurring arrangements of attacking pieces around the enemy king. Recognising these patterns instantly is one of the fastest ways to improve.

Back-Rank Mate

If the enemy king is castled behind a wall of unmoved pawns with no escape square ("luft"), a rook or queen can deliver mate by moving to the back rank. This pattern is covered in depth in Middlegame Tactics, but it is worth knowing from day one: always check whether your own back rank is safe before trading away your last defender.

Scholar's Mate (and how to avoid it)

One of the first "traps" beginners encounter: 1.e4 e5 2.Bc4 Bc5 3.Qh5 Nf6?? 4.Qxf7#. The queen and bishop combine to attack f7, the weakest square in Black's camp at the start of the game (defended only by the king). Developing knights before exposing the queen, and watching for early queen sorties, is the simple antidote.

Position before 4.Qxf7# — White queen on h5 and bishop on c4 both target f7

The Smothered Mate

A knight delivers checkmate to a king that is completely surrounded by its own pieces, unable to move or block. The classic sequence involves a queen sacrifice on g8 or h8 to force the king into the corner, followed by Nf7# (or similar), where the king cannot escape because its own rook and pawns block every square.

Ladder/Rolling Mate (Two Rooks or Queen + Rook)

Two heavy pieces (rooks or queen + rook) on adjacent ranks "roll" the enemy king to the edge of the board, each piece alternately giving check one rank closer to the edge until the king has nowhere left to go. This is one of the easiest mating techniques to learn and a building block for the K+R vs K technique covered in Theoretical Endgames.

8. Etiquette and Tournament Rules

Chess has a long tradition of etiquette, much of which is now codified in the official rules used in over-the-board tournaments (governed internationally by FIDE, the World Chess Federation).

Touch-Move Rule

In tournament play, if a player deliberately touches one of their own pieces, they must move that piece if it has a legal move (the "touch-move" rule). If a player touches an opponent's piece, they must capture it if a legal capture is possible ("touch-take"). Players who wish to adjust a piece on its square without being bound by touch-move must first announce "j'adoube" ("I adjust") before touching it.

Recording Moves

In standard time controls, both players are required to record every move on a scoresheet using algebraic notation. This serves to verify the result, settle disputes (e.g., claims of threefold repetition or the 50-move rule), and produce a record of the game for later study.

The Clock

Tournament games use a chess clock with two displays — one for each player's remaining time. After making a move, a player presses the clock to start the opponent's clock. Common time controls include "classical" (e.g., 90 minutes + 30-second increment per move), "rapid" (15–25 minutes), and "blitz" (3–5 minutes). Running out of time on the clock loses the game (unless the opponent has insufficient material to ever checkmate, in which case it is a draw).

General Conduct

  • Offer a handshake before and after the game.
  • Resign gracefully when the position is hopeless — playing on in a completely lost position against a much stronger opponent is considered poor form, though it is always a player's right to continue.
  • Do not talk, analyse out loud, or distract your opponent during the game.
  • Mobile phones must be switched off; in many federations, having a phone ring during a game results in an automatic loss.
  • Draw offers should be made only after making a move, and only once per "reasonable" interval — repeatedly offering draws to annoy an opponent is against the spirit of the rules.
The Golden Rule: If you touch it, you move it. If you can take it, you take it. Always think before you touch a piece!
