4. Check, Checkmate, Stalemate, and Draws

Check

A king is in check when it is directly attacked by one or more of the opponent's pieces. When in check, the player must resolve the check on their next move. There are exactly three ways to escape check:

  1. Move the king to a safe square
  2. Block the check by interposing a friendly piece between the attacker and the king (only possible against sliding pieces — rooks, bishops, queens)
  3. Capture the attacking piece

A player cannot make a move that leaves or places their own king in check. Check is notated with a + symbol (e.g., Qh5+).

Checkmate

Checkmate occurs when a king is in check and there is no legal move to escape. The game ends immediately — the player who delivered checkmate wins. Checkmate is notated with # (e.g., Qh7#). The goal of chess is to checkmate the opponent's king.

Checkmate: Black king on e8 has no escape from Qf7# — d8 and d7 are blocked by Black's own queen and pawn, e7 and f8 are covered by the queen, and f7 is defended by the bishop on h5

Stalemate

Stalemate occurs when a player has no legal moves and their king is not in check. The game ends as a draw. This is a crucial distinction: stalemate is not a loss, it is a draw! A player on the verge of losing can sometimes force stalemate as a last resort. Conversely, a winning player must avoid accidentally stalemating their opponent.

Other Ways to Draw

  • Insufficient Material: Neither player has enough pieces to force checkmate (e.g., K vs K, K+B vs K, K+N vs K).
  • 50-Move Rule: If 50 consecutive moves pass without a pawn move or a capture, either player may claim a draw.
  • Threefold Repetition: If the same position occurs three times (with the same player to move and same castling/en passant rights), either player may claim a draw.
  • Mutual Agreement: Both players may agree to a draw at any point during the game.
