5. Solving Strategy

Solving a threemover "cold" can feel intimidating: there are more candidate keys to try, more Black replies to check, and more places for a promising-looking line to quietly fail. The good news is that the same handful of questions, asked in the right order, will crack the overwhelming majority of threemovers — including the kind that appear in club bulletins, solving tourneys, and the FIDE Album. This chapter lays out that method step by step.

Step 1: Map the Mating Zone

Before considering any moves, survey the board purely in terms of geography. Where is the Black king, and which White pieces are within striking distance of it — either directly, or one move away from being so? Ask:

  • Which squares around the Black king are already covered by White pieces?
  • Which squares are not covered, i.e., potential flight squares?
  • Which White pieces, with one extra move, could cover those flight squares or deliver check?

This step doesn't yet tell you the key, but it tells you what the eventual mating position is likely to look like — which pieces will be doing the work in the final picture. In the Chapter 4 example, this step would have flagged the queen (e1, on the king's file) and the rook (b8, with 8th-rank and b-file reach) as the most likely mating pieces, with the knight on d4 as a probable supporting or sacrificial piece given its proximity.

Step 2: Hypothesize the Main Threat

Ask: if Black were simply forced to pass, what 3-move sequence would deliver mate? This hypothetical "main threat" is usually the backbone of the problem — the key's primary job is often to establish exactly this threat. Concretely:

  • Find a White move 1 that doesn't immediately do anything drastic to the position, but sets up a forcing sequence (often a check on move 2 followed by mate on move 3, assuming Black does nothing useful).
  • Verify this hypothetical sequence is actually legal and forcing — i.e., that the move-2 check genuinely restricts the king to squares where move 3 mates.

In the Chapter 4 example, the threat after 1.Nc6 was 2.Qe4+ followed by 3.Rxe8# — precisely this kind of "what if Black passes" sequence.

Step 3: Ask What Stops the Plan — Enumerate Defences

Now switch perspective to Black. Given the threat identified in Step 2, what are Black's candidate defences? Systematically consider:

  • Captures of the White piece that just moved (the key), if it's en prise.
  • Blocks — moves that interpose a piece on the threatened mating line.
  • Guards — moves that add defensive coverage to a square the mate would otherwise use.
  • Flights — king moves that step away from the threatened mate (if the king has any legal moves at all in the position).
  • Counter-threats — Black moves that don't directly address the threat but create a danger of their own (a check, a promotion threat, a discovered attack).

Each meaningful defence in this list becomes (or starts to become) a separate variation. In the Chapter 4 position, the two captures (1...Rxc6 and 1...Bxc6) and the king flight/capture (1...Kxd4) were exactly this kind of enumeration — three structurally different responses to one threat.

Step 4: For Each Defence, Find the Tailored Continuation

This is the heart of threemover solving and the step most often rushed. For each defence identified in Step 3, ask: has this defence changed the position in a way that opens a new mating idea? Specifically:

  • Did the defending piece move away from a square it was guarding, opening a new line for White?
  • Did the defending piece move onto a square where it now blocks one of Black's own pieces (a self-interference, as in the Nowotny and Grimshaw themes from Chapter 3)?
  • Did the defence create a new weakness near the king (a blocked flight square, an overloaded defender)?

The continuation is very often "the move that becomes possible because of what Black just did" — not a move that was already available before Black's defence. This is why a continuation can look completely different from the original threatened sequence: it's exploiting a new feature of the position, not forcing through the old plan against new resistance.

Step 5: Check for Short Mates

While enumerating defences in Step 3, keep a separate mental list of any defence that looks unusually weakening — a move that blocks the king's own escape, or walks a piece into a pin, or otherwise hands White an immediate mate-in-one on move 2. As discussed in Chapter 1, these short mates are legal and often present, and a solution write-up that omits them is incomplete. Don't let an unusually bad-looking Black move distract you from finding its (often very short and simple) refutation.

Step 6: Verify Uniqueness at Every Level

Once you believe you have a full solution, switch to verification mode and check uniqueness at each of the three White move-points:

  • Move 1 (the key): is there any other first move that also forces mate within three moves against every defence? If so, the problem is cooked and the "intended" key is not actually unique.
  • Move 2 (the continuation) in each variation: for the specific Black defence in that variation, is there more than one White second move that leads to mate on move 3? If so, that variation has a dual.
  • Move 3 (the mate) in each sub-variation: for the specific position reached after Black's second move, is there more than one mating move available? This is also a dual, at the final step.

A single dual in a minor, non-thematic line is often tolerated (and noted) in published problems; a dual in the main thematic variation, or any cook of the key, is considered a serious flaw and may render the problem unsound. This is why solving programs — software that exhaustively checks every line — became standard tools for composers: the combinatorial space of a threemover is large enough that a human can easily miss a dual or cook buried in an unglamorous side variation.

Putting It Together: A Worked Checklist

1. Map the mating zone — which White pieces could plausibly deliver mate near the Black king?
2. Hypothesize the main threat — what 3-move sequence works if Black "passes"?
3. Enumerate Black's defences — captures, blocks, guards, flights, counter-threats.
4. For each defence, find what it newly allows White to do — that's the continuation.
5. Check for short mates among unusually weakening defences.
6. Verify: no cook at move 1, no dual at move 2 or move 3 in any line.

Applying this checklist to the Chapter 4 position would proceed exactly along the lines of its annotated solution: map the zone (queen, rook, knight near the king), hypothesize 1.Nc6 with threat 2.Qe4+/3.Rxe8#, enumerate 1...Rxc6 / 1...Bxc6 / 1...Kxd4, find the tailored continuations 2.Qb4+ / 2.Rbe8+ / 2.Qe4+ respectively, note there are no short mates needed here, and verify each line ends in a unique mate. With a reliable method in hand, Chapter 6 turns from how to solve to how to judge — the aesthetic standards that separate a merely sound threemover from a celebrated one.
