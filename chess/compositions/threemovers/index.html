Threemovers

The threemover (#3) adds one full move of play to the twomover, and that single extra move opens a vastly richer universe of themes. White's key sets up a threat; Black defends; White plays a continuation that sets up a mating threat on move 3; Black defends again; and White mates. Each decision point multiplies the combinatorial complexity.

1. Definition and Structure

A threemover carries the stipulation #3. White moves first and must guarantee checkmate on or before White's third move, against any Black defence. The game tree has the structure:

  • Move 1: White plays the key.
  • Move 1 (Black): Black plays any legal defence.
  • Move 2: White plays a continuation (which may differ for each Black defence).
  • Move 2 (Black): Black plays any legal move.
  • Move 3: White mates.

Importantly, White may also deliver mate earlier (on move 2) if Black's first move is particularly weakening. These are called short variations and are fully legal — the stipulation says "mate in at most 3 moves."

The threemover is the second most studied genre after the twomover, and has its own dedicated competitions in the WCCT (World Chess Composition Tournament).

2. What Changes from the Twomover

Continuations

In a twomover, after the key, every Black reply is met immediately by a mating move. In a threemover, White's second move (the continuation) typically sets a new mating threat for move 3. This continuation can vary with each Black defence, making the problem much richer.

Strategic Content

The extra move allows White to carry out a genuine plan: prepare a battery, transfer a piece to a better square, force a specific pawn structure. The threemover thus admits more positional, strategic ideas alongside the tactical fireworks.

Changed Play Across Three Phases

A threemover can display changed play not just between "before and after the key" (as in twomovers) but across the try phase, the set play, and the post-key play — three separate phases. This expanded canvas allows composers to arrange intricate thematic cycles.

Secondary Threats

After the key threatens a specific mate, Black's defensive moves may carry their own "secondary threats." A good key anticipates these, ensuring White's continuation address both Black's defence and any counter-threat Black might be brewing.

3. Major Themes

The Nowotny Theme

A White piece sacrifices itself on the intersection square of a Black rook's file/rank and a Black bishop's diagonal. Both pieces can capture, but whichever one does, it interferes with the other. This was first demonstrated systematically by Anton Nowotny in 1854. In a threemover, the Nowotny sacrifice can appear on move 1 or move 2, with rich consequences branching from each capture.

The Grimshaw Theme

Two Black pieces — typically a rook and a bishop — each interfere with the other when they move to the same critical square. Unlike the Nowotny (which requires a White sacrifice), the Grimshaw arises purely from Black's own moves. When the Black bishop moves to square X, it blocks the Black rook's control of that square, and vice versa. The White continuation exploits whichever Black piece has been interfered with.

Half-Battery

A White battery in which the front piece has not yet been activated — the "half-battery" is primed to fire. The key moves one piece out of the way to reveal the battery's potential. In threemovers, half-batteries can appear on move 1 (key fires the battery) or be set up on move 2 for a move-3 mate.

The Turton Doubling

A long-range piece moves along a line, and a second long-range piece of the same type moves in behind it — but the first piece then continues further. This "doubling" manoeuvre creates a powerful battery that delivers mate. The Turton appears naturally in threemovers where the extra move provides space for the manoeuvre.

Logical Problems (Logische Aufgaben)

Developed by the German school, logical problems have a main plan that cannot be executed directly due to a "foreplan" obstacle. White must first play a preliminary move (the foreplan) to remove the obstacle, after which the main plan works. This idea of "preparation before execution" is the defining feature of the logical tradition in threemovers and moremovers.

5. Solving Strategy

Solving threemovers requires a systematic but theme-sensitive approach:

  1. Identify the mating zone. Which pieces are close enough to deliver mate in 3? Focus on the geometry near the Black king.
  2. Look for a main threat. What 3-move plan does White want to execute? This often means: key move → White's 2nd move gives check or creates threat → White's 3rd move mates.
  3. Ask what stops the plan. What Black defences disrupt this plan? These define the thematic variations.
  4. Check for short mates. If Black plays a particularly bad move, White may mate in 2. Don't overlook these — they must be accounted for.
  5. Verify the key is unique. Make sure no other first move achieves the same result (no dual on move 1).

The biggest pitfall in threemovers is dualism — where White has two different ways to mate in some variation. Even if the key is unique, a dual on move 2 or 3 is a serious flaw. This is why threemovers are harder to compose correctly than twomovers.

6. Aesthetic Ideals

The threemover is held to the same standards as the twomover, with additional expectations:

  • Thematic unity across all variations: The variations should share a common idea, not merely be a collection of distinct mating patterns.
  • No dualism: In every variation, White's continuation and mating move should be unique. Dualism (two equally valid mating sequences) is the most common flaw in threemover composition.
  • Interesting second move: The continuation (White's move 2) should not be trivially obvious. The finest threemovers have surprising or paradoxical second moves in the main variations.
  • Economical construction: With more pieces on the board (threemovers typically need more material to construct), economy becomes harder — and more impressive when achieved.
