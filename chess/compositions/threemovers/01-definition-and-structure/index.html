1. Definition and Structure

A threemover carries the stipulation #3 — "White to move, mate in three." It is the second-most-studied genre of orthodox direct-mate composition, after the twomover, and many composers consider it the genre where the art form truly opens up: the threemover is the smallest unit of composition in which White can carry out a genuine multi-step plan rather than a single tactical blow.

The Five-Ply Game Tree

Formally, a threemover is a tree of half-moves (plies) with the following shape:

  • Ply 1 (White): the key — White's first move. This must be a legal move that guarantees the rest of the tree exists, against every possible Black reply.
  • Ply 2 (Black): Black plays any legal move — there is no requirement that Black "cooperate." The solver (and the composer) must consider every single legal Black reply, however passive or however active.
  • Ply 3 (White): the continuation — White's second move. Crucially, the continuation may be different for each distinct Black defence at ply 2. This branching is what gives threemovers their combinatorial richness: a position with a dozen meaningful Black defences may need a dozen different (though often thematically related) continuations.
  • Ply 4 (Black): Black again plays any legal move.
  • Ply 5 (White): White delivers checkmate.

Every one of Black's replies at plies 2 and 4 must be considered — including checks, captures, and moves that look entirely irrelevant to the position's "main idea." A problem in which some obscure Black pawn push has no prepared mating reply is unsound, regardless of how beautiful the main line is. This obligation to handle the entire tree, not just the headline variation, is what makes composing (and verifying) threemovers by hand so demanding, and why computer-assisted testing ("solving programs") became essential to the craft in the late twentieth century.

Short Mates (Mate in Two)

The stipulation #3 means "mate at most in three moves" — White is not required to wait. If, after the key, some particular Black defence is so weakening that White can already force mate on move 2, that is entirely legal and is called a short mate or short variation.

Short mates are not bugs; they are a normal and often thematically significant part of the problem. A composer may deliberately arrange for one or two "obvious" Black defences to be punished with an immediate (short) mate, while the more subtle defences require the full three moves. That said, an excessive number of short mates, especially if they overshadow the main three-move lines, can be seen as a construction weakness — it can suggest the position is "too generous," giving White easy wins in lines that were not meant to be the heart of the problem.

White's Obligations: No "Mate in 1" Allowed Before the Key

For the problem to be meaningful, White must not already have a mate-in-one available in the diagram position (this would make the stipulation trivial or contradictory — the problem says "mate in 3", implying 3 is the minimum needed, achieved only by the unique key). More subtly, the position must not admit any other first move that also leads to mate within the stipulated number of moves — any such alternative is a cook, and a cooked problem is considered unsound and is typically withdrawn or corrected by the composer.

Set Play and Try Play

Two important auxiliary concepts carry over from twomovers, with extra richness in the longer genre:

  • Set play is the analysis of the diagram position before the key is played — "if it were Black to move now, what would happen?" In a threemover, set play can itself extend across two White moves, giving composers the option of changed play: the same Black defence might be answered completely differently depending on whether White has played the key yet.
  • Try play consists of plausible-looking first moves that fail to one (or more) Black defences. In threemovers, a try can fail not at move 1 but deep in a variation — for instance, a try's continuation might walk into a stalemate, or fail to deal with a counter-threat that only becomes apparent after Black's second move. The deeper the point of failure, the more instructive the try tends to be.

Diagram Conventions and Competition

Like all orthodox problems, a threemover is presented as a diagram (the starting position), the stipulation (#3), and an indication of which side is to move (White, by the convention of direct mates). The threemover has its own dedicated sections in major tourneys, including the triennial FIDE Album and the World Chess Composition Tournament (WCCT), where national teams submit threemovers judged specifically against threemover criteria — distinct from the criteria applied to twomovers or studies. This separate judging recognises that the threemover rewards different qualities: less emphasis on a single dazzling key, more emphasis on the coherence of an entire tree of variations.

A Minimal Worked Example of the Structure

To make the five-ply structure concrete before we look at a full thematic example in Chapter 4, consider the skeleton of a threemover's solution as it would be written out:

1. (key move) — sets up a threat for move 3
  1...(defence A) 2. (continuation A) (any Black move) 3. (mate)
  1...(defence B) 2. (continuation B) (any Black move) 3. (mate, possibly different)
  1...(weak defence C) 2. (short mate)

Note how the continuation can differ across A and B, while C collapses into a two-move finish.

This skeleton is exactly the shape we will fill in with a real position in Chapter 4, after first examining (Chapter 2) precisely what new possibilities the extra move and the extra ply create compared to the twomover.
