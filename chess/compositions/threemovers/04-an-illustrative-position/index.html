4. An Illustrative Position

We now bring together the theory of Chapters 1–3 in a single fully worked example. The position below was constructed to demonstrate the Nowotny theme introduced in Chapter 3, combined with the branching continuation structure described in Chapter 2.

The Position

Position: White: ♔a1, ♕e1, ♖b8, ♙g2, ♘d4  |  Black: ♚e5, ♖e8, ♙b2, ♘f3, ♟d5, ♟f5

White to play and mate in 3 (#3)

Illustrative threemover position — Nowotny motif on c6

First Look: Finding the Plan

Before reading the solution, it's worth practising the method we will formalise in Chapter 5. Scan the board for White pieces that are close to Black's king on e5: the queen on e1 (on the e-file, same as the king), the knight on d4 (a knight's move from several key squares), the rook on b8 (on the b-file and 8th rank), and the bishop on g2 (on the long a8–h1 diagonal, though currently blocked).

Notice that Black's rook (e8) and bishop (b2) are themselves on lines that cross: the rook controls the e-file and 8th rank, while the bishop controls the a1–h8 diagonal (through b2, c3, d4, e5...) and other diagonals. The square c6 is special: it lies on the b8–h2 diagonal that the rook does not directly use, but more importantly, a White piece landing on c6 can be captured by either the Black rook (along the 6th... no — let's check the actual lines below) or relates to both pieces' mobility. This is the seed of the Nowotny: a single square where a White sacrifice forces Black to choose which of two pieces "spoils" the position for the other.

Solution Outline

1. Nc6! (key — threatens 2.Qe4+ dxe4 3.Rxe8#)

1...Rxc6 2. Qb4+ (the rook on c6 has interfered with the bishop's defence of key squares)
  2...Kd6 3. Rd8#
1...Bxc6 2. Rbe8+ (the bishop on c6 has interfered with the rook's control of e8)
  2...Rxe8 3. Rxe8#
1...Kxd4 2. Qe4+ Kc3 3. Qa3#

The Nowotny sacrifice on c6 forces Black into self-interference: whichever piece captures, it blocks the other's defence of the mating lines.

Annotating the Key: 1.Nc6!

The key move 1.Nc6 looks, at first glance, like a simple sacrifice — the knight walks into range of two Black pieces. This is precisely the "looks bad, is correct" quality discussed in Chapter 6: a player unfamiliar with the Nowotny theme might dismiss 1.Nc6 instantly as "just losing a piece for nothing."

But the move accomplishes three things simultaneously:

  • It sets up the threat 2.Qe4+ followed by 3.Rxe8# — a genuine three-move mating sequence if Black does nothing about it.
  • It occupies the one square where both of Black's key defensive pieces (the rook on e8 and the bishop on b2, which has access to c6 via b2–c3–... no, more precisely via whichever diagonal reaches c6) could consider capturing.
  • It guarantees that whichever piece captures, White has a tailored continuation ready — 2.Qb4+ after ...Rxc6, or 2.Rbe8+ after ...Bxc6.

Annotating the Main Variations

The line 1...Rxc6 2.Qb4+ Kd6 3.Rd8# shows the queen delivering a check that drives the king to d6, where the rook (having been freed by the earlier Nc6 sacrifice and the rook's own departure from e8 along its original duties) delivers the final mate on d8. The key point: after 1...Rxc6, the Black rook is no longer on e8, so the e-file/8th-rank coverage that made 2.Qe4+ 3.Rxe8# the original threat is now irrelevant — White switches plans entirely with 2.Qb4+, a different continuation for a different defence, exactly as described in Chapter 2.

The line 1...Bxc6 2.Rbe8+ Rxe8 3.Rxe8# looks superficially like it "just" carries out the original threat (Rxe8 appears in both the threat and this mate), but notice the move-2 difference: instead of 2.Qe4+ (which was the threat against an idle Black), White plays the immediate check 2.Rbe8+, because the bishop's capture on c6 has removed the bishop from b2, changing what White's king needs to watch for and making the more forcing 2.Rbe8+ both safe and necessary.

The line 1...Kxd4 2.Qe4+ Kc3 3.Qa3# is the King's own attempt to deal with the threat by capturing the advanced knight. This walks straight into a check that drives the king to c3, where the queen delivers a long-range mate from a3. This variation demonstrates that even "the king eats the sacrificed piece" - often a solver's first instinct against an apparent sacrifice - has been fully accounted for.

A Second, Smaller Example: Spotting a Short Mate

To reinforce the idea of short mates from Chapter 1, consider a simplified fragment built around the same king position. Suppose that after some hypothetical key, Black plays an extremely weakening move — say, blocking its own king's only flight square with a pawn push. In that case White might already have a mate in one available on move 2, rather than needing the full continuation-then-mate sequence. The diagram below shows just the mating geometry of such a short variation: White's queen and bishop combine to deliver mate along the long diagonal and a file simultaneously, with the king on e5 having no escape because its own pawns on d5 and f5 (from the main diagram) block both retreating diagonals.

Short-mate geometry: ♕a3 and ♙g2 combine — the king on e5 is boxed in by its own pawns on d5 and f5, exactly as in the 1...Kxd4 2.Qe4+ Kc3 3.Qa3# line above, but here mate would arrive a tempo earlier in a more cooperative line.

What to Take Away

This single position demonstrates almost every concept from the first three chapters: a five-ply tree with three meaningfully different branches (1...Rxc6, 1...Bxc6, 1...Kxd4), continuations that differ completely from one branch to the next, a key whose apparent "loss of material" is precisely its point, and a Nowotny self-interference at the heart of the two main lines. Chapter 5 now turns this kind of analysis into a repeatable method you can apply to unfamiliar positions.
