Retrograde Analysis

Retrograde analysis — "retros" — asks not what will happen next, but what must have happened before. Given a position on the board, can you reconstruct the game that led to it? Retros occupy the intersection of chess and logic puzzles, demanding that solvers reason about legality, history, and the laws of the game with mathematical precision.

The six chapters below introduce retrograde analysis from first principles: what it is and why legality is the central concept, the major families of retro problems (proof games, shortest proof games, last-move problems, and castling/en passant deductions), the historical role of Sam Loyd in founding the genre, a deep dive into proof game construction, a fully worked illustrative position, and a general method for approaching any retro problem.

1. What Is Retrograde Analysis?

The core idea: positions must be reachable by legal play, and history — not just the rules of movement — matters.

2. Types of Retro Problems

Proof games, shortest proof games, last-move problems, castling/en passant analysis, and illegal positions.

3. Sam Loyd and the Birth of Retro Problems

How Sam Loyd pioneered retrograde analysis as an artistic medium in the 19th century.

4. Proof Games in Detail

Captured pieces, tempo moves, switchbacks, and excelsior — the building blocks of modern proof games.

5. An Illustrative Position

A fully worked castling-rights retro, with diagram and step-by-step retro analysis.

6. How to Solve Retro Problems

A general method: legality, captures, last moves, conventions, and verification.
