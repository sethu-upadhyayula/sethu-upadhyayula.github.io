4. Proof Games in Detail

Of all the retro genres, the proof game (PG) is the one in which modern composition is most active. A proof game is a chess problem of the form: "Reconstruct a legal game, starting from the initial position, that arrives at the given diagram after exactly n moves by White and m moves by Black." Unlike a forward mate-in-n, the solver does not need to find a winning continuation - only a consistent history. But "only" understates the difficulty enormously: a fully-specified diagram with, say, 16 moves per side has an astronomical number of candidate move sequences, and the composer's task (and the solver's) is to show that exactly one of them - the intended solution - actually reaches the diagram in the stipulated number of moves.

Dedicated solving and verification programs such as Popeye and Natch are now standard tools in the retro community, used both by composers (to verify that their intended solution is unique) and by solvers (to check candidate solutions). Despite this computational support, proof game composition remains a deeply human art, prized for its elegance, thematic unity, and the surprise of its key ideas. The sections below describe the principal artistic devices that recur throughout proof game composition.

Captured Pieces

In almost every proof game diagram, some pieces present in the starting array are missing from the final position. Each of these "disappearances" must be accounted for: which piece captured which, on which square, and at what point in the sequence? This bookkeeping exercise is often the single hardest part of both composing and verifying a proof game, for several reasons:

  • Pawn structure constraints. A pawn can only capture diagonally, one square at a time (except en passant). If, in the diagram, a White pawn stands on the c-file but started on the b-file, it must have captured exactly once, on a c-file square, to get there - and the captured piece must have been a Black piece that could legally have stood on that square at that time.
  • Move-order dependencies. The order in which captures occur can be forced by other constraints. For example, if a White bishop needs to pass through a square currently occupied by a Black piece that is later captured elsewhere, the capture that clears the square must happen before the bishop's transit - fixing a relative order on otherwise independent-seeming moves.
  • Counting captures against total moves. Every capture "uses up" one move from the capturing side. If the diagram is missing, say, three White pawns and two Black minor pieces, then at least five of the moves in the proof game must be captures (possibly more, if recaptures occurred), and these must be distributed consistently with the stipulated move count.

A well-constructed proof game often hides its capture sequence in plain sight - the diagram looks "normal," with a roughly balanced material count, but reconstructing exactly how each missing piece disappeared (and confirming that no other capture sequence of the same total length is possible) is the crux of the solution.

Tempo Moves

Sometimes the most direct route for a piece to reach its diagram square uses fewer moves than the side is "allowed" - i.e., fewer than the move count permits. The side must then "waste" one or more moves elsewhere, without disturbing the rest of the position, to use up the remaining tempo. A classic device for this is a knight tour: a knight makes a round trip - for example, Ng1-f3-g1 - returning to its starting square after exactly two moves, having achieved nothing positionally but having consumed exactly the two tempi needed to synchronise with the other side's move count.

Identifying where the tempo move occurs, and which piece makes it, is frequently the key insight of a proof game. A solver who correctly identifies all the "productive" moves (the ones that actually transform the starting position into the diagram) but miscounts the total will often find they are one or two moves short - and the missing moves are exactly the hidden tempo device.

Switchback

A switchback is a special case of a tempo device in which a piece moves away from its home square and then returns to that exact square later in the game - appearing, in the final diagram, as if it had never moved at all. Switchbacks are powerful because they can account for "extra" moves while leaving the piece's final position completely unchanged, and because they can have far-reaching consequences for castling rights: if a king or rook switches back to its home square, the piece looks exactly as it did at the start - but castling rights, once forfeited by the initial departure, do not return. A proof game built around a king or rook switchback is therefore often paired with a forward-play problem (as discussed in Chapter 2) where the "obvious" castling move is, in fact, illegal - a beautiful synthesis of the proof-game and castling-rights genres.

Excelsior in Proof Games

"Excelsior" themes - named after a famous Sam Loyd problem in which a humble pawn marches the entire length of the board to promote and deliver the decisive blow - appear in proof games whenever a pawn promotes to a piece other than a queen in a position where a queen would normally be the expected (and stronger) choice. The natural question this raises is: why did this side promote to a knight, rook, or bishop instead of a queen?

In the context of a proof game, the answer is almost always one of the following:

  • Stalemate avoidance. If promoting to a queen would (at some intermediate point in the sequence, or in the final diagram itself) leave the opponent with no legal moves while not in check - a stalemate - but promoting to a different piece does not, then the proof game's legality requires the non-queen promotion.
  • Check avoidance / move-count consistency. A queen on the promotion square might deliver an unwanted check, forcing a response that would consume an extra move and break the stipulated move count. A knight or rook promotion might avoid this check entirely.
  • Geometric necessity. In some constructions, only a specific minor piece, placed on the promotion square, can subsequently reach the square it occupies in the final diagram within the remaining number of moves - a queen's broader mobility is irrelevant if the piece needs to make a specific kind of move (e.g., a knight's-move jump) that only a knight can make.

Excelsior-themed proof games are prized because they combine a long, often visually striking pawn march with a logically forced "wrong" promotion - uniting a dramatic visual narrative with rigorous logical necessity, which is precisely the aesthetic proof game composers strive for.

A Worked Sketch: A Short Proof Game

To see these ideas in miniature, consider a (hypothetical, simplified) proof game in 3 moves for White and 3 for Black - "PG 3.0" - leading to a diagram where: White has played Nf3, Ng1 (a switchback!), and then e4; Black has played e5, Nc6, and Nf6. The "obvious" reading of the diagram - a White knight on g1 and pawn on e4, a Black knight on f6 and pawn structure with e5/Nc6 - looks like it could be reached in two moves per side (1.Nf3 Nc6 2.e4 - wait, that's only 1.5 moves for white and doesn't place the knight back on g1). The switchback (Nf3 then back to g1) is what "uses up" the third White move, while Black's third move (Nf6) is fully productive. Recognising that the knight's apparent "non-move" (g1 to g1) actually represents two real moves is exactly the kind of insight that makes proof games satisfying to solve - the diagram alone gives no hint that the White knight has been anywhere at all.
