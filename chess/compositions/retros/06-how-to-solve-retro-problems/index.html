6. How to Solve Retro Problems

Having seen what retro problems are (Chapter 1), the families they fall into (Chapter 2), their history (Chapter 3), the art of proof games (Chapter 4), and a single position worked through in full (Chapter 5), this final chapter steps back and asks a practical question: given an arbitrary retro problem, how should you actually go about solving it? Unlike a mate-in-n, where "try candidate moves and calculate" is always a reasonable starting strategy, retro problems reward a quite different — and more methodical — approach. What follows is a general toolkit, applicable to last-move problems, castling and en passant questions, proof games, and everything in between.

Step 1: Work Backwards From the Diagram, Not Forwards

The single most important habit shift, for a solver coming from forward-play problems, is to stop asking "what can happen next?" and start asking "what must have happened just before?" Every piece on the board got to its current square somehow, and every piece missing from its starting square left for a reason. Before considering any candidate solution, build a mental (or literal, on paper) list of every departure from the "everything is on its home square, nothing has been captured" baseline:

  • Which squares are occupied by a piece other than the one that starts there?
  • Which pieces present in the starting array are entirely absent from the diagram?
  • Which pawns have left their starting files, and onto which files have they moved?

This list is your set of "facts to be explained." A correct solution must account for every item on it — and, just as importantly, must not silently assume something the diagram doesn't support (such as castling rights that may have been forfeited, as in Chapter 5).

Step 2: Count Captures and Reconcile Material

Compare the material on the board to the starting array: 8 pawns, 2 rooks, 2 knights, 2 bishops, 1 queen, 1 king per side. Every piece that is "missing" was captured at some point (promotions can complicate this — see Step 3). The number of captures performed by each side is a hard constraint that interacts with everything else:

  • Total captures bound the total moves. In a proof game with a stipulated move count, each capture consumes exactly one move from the capturing side. If Black is missing a rook, a bishop, and three pawns, then White made at least five captures — and those five moves cannot simultaneously be doing anything else.
  • Captures must be geometrically possible. A piece can only be captured on a square it could legally have reached. If Black's missing bishop started on c8 (a dark square) and never promoted, it can only have been captured on a dark square — this alone can rule out large classes of otherwise-plausible capture sequences.
  • Pawn captures leave permanent fingerprints. A pawn that has changed files did so via exactly one capture per file-change (ignoring en passant, which changes files without the captured pawn ending up on the capturing pawn's destination file in the usual sense — captured en passant pawns vanish from a square the capturing pawn never occupies). A pawn now on the c-file that started on the a-file must have captured (at least) twice, moving a-file then b-file then c-file, or some such route — and each of those captures removed an enemy piece that must be accounted for in the overall material count.

Step 3: Check Pawn-Structure Consistency

Pawns are the most informative pieces in retro analysis because their movement rules are the most restrictive: forward only, one square (or two from the start), captures only diagonally, and — critically — pawns can never move backward or sideways. This irreversibility makes the pawn structure in a diagram a kind of fossil record of the game's history. Useful checks include:

  • Doubled or tripled pawns on a file can only arise if pawns from adjacent files captured onto that file (a pawn cannot pass through or jump over another pawn of either color on the same file). Each "extra" pawn on a file is evidence of at least one capture by a pawn that started on a neighboring file.
  • A pawn that has not moved (still on its second rank for White, seventh for Black) tells you that file's pawn-capture history is simple — that pawn played no part in any capture, which can simplify the bookkeeping enormously by letting you ignore that file entirely when counting captures.
  • Promoted pawns are the trickiest of all: a diagram with, say, three White rooks must have one rook that is a promoted pawn (since White starts with only two), and identifying which rook is the impostor — and which pawn promoted, on which square, and when — is often the central puzzle of a proof game. A useful heuristic: an "extra" piece of a given type, combined with a "missing" pawn on the file where that piece's promotion square lies, is strong (though not conclusive) evidence of the promotion's identity.

White has nine pawns (a2,b2,c2,c3,d4,e2,f2,g2,h2) and Black is missing its c-pawn. White to move. What does this tell us about the game's history?

Observation: White has nine pawns on the board, but each side starts with only eight. One of White's "pawns" on c2/c3/d4 must therefore be a promoted piece that has been demoted back to... no — pawns cannot be created by promotion; promotion only converts a pawn into a piece, never the reverse. A count of nine White pawns is therefore impossible in any legal position, regardless of history.

Resolution: This diagram, as given, cannot arise from any legal game — it is an illustration of an inconsistency check rather than a solvable problem. In a genuine composed position, the very first thing a solver should do is exactly this: count both sides' pawns and pieces of each type, and confirm that no count exceeds its legal maximum (8 pawns, and for each piece type, 2 plus the number of pawns "missing" from the board on that color, since only a missing pawn can have become an extra piece via promotion). A diagram that fails this check has either been mis-set-up or — as here — is being used purely to demonstrate the check itself. Always perform this arithmetic before investing time in deeper deductions.

Step 4: The Sherlock Holmes Method — Eliminate the Impossible

Sherlock Holmes's famous dictum — "when you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth" — is, almost word for word, the correct algorithm for retro problems. The method has three parts:

  1. Enumerate candidates. List every history (last move, castling status, capture sequence, etc.) that is superficially consistent with the diagram — don't filter yet, just generate the full set of possibilities suggested by the position.
  2. Eliminate using hard constraints. Cross off any candidate that violates a rule absolutely: a pawn moving backward, a piece passing through an occupied square, a capture on a square the capturing piece's color could never reach, a move count that doesn't add up, a king that would have been in check after the proposed "last move" by the side not to move (this is the single most common elimination tool in last-move problems — if the side not to move would be in check given a candidate last move, and that check is not delivered by the move itself, the candidate is illegal, because you cannot leave your own king in check).
  3. Check what remains for uniqueness. In a well-composed problem, exactly one candidate should survive Step 2. If two or more remain, either the problem is unsound (a flaw the composer failed to catch) or you have missed an eliminating constraint — go back and look harder at pawn structure, captured-piece counts, and check status before concluding the problem is flawed.

The power of this method is that it does not require you to find the "right" answer directly — it only requires you to find reasons every other answer is wrong. This is often dramatically easier, because "wrong" answers tend to fail one of a small number of hard, checkable constraints (illegal pawn moves, impossible capture geometry, leaving a king in check), whereas the "right" answer has no such failure to search for.

Common Traps for Solvers

  • Assuming castling rights without proof. As Chapter 5 demonstrated, the presence of a king and rook on their home squares does not, by itself, establish that neither has moved. The correct default is the opposite: castling is presumed legal only if no sequence of moves can be shown to force the king or rook to have moved — i.e., the burden of proof is on showing castling is illegal, not on showing it is legal. Conflating these two directions of proof is the single most common error in castling-rights problems.
  • Forgetting en passant's time limit. An en passant capture is only legal immediately after the enemy pawn's two-square advance. A diagram showing a pawn that "could" be captured en passant tells you nothing unless the diagram also establishes that the two-square advance was the most recent move — a fact that, again, requires retrograde reasoning to establish, not just a glance at pawn placement.
  • Ignoring the side to move. Many last-move deductions hinge entirely on whose turn it is. "What was White's last move?" presupposes it is Black's turn; if you lose track of this, you can end up "explaining" a move by the wrong side entirely, as nearly happened in the e5/d5 pawn example back in Chapter 1.
  • Over-fitting to the "obvious" story. A diagram often suggests a narrative — a piece "looks like" it just arrived somewhere, a pawn "looks like" it just captured. The Sherlock Holmes method exists precisely to guard against this: the obvious story is a candidate, not a conclusion, and must survive the same elimination process as every other candidate.
  • Miscounting tempo in proof games. As discussed in Chapter 4, it is easy to correctly identify all the "productive" moves in a proof game and then find you are one or two moves short of the stipulated total — the signature of a hidden tempo move or switchback that must be located before the solution is complete.

Putting It All Together

A practical solving session for a non-trivial retro problem typically proceeds in this order: (1) verify the diagram is materially legal (Step 3's pawn/piece counts); (2) build the list of "facts to be explained" — missing pieces, displaced pawns, unusual piece placements (Step 1); (3) use material and pawn-structure analysis to bound the number and rough nature of captures (Steps 2-3); (4) enumerate candidate last moves, capture sequences, or castling histories consistent with all of the above (Step 4, part 1); (5) eliminate candidates using check status, move-count parity, and geometric impossibility (Step 4, part 2); and (6) confirm uniqueness, or — if the problem permits — accept an ambiguous result (as in Chapter 5's castling example, where the "innocent" history could not be ruled out).

This six-chapter tour has covered the conceptual foundations, the major problem families, the genre's history from Sam Loyd onward, the special intricacies of proof games, a fully worked example, and now a general-purpose solving method. Retrograde analysis rewards exactly the kind of patient, rule-driven, elimination-based thinking outlined here — and, once the habit of "thinking backwards" becomes natural, many positions that once looked impenetrable resolve themselves with surprising speed.
