3. Sam Loyd and the Birth of Retro Problems

Sam Loyd (1841-1911) was an American puzzle composer best known to the general public for mathematical and mechanical puzzles such as the "15 Puzzle," but within chess composition he holds a special place as the figure most responsible for establishing retrograde analysis as a serious and artistic branch of the game. Before Loyd, chess problems were almost exclusively forward-looking: "White to play and mate in n." Loyd recognised that the history of a position - not just its future - could be the source of an entirely new category of puzzle, and he exploited this insight with characteristic showmanship.

"Prove the Last Move"

Loyd's most influential contribution was the "Prove the Last Move" problem, often dressed up as an apparently ordinary mate-in-n. The hidden trap was that the stated solution depended on a fact about the position's history that was not obvious from the diagram alone - typically, whether a particular pawn capture had just occurred, which determined whether an en passant capture was available as the key move.

The format typically presented a position and posed two questions simultaneously, although only the first was stated explicitly: "White to play and mate in n" - with the unstated second question being "...but first, can you prove that White's claimed key move (often an en passant capture or a castling move) is actually legal?" A solver who jumped straight to forward analysis, assuming the key move was available, would find what looked like a solution - but a more careful solver, performing the retro analysis first, might discover that the apparently winning move was illegal, and that the true solution was a completely different (and less obvious) line.

"Trick and Trap" Problems

Loyd's "Trick and Trap" problems often disguised a retro element within an otherwise ordinary-looking mate-in-n problem. The "trick" was the deceptively obvious solution that depended on an unproven (and often false) assumption about the position's history; the "trap" was that solvers who fell for the trick would submit an incorrect solution, having failed to notice that the apparently available resource - a capture, a castling move, a particular promotion - was retrogradely impossible.

This style of composition had a profound pedagogical effect: it forced solvers to internalise the idea that a chess position carries more information than what is visible on the board. The diagram alone is necessary but not sufficient; the rules of the game impose constraints on history that can overturn an apparently correct forward analysis.

The En Passant Proof

One of Loyd's most celebrated retro constructions involves proving that a particular pawn capture must have been an en passant capture, because no other sequence of legal moves can account for the pawn's current position and the configuration of the board around it. The logical structure of such a proof typically runs as follows:

  1. Observe that a White pawn stands on, say, c6, and that there is no Black piece on b7 or d7 that it could have captured by a normal diagonal pawn capture arriving from c5 (those squares are either empty or occupied by pieces that - by further retro reasoning - could not have been there for the capture to occur normally).
  2. Note that a Black pawn could have stood on b7 (or d7) and advanced two squares to b5 (or d5), passing through b6 (or d6) - and that the White pawn on c5, attacking b6 (or d6), could then have captured it en passant, landing on b6 (or d6) rather than c6. If the diagram instead shows the White pawn having advanced further to c6, this single line of reasoning must be adapted - but the underlying logic, that an en passant capture is the only way to account for the observed pawn configuration, is the key move of the proof.
  3. Conclude that, since en passant is the only legal explanation for the current pawn structure, the position is consistent with - indeed requires - an en passant capture having occurred. This proof then establishes, for the purposes of a forward mate-in-n problem elsewhere in the position, that en passant rights (if relevant to a different pawn pair) existed at the relevant moment, or conversely that they have already been exhausted.

The precise details vary from problem to problem, but the underlying technique - eliminating all candidate histories except one, and thereby "proving" a fact about the game's past from the evidence in the present - is the essence of Loydian retro composition and remains the essence of the genre today.

Loyd's Legacy

After Loyd, retrograde analysis was taken up and systematised by later composers, most famously the German problemist Wolfgang Hundsdorfer writing under the pseudonym "Friedrich Wilhelm" (W.A. Shinkman and others) and, in the 20th century, by figures such as Otto Wurzburg and the prolific Niels Hoeg. The field was eventually formalised under the broader umbrella of "fairy chess" and retro problems gained their own dedicated notations (PG, SPG, "Forsyth-Edwards with retro annotations," etc.) and dedicated solving software. But it was Loyd who first demonstrated, in the 1850s through the 1890s, that a chess diagram could be a puzzle about time as much as about space - and that insight underlies every retro problem composed since.

Why This Matters for the Solver

The historical lesson for any solver approaching a retro problem is Loyd's own central teaching: never assume that a move is legal just because the diagram seems to permit it. Castling, en passant, and even the simple act of moving a piece that "looks like" it has always stood where it stands - all of these can be undermined by retrograde analysis. Conversely, a move that looks impossible at first glance may turn out to be the forced and unique solution once the position's history is properly reconstructed. Holding both possibilities in mind - "is this legal resource actually available?" and "is this apparently illegal resource actually forced?" - is the mindset that Loyd's problems were designed to instill, and it remains the mindset required for every retro problem discussed in the remaining chapters.
