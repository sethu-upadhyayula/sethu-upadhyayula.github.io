4. Grid Chess

Grid Chess is a fairy condition in the same family as Circe, Madrasi, and the other rule-changes from Chapter 3 — but instead of altering captures or check, it overlays an entirely new piece of geometry onto the board: a grid of lines that every move must respect.

The Rule

The 8×8 board is partitioned into sixteen 2×2 "cells" by a set of grid lines — typically the lines running between files b/c, d/e, and f/g (vertically), and between ranks 2/3, 4/5, and 6/7 (horizontally). Every move, for every piece (including pawns and the king, and including captures), must cross at least one grid line. A move that begins and ends within the same 2×2 cell — i.e., a move that crosses no grid line at all — is illegal, even if it would otherwise be a perfectly legal move under the normal rules of chess.

Note that this is purely an additional restriction layered on top of orthodox movement: nothing becomes legal under Grid Chess that wasn't legal before, but some normally-legal moves become illegal because they stay "inside the cell."

Visualising the Grid

The standard Grid Chess partition divides the board into a 4×4 arrangement of 2×2 cells:
  • Vertical grid lines fall between the b- and c-files, between the d- and e-files, and between the f- and g-files.
  • Horizontal grid lines fall between ranks 2 and 3, between ranks 4 and 5, and between ranks 6 and 7.

So, for example, a1/a2/b1/b2 form one cell; c1/c2/d1/d2 form the next cell to its right; e1/e2/f1/f2 the next; and g1/g2/h1/h2 the last cell of the bottom-left row of cells. The pattern repeats upward in bands of two ranks.

How Grid Lines Change Mobility

The practical effect of the grid is best seen piece by piece:

  • Kings normally have up to eight one-square moves. Under Grid Chess, a king on a square in the "interior" of a cell — i.e., not adjacent to any grid line — actually has no legal king-moves at all in some directions, because every neighbouring square inside the same cell is off-limits. A king on b2, for instance, can step to a1, a2, a3, b1, b3, c1, c2, or c3 in the ordinary game; under Grid Chess, the steps to a1, a2, a3, and b1 (all of which stay within the a1-b2 cell, since a1/a2/b1/b2 form a cell together) are illegal, leaving only b3, c1, c2, c3 as legal king moves — and even some of those must be checked against the grid lines on the other side.
  • Pawns moving one square forward from their second rank to their third rank automatically cross the rank-2/rank-3 grid line, so that move remains legal. But a pawn already on its third rank moving to its fourth rank stays within the same horizontal band (ranks 3-4 sit inside the same 2-rank cell-band only if no grid line separates them — check the specific line: ranks 2/3 and 4/5 are grid lines, so a pawn moving from rank 3 to rank 4 crosses no horizontal grid line. If its file doesn't change, and the destination cell shares both the file-pair and rank-pair of the origin, the move is illegal). Pawn captures, which always change file, are far more likely to cross a vertical grid line and remain legal.
  • Knights are particularly interesting: a knight's (2,1)-shape jump can sometimes land in the very same cell it started in (e.g., from a square near the centre of a cell, a short knight hop might stay inside the 2×2 block), making that specific jump illegal under Grid Chess even though no other rule forbids it. Conversely, knight jumps that already span a cell boundary are completely unaffected.
  • Long-range pieces (rooks, bishops, queens) are least affected in absolute terms — any move of length two or more along a rank, file, or diagonal is virtually guaranteed to cross at least one grid line. The only moves at risk are single-step rook/bishop/queen moves that happen to stay inside one cell, which mirrors the king's situation above.

A Worked Position

Consider the following simple position, with the standard grid lines (vertical: b/c, d/e, f/g; horizontal: 2/3, 4/5, 6/7) overlaid in your mind's eye as described above.

White: Ke1, Ra1, Qh4  |  Black: Ke8, Ra8 — under Grid Chess with grid lines b/c, d/e, f/g (files) and 2/3, 4/5, 6/7 (ranks)

Checking some candidate White moves:
  • Ke1–d1: e1 sits in the cell e1/e2/f1/f2; d1 sits in the cell c1/c2/d1/d2. These are different cells (separated by the d/e file line), so the move crosses a grid line — legal.
  • Ke1–f1: e1 and f1 are both inside the same cell (e1/e2/f1/f2). No grid line is crossed — illegal under Grid Chess, even though it's a normal king move.
  • Ra1–a4: a1 is in cell a1/a2/b1/b2; a4 is in cell a3/a4/b3/b4. The rank-2/3 line separates them — legal.
  • Qh4–h3: h4 is in cell g3/g4/h3/h4; h3 is in the very same cell. No grid line crossed — illegal, despite being a one-square queen move that would be unremarkable in orthodox chess.
  • Qh4–e1 (diagonal): spans both a vertical line (f/g) and several horizontal lines — clearly legal.

Why Composers Use Grid Chess

Grid Chess rewards a kind of spatial awareness that has no analogue in orthodox composition: a king "trapped" in the interior of a cell may have drastically fewer flight squares than its raw piece-count would suggest, letting composers construct mates with far less material than an orthodox equivalent would need. Conversely, the condition can also save a king that would otherwise be mated — if every checking move available to the attacker happens to stay within a cell, the check itself may be illegal. Solving a Grid Chess problem therefore requires solvers to mentally trace the grid lines for every candidate move, on every move of the solution, for both sides — a discipline that, once internalised, often reveals geometric patterns in the position that were invisible under the orthodox rules alone.

Grid Chess also combines naturally with fairy pieces from Chapter 2: a Nightrider or Grasshopper's long path is even more likely to cross multiple grid lines, so grid restrictions interact most sharply with short-range pieces (kings, pawns, and single-step moves) — precisely the pieces whose mobility orthodox chess takes most for granted.
