1. What Is Fairy Chess?

The term "fairy chess" covers any chess composition that deviates from the rules of orthodox chess. The name has nothing to do with fairies in a literal sense — it simply marks the genre as "not the real thing," in the same spirit as "fairy tale." Within that broad umbrella, fairy compositions fall into several overlapping categories:

  • Fairy pieces — pieces with non-standard movement rules added to, or replacing, the standard set. A diagram might feature a nightrider instead of a knight, or a board with grasshoppers alongside the orthodox army.
  • Fairy conditions — altered rules of play that apply to the entire game and to both sides equally (e.g., captured pieces are reborn, the side to move must make the longest possible move, pieces of the same type freeze each other).
  • Non-standard board geometry — cylindrical boards (the a- and h-files are identified, so a piece can "wrap around"), toroidal boards (both files and ranks wrap, like the classic video game Asteroids), or boards of unusual dimensions.
  • Combined stipulations — fairy pieces or conditions layered onto orthodox stipulations such as helpmates or selfmates, producing a doubly (or triply) exotic composition.

A Spectrum, Not a Binary

It helps to think of "fairy-ness" as a spectrum rather than a yes/no label. At one end sit orthodox twomovers and helpmates — standard pieces, standard rules, standard board. Moving along the spectrum:

  • A problem with one grasshopper added to an otherwise normal army is only mildly fairy.
  • A problem played entirely under Circe, with several fairy pieces, on a cylindrical board, sits at the far fairy end.
  • Some "almost-orthodox" fairy conditions (like Madrasi) change very little about the board but completely reshape strategy — demonstrating that "fairy-ness" is about depth of rule-change, not just piece count.

This spectrum matters for solvers: a problem described as "Fairy Chess" on a tourney bulletin might require only a small adjustment to your usual analysis, or it might require learning an entirely new vocabulary of piece movements and legality checks before a single move can even be considered.

How Fairy Problems Are Organised

Fairy problems are governed and curated by the FIDE Commission for Chess Compositions (specifically its "Fairies" section), which:

  • Organises the fairy sections of the World Chess Composing Tournament (WCCT), where national teams submit compositions in defined genres including fairies.
  • Publishes the Codex for Chess Composition, which includes formal definitions of fairy pieces and conditions so that composers and judges share a common, unambiguous vocabulary.
  • Curates the FIDE Album, a periodically published anthology of the best compositions worldwide, with dedicated sections for fairy problems judged separately from orthodox twomovers, moremovers, studies, etc.

Specialist fairy chess magazines — such as Die Schwalbe (Germany), StrateGems (USA), and The Problemist's "Fairies" column (British Chess Problem Society) — run regular fairy sections, often with their own awards and informal "theme tourneys" devoted to a single fairy piece or condition.

Notation for Fairy Pieces

Because fairy pieces have no standard algebraic symbol, composers use a set of widely recognised abbreviations in diagrams and solutions: N for Nightrider, G for Grasshopper, S for the German "Springer" (knight, used to free up "N" for nightrider), L or LEO for Leo, MAO for Mao, and so on. Diagrams typically include a legend explaining every non-standard symbol used, since there is no single universal convention across all software and magazines.

Reading a fairy stipulation: A typical fairy problem heading looks like:

Circe, h#2 — Grasshoppers g1, g8

This tells the solver: (1) the fairy condition in force is Circe (captured pieces are reborn); (2) the stipulation is a helpmate in 2; and (3) the diagram contains fairy pieces — grasshoppers on g1 and g8. All three layers (condition, stipulation, pieces) must be understood together before solving can begin.
