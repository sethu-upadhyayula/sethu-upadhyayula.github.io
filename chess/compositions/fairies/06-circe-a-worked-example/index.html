6. Circe: A Worked Example

Chapter 3 introduced Circe as a fairy condition: every captured piece is reborn on its game-array square, provided that square is vacant. This chapter works through a complete example, showing how the rebirth rule shapes both the candidate moves available to each side and the final mating mechanism.

Recap: The Circe Rule

Whenever a piece is captured (by either side), it is immediately placed back on its game-array square — the square that piece's type and colour would occupy in the standard chess starting position — if that square is currently empty. If the game-array square is occupied (by any piece, of either colour), the captured piece is removed from the board permanently, exactly as in orthodox chess, with no rebirth. A rook reborn under Circe reappears on a1, h1, a8, or h8 (whichever corresponds to its colour and the colour of the square it was captured on); bishops reappear on c1/f1/c8/f8 similarly by square-colour; knights on b1/g1/b8/g8; queens on d1/d8; kings on e1/e8 (though king captures do not normally arise, since the game ends at checkmate); and pawns reappear on their own file, second rank for White or seventh rank for Black.

Strategic Implications

Circe inverts a basic assumption of orthodox chess: that capturing reduces the opponent's material. Under Circe, a capture can:

  • Hand the opponent a fresh piece on an excellent square — if the rebirth square is empty and happens to be well-placed for the reborn piece (e.g., a rebirth that gives check, or that newly guards a key square), the capturing side may have effectively helped their opponent.
  • Be entirely "free" — if the rebirth square is occupied (by either side's piece), the captured piece simply vanishes as normal, and the capture behaves exactly as in orthodox chess.
  • Block a future rebirth — a piece sitting on its own game-array square is, in effect, "insurance": if a piece of that same type and colour is later captured elsewhere, it cannot be reborn (the square is occupied), so it is permanently removed.

For the attacking side in a mating problem, this means every capture used in the mating sequence must be checked: does the captured piece reappear, and if so, does its reappearance let the defending king escape, block the mate, or capture the mating piece?

A Worked Position: Mate in 2 under Circe

White to play and mate in 2 (#2 Circe). White: king, queen, rook on a1. Black: lone king on e8.

White: Ke1, Qh5, Ra1  |  Black: Ke8 — #2 Circe

Why the "obvious" try fails:
1. Qe5+? Kd8 (or Kf8) — the check is met by a simple king step, and White has made no progress; this is not a forced mate in 2.

The key try:
1. Rxa8? — there is no piece on a8 to capture, so this isn't available here; but it illustrates the kind of "useless under Circe" capture composers must rule out: if Black had, say, a rook on a8, capturing it with Ra1xa8 would cause the Black rook to reborn on a8 (its own game-array square, currently vacated by the capture) — restoring the exact same position! Such "self-cancelling" captures are a classic Circe trap for solvers who don't check rebirths.
1. Qe5 (no check — a quiet waiting move, restricting the Black king's squares: from e5 the queen covers e6, e7, e8(check square if king stays... actually covers the e-file and both long diagonals through e5)
1...Kd8 (Black's only square that isn't covered by the queen on e5 along the e-file/diagonals; Ke8 itself remains on the e-file under direct attack, so the king must step off it — Kd8 or Kf8)
2. Qe7# or, if 1...Kf8, 2. Qf6# (followed similarly) — in either case, the queen delivers mate from a square adjacent to the king, with the rook on a1 cutting off the back rank (a8–h8) so the king cannot slide away along rank 8, and no Black piece exists to interpose or capture the queen.

Why Circe matters here even with no captures in the actual line: the composer must verify, for every White move that gives check along the way (and for the final mate), that if that move had instead been a capture, the Circe rebirth would not hand Black an escape. Since Black has only a lone king in this miniature, there's nothing to capture — but in richer positions (see below), this check is essential on every candidate mating move.

A Richer Example: Rebirth Changes the Outcome

Now suppose Black additionally has a bishop on c8 (its own game-array square) and a knight on f6. White: Ke1, Qh5, Ra1 as before; Black: Ke8, Bc8, Nf6.

White: Ke1, Qh5, Ra1  |  Black: Ke8, Bc8, Nf6 — Circe

1. Qxf6? — capturing the knight. Under Circe, the Black knight on f6 is reborn on its game-array square g8 (or b8, depending on which knight it "is" — conventionally determined by the colour of its current square versus the colour of b8/g8; f6 is a light square and g8 is also light, so the knight reborn here goes to g8). A freshly-reborn knight on g8 now guards f6 and h6 — among other squares — potentially interfering with White's follow-up if the mating plan relied on the queen controlling f6 or a knight check from g8 covering an escape square White was counting on being clear. Worse, if White's intended mating move next was, say, a queen move to a square the new Black knight on g8 now attacks, the "mate" would not be a legal final position (White cannot leave themselves... actually relevant only for Black's own king safety) — but more directly, the reborn knight may simply be able to interpose or block a mating line on Black's next move, ruining the #2.

The Circe-aware solution instead avoids capturing the knight, and finds a mate that either captures a piece whose rebirth square is already occupied (so it doesn't return), or avoids captures on the mating move entirely — exactly the discipline introduced in Chapter 3's discussion of checking every condition against every move.

The General Method for Solving Circe Problems

Working through these examples suggests a checklist that applies to any Circe problem:

  • For every candidate move that is a capture, identify the captured piece's game-array square and check whether it is currently occupied.
  • If the rebirth square is empty, place the captured piece there (mentally) and re-evaluate the resulting position — does the reborn piece give check, block a mate, defend a key square, or capture a White piece?
  • If the rebirth square is occupied, the capture behaves exactly as in orthodox chess — no special handling needed.
  • Remember that rebirths apply to both sides and to every move of the solution, not just the final mating move — an early "throwaway" capture can quietly reshape the board for several moves afterward.

With this worked example as a model, Chapter 7 steps back from individual mechanics and considers the broader question of why composers and solvers invest so much effort in problems governed by rules that exist nowhere in actual chess games.
