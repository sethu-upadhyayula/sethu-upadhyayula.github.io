Fairy Chess

Fairy chess is composition with non-standard rules — different pieces, altered conditions, or modified board geometry. Far from being a whimsical digression, fairy problems are a rigorous art form with their own rich theory, demanding composers and solvers alike develop entirely new intuitions about force, mobility, and mating geometry.

The seven chapters below introduce the genre from first principles: what counts as "fairy" at all, the most important fairy pieces (nightriders, grasshoppers, amazons, and more), the global fairy conditions that reshape the rules of capture and movement (Circe, Madrasi, Anticirce), the geometric oddities of grid chess, two fully worked example problems, and finally why composers are drawn to this corner of the art.

1. What Is Fairy Chess?

Defining the genre: fairy pieces, fairy conditions, non-standard boards, and how fairy compositions are organised and judged.

2. Fairy Pieces

Nightriders, grasshoppers, Chinese pieces (Mao, Leo, Pao, Vao), amazons, archbishops, chancellors, and more.

3. Fairy Conditions

Circe, Anticirce, Madrasi, Andernach, Anti-Kings, Maximummer, and Take & Make — global rule changes that affect both sides.

4. Grid Chess

Dividing the board into 2×2 cells and requiring every move to cross a grid boundary — and what that does to mobility.

5. A Grasshopper Problem

A fully worked helpmate using grasshoppers, with hurdle-by-hurdle analysis of the solution.

6. Circe: A Worked Example

How rebirth on the game-array square changes the logic of capture, with a diagrammed position.

7. Why Compose Fairy Problems?

New themes, extreme economy, and the artistic case for fairy chess as a serious discipline.
