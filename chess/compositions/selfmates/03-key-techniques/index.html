3. Key Techniques

Composers building a selfmate draw on a recognisable toolkit of mechanisms. None of these is unique to selfmates in the abstract — pins, batteries, and forced captures appear throughout chess composition — but each one acquires a distinctive, "reversed" flavour when its purpose is to force Black to deliver the final blow. This chapter surveys the five techniques that recur most often.

Zugzwang

Zugzwang — being forced to move when every available move worsens your position — is the dominant technique in selfmate construction. White manoeuvres into a position where every legal Black move delivers checkmate on White. Achieving this is extraordinarily difficult for two reasons:

  • Black must have at least one legal move (otherwise it's stalemate, see Chapter 2's Inversion 4), and
  • every legal Black move — however many there are — must result in checkmate on the White king.

A single escape square for a Black piece, or a single Black move that doesn't happen to deliver mate, breaks the problem entirely. This is why selfmate composition is often described as "combinatorially unforgiving" — the composer must check every Black piece's every legal move in the final position, not just the ones that look thematically relevant.

Pin Exploitation

A pinned piece cannot move (or, for a pinned pawn, cannot move off the pin-line). White can exploit this in two opposite ways:

  • Using pins to narrow Black's options. If several Black pieces are pinned, the only pieces left that Black can move are the unpinned ones — and if those are arranged so that all their legal moves mate White, the position is solved.
  • Deliberately unpinning a piece. Sometimes White's key move removes a pin from a Black piece specifically so that piece gains a legal move — and that newly available move is the one that mates White. This is a striking idea: White's move frees a Black piece, apparently helping Black, purely so that the freed piece has no choice but to walk into delivering mate.

Controlled Captures

White places a piece en prise — on a square where a specific Black piece is either forced to capture it (because it's the only legal move, or because not capturing allows Black some escape) or strongly incentivised to. When that Black piece captures, it lands on the square from which it delivers checkmate on the White king.

This "forced capture equals forced checkmate" mechanism is one of the most elegant in the selfmate repertoire because it has a built-in narrative: the diagram shows White's piece standing on a square that looks vulnerable; Black, with no better option, takes it; and the taking is the undoing. Battery Play (Reversed)

A battery is two pieces aligned so that one (the "front" piece) blocks a check from the other (the "rear" piece); moving the front piece away unleashes the rear piece's check. In normal directmates, White builds batteries pointing at the Black king. In selfmates, White may construct a battery pointing toward its own king — but composed of Black pieces, or arranged so that a Black move triggers it.

More precisely: White's own moves can arrange for a Black piece sitting in front of another Black piece (relative to the White king) to be the one that must move — and when it moves out of the way, the rear Black piece's latent check on the White king becomes real, delivering mate. The "battery" here belongs to Black, but it is White's manoeuvring throughout the problem that assembles it and then triggers its discharge.

Stalemate Avoidance

As discussed in Chapter 2, White must ensure Black always has exactly the right number of moves at every stage: enough that Black is not stalemated, but no more than the set that all lead to mate. A common micro-technique for managing this is for White's key (or an intermediate move) to add a move to Black's repertoire — for instance, by:

  • Giving Black a new piece via promotion or by un-blocking a square so a Black pawn can advance;
  • Clearing a square that a Black piece was previously prevented from occupying, opening up exactly one new (mating) move; or
  • Withdrawing a White piece from a square it was guarding, so that a Black piece that was stuck (all moves illegal/covered) now has one legal move — which mates.

This "adding exactly one move, and making sure that move is the mating move" is a recurring fine-tuning step in the final stages of constructing a selfmate, and recognising it is often the key to solving the trickiest s#2 and s#3 problems.

Combining Techniques

In practice, most selfmates combine two or more of these techniques in a single line. A typical s#2 might use a controlled capture to determine which Black piece ends up near the White king, a pin to eliminate alternative Black replies, and a precisely calculated zugzwang to ensure the only remaining Black move is the mating one — all triggered by a single, often paradoxical-looking, White key move. Chapter 4 looks at the named thematic families that recur when these techniques are combined in characteristic ways, and Chapter 5 walks through a complete worked example showing several of these ideas working together.
