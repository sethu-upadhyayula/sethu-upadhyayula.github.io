1. Definition

A selfmate carries the stipulation s#n. White moves first and, in at most n moves, forces Black to deliver checkmate on the White king - regardless of how hard Black tries to avoid doing so. Black, for its part, plays optimally to avoid giving checkmate: if at any point Black has a legal move that does not result in Black mating White, and that move keeps the game going within the stated number of moves, then the "solution" fails and the problem is unsound.

This single sentence conceals an enormous amount of structure, and unpacking it carefully is the purpose of this chapter.

Reading the Stipulation

Compare the three families of stipulation that dominate the orthodox problem world:

  • #n (directmate) - White moves first and checkmates Black within n moves, with Black defending as resourcefully as possible. This is the "normal" goal of chess, compressed into a puzzle.
  • h#n (helpmate) - Black moves first, and Black and White cooperate to get Black mated within n move-pairs. Both sides are trying to reach the same goal.
  • s#n (selfmate) - White moves first and tries to force Black to deliver mate on White within n moves, while Black tries to prevent this from happening.

Selfmates therefore sit in an unusual position relative to the other two: like the directmate, the two sides have opposing goals (it is a genuine contest, not a cooperation); but like the helpmate, the side that is "mated" at the end is not the side whose turn it is to move when the mate is delivered - Black delivers the mate, on White's king, even though White is nominally "to move" at the start of the stipulated sequence.

The Reluctant Executioner

Key asymmetry: Black is not trying to win in the conventional sense - Black is trying to avoid mating White. Black would, if it could, simply decline to deliver checkmate forever. Every Black move that does not result in checkmate on White is, from Black's perspective, a successful defence. This makes Black a reluctant executioner: forced, ultimately, into carrying out an act it does not want to perform.

White's task is therefore to construct - move by move - a position in which Black has literally no choice. Every legal Black reply must either (a) immediately deliver checkmate on White, in problems with n = 1 from the diagram, or (b) keep the game moving toward a position where, after White's next move, condition (a) becomes unavoidable. If at any branch of the analysis Black finds a single legal move that escapes this fate - including, sometimes, a move that loses material, walks into an apparently terrible square, or even allows White what looks like a "free" attack - the problem is unsound, no matter how pretty the main line looks.

A Note on "Mate" in the Stipulation

It is worth being precise about whose king is mated. In a selfmate s#n:

  • The White king is the one that ends up checkmated.
  • The piece that delivers this final, fatal check is a Black piece.
  • White's moves throughout the sequence are designed to engineer this outcome - White is the active, planning side, even though White's own king is the victim.

This is the opposite of what a newcomer expects from a chess "puzzle," and it is precisely this inversion that gives the genre its character. We will spend the next chapter examining exactly how disorienting - and, once internalised, how satisfying - this inversion can be.

Selfmates in the Wider World of Composition

Selfmates were once dismissed by some as a curiosity - a "joke" stipulation that produced absurd-looking diagrams with little aesthetic merit. That view has been thoroughly overturned. Selfmates are now a fully developed and richly theorised genre with their own thematic traditions, named mechanisms, specialist composers, and canonical masterworks spanning well over a century.

Some markers of how seriously the genre is taken in the composition world:

  • The FIDE Album - the triennial anthology recognised as the definitive record of the best compositions worldwide - contains a dedicated selfmate section running to hundreds of problems per cycle.
  • The World Chess Composing Tournament (WCCT), a team event run by the Permanent Commission of the FIDE for Chess Compositions, includes selfmates as one of its standard sections alongside twomovers, moremovers, helpmates, and studies.
  • National and international tourneys regularly run selfmate-specific sections, and several specialist magazines and column editors focus heavily - sometimes exclusively - on the genre.
  • Selfmates of length s#2 through very long s#n (occasionally s#20 or more) all exist as established sub-genres, each with their own typical themes and difficulty profile.
