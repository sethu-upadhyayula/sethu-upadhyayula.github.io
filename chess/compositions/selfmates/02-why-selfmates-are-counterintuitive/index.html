2. Why Selfmates Are Counterintuitive

Every instinct trained by years of normal chess must be reversed when approaching a selfmate. This is not a minor adjustment — it is a wholesale inversion of what "a good move" means, and it is the single biggest reason beginners find the genre disorienting. This chapter walks through each inversion individually, because internalising them is the real prerequisite for enjoying — and eventually solving — selfmates.

Inversion 1: Attacking Your Own King Is the Goal

In a normal game, you spend enormous effort keeping your own king safe: castling early, avoiding weakening pawn moves near it, keeping defenders nearby. In a selfmate, White does the precise opposite. White actively places its own king in danger, strips away its defenders, and — most strikingly — manoeuvres enemy pieces into positions from which they attack the White king.

A solver's first reaction to a selfmate diagram is often: "Why is White's king so exposed? Surely Black just wins material or gives check and wins?" The answer is that this exposure is not an oversight — it is the entire point. White has spent the diagram position (and will spend its moves) carefully arranging for Black's pieces to have exactly the right lines of attack against the White king, while ensuring Black cannot simply play a different, non-mating move instead.

Inversion 2: Giving Away Material Is Often Necessary

Sacrificing material to gain an attack is a familiar idea in normal chess — but in a selfmate, the sacrifice is not in service of an attack on Black at all. White may give away a powerful piece, sometimes its strongest piece, purely to force Black to capture it. The point of the capture is not material gain (Black does not "want" the material — remember, Black has no interest in winning) but rather the arrival of the capturing piece on a particular square.

Once that Black piece sits on the square where it captured, it may be perfectly placed — often the only placement possible — to deliver checkmate on the White king on a subsequent move. The sacrifice is therefore best understood not as "giving something up" but as "placing a piece where Black is compelled to pick it up, because picking it up is the only legal — or the only non-losing-for-White-in-the-stipulated-sense — option."

Inversion 3: Restricting Black Is Paradoxical

In a normal game, restricting your opponent's pieces is straightforwardly good — fewer options for them means less counterplay. In a selfmate, restriction is a double-edged tool. White must:

  • Restrict Black's freedom enough that Black cannot wander off into some quiet, harmless move that postpones the mate indefinitely (within the move limit) — i.e., Black must not have an "escape" that avoids ever mating White; and
  • Simultaneously ensure that whatever moves Black does have left all lead, directly or via a forced continuation, to Black delivering checkmate on White.

These two requirements pull in opposite directions. Restrict too little, and Black has some quiet move that never leads to mate. Restrict too much — in particular, restrict Black down to literally zero legal moves while Black's king is not in check — and you've created a stalemate, which is Inversion 4.

Inversion 4: Stalemate Is the Enemy

This is perhaps the most subtle and important inversion of all. In normal endgame play, a player facing a hopeless position often hopes for stalemate — it's a lifeline, a draw snatched from a lost position. In a selfmate, stalemate is not a "result" the stipulation recognises as success for either side; if Black is stalemated (no legal moves, king not in check) before delivering the mate, the position does not satisfy "Black mates White," and the line fails. From White's perspective as composer, a line that ends in stalemate rather than the stipulated mate is exactly as bad as a line where Black simply escapes by playing some harmless waiting move — both represent a defence that "gets away."

So White faces a knife-edge balancing act on every move: restrict Black just enough that every available Black move leads to mate, but never so much that Black runs out of moves altogether while not in check. Composers sometimes describe this as "leaving Black exactly one square of room to swing the axe — no more, no less."

Putting the Inversions Together

Combine all four inversions and you get the characteristic "feel" of a selfmate position: White's king often looks alarmingly loose, White's pieces are frequently aimed at squares that seem to help Black, and the position can look — to an untrained eye — like White has simply blundered everything away in one move. Yet beneath this surface, every piece placement has been chosen with surgical precision so that Black's only legal moves, whatever they are, all funnel into the one prescribed mating move (or short sequence of moves).

This is why selfmates reward a specific kind of "backwards" calculation, which we will formalise in Chapter 6: rather than asking "what's my best move?", the solver must ask "what final position, with Black to move, has every Black reply ending in mate on White — and how do I force the game into that position?"

Why This Inversion Creates Fascination

Problems that look absurd on first inspection — a White queen seemingly hanging on an empty square, a White king standing in an open file with no pawns nearby — reveal, on closer examination, an elegant and completely deterministic logic. The "absurdity" is a surface feature; the underlying structure is as tight and forcing as any directmate, just running through an unfamiliar lens. Many solvers report that once the inversion "clicks" — once you stop asking "is this good for White?" and start asking "does this leave Black with only mating moves?" — selfmates become not just solvable but addictive, precisely because the same move can look terrible and be perfect at the same time.
