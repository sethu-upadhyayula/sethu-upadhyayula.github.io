3. Famous Composers and Their Styles

The moremover has attracted composers from very different backgrounds — puzzle columnists, mathematicians, and dedicated problemists — and each tradition left its own stylistic fingerprint on the genre. This chapter introduces several of the most influential names and the styles associated with them, as a way of mapping the range of what a moremover can be.

Sam Loyd (1841–1911)

The American puzzle master produced moremovers of startling ingenuity, often published in newspaper columns aimed at a general audience rather than specialist problemists. Loyd's problems frequently had paradoxical keys — pieces moving backwards, quiet retreats setting up devastating chains of forced play, or moves that appeared to throw away material for no visible reason.

His most famous creation, "Excelsior", demonstrates a pawn marching from e2 to e7 over the course of the solution, delivering mate on its final step by promoting — in a setting where every other piece on the board seems far more likely to deliver the checkmate. The problem's nickname comes from a comment Loyd attributed to a strong player who, on being shown the diagram, said the pawn "would be the last piece I'd use." The entire artistic point of the problem is that exact pawn, marching the entire length of the board while the solver's attention is drawn elsewhere.

Loyd's style is often described as showmanship: his problems are constructed to produce a single, vivid "aha" moment, frequently built around a visual or narrative joke (a piece doing the opposite of what its role suggests, or a piece travelling an absurdly long distance to do something a nearby piece "should" have been able to do instead).

Henry Dudeney (1857–1930)

The British puzzlist worked at the boundary between chess problems and mathematical recreations, producing moremovers (and problems in adjacent genres) with elegant numerical symmetry and precise combinatorial structure. Dudeney is perhaps better known today for his work in recreational mathematics — dissection puzzles, number problems — and his chess compositions share that mathematician's sensibility: a Dudeney moremover often has a clean, almost geometric logic to its solution, where the route a piece takes traces out a shape or a repeating pattern on the board.

Where Loyd's problems aim for a single dramatic surprise, Dudeney's often reward the solver with a sense of inevitability — once you see the underlying pattern, the whole solution seems to fall out of it almost mechanically. This is a different kind of pleasure: not "I never saw that coming," but "of course it had to be this way."

Jan Hartong and Comins Mansfield

These composers specialised in moremovers with rich thematic content — multiple related variations, each triggered by a different Black defence, that each display a unified idea in a slightly different guise. Rather than a single "big idea" executed once, a Hartong or Mansfield moremover often presents the same motif three or four times, each time dressed differently, so the solver experiences a kind of theme-and-variations structure within a single diagram.

Comins Mansfield (1896–1984), an English composer and one of the first problemists to be awarded the title of International Master of Chess Composition, was celebrated across all problem lengths — not just moremovers — for the economy and surprise of his keys. In his longer works, this same gift for an unexpected first move is combined with the strategic depth that longer problems allow: the surprising key is not the end of the story but the beginning of a plan whose full shape only becomes clear several moves later.

Werner Speckmann

The German composer Werner Speckmann (1913–1999) is particularly associated with miniature moremovers — positions with the fewest possible pieces (traditionally, no more than seven pieces in total, including both kings) that still display a rich thematic idea. Miniatures are considered especially elegant precisely because economy is, in a sense, guaranteed by the piece count itself: with so little material on the board, every piece's role is immediately visible, and there is nowhere for an unnecessary piece to hide.

Composing a miniature moremover is, if anything, harder than composing a problem with more material, because the composer has so few resources with which to create the necessary obstacles, defences, and mating mechanisms. A Speckmann miniature often achieves a long, forced sequence using only a handful of pieces, where each piece must serve multiple purposes across different points in the solution — for instance, a single Black pawn might serve first as a target for a White piece to attack, and later as the piece that blocks its own king's escape square.

A Stylistic Comparison

To make the contrast between these styles concrete, consider how each composer might approach the same raw material — say, a position where a White knight must travel from one corner of the board to deliver the final mate. A Loyd-style treatment would emphasise the surprise: the knight's first move would look like a blunder or a retreat, and the "punchline" would be the realisation, several moves later, of where it was actually headed. A Dudeney-style treatment might choose the knight's route so that it traces a recognisable pattern — a loop, a straight diagonal march of knight-jumps — rewarding the solver who spots the geometry. A Mansfield-style treatment would likely surround the knight's journey with two or three other variations, each showing a different defence met by a related (but not identical) use of the same piece. And a Speckmann-style treatment would strip the position down to the bare minimum of pieces needed to force the knight's journey at all, so that the knight's manoeuvre is the entire content of the problem, with nothing left to spare.

Why Styles Matter to the Solver

Recognising these stylistic traditions is not merely a historical curiosity — it can genuinely help when solving. If a position has the hallmarks of a Loyd-style problem (an unusually quiet, seemingly pointless-looking position with one piece that appears completely disconnected from the action), the solver should specifically suspect that disconnected piece of being the eventual hero. If a position looks stripped to the bone (Speckmann-style), every piece's role should be interrogated immediately, since none of them can be "spare." And if a position seems to invite several plausible-looking first moves, each addressing a different Black resource (Mansfield-style), the solver should expect a thematically unified set of variations rather than hunting for one single trick that solves everything.
