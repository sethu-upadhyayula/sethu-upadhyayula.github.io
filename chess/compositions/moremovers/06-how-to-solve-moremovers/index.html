6. How to Solve Moremovers

Solving a moremover calls for a different mindset than solving a twomover. A twomover can often be cracked by systematically trying candidate key moves and checking them against Black's replies; this "brute force" approach becomes impractical as the move count grows, because the number of possible move sequences grows enormously with each additional ply. This chapter sets out a practical, step-by-step method — building directly on the ideas from Chapters 2 and 4 — for approaching a moremover in a way that scales.

Step 1: Ask — What Is White's Plan?

Before calculating any specific line, survey the position and ask: what is White trying to accomplish? Where do White's pieces need to end up for a mate to be plausible? Is there a piece that looks oddly placed, disconnected from the action, or "wasted" — and if so, could the plan involve bringing that piece into play (as in Chapter 4's knight manoeuvre)? Is the Black king already restricted, suggesting the plan is about tightening an existing net rather than building one from scratch? Is there a pawn structure that looks like it's setting up a future self-block, suggesting a zugzwang-based plan (Chapter 2)?

This step is about generating a hypothesis, not proving anything. A reasonable hypothesis at this stage might be as simple as "I think the knight needs to get to f3, and then the queen mates" — exactly the kind of hypothesis that turned out to be correct in Chapter 4. The goal is to have something to test, rather than starting from a blank slate.

Step 2: Identify the Main Line

Once you have a hypothesis about White's plan, ask: what is Black's best resistance to it? This is usually the longest and most forcing line — the defence that comes closest to working, or that delays the mate the longest. In many moremovers, this "main line" is also the line the composer most wants you to find, because it's the one that demonstrates the full depth of the idea.

It can help to ask the question from Black's perspective: if White's plan is "bring the knight to f3, then mate with the queen," what would Black most want to do to stop this? If Black has no way to interfere with the knight's journey at all (as in Chapter 4, where Black's king was almost completely boxed in), then the "main line" might simply be Black's only available moves, played one after another — and the solving problem reduces to verifying that White's plan actually works against this (forced) sequence.

Step 3: Look for the Mating Picture

Rather than calculating forwards move by move from the start, it's often more efficient to work backwards from a plausible final mating position. Ask: given the pieces on the board, what would a mate of the Black king actually look like? Which White piece would deliver the check? Which other pieces would need to be covering the king's escape squares at that moment?

Once you have a candidate mating picture in mind, the question becomes: how many moves does it take to reach that picture from the starting position, and does that match the stipulation? If the answer is "yes, and the route is forced," you very likely have the solution. If the answer is "the natural route takes one fewer move than the stipulation says," proceed to Step 4.

Step 4: Notice the Count — Look for a Tempo Move

If the most natural plan seems to take n−1 moves but the stipulation says #n, don't assume you've made an error. It's common for moremovers to require a waiting move (or "tempo move") — a move that doesn't directly advance the mating plan, but instead "passes the move" while preserving the position's essential features, so that the mating sequence can begin on the "correct" move and land exactly on move n.

Tempo moves are often the key move of the problem — the move that looks most puzzling on a first pass, because it appears to do nothing. Common forms of tempo moves include:

  • A piece shuffling to an adjacent square and back over two moves, where only the first of those two moves is the "real" key, and the return is part of the forced continuation.
  • A king move that doesn't change anything about the position's evaluation but uses up a move.
  • A move by a piece that is not yet involved in the mating net, timed so that the piece arrives at its useful square exactly when needed — neither too early (which might allow Black an extra defensive resource) nor too late.

Recognising the need for a tempo move — by comparing your natural plan's length to the stated stipulation — is often the difference between finding the solution and being stuck on an "almost-right" idea that's exactly one move short (or long).

Step 5: Watch for Traps

Black's best defences in a well-constructed moremover are rarely passive. Common defensive resources that composers build in deliberately include:

  • Counter-checks — a Black move that gives check to the White king, forcing White to respond to that before continuing the plan. If White's plan doesn't account for a possible counter-check, the "solution" may simply fail to a move the solver overlooked.
  • Stalemate threats — especially in positions with reduced material (as in many of the Chapter 3 miniatures), it's easy for an overly hasty mating attempt to leave Black with no legal moves at all but not in check — a stalemate, which draws the game and fails the stipulation entirely. Always check, especially in the final moves, that the position you're aiming for is actually checkmate and not stalemate.
  • Unexpected escapes — a square that looked covered might not be, once you account for the exact position of every piece at that point in the line (not just the starting position). Squares that are covered at move 1 might become uncovered by move 4 if a covering piece has since moved.

The composer will have set these traps up deliberately, often specifically to catch solvers who found the "right idea" too quickly and didn't check it carefully enough against Black's best defence. A useful habit is: once you think you've found the solution, deliberately try to find a way for Black to escape at each move of your proposed line, not just the first one.

Putting the Method Together

Applied to the Chapter 4 example, the method runs as follows: Step 1 (the knight on a3 looks disconnected — hypothesis: it needs to travel somewhere useful); Step 2 (Black's king is so boxed in that its replies are essentially forced shuffles — the "main line" is just these forced replies); Step 3 (the mating picture is a queen check on g4 or similar, with the knight on f3 covering the escape squares — working backwards, the knight needs three moves to get from a3 to f3); Step 4 (three knight moves plus two queen moves equals five, matching the #5 stipulation exactly — no tempo move needed in this case); Step 5 (checking each Black reply confirms the king has no escape and the final position is mate, not stalemate).

This five-step method won't instantly solve every moremover — some positions are genuinely difficult, with multiple plausible plans to evaluate and subtle defences that take real calculation to refute. But it provides a structure for that calculation, turning "try to find the solution to a #8" from an undirected search into a sequence of focused questions, each of which narrows down the possibilities considerably. That structure — plan first, then verify — is, in the end, the same structure the composer used to build the problem in the first place, which is perhaps the deepest reason it works so well for the solver too.
