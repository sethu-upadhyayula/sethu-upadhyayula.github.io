5. Moremovers vs. Endgame Studies

Moremovers and endgame studies are close relatives — both are long-form composed problems that reward strategic, plan-based thinking, and both can feature material and positions that look like they could arise from a real game. Yet the two genres are judged by quite different standards, and a position that would be a fine moremover would often be considered a poor study, and vice versa. This chapter sets out the distinctions in detail and explains why each one exists.

Stipulation: Exact vs. Open-Ended

The most fundamental difference is the stipulation itself:

  • A moremover's stipulation is #n — mate in exactly n moves, where n is a specific number printed alongside the diagram.
  • An endgame study's stipulation is simply "White to play and win" (or, less commonly, "draw") — with no move count specified at all.

This single difference cascades into almost everything else. Because the moremover's move count is exact, mating in fewer moves than stated (against best defence) is a flaw, and the composer must construct the position so that the "real" defence takes exactly n moves to refute. Because the study's stipulation has no move count, winning in fewer moves than some hypothetical alternative is not merely acceptable — it's often exactly the point. A study might show "White wins in 6 moves with this method, but only draws with the more obvious-looking 4-move attempt" — the contrast between the fast wrong try and the (longer but correct) winning method is itself the theme.

A moremover's diagram position is judged primarily by what it allows the composer to say — the position can place pieces on unusual squares, in configurations that would be very unlikely to arise in a real game, if that's what's needed to set up the theme. A study, by contrast, is conventionally expected to be plausible: a position that a strong player might recognise as "this could be my position after move 35," with pawns on natural squares and pieces that haven't wandered to obviously artificial posts. This isn't a hard rule — some celebrated studies use quite unusual material balances — but "could this have arisen from a game?" is a question study judges ask in a way that moremover judges generally do not.

Criterion: Thematic Economy vs. Practical Surprise

A moremover is judged heavily on the criteria introduced for twomovers and threemovers — economy of force, unity of theme, the absence of duals and cooks — scaled up to account for the longer solution. A study is judged more on whether it teaches the solver something useful: a technique, a resource, or a saving idea that might apply in actual play. The most admired studies often hinge on a single surprising resource — a stalemate trick, an unexpected fortress, a way to give up material to reach a known drawn endgame — that a strong player could plausibly use at the board.

This difference in criteria explains why studies often feel more "practical" even when they are just as artificial in construction as any moremover: the idea being demonstrated is one that has value beyond the diagram, whereas a moremover's idea (a particular thematic combination, an aesthetic pattern) is valuable primarily within the world of composed problems.

Main Variations: Forced vs. Single-Line

Because a moremover must refute every Black defence within the stated move count, a rich moremover typically has several distinct variations — one for each meaningfully different Black try — each of which must independently lead to mate in the right number of moves. A study, by contrast, often only needs to demonstrate one winning (or drawing) line against best defence; subsidiary tries by Black may simply lose faster or be dismissed with a brief note, without requiring the same exhaustive treatment.

This is part of why moremovers can feel more "puzzle-like" even at long lengths: the solver is implicitly being asked "did you find a mate against every defence?", whereas a study solver is more often asked "did you find the winning idea?"

Black's Role: Adversary vs. Best Defence

In a moremover, Black's defences are an integral part of the composition — the composer has chosen Black's possible replies deliberately, and each one typically has a specific, prepared refutation. There should be no ambiguity about what Black's "best" defence even means, because every defence must be met.

In a study, Black is assumed to defend as well as possible, but "as well as possible" can sometimes be a matter of judgement or even controversy — especially in studies that were composed before exhaustive computer analysis was possible, where a "best defence" that was thought to lose might later be found to draw (or vice versa), without this necessarily being considered a "cook" in the same harsh sense that a cooked moremover would be. Studies have, historically, had somewhat more tolerance for this kind of ambiguity, partly because their open-ended stipulation makes "is there a faster win?" less central to soundness than "is the *only* defence refuted?" is for a moremover.

Move Count: A Source of Structure vs. A Non-Issue

As discussed in Chapter 4, the exact move count of a moremover is often a load-bearing part of its structure — the number of moves a manoeuvring piece needs to take, the number of tempo moves required to reach a zugzwang position, and so on, all interact with the stated stipulation. Change the stipulation by one move, and the position likely needs to change too.

A study has no such constraint. If a study's main line happens to take 7 moves, but an alternative defence allows White to win in 5, this isn't a problem — it might even be a virtue, since it can highlight that the "obvious" defence is actually worse for Black than a less obvious one. The absence of a move-count constraint gives study composers a kind of freedom that moremover composers don't have — but it also removes a tool: the precise rhythmic effects (echo mates separated by a fixed number of moves, manoeuvres timed to arrive exactly on schedule) that depend on an exact move count are simply unavailable to the study composer.

A Hybrid Case: The "Mate in Many" Study-Like Moremover

The boundary is not always sharp. Some very long moremovers (#15, #20, and beyond) are constructed around positions that look almost exactly like studies — plausible, game-like material, a single long winning plan — with the only difference being that the composer has additionally verified the exact move count and stated it as a stipulation. Conversely, some studies have such forcing, nearly-unique lines that they could almost be re-stated as a #n moremover with very little change.

What this shows is that the distinction between the genres is less about the raw material of the position and more about what the composer is claiming, and what the solver is being asked to verify. "I claim mate in exactly n, against any defence" is a different (and in some ways stronger) claim than "I claim White wins, against best defence" — even if, in a particular case, the underlying chess might be very similar.

Why the Distinction Matters Artistically

The strictness of the move count in moremovers is their defining constraint — and their greatest artistic strength. Because White must mate in exactly n moves, rhythmic patterns (pieces repeating motifs at precise intervals), echo mates (the same mating position reappearing in a different part of the board, on a different move), and precise timing (tempo moves arriving exactly when needed) become possible artistic goals that are simply unavailable to study composers, whose open stipulation removes the temporal "frame" within which such patterns could be measured. Conversely, the study's freedom from an exact move count is what allows it to feel like a genuine demonstration of practical technique, rather than a constructed puzzle — a freedom the moremover, by its nature, cannot fully share.
