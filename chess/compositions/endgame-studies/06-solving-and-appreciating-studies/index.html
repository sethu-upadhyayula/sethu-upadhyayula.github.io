6. Solving and Appreciating Studies

Solving studies requires different mental habits from solving problems. A directmate solver scans for forcing sequences within a tight move budget; a study solver must think more like an endgame technician - assessing plans, weighing pawn structures, and being alert to the single geometric or tactical idea that overturns the "obvious" assessment. This chapter lays out a practical method, then discusses what separates a merely sound study from a truly great one.

A Method for Solving

  1. Ask: what is the most natural move? Then ask: why doesn't it work? Studies are designed so the natural move fails, and the surprise lies in why. In the Réti study (Chapter 4), the natural move is 1.Kh7 (chase the pawn); identifying why it fails - one tempo too slow, and abandons the c-pawn entirely - is most of the work of finding 1.Kg7.
  2. Consider all checks, captures, and promotions first. These are forcing moves. The study's key move often appears only after these forcing lines are exhausted and shown to fail or fall short. In many studies, several promising-looking captures must be calculated and rejected before the quiet, "non-forcing" key move even enters consideration.
  3. Look for geometric ideas. King triangulation, diagonal threats toward two objectives (the Réti idea), zugzwang setups, opposition - the solution often hinges on a geometric or positional relationship between pieces and pawns rather than a tactical shot. Ask: is there a square that is equidistant (in king-moves) from two different objectives? Is there a tempo move available to one side but not the other (as with Troitzky's pawn, Chapter 5)?
  4. Think about stalemate. Drawing studies often exploit stalemate or perpetual check as the saving resource. Winning studies often must navigate around stalemate traps - a move that looks like it makes progress might actually stalemate the opponent's king, turning a win into a draw. The K+2N vs K+P theory of Chapter 5 is, at its core, entirely a story about stalemate and how to avoid (or, for the defender, how to reach) it.
  5. Appreciate the try lines. The best studies include "tries" - lines that appear to win (or draw) but fail - just like the best problems. Understanding why the try fails is as instructive as finding the main line, and is often where the deepest theoretical content of the study actually lives.
  6. Verify the final position is actually theoretically drawn or won. A study's solution often ends not in an immediate mate or visible material gain, but in a reduction to some other known endgame (a textbook K+P vs K, a fortress, a known drawn rook ending). Part of solving the study is recognising that the final position belongs to a class you already know the verdict for - and if you don't recognise it, that's a signal to go learn that piece of theory.

Worked Example: Applying the Method

Consider the following position, designed to exercise several of the above steps at once. White appears to be down material in a way that should be hopeless, yet the stipulation is "White to play and draw."

White: Kg1, Ph2. Black: Ka8, Pb7, Ra2. White to play and draw.

Step 1 (natural move): The "natural" move might be to push the h-pawn, h2-h4-h5... hoping to create a passed pawn and queen first. But Black's rook on a2 and extra pawn on b7 give Black overwhelming material superiority (a full rook), and a simple pawn race loses easily - Black's rook can both shepherd Black's own pawns and stop White's h-pawn from a distance. So pushing the h-pawn fails; we need something else.

Step 2 (checks/captures/promotions): White has no checks or captures available at all - the position is quiet. This tells us the key idea is not tactical in the usual sense; it must be positional or geometric.

Step 3 (geometric ideas) and Step 4 (stalemate): With White having only a king and a single pawn against a king, pawn, and rook, the only realistic drawing resource against such material is stalemate of White's own king, engineered so that Black cannot make progress without releasing it, or a fortress where White's king and pawn occupy a self-sufficient defensive structure that Black's extra rook cannot break down (rook endings have many such fortresses, particularly when the defending king is confined to a corner with a pawn that cannot be dislodged). The White king on g1 with a pawn on h2 is already suggestive of exactly this kind of corner fortress - g1/h1/h2 is one of the most common stalemate nests in all of chess.

Step 5 (the actual idea): White's plan is to do nothing useful at all - shuffle the king between g1 and h1 (or similar waiting moves) - while Black, despite the overwhelming material advantage, can never make progress without eventually being forced to either (a) allow ...Rxh2 stalemate (if the rook captures the last pawn while the White king is boxed into h1/g1 with no other moves, White is stalemated and it's a draw) or (b) walk into some other repetition. The "surprise" here is the same one that appears in countless real grandmaster games: a vast material advantage can be completely neutralised by a well-placed stalemate fortress, and the defending side's only "moves" are a kind of permanent waiting game.

This worked example is illustrative of the solving process rather than a specific famous composition - but the underlying idea (corner stalemate fortress against overwhelming material) is one of the most common "draw" resources in the entire study literature, and recurs constantly in practical rook endings.

What Separates a Good Study from a Great One

Just as Chapter 6 of the Twomovers section discusses aesthetic criteria for directmates, studies have their own hierarchy of quality:

  • Soundness is the bare minimum - the stated result must actually hold against best defence. An unsound study (a "cooked" study, in the same sense as a cooked problem, or one with a refutation the composer missed) is simply broken, however beautiful the intended idea.
  • A single, surprising main idea, ideally one that cannot be found by "just calculating" - it requires recognising a pattern, an analogy to a known ending, or a geometric relationship.
  • Economy, as with problems - the fewer pieces needed to express the idea, the more striking the demonstration. The Réti study's economy (one king and one pawn per side) is a large part of why it remains the most cited study in history.
  • Rich try play - alternatives that look equally or more plausible than the actual solution, each failing for an instructive reason, so that the solver who explores them comes away having learned several things, not just one.
  • A connection to general theory - the very best studies don't just solve themselves, they illuminate an entire class of positions, as Troitzky's studies did for K+2N vs K+P (Chapter 5). A study that generalises is doing double duty as both art and theorem.

Why Studies Reward Repeated Study

Studies are deeply instructive for practical play in a way that pure problems are not. The endgame positions that arise - lone kings chasing pawns, rooks versus bishops in open positions, knight coordination, fortress structures - recur in real games. A player who has absorbed fifty great studies has broadened their pattern library in a way that hours of opening preparation cannot match.

Moreover, because a study's "answer" is a single bit of information (win or draw), but the path to that answer is rich with technique, studies reward re-solving. A player who solved the Réti study years ago, encountering it again, will often find themselves appreciating new details - why a particular move order matters, why a seemingly equivalent try actually isn't equivalent - that they missed the first time. This is the mark of genuinely deep composition: it has more to teach than any single solving session can extract, which is precisely why the same handful of classic studies (Réti's chief among them) keep reappearing in books, courses, and articles a full century after they were composed.
