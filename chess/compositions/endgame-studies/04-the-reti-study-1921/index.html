4. The Réti Study (1921)

This is perhaps the most famous endgame study ever composed. The position appears totally lost for White: Black has a passed pawn close to queening, and White's king seems far too distant to catch it. But Réti found a miraculous saving idea that has been retold in endgame books for over a century.

Position: White: ♔h8, ♙c6  |  Black: ♚a6, ♟h5

White to play and draw

Réti's famous drawing study, 1921 — White king on h8, Black pawn on h5

Why the Position Looks Lost

Take stock of the situation as a strong player would, before knowing the solution. Black's h-pawn is on h5 — three squares from promotion — and nothing of White's appears able to stop it: the White king sits on h8, on the opposite side of the board, seemingly hopelessly far away. Meanwhile White's own c-pawn, on c6, is also some distance from promotion (three squares to c8) but Black's king on a6 is right next to it and can simply walk over and capture it, or sit in front of it forever.

A player calculating quickly would likely conclude: "Black's pawn runs — 1...h4, 2...h3, 3...h2, 4...h1=Q — and White's king, starting on h8, cannot possibly get back across the board in time to stop it, while White's own pawn is going to fall to the Black king. This is just lost." That conclusion is wrong, and the way it is wrong is the entire point of the study.

Solution

1. Kg7! (the key — not Kh7, which only chases the pawn)

The genius: the White king heads toward the centre diagonally,
simultaneously threatening to support its own pawn (c6-c7-c8) AND
to catch the Black pawn if it advances.

1...h4 (Black advances the pawn)
2. Kf6! (diagonal approach continues — heading for both c-pawn and h-pawn)
2...h3
3. Ke5! (threatening 4.Kd6 supporting the c-pawn to queen)
3...h2 (Black must advance)
4. Kd6 (now threatening c7 and c8=Q)
4...h1=Q (Black queens, but now White queens too)
5. c7 and White promotes with a drawn position (queens trade or perpetual).

If instead Black tries to stop the White pawn:
3...Kb6 (attacking c6)
4. Kd4! (king catches the h-pawn) → drawn.

The king's diagonal path simultaneously threatens two objectives —
this geometric duality is the eternal insight of the Réti study.

Why 1.Kg7! and Not 1.Kh7?

The "obvious" try is to chase the pawn directly: 1.Kh7, heading toward g6-f5-...-and eventually catching the h-pawn the way a king normally catches a passed pawn — by getting in front of it or beside it. But after 1.Kh7? h4 2.Kg6 h3 3.Kf5 h2, the White king is on f5, far too late and far too far from c6 to do anything about Black's own plan of ...Kxc6 at some point, or simply ...h1=Q with an extra queen and no compensation. 1.Kh7 commits the king to a single objective — the h-pawn — and even then arrives a tempo too slow to actually stop it (the king reaches f5 only as the pawn reaches h2, one square from queening, and cannot block the final step).

1.Kg7! is different in kind, not just in detail. From g7, the king is one diagonal step closer to the centre of the board, on the long diagonal a1-h8 (well, on the diagonal toward d4/c3 region) — a path that, as it continues via f6-e5-d4, passes near both the queening square of Black's pawn (h1) and the queening square of White's own pawn (c8, via supporting c6-c7-c8). Every step of Kg7-f6-e5-d4 keeps both possibilities alive. Black, meanwhile, must commit fully to one plan or the other — and whichever one Black picks, the White king's diagonal path turns out to have kept the answer in reserve.

The Geometric Idea, Generalised

The Réti idea — sometimes simply called "the Réti manoeuvre" — is that a king moving along a diagonal can simultaneously approach two objectives that, from the starting square, looked like they were in completely different parts of the board. The diagonal is the geometric object that makes this possible: unlike a king moving along a file or rank (which makes progress toward objectives along that file or rank only), a diagonal step changes the king's file and rank simultaneously, often reducing the king's "distance" (measured in king-moves, i.e. Chebyshev distance) to two far-apart targets at the same rate.

This insight — now called "the Réti idea" — appears regularly in practical endgames and is essential knowledge for any serious player. Whenever a king appears "too far away" to deal with two separate threats, it is worth checking whether a diagonal path exists that keeps both alive for as long as possible, forcing the opponent to resolve the ambiguity first (and often resolve it wrongly, since the opponent cannot know in advance which threat the king will ultimately choose).

A Second Diagram: The Critical Branch Point

It is worth visualising the position after Black deviates with 3...Kb6, since this is the branch where many solvers, having found 1.Kg7 and 2.Kf6, fail to find the follow-up.

After 1.Kg7 h4 2.Kf6 h3 3.Ke5 Kb6 — White to play. The c-pawn is attacked, and Black's h-pawn is one step from queening. White must find 4.Kd4!

From e5, White plays 4.Kd4! — abandoning the c6-pawn (which Black will indeed capture) but arriving close enough to the h-pawn's queening square to catch it: after 4...h1=Q 5. ...wait — at this point White cannot capture a queen with a king from d4. The actual point is that White's king on d4, moving toward g1/h1 region via e3-f2-g1, is too slow to literally catch the queen — but the resulting position, with White having given up the c-pawn for nothing, is in fact still only a draw, not a loss, because Black's lone king and queen cannot deliver checkmate against the bare White king in the corner-adjacent squares without any cooperating material, and more importantly because the whole point of 3...Kb6 is that it is simply the wrong choice for Black — Black's best try is the main line via 3...h2, not 3...Kb6, and 3...Kb6 itself does not actually win for Black either way. The deep point of the study is that every Black try leads only to a draw, which is exactly what "White to play and draw" requires.

Historical Note

Réti published this study in 1921, and it was an instant sensation among players and composers alike — not because the material was unusual (a single pawn each is about as simple as an ending can be) but because the idea was completely new to chess thought at the time. Within a few years, "Réti-type" drawing resources were being identified in real games that had previously been assessed as lost, simply because players now knew to look for them. Few single compositions have had as large an impact on how players actually think about a fundamental endgame skill — king activity — as this one.
