5. Troitzky's K+2N vs K+P

It is well-known that K+2N cannot force checkmate against a lone king - the defending king can always reach a corner and the attacking side cannot force the final position without stalemating. This is one of the foundational "drawn" results of basic endgame theory, taught alongside K+B vs K and K+N vs K as a position with insufficient material to mate.

However, Troitzky proved that if Black has any extra pawn at all, the situation can change dramatically: the pawn's advance can be used to avoid the stalemate that would otherwise save Black. In other words, the extra Black pawn is - counter to every normal intuition about material - a liability, not an asset. This is one of the most startling results in all of elementary endgame theory, and it was Troitzky's painstaking, decades-long analysis that established exactly when it holds.

Why K+2N vs K Alone Is a Draw

Start with the baseline case: King and two Knights versus a lone King. White has more material (two minor pieces vs. none) but cannot force mate. The reason is purely about stalemate geometry: to deliver mate, White must drive the Black king into a corner (a1, a8, h1, or h8) and deliver a knight check. But constructing such a mating net with two knights requires the attacking side to approach the corner in a sequence of moves during which the defending king has only one legal square - and at the critical moment, the defending king finds itself with no legal moves and not in check, i.e., stalemated, before White can deliver the actual mating check. Every attempt to avoid this ends up either repeating the stalemate problem in a mirrored form or allowing the king to slip back out of the corner.

This is in sharp contrast to K+R vs K, K+Q vs K, or even K+B+N vs K (which is winning, though with a famously difficult technique), where mating nets without this stalemate obstruction exist.

King and two Knights vs. lone King - a textbook draw. Black's king, driven to h8, is stalemated by any attempted mating attempt; there is no way to deliver check at the critical moment.

The Pawn Changes Everything

Now add a single Black pawn somewhere on the board. At first this seems only to make White's task harder - White must now also worry about Black's pawn promoting, and giving Black extra material can hardly help White force a mate that wasn't forceable before. But the resolution is the opposite: the pawn gives Black an extra legal move at the critical stalemate position. Where previously the lone king had no moves and the position was stalemate (a draw), now the pawn itself may have a legal move (or, after the pawn has advanced enough, the king may have an additional flight square that the pawn's presence opens up, or - in the cases that matter most - the pawn's forced advance, which Black must sometimes make to avoid immediate loss of the pawn, walks Black's own position into a configuration where mate becomes possible).

The mechanism is delicate and was the subject of Troitzky's life's work: depending on which file the pawn is on and how far advanced it is, the extra tempo provided by the pawn's movement is sometimes enough to break the stalemate barrier described above, and sometimes not. When it is enough, K+2N is a forced win for White - an extraordinary reversal in which Black's "extra" pawn is the very thing that dooms Black.

The Troitzky Line

Troitzky spent decades analysing every pawn-on-every-file scenario and constructed the "Troitzky Line" - for each file, the furthest rank to which the Black pawn can have advanced for White to still win with best play. If the pawn is behind the line (closer to its starting rank), White wins; if the pawn has advanced past the line, the position reverts to a draw, because the pawn promotes (or threatens to promote) too quickly for White's slow knight manoeuvres to construct the mating net first.

This was an enormous computational and compositional achievement for the pre-computer era - Troitzky worked out these boundaries entirely by hand analysis, file by file, verifying both the winning lines for pawns behind the line and the drawing/escaping lines for pawns that had advanced too far. Modern endgame tablebases (computer-generated exhaustive databases covering every position with a given material configuration) have confirmed Troitzky's results were, with only minor refinements at the most extreme edge cases, essentially correct - a remarkable testament to the depth of pre-computer endgame analysis.

An Illustrative Troitzky Position

The diagram below shows a representative position of the type Troitzky analysed: Black's pawn is on the queenside, still close enough to its starting square to be "behind the line," and White's task is to use the two knights and king to force the pawn's eventual loss or a mate, without allowing Black to either promote the pawn or escape into the drawn K+2N vs K configuration via stalemate.

An illustrative Troitzky-type position: White Ke5, Nf4, Nd4; Black Ka8, Pa6. The a6-pawn is behind the Troitzky line for the a-file, so this position is a theoretical win for White despite the "insufficient material" of bare K+2N.

The winning method (in outline, following Troitzky's general approach) is a slow squeeze: White's king and knights coordinate to restrict the Black king to the a8/b8 corner region while keeping the a6-pawn under control (it cannot safely advance to a5 without losing a vital tempo, and cannot be defended indefinitely). Eventually White reaches a position where Black is in a near-zugzwang: the only "spare" move available to Black is a pawn move (e.g. ...a5), and that pawn move is precisely the tempo White needs to deliver the final mating net in the corner - the very net that would have been stalemate without the pawn's availability as a waiting move. The full technique requires dozens of precise moves and is one of the most demanding "won" endings to execute correctly in all of chess, which is part of why it remains a celebrated curiosity rather than a commonly seen practical ending.

Practical Relevance

How often does K+2N vs K+P actually arise in real games? Quite rarely - but when it does, the practical implications of the Troitzky Line are real: a player with K+2N against a lone king, offered the chance to either (a) capture Black's last pawn, reaching the textbook drawn K+2N vs K, or (b) leave the pawn alone and try to win the K+2N vs K+P version, must know the Troitzky Line to make the correct choice. Capturing the pawn might convert a winning position into a drawn one! This is a striking example of how a piece of pure composition-driven theory (Troitzky's studies were composed specifically to demonstrate these results) directly informs a concrete decision a player might face at the board.

More broadly, the Troitzky Line is often cited as the paradigmatic example of how endgame study composition and rigorous endgame theory are, at the highest level, simply the same activity - a composed study that demonstrates a forced win or draw is a piece of theory, expressed in the form of a single illustrative position rather than a general theorem.
