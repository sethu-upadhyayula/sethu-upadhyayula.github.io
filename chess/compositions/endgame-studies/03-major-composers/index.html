3. Major Composers

The endgame study has been shaped by a relatively small number of towering figures, each associated with a distinct style or theoretical contribution. This chapter surveys the most important of them, with one illustrative diagram drawn from the spirit of their work.

Henri Rinck (1870-1952)

The most prolific study composer in history, with over 1,700 studies published. Rinck was a Spanish-based French chemist who composed prolifically throughout the early 20th century. His specialty was rook and bishop endgames, and he systematically catalogued winning and drawing techniques that remain standard reference material. His style was direct and instructive rather than spectacular - many of his studies read like beautifully selected exercises from an endgame manual, each isolating one technical idea (a particular rook cut-off, a bishop's control of a long diagonal, a king's race to a key square) with total clarity.

Rinck's sheer output means that for almost any rook-and-minor-piece configuration, there is likely a Rinck study illustrating the critical technique. His studies were widely used by Soviet trainers precisely because of this systematic, encyclopaedic quality.

Alexei Troitzky (1866-1942)

The Russian master produced studies of extraordinary depth, including his famous analysis of the K+2N vs K+P endgame - a theoretically drawn endgame that is winning in practice when the pawn has not advanced too far. Troitzky's "Troitzky Line" (the set of pawn positions where White wins with two knights) remains one of the most celebrated contributions to both composition and theory, and is the subject of Chapter 5.

Beyond the two-knights endings, Troitzky composed extensively in many other endings, often choosing material balances (such as two knights, or a knight and bishop against a pawn or two) that other composers avoided because the underlying theory was simply unknown - Troitzky's studies were frequently the first rigorous demonstrations of results that later became textbook knowledge.

Richard Réti (1889-1929)

The Czech hypermodern grandmaster composed one of the most famous studies in history (the subject of Chapter 4), demonstrating a profound geometric insight about king movement that permanently changed how players understand king activity in the endgame. Réti's few studies are models of economy - typically just a handful of pieces, with the entire universe of the position contained in those sparse forces. This minimalism mirrors his approach to the middlegame as a player: Réti was famous for openings (the Réti Opening, the hypermodern fianchetto systems) that controlled the centre from a distance rather than occupying it directly, and his studies show the same "indirect control" aesthetic applied to king-and-pawn endings.

Josef Kling and Bernhard Horwitz (19th century)

The earliest serious study composers, whose 1851 collection Chess Studies established the endgame study as an independent art form, separate from both the "problem" tradition (which by then already had its own conventions) and from mere game analysis. Many of their compositions are still cited in endgame theory books, particularly their K+R vs K+B and K+Q vs K+R studies, which laid out fortress and drawing techniques for the defending side that remain the standard reference today.

What Kling and Horwitz demonstrated, in effect, was that the endgame itself - not just the middlegame combination - could be a subject for composed art. Before their work, "interesting" positions were drawn almost entirely from games; afterward, composers began deliberately constructing endgame positions to showcase ideas that might never arise naturally, or that arise so rarely in practice that a composed illustration was the only practical way to teach them.

Mark Dvoretsky (1947-2016)

The legendary trainer used endgame studies as training tools, and his books - most famously the Endgame Manual - introduced countless players to the study tradition. Dvoretsky argued, and demonstrated through the results of the players he coached (including several future World Championship contenders), that mastering studies is essential for anyone who wants to develop a truly deep understanding of chess endings. Dvoretsky was not primarily a composer himself, but his role as a curator and teacher of the existing study literature - selecting the studies with the highest training value and explaining precisely what each one teaches - had as much influence on how 20th and 21st century players relate to studies as any individual composer's output.

An Illustrative Rinck-Style Study

To give a flavour of the "technical" study tradition associated with Rinck, consider the following rook-versus-bishop fragment. White's rook and king must coordinate to round up Black's bishop, which is momentarily stranded on the edge of the board.

An illustrative Rinck-style fragment: White Ke4, Ra1; Black Kh8, Bh1, Pg7. White to play and win.

The bishop on h1 is badly placed - it has only the long diagonal, and the rook on a1 already cuts across that diagonal's far end. White's plan is to bring the king toward h1 while the rook prevents the bishop from escaping along the diagonal: 1.Kf3 (heading toward g2/h2 to attack the bishop directly), and if Black tries 1...Bxa1?? this would be answered by ideas around promoting the g- or h-pawns with king support - but more simply, the bishop is simply trapped: any bishop move along the h1-a8 diagonal can be met by the rook shifting along the first/eighth rank to maintain the pin-like restriction, while the king closes in. Eventually Kg2 traps the bishop entirely, winning it for nothing. This kind of "geometric trap" of a long-range piece by a slower one is a recurring Rinck theme.

Each of these composers represents a different facet of what a study can be: Rinck the systematic technician, Troitzky the theoretician, Réti the minimalist poet, Kling and Horwitz the founders, and Dvoretsky the great curator and teacher.
