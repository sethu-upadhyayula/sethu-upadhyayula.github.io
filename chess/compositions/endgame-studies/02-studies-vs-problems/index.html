2. Studies vs. Problems

Endgame studies and chess problems (directmates, helpmates, selfmates, fairies, retros) are both forms of chess composition — positions invented by a composer rather than arising from a game — but they belong to genuinely different artistic traditions, with different goals, different vocabularies of appreciation, and even a different relationship to "real" chess. This chapter lays out the key distinctions.

FeatureEndgame StudyChess Problem
StipulationWin or draw (no move count)Mate in exactly n moves (or helpmate/selfmate in n, etc.)
AppearanceNatural, game-like positionsOften artificial arrangements
Key criterionSurprise, instruction, beautyTheme, economy, key quality
VariationsOne main line required; sidelines optionalEvery defence must be covered
Move countIrrelevant — result is what mattersExact count is mandatory
Piece countTypically 4-10 piecesVaries widely; miniatures possible
Relation to practiceDirectly instructive for real playPrimarily an abstract artistic form

Naturalness of Position

Perhaps the single most visible difference is how the diagrams look. A directmate problem will routinely feature unusual concentrations of pieces — three or four minor pieces clustered around a king, pawns on the second rank that have clearly never moved, kings in corners — because the composer's only goal is to set up a particular mating mechanism, and naturalness of the resulting "game" is not a consideration. A study, by contrast, is judged in part by whether it looks like it could have arisen from a real game: a handful of pawns on plausible squares, kings that have plausibly walked to where they are, perhaps one or two pieces left on each side. Many of the greatest studies could, with a little imagination, be mistaken for adjourned positions from real tournament games — indeed, several historically famous "studies" began life as analyses of unfinished games before being polished into independent compositions.

What Must Be Proven

In a directmate, every Black defence must be refuted within the stipulated number of moves — there can be no exceptions, no "Black could try this but it's clearly hopeless so we won't analyse it." Soundness is total and exact.

A study's soundness requirement is, in one sense, just as total: the result (win or draw) must hold against every defence, with no exceptions — a "won" study with even one drawing defence for Black is unsound, exactly as a "mate in 3" with an undefeated Black try is unsound. But in another sense a study has more room to breathe, because there is no move-count bookkeeping. A defence that delays the result by ten extra moves is just as "refuted" as one that is met immediately, provided the eventual result is forced. This is why study solutions often read like a normal grandmaster's thinking — establishing small positional gains one at a time — rather than the tightly compressed, every-tempo-counts calculation of a moremover.

The Dual Nature: Art and Instruction

Many grandmasters study endgame studies specifically for practical benefit. The patterns they reveal — king geometry, piece coordination, pawn breakthrough timing, the boundary between fortress and zugzwang — recur in real games. This dual nature (art and instruction) is unique to the study form. A solver who has worked through Réti's 1921 study (Chapter 4) has not just admired a clever composition; they have internalised a geometric principle about king movement that will help them find resources in drawn-out king-and-pawn endings for the rest of their playing career.

Chess problems, by contrast, are almost never described as "useful" in this direct sense. A player does not become better at finding mates in real games by solving twomovers — the mating configurations in problems are typically far too artificial to recur in practice. Problem composers and solvers value the form for its own sake, as a branch of combinatorial art, much as a crossword setter values wordplay for its own sake rather than for any practical linguistic skill it imparts.

Tries, Not Just Refutations

Both forms make heavy use of tries — plausible-looking moves that fail. But the character of a study's tries is different. In a directmate, a try usually fails to a single specific defence that the composer has carefully arranged. In a study, a try often fails for a deeper structural reason: it leads to a theoretically known draw when a win was required, or it allows a stalemate that a slightly different move-order avoids, or it simply leads to a position one tempo too slow. Understanding why a try fails in a study often means understanding a piece of endgame theory in its own right — which is part of why solving studies builds practical strength in a way that solving problems does not.

As a study: White to play and win. The question is purely "does White's connected pawn majority and active king secure the full point?" — answered the way any grandmaster would analyse a real ending.

As a study, the position above invites ordinary endgame thinking: White's king is far more active, the queenside pawns are connected and mobile, and Black's lone h-pawn and passive king cannot generate enough counterplay. The "solution" is simply correct technique — advance the queenside pawns, bring the king back if needed, and convert the extra structural trumps into a full point. There is no trick; the appeal (if any) lies in seeing the technique executed cleanly. Such a position would rarely be published as a standalone study precisely because it lacks surprise — but it is exactly the kind of position a study composer might use as a "base" before adding the twist that makes it special (for instance, by making one of White's queenside pawns weaker, so that what looks like the same easy technique actually requires an unexpected detour).

A problem composer looking at the same material would approach it completely differently: rather than asking "what is the right plan," they would ask "what striking mating idea can I build using exactly this army?" — likely abandoning the "natural" placement of the pawns entirely in favour of whatever configuration best sets up the desired pattern. The material is the same; the entire creative process, and the criteria by which the result is judged, are not.
