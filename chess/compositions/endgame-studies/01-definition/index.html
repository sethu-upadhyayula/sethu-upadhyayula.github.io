1. Definition

An endgame study is a composed chess position — typically with few pieces — carrying one of two stipulations:

  • White to play and win (+) — White must achieve a theoretically won position against Black's best resistance.
  • White to play and draw (=) — White, seemingly in a losing or desperate position, finds the only path to a draw.

There is no specified move count. White wins (or draws) "by force," meaning the result holds against every Black defence, however many moves it takes. This distinguishes studies from moremovers: the freedom from a fixed move count allows studies to feel like genuine chess — with decisions made on principle and intuition rather than precise counting. A solver working through a study is not ticking off a clock; they are establishing a theoretical truth about the position, in the same way an endgame-theory book establishes that, say, K+R vs K is always a win or that K+B vs K is always a draw.

The Two Stipulations in Practice

The "win" stipulation (+) asks White to convert what is often a tiny, fragile advantage into a forced victory. The defending side — Black — is assumed to defend with maximum resourcefulness; the study is only "sound" if no defence, however clever, allows Black to escape with a draw or better. This is a strong requirement: it is not enough for White's main line to work against a normal opponent, it must work against the best possible opponent.

The "draw" stipulation (=) is, if anything, the more celebrated form in the study tradition, because it usually begins from a position that looks lost. White (or sometimes Black, in studies with reversed colours) must locate the single resource — a stalemate trick, a fortress, a perpetual check, a theoretically drawn reduced material balance — that turns apparent defeat into a guaranteed half-point. The "draw" stipulation rewards positions where material counting gives entirely the wrong answer, which is part of why such studies feel almost magical.

No Move Count: Why It Matters

In a moremover problem (see the Moremovers chapter), the stipulation "Mate in 5" fixes both the result and the number of moves in which it must occur — every line must terminate in exactly five moves, no more, no less. A study has no such constraint. The position is simply won or drawn, and White may take however many moves are necessary (within the 50-move rule of practical play, though composed studies are usually evaluated against the deeper "theoretical" result rather than the 50-move rule).

This freedom has a profound effect on how a study reads. Because there's no artificial deadline, the moves feel like the moves a strong player would actually choose — improving the position, restricting the opponent, marching the king toward where it's needed — rather than a forced march toward an externally imposed finish line. A study can include long manoeuvring phases, just like a real endgame, provided the eventual result is forced.

The Aesthetic Criterion: Surprise

The aesthetic criterion for studies is surprise: the winning or drawing mechanism should be unexpected, beautiful, and non-obvious. A study whose solution is simply "play the best moves and the advantage converts naturally" has no artistic merit — it is just an endgame, not a study. The key move (or the key idea, since studies often unfold over many moves rather than hinging on a single key) must astonish.

Surprise in a study typically comes from one of a small number of sources:

  • A move that looks like it loses immediately but is in fact the only move that saves the game (sacrificing material, walking the king into apparent danger, allowing a passed pawn to run).
  • A geometric idea — a piece's path or reach turns out to cover far more of the board than intuition suggests (the diagonal king march of the Réti study, discussed in Chapter 4, is the canonical example).
  • A reduction to a known theoretical ending that the position did not appear to be heading toward at all — material is traded off in a forced sequence, and what emerges is a textbook draw or win that nobody would have guessed from the starting diagram.
  • A stalemate or fortress resource that appears only at the very end, redeeming a position that looked completely lost for dozens of moves.

A Minimal Example

To make the abstract description concrete, consider a tiny illustrative position with just kings and a single pawn each — about as few pieces as a study can have while still containing a real idea.

A minimal illustrative position: White King e6, Pawn e4; Black King g8, Pawn h7. White to play and win.

With the White king already far advanced and the Black king cut off on the back rank, White's plan is simple in outline — march the king to support the e-pawn's promotion, since Black's h-pawn and king are too slow to create meaningful counterplay. 1.Kd6 followed by Kc5(-b6-...)-supporting e4-e5-e6-e7-e8 with the king escorting the pawn home, while Black's lone king and h-pawn cannot generate a passed pawn of their own in time. The "surprise," such as it is in this minimal example, is simply how comfortably the win is achieved despite material being nominally level — the position is really about king activity, not material, which is the seed of every more elaborate study.

Real studies, of course, go far beyond this — they combine multiple ideas, hide tries that almost refute the solution, and often reduce a seemingly hopeless position to a known theoretical draw or win through a sequence that no reasonable player would find without having seen it before.
