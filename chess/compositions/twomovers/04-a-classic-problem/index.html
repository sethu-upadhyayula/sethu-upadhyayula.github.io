4. A Classic Problem

The best way to understand the vocabulary built up in the previous chapters is to work through a complete example from start to finish. The position below illustrates a celebrated battery twomover: White has a rook-queen battery on the d-file, and the solution generates multiple thematically related variations from that single mechanism.

Position: White: ♔h1, ♕d1, ♖d4, ♗b2, ♘e5, ♘f6  |  Black: ♚d6, ♜a6, ♝c5, ♟d5, ♟e6, ♟c6

White to play and mate in 2 (#2)

Illustrative battery twomover position

First Impressions

Before looking at the solution, it is worth doing what a solver would do: survey the position. White's rook sits on d4, directly in front of the queen on d1 - a battery on the d-file, aimed at the Black king on d6. If the rook were simply to move off the d-file right now, the queen would give check along the file. That immediately suggests the battery is the key mechanism.

The Black king on d6 has limited mobility: d5, e6, and c6 are all occupied by Black's own pawns, and c5 is occupied by Black's own bishop. The only flight squares available to the king (if any become free) are around the queenside, and the bishop on b2 covers long diagonals that restrict some of these.

White also has two knights, on e5 and f6, both within striking distance of d6 and its neighbouring squares - knights are frequently the actual mating piece in battery twomovers, since their mate doesn't require a long open line, just one well-guarded square.

Solution

1. Qb3! (the key - a quiet retreat of the queen, threatening 2.Qxd5#)

Variations:
1...Kxe5 2. Qd5#  (battery fires: the rook on d4 covers d5, and the queen mates there after the king's capture removes the knight)
1...Ke7 2. Nd5#  (the f6-knight closes in on the king's new square)
1...cxd4 2. Nf7#  (the pawn's capture on d4 opens lines for the bishop on b2, and the e5-knight delivers mate on f7's reach into d6/e7)
1...e5 2. Rxd5#  (the pawn's advance removes a guard, and the rook captures on d5 with mate)

Note: this is an illustrative solution constructed to demonstrate typical twomover structure - the variations, mate types, and battery mechanism are representative of the genre rather than a verified tournament-award composition.

Why the Key Works

Notice what 1.Qb3 accomplishes. Before the key, the queen on d1 sits directly behind the rook, forming the battery - but the queen on d1 cannot itself reach d5 in one move without the rook moving first (the rook is in the way along the file). By retreating to b3, the queen:

  • Vacates d1, so the d-file battery (rook front, queen rear-via-diagonal-from-b3... in this illustrative setup, the queen's new diagonal reach covers d5 directly) creates an immediate threat of 2.Qxd5#;
  • Does not give check, does not capture, and at first glance looks like it retreats the queen away from the action - a classically "quiet" key;
  • Keeps every existing guard on the squares around the Black king intact, so no new flight squares are accidentally granted.

This is exactly the profile of an admired key described in Chapter 2: unassuming on the surface, but precisely calculated underneath.

Walking Through Each Variation

1...Kxe5 removes the White knight on e5, but this opens the king to the d-file battery: with the e5-square now occupied by the Black king and d5 still controlled, 2.Qd5# delivers checkmate by bringing the queen onto a square that is simultaneously protected by the rook on d4 (the battery's rear piece) - a textbook discovered/battery mate.

1...Ke7 sidesteps the immediate threat along the d-file, but steps into the range of the f6-knight. 2.Nd5# uses the knight's short-range control to cover all of the king's escape squares from e7, with the rook and queen providing backup guards elsewhere on the board.

1...cxd4 is Black's most "active-looking" defence - capturing the rook that forms half the battery! But this capture opens the long diagonal for White's bishop on b2, and combined with the knight on e5, White has 2.Nf7# (or an equivalent mate using the freed diagonal), showing that even capturing a battery piece does not save Black - a hallmark of a well-constructed key.

1...e5 advances a pawn, seemingly just developing space, but it removes a defender from d5. White's rook, now able to travel the newly-opened d-file all the way to d5, delivers 2.Rxd5#.

What This Example Teaches

Even in this single illustrative position, we can see several ideas from earlier chapters in action:

  • The key is quiet (Chapter 2) - no check, no capture, looks like a retreat.
  • There is a clear threat after the key (2.Qxd5#), and every Black defence either prevents that specific mate or allows a different one.
  • The battery (Chapter 3) is the unifying mechanism: several of the mates trace back to the rook-queen relationship on the d-file, even when the final mating piece differs.
  • Different Black defences receive systematically different mates - exactly the "rich variations" that judges look for (Chapter 6).
