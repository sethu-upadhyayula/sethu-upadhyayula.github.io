2. Key Concepts

The Key Move

The key is White's first and only "free" move - everything in a twomover hinges on finding it. Once the key is played, White must already know (in principle) the mating reply to every legal Black move, with no exceptions. Solvers therefore search for the key by working backwards: imagine candidate first moves, and for each one ask whether a complete set of mating replies exists.

A good key is typically surprising and non-obvious. Composers deliberately avoid keys that capture material, give check, or immediately restrict the Black king's mobility, because such moves are the first things a solver tries - they are "obvious" and therefore aesthetically weak, even when they happen to work. The most admired keys:

  • Are quiet moves (no capture, no check)
  • Appear to do little, or even seem to help Black, on the surface
  • May grant the Black king a flight square it did not have before
  • May involve a piece sacrifice - offering a piece for capture
  • Often self-block a square (the White king or another White piece moves onto a square Black might otherwise have used) or open a battery by moving a piece off a line

The phrase "the key is the soul of the problem" is a cliché among composers precisely because so much of a twomover's artistic value is concentrated in this single move.

The Threat

After most keys, if Black could somehow "pass" - play a null move - White would have an immediate follow-up that delivers mate. This hypothetical follow-up is called the threat. A twomover with a threat is called a threat problem, and it is by far the most common type.

Black's actual defences exist precisely to parry the threat: each legal Black move must either prevent the threatened mate from working, or else allow some other mate (because the defending move itself created a new weakness). Either way, White has an answer prepared. The set of "threat plus all the ways Black avoids it, each met by its own mate" is the full solution.

Some twomovers have no threat at all - after the key, the position is in zugzwang: it is now Black's turn, and literally every legal Black move creates some new weakness that White can immediately exploit, even though White had no single "automatic" follow-up to a null move. These are called pure zugzwang (or "waiter") problems, and they are prized because the key appears to do absolutely nothing except pass the obligation to move to Black - and that alone is fatal for Black.

Variations

A variation is one specific Black defence paired with the White move that mates it. If, after the key, Black plays 1...Bg5 and White answers 2.Nf6#, the pair "1...Bg5 2.Nf6#" is one variation. A rich twomover typically has somewhere between four and a dozen variations.

What makes variations interesting is not merely that they exist, but how they relate to one another. Strong twomovers exhibit:

  • Thematic unity - the mates share a common mechanism (e.g., they are all delivered by the same battery, firing in different directions)
  • Correspondence - Black defences that look similar (e.g., the rook moving to different squares on the same file) provoke systematically related White mates
  • Surprise mates - at least one variation ends with a mate that uses a piece or square in an unexpected way

Tries

A try is a candidate first move for White that looks exactly like it should work - it sets up a plausible threat and seems to handle most Black defences - but fails to exactly one Black response, called the refutation. Composers deliberately construct tries to mislead solvers and to enrich the artistic content of the problem; a try is denoted with a question mark, e.g. 1.Qd5?, and its refutation with an exclamation mark, e.g. 1...Nf4!.

A problem with one or more well-constructed tries, each refuted by a single precise Black move, alongside the genuine key (marked with "!"), is considered especially fine. The relationship between the tries and the key often forms its own theme - for instance, several tries might all fail because they vacate a square the king needs, while the key avoids this by choosing a different square entirely.

Example try-and-key pair:
1.Qd5? (try - threatens 2.Qd6#, looks decisive)
  1...Nf4! (refutation - now 2.Qd6 is met by 2...Nxd6, no mate)
1.Qg4! (the actual key - works against every Black defence, including 1...Nf4)

Set Play, Changed Play, and Mutates

Before White even makes a move, it is sometimes possible to ask: if it were (hypothetically) Black's turn to move right now, and Black played each of their legal moves, would White already have a mate ready for each one? This hypothetical pre-key analysis is called set play.

If set play already covers every Black move, the position is called a complete block. In a complete block, the key cannot be "needed" to create new mates against moves that were already covered - so what does the key actually do? There are two possibilities:

  • Block problem: the key preserves all the existing set mates unchanged, while perhaps adding a mate to a move Black didn't have before (e.g., the key grants a new flight square, and the key move itself now provides the mate for that flight)
  • Mutate: the key changes one or more of the set mates - the same Black defence that was met by mate A before the key is now met by a different mate B after the key. This "changed play" is widely regarded as one of the most sophisticated and satisfying effects in twomover composition, because it shows the key doing real work even in a position that already looked "solved."

More advanced still are reciprocal change and cyclic change problems, where pairs (or cycles) of mates swap roles between the set play, one or more tries, and the actual play after the key - but these belong to the deeper end of the genre and are mentioned here only so the terms are recognisable.
