5. How to Solve Twomovers

Solving a #2 is a different skill from playing or even analysing a normal game. The position is static, the move count is fixed, and the solver's job is essentially to prove a small theorem: "this move, followed by a mate for every reply." The following six-step method, used (in some form) by virtually every experienced solver, turns this into a manageable process.

1. Survey the Position

Before considering any candidate moves, build a mental picture:

  • What material does White have, and where does each piece stand relative to the Black king?
  • Where is the Black king, and which squares around it are currently flight squares (empty and not attacked by White)?
  • Which White pieces are already guarding squares near the Black king, even without moving?
  • Are there any White pieces that look "out of place" — far from the king, seemingly doing nothing? In a well-constructed problem, every piece has a purpose, so an oddly-placed piece is often a major clue to the key.

2. Check for Set Play

Ask the question from Chapter 2: if it were hypothetically Black's move right now (before White plays anything), does White already have a mate ready for each Black reply? Working out the set play — even if incomplete — tells you a great deal:

  • If set play covers everything, you are likely looking at a block problem or mutate, and the key will be a move that doesn't disturb (or that deliberately changes) the existing mates.
  • If set play covers almost everything except one or two Black moves, the key probably needs to add coverage for exactly those moves — a strong narrowing of the search.
  • If set play covers very little, you are likely looking at a threat problem, where the key's main job is to create one new big threat that most Black moves must respond to.

3. Look for Batteries

Scan for any pair of White pieces aligned on a rank, file, or diagonal pointing toward (or near) the Black king. If you find one, ask: what happens if the front piece moves off that line? Does the rear piece then give check or threaten mate? Battery-based keys are extremely common (Chapter 3), so this check often finds the key — or at least the threat — very quickly.

4. Consider Quiet Keys

If checks, captures, and battery-openings don't immediately solve the problem, broaden the search to quiet moves — especially ones that:

  • Move a piece to a square that looks completely passive
  • Retreat a piece that seemed to be doing important work (this is often a self-block or a way of opening a different line, as in the Indian and Bristol themes)
  • Place a White piece on a square where Black could capture it (anticipate a sacrifice — see the Novotny theme)
  • Grant the Black king a flight square it didn't have before (a generous-looking move that is secretly covered by a new mate)

Composers know solvers check "loud" moves first, so the key is disproportionately likely to be one of these quiet candidates — especially in problems that have received composition awards, where an obvious key would be considered a flaw.

5. Verify Universality

This is the step that separates a careful solver from a hasty one, and it cannot be skipped. Once you have a candidate key:

  1. List every legal Black move in the resulting position — not just the "natural-looking" ones. Include pawn moves, rook shuffles along open files, and any moves of pieces that seem far from the action.
  2. For each one, find a White mating reply.
  3. If you find even one Black move with no mating reply, your candidate key is refuted (it was a try, not the key) — return to step 4.

In a position with fifteen or more legal Black replies, this verification is the bulk of the solving effort — often taking far longer than finding the candidate key itself. Experienced solvers develop habits for this: working systematically square-by-square around the king, then checking distant pieces, then checking pawn moves last (since pawns often have the fewest options but are easiest to overlook).

6. Spot the Tries — and Learn From Their Failure

If a plausible candidate fails verification, don't discard it immediately — understand precisely why it failed. The single refuting move often reveals exactly what property the true key must have. For example:

Try: 1.Rd5+? — gives check immediately, looks forcing.
Refutation: 1...Kxd5! — the king simply captures the rook, since nothing recaptures.

Lesson: any candidate key that hands over a piece for free, with nothing covering the capturing square, cannot be the key. The true key must either avoid offering this piece, or must ensure the capturing square is covered before the sacrifice — which often points toward a battery or a piece that needs to move first.

This kind of "failure analysis" of a try is not wasted effort — composers deliberately design tries to teach the solver something about the position that makes the real key easier to find afterward.

A Worked Mini-Example

Consider a position where White has ♔g1, ♖a1, ♘e4, against ♚e8 with a pawn on f7 and nothing else nearby. Applying the method:

  • Survey: the Black king on e8 has flight squares on d7, d8, f8 (e7 and f7 are blocked by its own pawn structure in this sketch). White's rook on a1 is far away on the queenside — possibly a clue.
  • Set play: with the knight on e4, does White already threaten anything on the king's flights? The knight covers d6 and f6 — not directly useful yet.
  • Batteries: none visible — rook and king are not aligned with anything threatening.
  • Quiet keys: what if the rook on a1 swings to a8, pinning nothing but cutting off d8 and f8 along the back rank? Now the king's only flight is d7 — and the knight on e4 already covers d7 (no wait — check the actual square): if the knight covers the one remaining flight, then 1.Ra8 followed by 2.Nf6# (covering escape) or similar becomes the key.
  • Verify: check every other Black move (pawn pushes, etc.) — does the rook on a8 or knight on e4 still provide mate after, say, 1...f6 or 1...f5? Each must be checked individually.

This sketch deliberately leaves some details unresolved — the point is the process: notice the oddly-placed rook, hypothesize a quiet long-distance move that restricts the king, and then grind through verification. With practice, steps 1–4 become almost instantaneous for experienced solvers, leaving step 5 (verification) as the main remaining work — exactly as it should be, since verification is what guarantees correctness.
