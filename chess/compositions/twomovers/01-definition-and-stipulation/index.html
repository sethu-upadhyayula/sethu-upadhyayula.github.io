1. Definition and Stipulation

A twomover is a chess composition carrying the stipulation #2 (read "mate in two"). The position is set up so that, with White to move, White can force checkmate within two of White's own moves - that is, White moves once, Black replies, and White's second move must deliver checkmate, regardless of what Black plays.

This sounds almost trivial when stated abstractly, but the constraint is absolute. White's first move - called the key - must lead to a position in which White has a mating response prepared for every single legal move Black might make. If Black has, say, fourteen legal replies to the key, White needs fourteen (not necessarily distinct) mating continuations, one for each. If even one Black defence escapes mate, the problem fails its stipulation and is considered unsound.

Reading the Stipulation

Composition stipulations follow a compact notation:

  • #2 - mate in 2 moves, White to play and force mate (the standard "direct mate" twomover)
  • #3, #4, ... - mate in 3, 4, etc. (multimovers, a separate genre)
  • =2 - stalemate in 2 (a different goal entirely)
  • h#2 - helpmate in 2, where Black cooperates to be mated (the opposite spirit)

This chapter - and the rest of this topic - concerns only the #2 direct mate, the most popular and widely composed stipulation in the history of chess problems.

White's Two Moves

It is worth being precise about what "two moves" means. The sequence of play is:

  1. Move 1 (White): the key move
  2. Move 1 (Black): any legal defence
  3. Move 2 (White): checkmate

So "mate in two" really describes two White moves, with one Black move sandwiched between them. There is no move 3 - the problem is solved entirely within this three-ply sequence (White, Black, White).

Black Does Not Cooperate

This is the single most important rule to internalise, and it is what separates the twomover from many other composition genres. Black plays to avoid being mated. If Black has a move that escapes all of White's prepared mates - even a move that looks pointless, like shuffling a rook back and forth on an open file - and White has not accounted for it, the problem is broken.

This is fundamentally different from a helpmate, where Black actively assists White in reaching a mating position, or a selfmate, where White forces Black (who does not want to) to deliver mate. In the direct-mate twomover, the two sides have diametrically opposed goals, exactly as in a normal game - except compressed into the smallest possible space.

"Cooks" and Soundness

A problem is sound if it has exactly the solution(s) intended by the composer and no others. An unintended additional solution is called a cook. A single extraneous pawn, an extra defender, or a misplaced piece on an otherwise irrelevant square can introduce a cook - a second key that also solves the problem, undermining its uniqueness - or worse, can mean that the intended key fails to one defence (making the problem unsound rather than merely cooked).

Because of this fragility, composers test candidate positions exhaustively - historically by hand, today almost always with computer solving programs - before publishing. Even so, cooks are occasionally discovered in problems decades after publication, sometimes overturning long-standing judgments about a composition's quality.

Why So Much Rides on So Little

A twomover typically uses far fewer pieces than a full chess game - often just 6 to 12 pieces total. With so few moves and so few pieces, every piece must justify its presence on the board. A piece that does nothing - that could be removed without affecting the solution - is considered a flaw (a violation of the economy principle, discussed in Chapter 6). At the same time, every Black defensive resource, however obscure, must be accounted for.

This tension - maximum effect from minimum material, against a maximally resourceful opponent, within the smallest possible move count - is the entire creative challenge of the twomover. The chapters that follow build up the vocabulary and techniques needed to appreciate (and eventually solve) these miniature masterpieces.
