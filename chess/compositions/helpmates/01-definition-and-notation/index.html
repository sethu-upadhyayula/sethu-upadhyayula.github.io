1. Definition and Notation

A helpmate is a chess problem in which both sides cooperate toward a single shared goal: White must deliver checkmate on a specified move. Unlike a normal game — where the two players have opposing aims — in a helpmate Black actively assists White in constructing the mating position. Neither side is "trying" to win in the conventional sense; instead, the position is treated as a puzzle to be solved jointly.

The h#n Stipulation

A helpmate carries the stipulation h#n, where n is the number of moves White needs to deliver checkmate, counting from White's perspective. The crucial and often surprising rule is this:

Black moves first. This is not a misprint — it is the defining convention of the genre.

After Black's first move, the two sides alternate normally: Black, White, Black, White, ... until White's n-th move delivers checkmate. So in an h#n problem there are exactly 2n half-moves in total, and the final half-move (White's n-th) is the checkmating move.

Reading the Notation

  • h#1 — Helpmate in 1: Black plays one move, then White immediately delivers checkmate. This sounds trivial but is often surprisingly tricky, because Black's single move must simultaneously open a mating line for White and avoid accidentally giving White an even faster, unintended mate (which would "cook" the problem).
  • h#2 — Helpmate in 2: Black plays, White plays, Black plays, White plays and mates. Four half-moves total. This is by far the most popular length for helpmates, since it is long enough to contain real thematic content but short enough to be solved by hand in a reasonable time.
  • h#3 — Helpmate in 3: Black, White, Black, White, Black, White mates. Six half-moves. Often used for problems with more elaborate manoeuvring or where two pieces need to be redeployed before the mating net closes.
  • h#4, h#5, ... — Longer helpmates exist but are comparatively rare; the combinatorial search space grows quickly, and composers generally prefer to express an idea in the fewest moves possible (the principle of economy).
  • h#½ — Occasionally seen in older or informal sources to describe a position where White can mate "for free" without any preparatory Black move, but this is non-standard and rarely used in serious composition.

Why Cooperative Play Changes Everything

In an ordinary game, or in a direct-mate problem (where White is "to move and mate in n" against best defence), the composer's job is to construct a position where White's attack overwhelms Black's best resistance. In a helpmate, there is no resistance at all — Black's moves are chosen purely to help White. This single change in the rules of engagement opens up an entirely different aesthetic space:

  • Positions can look quiet, even harmonious, because there is no need to depict an "attack" — the position is a snapshot just before a cooperative dance toward mate.
  • Mating configurations that would be unrealistic in a real game (because no defender would allow them) become the natural subject matter. Ideal mates and elegant geometric patterns are far more achievable.
  • The solver's job shifts from calculating threats and defences to pure combinatorial deduction: among all the legal move sequences, which single sequence allows both sides, cooperating fully, to reach a checkmate exactly on White's n-th move?

What "Cooperate" Does Not Mean

Cooperation does not mean either side can make illegal moves, leave their own king in check illegally, or otherwise violate the rules of chess. Every move in the solution must be a fully legal chess move under the standard rules. The only thing that changes is the motivation: instead of each side seeking its own advantage, both sides seek the single shared outcome of checkmate on White's last move. A helpmate solution is therefore best thought of as a kind of "joint plan" — a unique sequence of legal moves that both sides execute, with neither side deviating, because deviating would prevent the mate (and thus fail to solve the puzzle).

Soundness: Uniqueness of Solution

A well-composed helpmate has exactly one solution (or, for twins, exactly one solution per twin — see Chapter 2). If a second, unintended sequence of moves also achieves checkmate on the required move, the problem is said to be cooked, and is considered defective regardless of how elegant the intended solution is. Composers spend enormous effort checking for "tries" — plausible-looking alternative first moves that almost work but fail for a subtle reason — both because tries add to the problem's interest (when they fail instructively) and because an undetected try that actually succeeds would cook the problem.
