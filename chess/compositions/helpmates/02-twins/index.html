2. Twins

A defining feature of helpmate composition - far more prevalent here than in any other genre - is the twin. A twin problem presents the same (or only slightly modified) starting position together with two or more stipulations, conventionally labelled (a), (b), (c), and so on. Each labelled part is solved independently, as if it were its own diagram, but the solver only needs to be given the original diagram plus a short instruction describing how each twin differs from it.

A Typical Twin Specification

h#2   (a) diagram   (b) ♙b4→c3   (c) ♘f6→e4

This means: solve the helpmate-in-2 with the diagram position exactly as given for part (a); then, for part (b), move the bishop currently on b4 to c3 (everything else unchanged) and solve again; then, for part (c), move the knight from f6 to e4 (relative to the original diagram, not to (b)) and solve a third time. Each of (a), (b), and (c) typically has a completely different solution - different pieces move, different mating squares are used, different themes appear.

Common Twinning Devices

  • Piece shift (e.g. ♘f6→e4): the most common twinning device. A single piece is relocated to a new square, and the resulting position is solved as a fresh problem.
  • Colour exchange: in twin (b), the colours of one or more pieces are swapped (a white piece becomes black, or vice versa) without moving it.
  • Stipulation change: twin (b) might ask for h#2 while twin (a) asked for h#3, using the same diagram for both.
  • Rotation or reflection: rarely, the whole position is rotated 180° or reflected, producing a geometrically related but distinct problem.
  • Addition or removal of a piece: twin (b) might add a pawn on a specified square, or remove a piece present in the diagram.
  • Successive twinning: twin (c) can be defined relative to twin (b) rather than to the original diagram - always stated explicitly, e.g. "(c) further → ...".

A Small Illustrative Twin

Consider the simple position below, used as the base diagram for a twin pair. Twin (a) is the diagram as given; twin (b) shifts the black bishop from c8 to a6.

Twin (a) - diagram position

Twin (b) - ♙c8→a6. Same diagram, bishop relocated, completely different solution path is now required.

Moving the bishop from c8 to a6 changes which squares are guarded and which lines are open, which in turn changes which mating configuration is reachable on White's second move. The solver must treat (b) as an entirely fresh problem: nothing about the solution to (a) can be assumed to carry over.

Why Twins Matter Aesthetically

Twins are so central to helpmate aesthetics that a helpmate without twins (sometimes called a unate or simply a "single") is comparatively unusual at the higher levels of composition, though plenty of fine single-solution helpmates exist, especially among shorter and more accessible problems. The artistic point of a strong twin set is thematic unity: the different solutions should not merely be unrelated puzzles sharing a diagram, but should illuminate the same underlying idea from multiple angles.

Examples of thematic unity across twins include:

  • Same mating piece, different routes: in each twin, the same White piece delivers the final mate, but it travels by a different path and the supporting cast of pieces plays different roles.
  • Same interference, different square: a recurring tactical motif (e.g., a black piece interposing to block a check) recurs in each twin but on a different square or with a different piece performing the interference.
  • Geometric correspondence: the mating positions in (a) and (b) are related by a symmetry of the board - reflected across a diagonal, or rotated - producing an "echo" effect (explored further in Chapter 6).

How Twins Affect Solving Strategy

When solving a twin helpmate, it is a mistake to assume that the solution to (a) gives you a head start on (b). In well-constructed twins, the changed feature of the position (the relocated piece, the swapped colour, etc.) is precisely the thing that breaks the (a) solution and opens up a different one. The practical approach is:

  1. Solve (a) completely first, using the backward-analysis method described in Chapter 4.
  2. Apply the stated change to get the diagram for (b).
  3. Ask: does the (a) solution still work? (Almost always, no - check explicitly why not.)
  4. Restart the backward analysis for (b) from scratch, paying special attention to how the changed feature interacts with possible mating squares.

With the concept of twins established, Chapter 3 introduces a related but distinct device - the duplex - in which a single diagram is solved twice with the roles of the two colours reversed.
