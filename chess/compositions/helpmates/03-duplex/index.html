3. Duplex

A duplex helpmate carries the stipulation h#n duplex (sometimes written h#n* or with the abbreviation "Dup"). It asks the solver to find two solutions from the same diagram:

  • Solution 1 - the problem as normally stated: Black moves first and cooperates, White delivers checkmate on move n.
  • Solution 2 - the colours are reversed: White moves first and cooperates, and Black delivers checkmate on move n.

Both solutions must be valid, legal, and (in a sound problem) each must be unique within its colour assignment. A duplex is therefore really two helpmate problems compressed into a single diagram and a single short stipulation word.

Why "Duplex" Is Different from a Twin

It is easy to confuse duplex with twins (Chapter 2), since both let one diagram yield multiple problems, but they are conceptually distinct:

  • Twins change the position (a piece moves, a colour swaps, etc.) between parts, and each part keeps the same colour-to-move convention (Black still moves first in each twin).
  • Duplex keeps the position identical between the two solutions, but reverses who moves first and who is mated.

The two devices can be combined - a "duplex twin" exists where each twin part itself has two duplex solutions - but this is rare and reserved for the most ambitious compositions.

A Simple Illustration

Consider a position where, depending on which side is asked to cooperate first, two entirely different mating ideas are latent in the same arrangement of pieces:

h#2 duplex - a single diagram supporting two distinct cooperative mates

Solution 1 (Black moves first, White mates):
1...Re8-e4 (clears the back rank and controls e-file) 1.Nc4 (knight repositions)
2...Kf8 (king steps into the net) 2.Nd6# (knight delivers mate, supported by the rook on e4 cutting off escape via e8)

Solution 2 (White moves first, Black mates):
1.Rd1-d8+ Kxd8 (forced, only legal response) 1...Bb5-e2 (bishop repositions onto a mating diagonal)
2.Ne3-c4 Bxc4# (bishop delivers mate along the long diagonal, with the white king on g1 boxed in by its own rook's vacated lines)

(This illustrative duplex is constructed for explanatory purposes; in genuine compositions, every intermediate position must be carefully checked for legality and the solutions must be unique. The point here is simply to show the shape of a duplex pair: the same starting array, two opposite-coloured cooperative sequences, two different mating pictures.)

Composing Constraints

Duplex composition is considerably harder than single-solution composition because the composer must satisfy two independent sets of constraints simultaneously from one static diagram:

  • The position must be legal as a starting point for both "Black to move" and "White to move" interpretations - in particular, it must not already be check for the side that is about to move cooperatively (unless the very first move is a checking move that is part of the intended solution).
  • Each solution must be unique on its own - a second, unintended way to achieve either Solution 1 or Solution 2 cooks the problem, exactly as in a single helpmate.
  • Ideally, the two solutions should be thematically related - sharing a motif, an echoing mate, or a reversed role for the same pieces - so that the duplex feels like a single artistic statement rather than two unrelated puzzles glued together.

Why Duplexes Are Prized

A successful duplex is considered especially elegant because of its economy: one diagram, one set of pieces, one stipulation word, and yet the solver is rewarded with two complete cooperative narratives. When the two mates are related by some symmetry - for instance, the mating piece in Solution 1 is the piece that gets mated in Solution 2, or the two mating squares are reflections of each other across the board - the duplex achieves a kind of "two-sided economy" that single-solution helpmates cannot match.
