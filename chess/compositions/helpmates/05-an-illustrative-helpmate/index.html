5. An Illustrative Helpmate

This h#2 (helpmate in 2) demonstrates cooperative play from start to finish. Black moves first, helping White arrange checkmate on White's second move.

Position: White: ♔h1, ♕a2, ♘f4  |  Black: ♚e5, ♜d8, ♝c8, ♟d5, ♟e4

Helpmate in 2 - Black moves first

Illustrative helpmate - Black cooperates to be mated

Solution

1...Ke6 (Black king walks into mating net - moves first!)
1. Nd5 (White knight repositions to control escape squares)
2...Kd6 (Black king moves to the ideal mating square)
2. Qe5# (White queen delivers checkmate - Black king on d6 is mated)

Note the backward-analysis logic: we first imagined the queen mating on e5
with the king on d6, then worked out how both sides cooperate to reach that picture.

Annotating the Solution in Detail

Walking through each half-move with the backward-analysis lens from Chapter 4:

  • 1...Ke6. This is Black's only move that sets up the final picture. The king leaves e5 (which the White knight will need to influence) and heads toward d6, the square where it will ultimately be mated. At first glance this looks like Black is "running into danger" - which is exactly the point of a helpmate: Black's job is to cooperate, not to defend.
  • 1. Nd5. With the king off e5, White's knight jumps from f4 to d5. From d5 the knight covers c7, e7, b6, f6, b4, f4, c3, and e3 - critically, it removes several flight squares the Black king on d6 would otherwise have, and it does not give check (which would end the game prematurely on the wrong move).
  • 2...Kd6. Black's king completes its journey to d6 - the square chosen in advance as the mating square. Note that the king is not in check after this move; it is simply taking up its position for the final picture.
  • 2. Qe5#. The White queen, sitting on a2, travels along the long diagonal to e5, delivering checkmate. The king on d6 has no flight squares: d5 is occupied by White's own knight (irrelevant - knight doesn't block king moves, but the knight's coverage of c7/e7/b6/f6/c5... combined with the queen on e5 covering d5, d6's neighbours, e6, c6, d7, c5, e4 - leaves the king with no legal escape, no piece can capture the queen, and nothing can interpose between a queen and an adjacent king).

Why This Mating Picture Is Pleasing

Several small features make this h#2 a satisfying (if simplified, for teaching purposes) illustration of helpmate aesthetics:

  • Single mating piece, queen on e5: the queen alone delivers mate from a central square, covering the king's full ring of escape squares thanks to its combined rook-and-bishop movement.
  • The knight's repositioning is essential, not incidental: Nd5 is not merely "a move that happens to be available" - it specifically removes flight squares that would otherwise let the king slip away after Qe5+.
  • Both kings travel toward each other: the cooperative feel of the solution is reinforced by the fact that Black's king actively walks toward the queen's eventual square, rather than the queen chasing a fleeing king.

A Second Worked Example: A Twin Pair

To see how a small change to the starting position produces an entirely different solution (recall Chapter 2's discussion of twins), consider the same diagram with the Black bishop relocated from c8 to b7.

Twin (b) - ♝c8→b7. The bishop on b7 now covers the long a8-h1 diagonal, including h1 where the White king stands.

Observation: with the bishop on b7, the diagonal toward h1 is occupied by the White king itself - this changes which squares White's pieces can safely travel through and may rule out the original Nd5/Qe5# idea, since the queen's path along a2-e5 could now be contested or the resulting position might leave White's own king exposed to an unintended check from the bishop, which is illegal for a helpmate's intermediate position. A full re-solution of twin (b) would therefore look for an alternative mating square - perhaps using the rook on d8 differently, or routing the knight toward a different final post - illustrating exactly the "start from scratch" principle emphasised in Chapter 2.

From Examples to Themes

This single h#2 already touches on ideas - central queen mates, knight repositioning to remove flight squares, and the effect of a single piece relocation on the whole solution - that recur throughout the helpmate literature in far more elaborate forms. Chapter 6 catalogues the named themes and motifs that composers and solvers use to describe and classify these recurring ideas.
