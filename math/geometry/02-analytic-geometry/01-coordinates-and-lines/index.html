2.1 Coordinates and Lines

The Cartesian coordinate system assigns an ordered pair $(x,y)$ to each point in the plane, converting geometric questions about distance and direction into algebraic ones.

Distance and Midpoint

Definition 2.1.1 - Distance Formula

The distance between points $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$ is $$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.$$ This follows directly from the Pythagorean theorem.

Definition 2.1.2 - Midpoint Formula

The midpoint of the segment from $(x_1,y_1)$ to $(x_2,y_2)$ is $$M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right).$$

Equations of Lines

Forms of a Line

Slope-intercept form: $y = mx + b$, where $m$ is the slope and $b$ is the $y$-intercept.

General form: $ax + by + c = 0$ (with $a,b$ not both zero).

Point-slope form: $y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$ through $(x_0,y_0)$ with slope $m$.

Parametric form: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{d}$, where $\mathbf{p}$ is a point on the line and $\mathbf{d}$ is the direction vector.

Definition 2.1.3 - Slope

The slope of a line making angle $\theta$ with the positive $x$-axis is $m = \tan\theta$. For a line through $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$: $m = (y_2-y_1)/(x_2-x_1)$.

Relationships Between Lines

Theorem 2.1.1 - Perpendicularity Condition

Two lines with slopes $m_1$ and $m_2$ are perpendicular if and only if $m_1 m_2 = -1$ (or one line is vertical and the other horizontal).

Theorem 2.1.2 - Distance from Point to Line

The distance from point $(x_0,y_0)$ to the line $ax + by + c = 0$ is $$d = \frac{|ax_0 + by_0 + c|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}}.$$

Theorem 2.1.3 - Angle Between Lines

The acute angle $\theta$ between lines with slopes $m_1$ and $m_2$ satisfies $$\tan\theta = \left|\frac{m_1 - m_2}{1 + m_1 m_2}\right|.$$

Example

Find the distance from $(3,4)$ to the line $3x + 4y - 10 = 0$. Here $a=3$, $b=4$, $c=-10$, so $d = |9+16-10|/\sqrt{9+16} = |15|/5 = 3$.
