2.2 Conic Sections

A conic section is the curve obtained by intersecting a plane with a right circular double cone. Depending on the angle of the plane, one obtains an ellipse, parabola, or hyperbola (and the degenerate cases: point, line, or two lines).

Ellipse

Definition 2.2.1 — Ellipse

An ellipse is the locus of points whose distances to two fixed foci $F_1, F_2$ sum to a constant $2a$. In standard position with foci on the $x$-axis: $$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \quad a > b > 0.$$ Foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ where $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$. Semi-major axis $a$, semi-minor axis $b$.

For any point $P$ on the ellipse: $PF_1 + PF_2 = 2a$.

Hyperbola

Definition 2.2.2 — Hyperbola

A hyperbola is the locus of points where the absolute difference of distances to two foci is constant $2a$: $$\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1.$$ Foci at $(\pm c,0)$ where $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. For any point $P$: $|PF_1 - PF_2| = 2a$.

The hyperbola has two asymptotes $y = \pm(b/a)x$ which the curve approaches but never meets.

Parabola

Definition 2.2.3 — Parabola

A parabola is the locus of points equidistant from a focus and a directrix line. Standard form: $$y^2 = 4px$$ with focus $F = (p,0)$ and directrix $x = -p$. For any point $P$ on the parabola, $PF$ equals the distance from $P$ to the directrix.

The reflective property: rays parallel to the axis reflect through the focus — the basis of parabolic mirrors and satellite dishes.

Classification of Second-Degree Curves

Theorem 2.2.1 — Discriminant Classification

The general second-degree equation $Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ (not all of $A,B,C$ zero) is classified by the discriminant $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC$:

  • $\Delta < 0$: ellipse (or circle if $A = C$ and $B = 0$, or empty set).
  • $\Delta = 0$: parabola (or degenerate: two parallel lines, one line, or empty).
  • $\Delta > 0$: hyperbola (or two intersecting lines).

This is preserved under rotation of axes, which can be used to eliminate the $Bxy$ cross term.

Eccentricity

All conics can be described by a single parameter: the eccentricity $e = c/a$. For an ellipse $0 < e < 1$; circle $e=0$; parabola $e=1$; hyperbola $e>1$. In polar coordinates with focus at origin: $r = \ell/(1 - e\cos\theta)$ where $\ell$ is the semi-latus rectum.
