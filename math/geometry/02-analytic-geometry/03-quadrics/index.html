2.3 Quadrics

A quadric surface (or quadric) in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is the set of solutions to a degree-2 polynomial equation in $x, y, z$. They are the three-dimensional analogues of conic sections.

Definition 2.3.1 — Quadric Surface

A quadric is the solution set of $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{x} + c = 0$ for a symmetric $3\times 3$ matrix $A$, vector $\mathbf{b}$, and scalar $c$. By an orthogonal change of coordinates (diagonalising $A$), we can bring it to standard form.

Standard Forms

Ellipsoid

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} + \frac{z^2}{c^2} = 1.$$ A sphere when $a = b = c$. All cross-sections parallel to coordinate planes are ellipses (or circles).

Hyperboloid of One Sheet

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} - \frac{z^2}{c^2} = 1.$$ Connected surface; horizontal cross-sections are ellipses, vertical cross-sections are hyperbolas. It is a ruled surface — contains two families of straight lines.

Hyperboloid of Two Sheets

$$-\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} + \frac{z^2}{c^2} = 1.$$ Two separate components (one for $z > c$, one for $z < -c$). Horizontal cross-sections (for $|z| > c$) are ellipses.

Elliptic Paraboloid

$$z = \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2}.$$ Opens upward; all horizontal cross-sections are ellipses; vertical cross-sections are parabolas. Has a single minimum at the origin.

Hyperbolic Paraboloid (Saddle)

$$z = \frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2}.$$ Has a saddle point at the origin. Cross-sections $z = k$ are hyperbolas (opening in different directions for $k>0$ and $k<0$). Also a ruled surface.

Classification

Theorem 2.3.1 — Eigenvalue Classification

The type of a quadric $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{x} + c = 0$ is determined by the signs of the eigenvalues of $A$: all positive (ellipsoid or empty), mixed signs (hyperboloid or saddle), and the rank of $A$ (to distinguish paraboloids from central quadrics).

Remark

Quadrics appear throughout physics and engineering: the inertia ellipsoid, quadratic forms in optimization, and the shape of potential energy surfaces near equilibria.
