1.3 Circles

A circle is the locus of all points in the plane equidistant from a fixed centre. Circles give rise to some of the most beautiful and surprising theorems in elementary geometry.

Definition 1.3.1 - Circle Terminology

A circle with centre $O$ and radius $r$ is $\{P : |OP| = r\}$.

A chord is a segment with both endpoints on the circle. A diameter is a chord through the centre. An arc is a connected part of the circle. A sector is the region bounded by two radii and an arc.

A tangent to a circle touches it at exactly one point. A secant is a line meeting the circle at two points.

Inscribed Angle Theorem

Theorem 1.3.1 - Inscribed Angle Theorem

An inscribed angle (vertex on the circle) is half the central angle subtending the same arc. If $\angle BAC$ is inscribed in the major arc and $\angle BOC$ is the central angle subtending arc $BC$, then $\angle BAC = \frac{1}{2}\angle BOC$.

Theorem 1.3.2 - Thales' Theorem

The angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle. That is, if $BC$ is a diameter and $A$ is any other point on the circle, then $\angle BAC = 90°$. This follows from the inscribed angle theorem with central angle $180°$.

Theorem 1.3.3 - Tangent-Radius Perpendicularity

The tangent to a circle at a point $P$ is perpendicular to the radius $OP$ at $P$.

Power of a Point

Theorem 1.3.4 - Power of a Point

Let $P$ be any point and let any line through $P$ meet a given circle at points $A$ and $B$ (if $P$ is outside the circle) or $A$ and $B$ on opposite sides (if inside). Then the product $PA \cdot PB$ is the same for all such lines through $P$.

This constant is the power of the point $P$ with respect to the circle. If $P$ has distance $d$ from the centre and the radius is $r$, the power equals $d^2 - r^2$ (negative if $P$ is inside).

Example

If two chords $AB$ and $CD$ of a circle intersect at interior point $P$, then $PA \cdot PB = PC \cdot PD$.

Ptolemy's Theorem

Theorem 1.3.5 - Ptolemy's Theorem

For a cyclic quadrilateral $ABCD$ (all four vertices on a common circle), the product of the diagonals equals the sum of the products of opposite sides: $$AC \cdot BD = AB \cdot CD + AD \cdot BC.$$

Remark

Ptolemy's theorem implies the law of cosines and many classical identities. The generalisation to non-cyclic quadrilaterals gives Ptolemy's inequality: $AC \cdot BD \leq AB \cdot CD + AD \cdot BC$, with equality iff $ABCD$ is cyclic.
