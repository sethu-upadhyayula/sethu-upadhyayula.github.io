1.2 Triangles and Congruence

Triangles are the fundamental polygons of Euclidean geometry — every polygon decomposes into triangles. The notion of congruence captures the idea of two figures being identical in shape and size, and the congruence criteria give efficient tests for this.

Congruence Criteria

Congruence Criteria

Two triangles are congruent if any of the following hold:

  • SSS: All three pairs of corresponding sides are equal.
  • SAS: Two sides and the included angle are equal.
  • ASA: Two angles and the included side are equal.
  • AAS: Two angles and a non-included side are equal.

Note: SSA (two sides and a non-included angle) does not in general guarantee congruence (the ambiguous case).

Angle Sum and Related Theorems

Theorem 1.2.1 — Triangle Angle Sum

The interior angles of any triangle sum to $180°$.

Proof

Let $\triangle ABC$ have interior angles $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ at $A, B, C$ respectively. Draw line $\ell$ through $A$ parallel to $BC$ (using the parallel postulate). The alternate interior angles give $\angle BAC' = \beta$ and $\angle CAC'' = \gamma$ on either side of $A$ along $\ell$. Since $\ell$ is a straight line, $\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 180°$. $\square$

Theorem 1.2.2 — Isosceles Triangle Theorem

If $AB = AC$ in $\triangle ABC$, then $\angle ABC = \angle ACB$. Conversely, if two angles of a triangle are equal, the opposite sides are equal.

Pythagorean Theorem

Theorem 1.2.3 — Pythagorean Theorem

In a right triangle with legs $a, b$ and hypotenuse $c$: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$.

Proof (via altitude)

Drop an altitude from the right angle $C$ to hypotenuse $AB$, meeting it at $H$. The two smaller triangles $\triangle ACH$ and $\triangle CBH$ are each similar to $\triangle ACB$. From $\triangle ACH \sim \triangle ACB$: $b/c = AH/b$, so $AH = b^2/c$. From $\triangle CBH \sim \triangle ACB$: $a/c = BH/a$, so $BH = a^2/c$.

Since $AH + BH = c$: $b^2/c + a^2/c = c$, giving $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$. $\square$

Triangle Inequality

Theorem 1.2.4 — Triangle Inequality

For any triangle with vertices $A, B, C$: $|AC| < |AB| + |BC|$. Equality holds only when the three points are collinear.

Concurrency Points

The Four Classical Centres

Centroid $G$: Intersection of the three medians (each connecting a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side). $G$ divides each median in ratio $2:1$ from vertex to midpoint.

Orthocentre $H$: Intersection of the three altitudes (perpendiculars from each vertex to the opposite side).

Incentre $I$: Intersection of the three angle bisectors. $I$ is equidistant from all three sides and is the centre of the inscribed circle (incircle).

Circumcentre $O$: Intersection of the three perpendicular bisectors of the sides. $O$ is equidistant from all three vertices and is the centre of the circumscribed circle (circumcircle).

Euler Line

The centroid $G$, orthocentre $H$, and circumcentre $O$ are collinear (lying on the Euler line), with $OG:GH = 1:2$.

Similar Triangles

Similarity Criteria

AA: Two triangles with two pairs of equal angles are similar (the third angles are then also equal).

SAS Similarity: Two sides in proportion and the included angle equal implies similar triangles.

Theorem 1.2.5 — Angle Bisector Theorem

The angle bisector from vertex $A$ in $\triangle ABC$ divides the opposite side $BC$ in the ratio $AB : AC$.
