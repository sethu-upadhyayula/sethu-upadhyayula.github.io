1.1 Axioms and Basic Theorems

Euclid's Elements opens with a small set of postulates from which an enormous body of geometry is deduced. These postulates capture our intuitions about points, lines, and circles in the plane. Modern mathematics has refined and completed this axiomatic foundation through Hilbert's axioms.

Euclid's Five Postulates

Postulate 1

Given any two distinct points, there is a unique straight line passing through them.

Postulate 2

Any line segment can be extended continuously in a straight line (indefinitely).

Postulate 3

Given any centre and any radius, a circle can be drawn.

Postulate 4

All right angles are equal to one another.

Postulate 5 (Parallel Postulate)

If a line segment intersects two straight lines forming interior angles on the same side that sum to less than two right angles, then those two lines, extended indefinitely, meet on that side. Equivalently (Playfair's axiom): through a point not on a given line, there is exactly one line parallel to the given line.

The first four postulates are uncontroversial. The fifth sparked centuries of controversy — many geometers tried to prove it from the others, not realising that its negation gives consistent non-Euclidean geometries.

Basic Theorems

Theorem 1.1.1 — Vertical Angles

When two lines intersect, the opposite (vertical) angles are equal.

Proof

Let lines intersect at $O$, forming angles $\angle AOC$, $\angle COB$, $\angle BOD$, $\angle DOA$. Since $AOB$ is a straight line:

$$\angle AOC + \angle COB = 180°$$

$$\angle BOD + \angle COB = 180°$$

Subtracting: $\angle AOC = \angle BOD$. Similarly $\angle COB = \angle DOA$. $\square$

Theorem 1.1.2 — Angles on a Straight Line

The angles on one side of a straight line at a point sum to $180°$.

Theorem 1.1.3 — Exterior Angle Theorem

An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles. If $\triangle ABC$ has exterior angle $\angle ACD$ at $C$, then $\angle ACD = \angle BAC + \angle ABC$.

Hilbert's Axioms (Modern Refinement)

Euclid's postulates contain implicit assumptions Hilbert made explicit in 1899 in Grundlagen der Geometrie. His axioms are grouped into five categories:

Hilbert's Axiom Groups

I. Incidence: Axioms about which points lie on which lines and planes (e.g., two points determine a unique line).

II. Betweenness (Order): Axioms formalising the notion that one point lies between two others on a line.

III. Congruence: Axioms giving precise meaning to congruence of segments and angles.

IV. Continuity (Completeness): The Archimedean axiom and Cantor's axiom ensuring the line has no gaps.

V. Parallelism: Playfair's axiom — through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel exists (Euclidean case).

Remark

Hilbert's system is categorical: any two models satisfying all his axioms are isomorphic to the Cartesian plane $\mathbb{R}^2$. This vindicates Euclid's geometry as the unique complete, consistent axiomatisation of the plane.
