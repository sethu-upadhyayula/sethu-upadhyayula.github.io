Chapter 1: Euclidean Geometry

Euclidean geometry studies flat space from Euclid's five postulates, developing theorems about triangles, circles, and their relationships. For over two millennia, this axiomatic system served as the model of rigorous mathematical reasoning — Euclid's Elements (c. 300 BCE) systematically derived hundreds of propositions from five simple postulates.

The key distinction of Euclidean geometry is the parallel postulate: through any point not on a given line, there is exactly one line parallel to it. Removing or modifying this postulate leads to the non-Euclidean geometries studied in Chapter 4.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Axioms and Basic Theorems

Euclid's five postulates are stated and examined. We derive fundamental theorems: vertical angles, angles on a straight line, and the exterior angle theorem. Hilbert's modern refinement of the axiomatic system is also discussed.

1.2 — Triangles and Congruence

The congruence criteria (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS) are established. We prove the triangle angle sum theorem, the Pythagorean theorem, and study the four classical concurrency points: centroid, orthocentre, incentre, and circumcentre.

1.3 — Circles

The inscribed angle theorem, Thales' theorem, the tangent-radius perpendicularity, the power of a point, and Ptolemy's theorem for cyclic quadrilaterals are developed.
