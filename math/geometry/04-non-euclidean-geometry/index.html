Chapter 4: Non-Euclidean Geometry

For over two thousand years, Euclid's fifth postulate - the parallel postulate - was suspected to be provable from the other four. Attempts to derive a contradiction from its negation instead produced two entirely consistent geometries: hyperbolic and elliptic. This discovery, made independently by Gauss, Bolyai, and Lobachevsky in the early nineteenth century, fundamentally changed mathematics by demonstrating that geometry need not be unique.

Replacing the parallel postulate leads to two distinct systems. In hyperbolic geometry, the plane has negative curvature and infinitely many lines through a given point fail to meet a given line. In elliptic geometry, the plane has positive curvature and no two lines are ever parallel - every pair of lines meets.

The Parallel Postulate

Euclid's fifth postulate (in Playfair's equivalent form): through a point not on a given line, there is exactly one line parallel to it.

  • Euclidean geometry: exactly one parallel - flat space, $K = 0$.
  • Hyperbolic geometry: infinitely many parallels - negatively curved space, $K < 0$.
  • Elliptic geometry: no parallels - positively curved space, $K > 0$.

Chapter Overview

4.1 - Hyperbolic Geometry

The Lobachevsky-Bolyai postulate replaces Euclid's fifth. We study the Poincaré half-plane model with its metric, geodesics, and isometry group PSL$(2,\mathbb{R})$. Triangle defect, constant curvature $K = -1$, hyperbolic trigonometry, and the ideal boundary are developed.

4.2 - Elliptic Geometry

Any two great circles on $S^2$ meet, giving a geometry with no parallels. The elliptic plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$ is formed by identifying antipodal points. Triangle excess, Girard's theorem, and the spherical laws of cosines and sines are established. The chapter closes with a unified comparison of all three geometries.
