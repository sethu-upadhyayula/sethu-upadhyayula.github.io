4.1 Hyperbolic Geometry

Hyperbolic geometry arises when Euclid's parallel postulate is replaced by its logical opposite: through a point not on a given line, there exist infinitely many lines that do not meet the given line. This postulate was proposed independently by Nikolai Lobachevsky (1830) and János Bolyai (1832), and the geometry they discovered is internally consistent - free of contradiction.

Postulate 4.1.1 - Lobachevsky-Bolyai (1830)

Through a point $P$ not on a line $\ell$, there exist infinitely many lines that do not intersect $\ell$. Equivalently, in the hyperbolic plane there is no unique parallel - the set of non-intersecting lines through $P$ forms an open angular region bounded by two limiting parallel rays.

The Poincaré Half-Plane Model

Definition 4.1.1 - Poincaré Half-Plane

The Poincaré upper half-plane is the set $$\mathbb{H} = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : y > 0\},$$ equipped with the hyperbolic metric $$ds^2 = \frac{dx^2 + dy^2}{y^2}.$$ This is a Riemannian metric of constant Gaussian curvature $K = -1$.

Using complex coordinates $z = x + iy$, the metric becomes $ds = |dz|/\operatorname{Im}(z)$.

Definition 4.1.2 - Hyperbolic Distance

For two points $P, Q \in \mathbb{H}$, let $\bar{Q}$ denote the reflection of $Q$ across the $x$-axis (i.e., the complex conjugate $\bar{Q} = \overline{Q}$). The hyperbolic distance is $$d(P,Q) = 2\,\operatorname{arctanh}\frac{|PQ|}{|P\bar{Q}|},$$ where $|PQ|$ and $|P\bar{Q}|$ are ordinary Euclidean distances.

Equivalently, $d(P,Q) = \operatorname{arccosh}\!\left(1 + \frac{|P-Q|^2}{2\,\operatorname{Im}(P)\operatorname{Im}(Q)}\right).$

Geodesics

Theorem 4.1.1 - Geodesics in $\mathbb{H}$

The geodesics (shortest paths) in the Poincaré half-plane are exactly:

  • Vertical lines: $\{x = c,\; y > 0\}$ for $c \in \mathbb{R}$.
  • Semicircles: $\{(x-a)^2 + y^2 = r^2,\; y > 0\}$ with centre $(a, 0)$ on the $x$-axis and radius $r > 0$.
Many Parallels

Given a geodesic $\ell$ and a point $P \notin \ell$, infinitely many geodesics through $P$ do not meet $\ell$. The two limiting parallels are the geodesics through $P$ that touch $\ell$ at its ideal endpoints on the $x$-axis; all geodesics through $P$ lying in the angular region between them are also non-intersecting with $\ell$.

The Isometry Group

Definition 4.1.3 - PSL$(2,\mathbb{R})$

The orientation-preserving isometries of $\mathbb{H}$ are the Möbius transformations $$z \mapsto \frac{az+b}{cz+d}, \qquad a,b,c,d \in \mathbb{R},\quad ad-bc > 0.$$ These form the group PSL$(2,\mathbb{R}) = \text{SL}(2,\mathbb{R})/\{\pm I\}$, acting on $\mathbb{H}$ by fractional linear transformations.

The full isometry group $\text{Isom}(\mathbb{H})$ also includes orientation-reversing isometries of the form $z \mapsto \frac{a\bar{z}+b}{c\bar{z}+d}$ with $ad - bc > 0$.

Triangles and Area

Theorem 4.1.2 - Hyperbolic Triangle Angle Sum

In hyperbolic geometry, the sum of interior angles of any triangle satisfies $$\alpha + \beta + \gamma < \pi.$$ The defect is defined as $\delta = \pi - \alpha - \beta - \gamma > 0$.

The area of a hyperbolic triangle is $\text{Area} = |K|\,\delta = \delta$ (since $K = -1$). Larger triangles have larger defect.

Example - Ideal Triangle

An ideal triangle has all three vertices on the ideal boundary $\partial\mathbb{H} = \mathbb{R} \cup \{\infty\}$. Its angles are all $0$, so defect $\delta = \pi$ and area $= \pi$. All ideal triangles are congruent to each other under PSL$(2,\mathbb{R})$.

Hyperbolic Trigonometry

Theorem 4.1.3 - Hyperbolic Pythagorean Theorem

In a right hyperbolic triangle with legs $a$, $b$ and hypotenuse $c$ (right angle opposite $c$): $$\cosh c = \cosh a \cosh b.$$ Compare to the Euclidean $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$; as $a, b \to 0$ this reduces to it via $\cosh x \approx 1 + x^2/2$.

The general hyperbolic law of cosines is $\cosh c = \cosh a \cosh b - \sinh a \sinh b \cos C$.

Hyperbolic Functions Reference

$\cosh t = \dfrac{e^t + e^{-t}}{2}$, $\quad\sinh t = \dfrac{e^t - e^{-t}}{2}$, $\quad\tanh t = \dfrac{\sinh t}{\cosh t}$, $\quad\operatorname{arctanh} t = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\dfrac{1+t}{1-t}$.

The Ideal Boundary

Definition 4.1.4 - Ideal Boundary

The ideal boundary (or boundary at infinity) of $\mathbb{H}$ is $$\partial\mathbb{H} = \mathbb{R} \cup \{\infty\} \cong S^1.$$ Points on $\partial\mathbb{H}$ are called ideal points; they lie at infinite hyperbolic distance from any interior point. Every geodesic has exactly two ideal endpoints.

The action of PSL$(2,\mathbb{R})$ on $\mathbb{H}$ extends continuously to $\partial\mathbb{H}$, acting by Möbius transformations of the real line.
