4.2 Elliptic Geometry

Elliptic geometry takes the opposite path from hyperbolic: instead of many parallels, there are none. Every pair of distinct lines meets. The natural model is the round sphere $S^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^3$, where great circles play the role of straight lines and any two of them intersect in two antipodal points. Identifying antipodal points gives the elliptic plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$, in which any two lines meet in exactly one point.

Definition 4.2.1 — Spherical Model

Consider the unit sphere $S^2 = \{(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : x^2+y^2+z^2 = 1\}$ with the metric induced from $\mathbb{R}^3$. The geodesics are the great circles — intersections of $S^2$ with planes through the origin. Distance between two points equals the arc length along the great circle connecting them: $$d(P,Q) = \arccos(P \cdot Q), \qquad P, Q \in S^2.$$

The sphere has constant Gaussian curvature $K = +1$ (or $K = 1/R^2$ for a sphere of radius $R$).

Definition 4.2.2 — Elliptic Plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$

The real projective plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$ is obtained from $S^2$ by identifying each pair of antipodal points: $P \sim -P$. In $\mathbb{RP}^2$:

  • Each point corresponds to a pair of antipodal points on $S^2$, equivalently a line through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
  • Each line corresponds to a great circle on $S^2$, equivalently a plane through the origin.
  • Any two distinct lines meet in exactly one point.

The postulate of no parallels holds: there are no parallel lines in $\mathbb{RP}^2$.

Spherical Triangles

Theorem 4.2.1 — Girard's Theorem (1625)

Let $T$ be a spherical triangle on the unit sphere with interior angles $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$. Then: $$\alpha + \beta + \gamma > \pi.$$ The spherical excess is $\varepsilon = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi > 0$, and the area of $T$ equals its excess: $$\operatorname{Area}(T) = \varepsilon = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi.$$

(For a sphere of radius $R$: $\operatorname{Area}(T) = R^2\,\varepsilon$.) This is the direct analogue of the hyperbolic defect formula, with the sign flipped.

Spherical Trigonometry

Theorem 4.2.2 — Spherical Law of Cosines

In a spherical triangle with sides $a, b, c$ (arc lengths on the unit sphere) opposite to angles $A, B, C$: $$\cos c = \cos a \cos b + \sin a \sin b \cos C.$$

The dual law: $\cos C = -\cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B \cos c$.

Theorem 4.2.3 — Spherical Law of Sines

$$\frac{\sin a}{\sin A} = \frac{\sin b}{\sin B} = \frac{\sin c}{\sin C}.$$ This is the spherical analogue of the Euclidean law of sines $a/\sin A = b/\sin B = c/\sin C$.

Example — Right Spherical Triangle

A spherical right triangle with $C = \pi/2$ satisfies $\cos c = \cos a \cos b$ (compare with hyperbolic: $\cosh c = \cosh a \cosh b$, and Euclidean: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$). As side lengths tend to zero, both spherical and hyperbolic formulas reduce to the Euclidean one.

Comparison of the Three Geometries

Unified Comparison

All three classical geometries are unified by their curvature $K$:

GeometryCurvature $K$ParallelsAngle sumArea of triangle
Euclidean$K = 0$exactly one$= \pi$independent of angles
Hyperbolic$K < 0$infinitely many$< \pi$$= |K|^{-1}\delta$, defect $\delta = \pi - \sum\alpha$
Elliptic$K > 0$none$> \pi$$= K^{-1}\varepsilon$, excess $\varepsilon = \sum\alpha - \pi$

As triangles shrink to a point, all three geometries become locally indistinguishable — they share the same infinitesimal (first-order) structure.

Isometry Groups

The isometry group of $S^2$ (orientation-preserving) is SO$(3)$, the group of rotations of $\mathbb{R}^3$. For $\mathbb{RP}^2$ with the round metric it is also SO$(3)$ (since antipodal maps commute with all rotations). This should be compared with PSL$(2,\mathbb{R})$ for $\mathbb{H}$ and SE$(2) = \mathbb{R}^2 \rtimes SO(2)$ for the Euclidean plane.
