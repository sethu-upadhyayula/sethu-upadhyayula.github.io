5.1 Curves in Space

A smooth curve in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a smooth map $\boldsymbol{\alpha} : (a,b) \to \mathbb{R}^3$. The curve is called regular if its velocity vector is never zero: $|\boldsymbol{\alpha}'(t)| \ne 0$ for all $t$. Regularity ensures the curve has a well-defined tangent direction at every point.

Arc Length and Reparametrisation

Definition 5.1.1 — Arc Length

The speed of $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ at $t$ is $|\boldsymbol{\alpha}'(t)|$. The arc length from $a$ to $t$ is $$s(t) = \int_a^t |\boldsymbol{\alpha}'(u)|\,du.$$ For a regular curve, $s'(t) = |\boldsymbol{\alpha}'(t)| > 0$, so $s$ is strictly increasing and may be used as a new parameter.

A curve is unit-speed (or parametrised by arc length) if $|\boldsymbol{\alpha}'(s)| = 1$ for all $s$. Every regular curve admits a unit-speed reparametrisation.

The Frenet-Serret Frame

For a unit-speed regular curve, we define a canonical orthonormal frame — the Frenet-Serret frame — that travels along the curve and captures its bending and twisting.

Definition 5.1.2 — Frenet-Serret Frame

Let $\boldsymbol{\alpha}(s)$ be a unit-speed curve with $\boldsymbol{\alpha}''(s) \ne \mathbf{0}$. Define:

  • Unit tangent: $\mathbf{T}(s) = \boldsymbol{\alpha}'(s)$, so $|\mathbf{T}| = 1$.
  • Curvature: $\kappa(s) = |\boldsymbol{\alpha}''(s)| = |\mathbf{T}'(s)| \geq 0$.
  • Principal normal: $\mathbf{N}(s) = \boldsymbol{\alpha}''(s)/\kappa(s)$ (unit vector, perpendicular to $\mathbf{T}$).
  • Binormal: $\mathbf{B}(s) = \mathbf{T}(s) \times \mathbf{N}(s)$ (unit vector, perpendicular to both $\mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{N}$).
  • Torsion: $\tau(s)$ defined by $\mathbf{B}'(s) = -\tau(s)\mathbf{N}(s)$.

The triple $(\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{B})$ is a right-handed orthonormal frame at each point, called the Frenet-Serret frame or moving trihedron.

The Frenet-Serret Equations

Theorem 5.1.1 — Frenet-Serret Formulas

The derivatives of the Frenet-Serret frame vectors satisfy: $$\mathbf{T}' = \kappa\,\mathbf{N},$$ $$\mathbf{N}' = -\kappa\,\mathbf{T} + \tau\,\mathbf{B},$$ $$\mathbf{B}' = -\tau\,\mathbf{N}.$$ In matrix form: $\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{T}'\\\mathbf{N}'\\\mathbf{B}'\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0&\kappa&0\\-\kappa&0&\tau\\0&-\tau&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{T}\\\mathbf{N}\\\mathbf{B}\end{pmatrix}.$

The skew-symmetry of the matrix reflects the fact that $(\mathbf{T},\mathbf{N},\mathbf{B})$ is an orthonormal frame.

Geometric Interpretation

Meaning of Curvature and Torsion
  • Curvature $\kappa$ measures how fast the tangent turns — the reciprocal of the radius of the osculating circle. A straight line has $\kappa = 0$; a circle of radius $r$ has $\kappa = 1/r$.
  • Torsion $\tau$ measures how fast the curve twists out of the osculating plane spanned by $\mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{N}$. A plane curve has $\tau = 0$ everywhere. A helix has constant nonzero $\tau$.

The osculating plane at a point is the plane through that point spanned by $\mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{N}$; the curve hugs this plane to second order.

Example — Circular Helix

The helix $\boldsymbol{\alpha}(t) = (a\cos t,\, a\sin t,\, bt)$ for $a > 0, b \ne 0$ has speed $|\boldsymbol{\alpha}'| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$. After reparametrising by arc length, the curvature and torsion are constant: $$\kappa = \frac{a}{a^2+b^2}, \qquad \tau = \frac{b}{a^2+b^2}.$$

A helix is the only curve with both $\kappa$ and $\tau$ constant. When $b=0$ it degenerates to a circle ($\tau = 0$, $\kappa = 1/a$).

Fundamental Theorem of Curves

Theorem 5.1.2 — Fundamental Theorem of Space Curves

Given smooth functions $\kappa(s) > 0$ and $\tau(s)$ on an interval, there exists a unit-speed curve $\boldsymbol{\alpha}(s)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with curvature $\kappa$ and torsion $\tau$. Moreover, this curve is unique up to a rigid motion (isometry) of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

In other words, the pair $(\kappa(s), \tau(s))$ is a complete set of local invariants for smooth space curves.
