5.2 Surfaces

A smooth surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is locally described by a smooth map $\mathbf{r} : U \to \mathbb{R}^3$ where $U \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is open. The map $\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v))$ is called a parametrisation. The surface is regular if the partial derivatives $\mathbf{r}_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v$ are linearly independent at every point, i.e., $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v \ne \mathbf{0}$.

Tangent Plane and Normal

Definition 5.2.1 — Tangent Plane and Unit Normal

At a point $p = \mathbf{r}(u_0,v_0)$, the tangent plane is spanned by the partial derivatives: $$T_p S = \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf{r}_u,\, \mathbf{r}_v\}.$$ The unit normal is $$\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|}.$$

Regularity ensures $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| \ne 0$, so $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is well-defined and varies smoothly with $(u,v)$.

The First Fundamental Form (Intrinsic Metric)

Definition 5.2.2 — First Fundamental Form

The first fundamental form $I$ is the restriction of the Euclidean inner product to the tangent plane. In the $(u,v)$ coordinates: $$I = E\,du^2 + 2F\,du\,dv + G\,dv^2,$$ where the coefficients are $$E = \mathbf{r}_u \cdot \mathbf{r}_u, \qquad F = \mathbf{r}_u \cdot \mathbf{r}_v, \qquad G = \mathbf{r}_v \cdot \mathbf{r}_v.$$

The matrix $\begin{pmatrix}E&F\\F&G\end{pmatrix}$ is positive definite (since the surface is regular).

What $I$ Computes
  • Arc length of a curve $\mathbf{r}(u(t),v(t))$: $\displaystyle L = \int \sqrt{E\dot{u}^2 + 2F\dot{u}\dot{v} + G\dot{v}^2}\,dt$.
  • Area element: $dA = \sqrt{EG - F^2}\,du\,dv$, so $\operatorname{Area}(S) = \iint_U \sqrt{EG-F^2}\,du\,dv$.
  • Angle between curves: via $\cos\theta = I(\mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v)/(|\mathbf{r}_u||\mathbf{r}_v|)$.
Example — Sphere of Radius $R$

Parametrise $S^2_R$ by $\mathbf{r}(\theta,\phi) = (R\sin\phi\cos\theta, R\sin\phi\sin\theta, R\cos\phi)$. Then: $$E = R^2\sin^2\phi,\quad F = 0,\quad G = R^2,$$ giving $I = R^2\sin^2\phi\,d\theta^2 + R^2\,d\phi^2$ and area element $dA = R^2\sin\phi\,d\theta\,d\phi$.

Integrating gives total area $= 4\pi R^2$, as expected.

The Second Fundamental Form (Extrinsic Shape)

Definition 5.2.3 — Second Fundamental Form

The second fundamental form $II$ measures how the surface bends away from its tangent plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$II = L\,du^2 + 2M\,du\,dv + N\,dv^2,$$ where $$L = \mathbf{r}_{uu} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}, \qquad M = \mathbf{r}_{uv} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}, \qquad N = \mathbf{r}_{vv} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}.$$

Equivalently, $L = -\mathbf{r}_u \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}_u$, $M = -\mathbf{r}_u \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}_v$, $N = -\mathbf{r}_v \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}_v$ (the Weingarten equations).

Definition 5.2.4 — Normal Curvature

For a unit tangent vector $\mathbf{v}$ in $T_pS$, the normal curvature in direction $\mathbf{v}$ is $$\kappa_n(\mathbf{v}) = II(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}) = L\,du^2 + 2M\,du\,dv + N\,dv^2,$$ where $(du:dv)$ is the direction of $\mathbf{v}$.

It equals the curvature of the curve obtained by intersecting the surface with the plane through $p$ spanned by $\mathbf{v}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

The Fundamental Forms Together

The first and second fundamental forms $I$ and $II$ together determine a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ up to rigid motion (this is the analogue of the fundamental theorem of curves). The first form alone determines the intrinsic geometry; the second form captures the additional extrinsic data. Their combination gives rise to curvature, studied in the next section.
