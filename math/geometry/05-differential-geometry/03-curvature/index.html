5.3 Curvature

Having set up the first and second fundamental forms, we can now define the curvature invariants of a surface. The principal curvatures are the extremal normal curvatures; from them we extract the Gaussian curvature $K$ and mean curvature $H$. Gauss's celebrated Theorema Egregium (1827) reveals that $K$ is intrinsic - detectable by measurements within the surface alone - leading directly to the connection between geometry and topology in the Gauss-Bonnet theorem.

Principal Curvatures and the Shape Operator

Definition 5.3.1 - Shape Operator (Weingarten Map)

The shape operator $S : T_pS \to T_pS$ is defined by $S = -d\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, where $d\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the differential of the Gauss map $\hat{\mathbf{n}} : S \to S^2$. In coordinates it is represented by the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}E&F\\F&G\end{pmatrix}^{-1}\begin{pmatrix}L&M\\M&N\end{pmatrix}.$

The shape operator is self-adjoint with respect to $I$, so it has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.

Definition 5.3.2 - Principal Curvatures and Directions

The principal curvatures $\kappa_1 \leq \kappa_2$ are the eigenvalues of the shape operator $S$. The corresponding eigenvectors (unit vectors in $T_pS$) are the principal directions.

By the spectral theorem, the principal directions are orthogonal. The normal curvature in any unit tangent direction $\mathbf{v} = \cos\theta\,\mathbf{e}_1 + \sin\theta\,\mathbf{e}_2$ (where $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$ are principal directions) satisfies Euler's formula: $\kappa_n(\mathbf{v}) = \kappa_1\cos^2\theta + \kappa_2\sin^2\theta$.

Gaussian and Mean Curvature

Definition 5.3.3 - Gaussian and Mean Curvature

The Gaussian curvature is the determinant of the shape operator: $$K = \kappa_1\kappa_2 = \frac{LN - M^2}{EG - F^2}.$$ The mean curvature is half the trace: $$H = \frac{\kappa_1 + \kappa_2}{2} = \frac{EN - 2FM + GL}{2(EG-F^2)}.$$

The sign of $K$ determines the local shape: $K > 0$ (elliptic point, like a sphere), $K < 0$ (hyperbolic point, like a saddle), $K = 0$ (parabolic or flat point).

Example - Standard Surfaces
  • Sphere of radius $R$: $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 1/R$, so $K = 1/R^2$, $H = 1/R$. All points are elliptic.
  • Cylinder of radius $R$: $\kappa_1 = 1/R$ (across the axis), $\kappa_2 = 0$ (along the axis), so $K = 0$, $H = 1/(2R)$. All points are parabolic.
  • Saddle surface $z = xy$: $\kappa_1 > 0$, $\kappa_2 < 0$ at the origin, so $K < 0$. Hyperbolic point.
  • Plane: $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 0$, $K = H = 0$ everywhere.

Theorema Egregium

Theorem 5.3.1 - Theorema Egregium (Gauss, 1827)

The Gaussian curvature $K$ is an intrinsic invariant of the surface: it can be expressed entirely in terms of the first fundamental form coefficients $E, F, G$ and their partial derivatives (via the Brioschi formula or the Gauss equation), without using $L, M, N$.

Consequently, $K$ is preserved by local isometries - smooth maps that preserve the first fundamental form. If two surfaces are locally isometric, they have the same Gaussian curvature at corresponding points.

Consequence: You Cannot Flatten a Sphere

A flat sheet of paper has $K = 0$ everywhere. The unit sphere has $K = 1$ everywhere. Since $K$ is intrinsic and $0 \ne 1$, no region of a sphere can be mapped isometrically to a plane. This is why every flat map of the Earth must distort either distances or angles (or both). A cylinder, having $K = 0$, can be unrolled flat without distortion.

Minimal Surfaces

Definition 5.3.4 - Minimal Surface

A surface is minimal if $H = 0$ everywhere, i.e., $\kappa_1 + \kappa_2 = 0$ at every point. Minimal surfaces locally minimise area (for fixed boundary) - they are the shapes taken by soap films.

Examples: the catenoid (surface of revolution of a catenary $y = \cosh x$) and the helicoid (a twisted plane, parametrised by $\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (v\cos u, v\sin u, cu)$). These are locally isometric to each other - an extraordinary consequence of the Theorema Egregium.

The Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

Theorem 5.3.2 - Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

Let $M$ be a compact oriented surface with boundary $\partial M$, and let $\kappa_g$ denote the geodesic curvature of $\partial M$. Then: $$\iint_M K\,dA + \oint_{\partial M} \kappa_g\,ds = 2\pi\,\chi(M),$$ where $\chi(M) = V - E + F$ is the Euler characteristic of $M$.

For a closed surface (no boundary): $\displaystyle\iint_M K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(M).$

Examples of Gauss-Bonnet
  • Sphere $S^2$: $K = 1/R^2$, area $= 4\pi R^2$, so $\iint K\,dA = 4\pi = 2\pi \cdot 2 = 2\pi\chi(S^2)$ (since $\chi(S^2) = 2$). ✓
  • Torus $T^2$: $\chi(T^2) = 0$, so $\iint K\,dA = 0$. Indeed the positive curvature on the outer part and negative on the inner part cancel exactly.
  • Genus-$g$ surface: $\chi = 2-2g$, so $\iint K\,dA = 2\pi(2-2g) = 4\pi(1-g)$.
  • Geodesic triangle: $\oint_{\partial T}\kappa_g\,ds = 0$ (geodesic sides) plus corner contributions $(\pi - \alpha_i)$, giving $\iint_T K\,dA = \alpha+\beta+\gamma - \pi$ - recovering Girard's theorem on the sphere.
Significance

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem is one of the deepest results in mathematics: it asserts that the integral of a local differential-geometric quantity ($K$) over a surface equals a global topological invariant ($\chi$). No matter how you bend or deform the surface (without tearing), the total curvature is fixed by the topology alone. This prefigures the vast generalisation known as the Atiyah-Singer index theorem.
