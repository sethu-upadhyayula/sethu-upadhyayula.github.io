3.3 Affine and Projective Maps

Moving further up Klein's hierarchy, affine and projective geometries preserve progressively weaker properties — from parallelism and ratios (affine) to collinearity alone (projective).

Affine Geometry

Definition 3.3.1 — Affine Map

An affine map is $f(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$ where $A$ is an invertible $n\times n$ matrix and $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$. The set of all affine maps forms the affine group $\text{Aff}(n)$.

Affine Invariants

Affine maps preserve:

  • Collinearity: points on a line map to points on a line.
  • Parallelism: parallel lines remain parallel.
  • Midpoints and ratios: the midpoint of $AB$ maps to the midpoint of $f(A)f(B)$; more generally, ratios of division along a line are preserved.
  • Area ratios: areas scale by $|\det A|$.

Note: distances, angles, and perpendicularity are not generally preserved.

Barycentric Coordinates

Every point $P$ in the plane of $\triangle ABC$ can be written uniquely as $P = \lambda_1 A + \lambda_2 B + \lambda_3 C$ with $\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\lambda_3=1$. The $\lambda_i$ are the barycentric coordinates of $P$, and they are preserved by affine maps.

Projective Geometry

Definition 3.3.2 — Projective Space

The real projective plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$ is the set of equivalence classes $[x:y:z]$ of nonzero vectors $(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3$, where $(x,y,z) \sim (\lambda x, \lambda y, \lambda z)$ for $\lambda \neq 0$.

Ordinary affine points $(x,y)$ correspond to $[x:y:1]$. The "line at infinity" consists of points $[x:y:0]$.

Definition 3.3.3 — Projective Map (Homography)

A projective map (collineation or homography) is given by $[x':y':z'] = A[x:y:z]$ for an invertible matrix $A \in GL_3(\mathbb{R})$. Two matrices give the same map iff they are scalar multiples of each other, so the group of homographies is $PGL_3(\mathbb{R}) = GL_3(\mathbb{R})/\{\lambda I\}$.

Projective Invariants

Projective maps preserve:

  • Collinearity: lines map to lines.
  • Cross-ratio: for four collinear points $A,B,C,D$, the cross-ratio $(A,B;C,D)$ is invariant.

Parallelism, ratios, distances, angles, and areas are not preserved.

Point-Line Duality

In $\mathbb{RP}^2$, points and lines play symmetric roles: any two distinct points determine a unique line, and any two distinct lines meet at a unique point (no parallel lines). This duality principle means every true theorem has a dual theorem obtained by swapping "point" and "line".
