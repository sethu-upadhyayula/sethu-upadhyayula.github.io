3.1 Isometries

An isometry is a distance-preserving bijection of the plane (or space) to itself. Isometries are the symmetries of Euclidean geometry — the transformations under which all geometric properties are preserved.

Definition 3.1.1 — Isometry

A bijection $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$ is an isometry if $d(f(x),f(y)) = d(x,y)$ for all $x,y\in\mathbb{R}^n$.

Classification in $\mathbb{R}^2$

Theorem 3.1.1 — Classification of Plane Isometries

Every isometry of $\mathbb{R}^2$ is one of exactly four types: translation, rotation, reflection, or glide reflection.

The Four Types

Translation $T_\mathbf{v}$: $T_\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{v}$. No fixed points (unless $\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$). Direct isometry (orientation-preserving).

Rotation $R_{\theta,O}$: Rotation by angle $\theta$ about centre $O$. Fixed point set: $\{O\}$ (unless $\theta = 0$). Direct isometry.

Reflection $\sigma_\ell$: Reflection in line $\ell$. Fixed point set: the line $\ell$ itself. Opposite isometry (orientation-reversing).

Glide Reflection: Composition of a reflection in line $\ell$ and a translation along $\ell$. No fixed points. Opposite isometry.

The Euclidean Group

Definition 3.1.2 — Euclidean Group

The set of all isometries of $\mathbb{R}^2$ forms a group under composition, the Euclidean group: $$E(2) = \mathbb{R}^2 \rtimes O(2).$$ Every isometry can be written as $\mathbf{x} \mapsto A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$ where $A \in O(2)$ (orthogonal matrix).

The subgroup of direct (orientation-preserving) isometries is $SE(2) = \mathbb{R}^2 \rtimes SO(2)$, consisting of translations and rotations.

Fixed Points

Fixed-Point Sets

An isometry of $\mathbb{R}^2$ is completely determined by the images of three non-collinear points.

  • Translations: No fixed points (except the identity).
  • Rotations: The centre of rotation is the unique fixed point.
  • Reflections: Every point on the mirror line is fixed.
  • Glide reflections: No fixed points.

A direct isometry with a fixed point is a rotation. A direct isometry without fixed points is a translation.

Isometries in $\mathbb{R}^3$

In three dimensions, new types appear: screw motions (rotation + translation along the same axis) and rotoreflections (rotation + reflection in a perpendicular plane). Every isometry of $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a translation, rotation, reflection, glide reflection, screw motion, or rotoreflection.
