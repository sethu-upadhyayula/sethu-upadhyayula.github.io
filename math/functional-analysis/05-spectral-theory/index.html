Chapter 5: Spectral Theory

The spectrum of a bounded operator generalises the set of eigenvalues from finite-dimensional linear algebra to infinite dimensions. Spectral theory is the backbone of quantum mechanics, where self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space represent physical observables and their spectra represent possible measurement outcomes.

Chapter Overview

5.1 - Spectrum of an Operator

The spectrum $\sigma(T)\subseteq\mathbb{C}$ is always compact and non-empty, contained in the disk of radius $\|T\|$. It decomposes into the point spectrum (eigenvalues), continuous spectrum, and residual spectrum. The spectral radius formula $r(T)=\lim\|T^n\|^{1/n}$ holds.

5.2 - Spectral Theorem

For compact self-adjoint operators the spectral theorem takes a concrete form: real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors forming a basis, and the representation $T=\sum\lambda_n\langle\cdot,e_n\rangle e_n$. For general self-adjoint operators, the theorem is expressed via a projection-valued measure and the integral $T=\int\lambda\,dP(\lambda)$.
