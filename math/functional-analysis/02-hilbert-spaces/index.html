Chapter 2: Hilbert Spaces

Hilbert spaces add geometry - via an inner product - to the structure of a Banach space. The inner product furnishes orthogonality, projections, and an elegant theory of bases, making Hilbert spaces the most tractable class of infinite-dimensional spaces and the natural setting for quantum mechanics and Fourier analysis.

Chapter Overview

2.1 - Inner Products and Hilbert Spaces

We introduce inner products, derive the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, show the parallelogram law characterises inner product spaces, and prove the Riesz representation theorem: every bounded linear functional on a Hilbert space is of the form $\phi(\cdot)=\langle\cdot,y\rangle$ for a unique $y$.

2.2 - Orthonormal Bases

Orthonormal systems satisfy $\langle e_i,e_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$. Bessel's inequality bounds the sum of squared Fourier coefficients; an orthonormal basis (Hilbert basis) achieves equality - Parseval's identity. Every separable Hilbert space is isomorphic to $\ell^2$.

2.3 - Projection Theorem

Every closed convex set has a unique nearest point (projection). For closed subspaces, the projection is linear, bounded with $\|P\|\le1$, self-adjoint ($P^*=P$), and idempotent ($P^2=P$), and gives the orthogonal decomposition $H=M\oplus M^\perp$.
