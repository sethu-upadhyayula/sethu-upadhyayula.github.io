2.1 Inner Products and Hilbert Spaces

An inner product is a bilinear (or sesquilinear) form that encodes angles and orthogonality. It determines a norm — via $\|x\|=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}$ — and thus a topology. A Hilbert space is an inner product space that is complete with respect to this norm.

Inner Products

Definition 2.1.1 — Inner Product

An inner product on a vector space $H$ over $\mathbb{F}$ is a map $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle:H\times H\to\mathbb{F}$ satisfying for all $x,y,z\in H$ and $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}$:

  • Positive definiteness: $\langle x,x\rangle\ge0$ and $\langle x,x\rangle=0\iff x=0$.
  • Conjugate symmetry: $\langle x,y\rangle=\overline{\langle y,x\rangle}$.
  • Sesquilinearity: $\langle\alpha x+y,z\rangle=\alpha\langle x,z\rangle+\langle y,z\rangle$.

Setting $\|x\|=\sqrt{\langle x,x\rangle}$ gives a norm. A Hilbert space is an inner product space that is complete under this norm.

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

Theorem 2.1.2 — Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

For all $u,v\in H$: $$|\langle u,v\rangle| \le \|u\|\|v\|,$$ with equality if and only if $u$ and $v$ are linearly dependent.

Proof

If $v=0$ the result is trivial. Otherwise, for any $t\in\mathbb{F}$: $$0 \le \|u - tv\|^2 = \|u\|^2 - t\langle u,v\rangle - \bar t\langle v,u\rangle + |t|^2\|v\|^2.$$ Setting $t=\langle u,v\rangle/\|v\|^2$ gives $0\le\|u\|^2-|\langle u,v\rangle|^2/\|v\|^2$.

Rearranging gives the inequality. Equality holds iff $\|u-tv\|^2=0$, i.e.\ $u=tv$. $\square$

Parallelogram Law

Theorem 2.1.3 — Parallelogram Law

In any inner product space: $$\|x+y\|^2 + \|x-y\|^2 = 2(\|x\|^2+\|y\|^2).$$

Conversely (Jordan-von Neumann), a normed space satisfying the parallelogram law for all $x,y$ is an inner product space, with inner product recovered by the polarisation identity $\langle x,y\rangle=\frac{1}{4}(\|x+y\|^2-\|x-y\|^2)$ (over $\mathbb{R}$). This characterises which Banach spaces are Hilbert spaces: $L^p$ is a Hilbert space only for $p=2$.

Riesz Representation Theorem

Theorem 2.1.4 — Riesz Representation Theorem

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space and $\phi\in H^*$ a bounded linear functional. Then there exists a unique $y\in H$ such that $$\phi(x) = \langle x,y\rangle \quad \text{for all } x\in H.$$ Moreover $\|\phi\|=\|y\|$. The map $y\mapsto\langle\cdot,y\rangle$ is a conjugate-linear isometric isomorphism $H\to H^*$.

Proof Sketch

If $\phi=0$, take $y=0$. Otherwise $M=\ker\phi$ is a closed proper subspace, so $M^\perp\ne\{0\}$. Pick $z\in M^\perp$ with $\phi(z)=1$. For any $x\in H$, set $w=x-\phi(x)z$; then $w\in M$, so $\langle w,z\rangle=0$, giving $\langle x,z\rangle=\phi(x)\|z\|^2$. Set $y=z/\|z\|^2$; then $\phi(x)=\langle x,y\rangle$.

Uniqueness: if $\langle x,y_1\rangle=\langle x,y_2\rangle$ for all $x$, take $x=y_1-y_2$ to get $y_1=y_2$. The norm equality $\|\phi\|=\|y\|$ follows from Cauchy-Schwarz and the fact that $\phi(y/\|y\|)=\|y\|$. $\square$

Remark — Self-Duality

The Riesz theorem says $H\cong H^*$ (conjugate-linearly and isometrically), so Hilbert spaces are "self-dual." This is a very special property not shared by general Banach spaces. It is what makes the spectral theory of self-adjoint operators on Hilbert spaces so clean.
