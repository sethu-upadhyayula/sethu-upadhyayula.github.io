4.2 Weak and Weak* Topologies

The norm topology on an infinite-dimensional Banach space is often "too strong" - bounded sequences need not have convergent subsequences. The weak and weak* topologies are coarser topologies that allow more convergence, at the cost of losing some metric structure. The payoff is compactness results that are indispensable in analysis.

The Weak Topology

Definition 4.2.1 - Weak Topology on $X$

Let $X$ be a normed space. The weak topology on $X$ is the coarsest topology making all functionals $\phi \in X^*$ continuous. A net (or sequence) $(x_\alpha)$ in $X$ converges weakly to $x$, written $x_\alpha \rightharpoonup x$, if

$$\phi(x_\alpha) \to \phi(x) \quad \text{for every } \phi \in X^*.$$

Equivalently, the weak topology is generated by the sets $\phi^{-1}(U)$ for $\phi \in X^*$ and open $U \subseteq \mathbb{K}$.

Proposition 4.2.2 - Properties of Weak Convergence
  1. Strong convergence ($\|x_n - x\| \to 0$) implies weak convergence, but not conversely in infinite dimensions.
  2. A weakly convergent sequence is norm-bounded: if $x_n \rightharpoonup x$ then $\sup_n \|x_n\| < \infty$ (by the uniform boundedness principle).
  3. Weak limits are unique.
  4. In finite dimensions, weak and strong convergence coincide.
Example 4.2.3 - Weak but Not Strong Convergence

In $\ell^2$, let $e_n$ be the standard basis vectors. Then $e_n \rightharpoonup 0$ weakly: for any $y \in \ell^2$, $\langle e_n, y \rangle = y_n \to 0$ since $y \in \ell^2$ implies $y_n \to 0$. But $\|e_n\| = 1 \not\to 0 = \|0\|$, so $e_n$ does not converge strongly.

Similarly, in $L^2[0,1]$, the sequence $\sin(n\pi t)$ converges weakly to $0$ (by Riemann-Lebesgue) but not strongly (its norm is $1/\sqrt{2}$ throughout).

The Weak* Topology

Definition 4.2.4 - Weak* Topology on $X^*$

Let $X$ be a normed space with canonical embedding $J: X \to X^{**}$. The weak* topology on $X^*$ is the coarsest topology making all evaluation functionals $J(x): X^* \to \mathbb{K}$ continuous (for each $x \in X$). A net $(\phi_\alpha)$ in $X^*$ converges weak* to $\phi$, written $\phi_\alpha \xrightarrow{w^*} \phi$, if

$$\phi_\alpha(x) \to \phi(x) \quad \text{for every } x \in X.$$

The weak* topology is coarser than the weak topology on $X^*$ (which requires convergence against all elements of $X^{**}$), and strictly coarser when $X$ is not reflexive.

Banach-Alaoglu Theorem

Theorem 4.2.5 - Banach-Alaoglu

Let $X$ be a normed space. The closed unit ball

$$B_{X^*} = \{\phi \in X^* : \|\phi\| \le 1\}$$

is compact in the weak* topology on $X^*$.

Proof Sketch

Embed $X^*$ into $\prod_{x \in X} \overline{D}(0, \|x\|)$ (a product of closed disks) via $\phi \mapsto (\phi(x))_{x \in X}$. By Tychonoff's theorem, this product is compact. The image of $B_{X^*}$ is closed in this product (the conditions $\phi(\alpha x + y) = \alpha\phi(x) + \phi(y)$ are closed conditions), hence compact. The subspace topology on the image agrees with the weak* topology on $B_{X^*}$. $\square$

Corollary 4.2.6 - Sequential Version for Separable Spaces

If $X$ is separable, then $B_{X^*}$ is sequentially compact in the weak* topology: every bounded sequence in $X^*$ has a weak* convergent subsequence. This follows because the weak* topology on $B_{X^*}$ is metrizable when $X$ is separable.

Remark - Significance

The Banach-Alaoglu theorem is used throughout analysis. In PDE theory, one shows solutions exist by extracting weak* convergent subsequences from bounded approximating sequences. In optimization, it provides compactness in function spaces needed for existence of minimizers.
