4.3 Reflexivity

A Banach space is reflexive if it "looks the same from both sides of the duality pairing" — the natural embedding into the bidual is surjective. Reflexivity is a rich structural property with important geometric and analytic consequences, including weak compactness of the unit ball.

Definition and Characterization

Definition 4.3.1 — Reflexive Banach Space

A Banach space $X$ is reflexive if the canonical embedding $J: X \to X^{**}$ is surjective (hence an isometric isomorphism). Equivalently, every element of $X^{**}$ is of the form $\phi \mapsto \phi(x)$ for some $x \in X$.

Note: it is not sufficient that $X \cong X^{**}$ via some isomorphism; reflexivity specifically requires $J$ to be surjective.

Theorem 4.3.2 — Kakutani's Theorem

A Banach space $X$ is reflexive if and only if the closed unit ball $B_X = \{x \in X : \|x\| \le 1\}$ is weakly compact (compact in the weak topology).

Proof Sketch

($\Rightarrow$) If $X$ is reflexive, then $J(B_X) = B_{X^{**}}$. By the Banach-Alaoglu theorem, $B_{X^{**}}$ is weak* compact in $X^{**}$. Under the isomorphism $J$, the weak* topology on $X^{**}$ pulls back to the weak topology on $X$. Hence $B_X$ is weakly compact.

($\Leftarrow$) If $B_X$ is weakly compact, one shows that $J(B_X)$ is weak* dense in $B_{X^{**}}$ (by Goldstine's theorem) and weakly compact, hence weak* closed. Thus $J(B_X) = B_{X^{**}}$, giving surjectivity of $J$. $\square$

Examples

Example 4.3.3 — Reflexive Spaces
  • Hilbert spaces: Every Hilbert space $H$ is reflexive. By the Riesz representation theorem, $H^* \cong H$ and $H^{**} \cong H^* \cong H$. The canonical embedding is surjective.
  • $L^p$ spaces ($1 < p < \infty$): For a $\sigma$-finite measure space, $L^p(\mu)$ is reflexive. The dual chain is $(L^p)^* \cong L^q$ and $(L^p)^{**} \cong (L^q)^* \cong L^p$.
  • $\ell^p$ ($1 < p < \infty$): Reflexive, for the same reason.
  • Finite-dimensional spaces: All finite-dimensional normed spaces are reflexive.
Example 4.3.4 — Non-Reflexive Spaces
  • $L^1(\mu)$: Not reflexive (for infinite measure spaces). We have $(L^1)^* \cong L^\infty$ but $(L^\infty)^*$ is strictly larger than $L^1$ — it contains functionals corresponding to finitely additive measures that are not $\sigma$-additive. Hence $J(L^1) \subsetneq (L^1)^{**}$.
  • $L^\infty(\mu)$: Not reflexive (for non-atomic infinite measure spaces), as $(L^\infty)^* \not\cong L^\infty$ in general.
  • $c_0$ (sequences converging to 0): Not reflexive. $(c_0)^* \cong \ell^1$ and $(\ell^1)^* \cong \ell^\infty \not\cong c_0$.
  • $C[0,1]$: Not reflexive. Its dual is the space of regular signed Borel measures, which is much larger.

Consequences of Reflexivity

Proposition 4.3.5 — Weak Sequential Compactness

If $X$ is a reflexive Banach space, then every bounded sequence in $X$ has a weakly convergent subsequence. This is the Eberlein-Smulian theorem restricted to sequences. It is extremely useful in variational methods: one extracts weakly convergent subsequences from minimizing sequences.

Proposition 4.3.6 — Closed Subspaces of Reflexive Spaces

Every closed subspace of a reflexive Banach space is reflexive. Every quotient of a reflexive Banach space by a closed subspace is reflexive.

Remark — James's Theorem

A deep theorem of R.C. James (1964) gives an intrinsic characterization: a Banach space $X$ is reflexive if and only if every continuous linear functional on $X$ attains its supremum on the closed unit ball. This connects reflexivity to optimization and geometry of Banach spaces.
