4.1 The Dual of a Normed Space

The dual space $X^*$ consists of all bounded linear functionals on $X$. It is itself a Banach space (even if $X$ is only a normed space), and identifying $X^*$ concretely for classical spaces is one of the central results of functional analysis.

Definition and Basic Properties

Definition 4.1.1 — Dual Space

Let $X$ be a normed space over $\mathbb{K}$ (where $\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$). The dual space (or conjugate space) of $X$ is

$$X^* = B(X, \mathbb{K}) = \{\phi: X \to \mathbb{K} : \phi \text{ is linear and bounded}\},$$

equipped with the operator norm $\|\phi\| = \sup_{\|x\| \le 1} |\phi(x)|$. Elements of $X^*$ are called bounded linear functionals or continuous linear functionals.

Proposition 4.1.2 — $X^*$ Is Always Banach

For any normed space $X$, the dual space $X^*$ is a Banach space. This holds even if $X$ itself is not complete, because $\mathbb{K}$ is complete and the pointwise limit of a Cauchy sequence of functionals is again a bounded linear functional.

Duals of Classical Spaces

Theorem 4.1.3 — Dual of $\ell^p$ for $1 \le p < \infty$

Let $1 < p < \infty$ with conjugate exponent $q$ satisfying $\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$. Then $(\ell^p)^* \cong \ell^q$ isometrically: every $\phi \in (\ell^p)^*$ has the form

$$\phi(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty y_n x_n$$

for a unique $y = (y_n) \in \ell^q$, and $\|\phi\| = \|y\|_q$. For $p = 1$: $(\ell^1)^* \cong \ell^\infty$ via the same formula.

The isometric isomorphism $\ell^q \to (\ell^p)^*$ is given by $y \mapsto \phi_y$ where $\phi_y(x) = \sum y_n x_n$. Boundedness follows from Hölder's inequality; surjectivity follows by evaluating $\phi$ on basis vectors $e_n$.

Theorem 4.1.4 — Dual of $L^p(\mu)$ for $1 \le p < \infty$

Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ be a $\sigma$-finite measure space. For $1 < p < \infty$ with $\frac{1}{p}+\frac{1}{q}=1$:

$$(L^p(\mu))^* \cong L^q(\mu)$$

isometrically, via $g \in L^q \mapsto \phi_g \in (L^p)^*$ where $\phi_g(f) = \int f\, g\, d\mu$. For $p = 1$: $(L^1(\mu))^* \cong L^\infty(\mu)$.

Note: $(L^\infty)^*$ is strictly larger than $L^1$ — it includes finitely additive measures that are not $\sigma$-additive. This asymmetry foreshadows the failure of reflexivity for $L^1$ and $L^\infty$.

Theorem 4.1.5 — Dual of $C[0,1]$ (Riesz Representation)

Every bounded linear functional $\phi$ on $C[0,1]$ has the form

$$\phi(f) = \int_0^1 f\, d\mu$$

for a unique regular signed Borel measure $\mu$ on $[0,1]$, and $\|\phi\| = |\mu|([0,1])$ (the total variation). Thus $C[0,1]^* \cong M[0,1]$, the space of regular signed Borel measures.

The Canonical Embedding

Definition 4.1.6 — Canonical Embedding into $X^{**}$

The bidual $X^{**} = (X^*)^*$ is the dual of the dual. Define the canonical embedding $J: X \to X^{**}$ by

$$J(x)(\phi) = \phi(x) \quad \text{for all } \phi \in X^*, x \in X.$$

That is, $J(x)$ is the evaluation functional at $x$: it sends a functional $\phi$ to the number $\phi(x)$.

Theorem 4.1.7 — $J$ Is an Isometric Embedding

The canonical embedding $J: X \to X^{**}$ is a linear isometry: $\|J(x)\|_{X^{**}} = \|x\|_X$ for all $x \in X$.

Proof: Clearly $|J(x)(\phi)| = |\phi(x)| \le \|\phi\|\|x\|$ so $\|J(x)\| \le \|x\|$. By the Hahn–Banach theorem (Corollary 3.1.3), there exists $\phi \in X^*$ with $\|\phi\| = 1$ and $\phi(x) = \|x\|$. Then $J(x)(\phi) = \phi(x) = \|x\|$, giving $\|J(x)\| \ge \|x\|$. $\square$

Remark — Reflexivity

Although $J$ is always an isometric embedding, it need not be surjective: in general $X$ only embeds isometrically into $X^{**}$ but does not equal it. When $J$ is surjective (an isometric isomorphism), $X$ is called reflexive. This is studied in detail in Section 4.3.
