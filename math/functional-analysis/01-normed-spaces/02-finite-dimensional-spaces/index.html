1.2 Finite-Dimensional Spaces

Finite-dimensional normed spaces are essentially rigid: they are all isomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ and share compactness properties foreign to the infinite-dimensional setting. The contrast sharpens our understanding of what makes infinite-dimensional analysis genuinely harder.

Isomorphism with $\mathbb{R}^n$

Theorem 1.2.1 — Classification of Finite-Dimensional Spaces

Every $n$-dimensional normed space over $\mathbb{R}$ is isomorphic (as a normed space) to $(\mathbb{R}^n,\|\cdot\|_2)$. Consequently, any two $n$-dimensional normed spaces are isomorphic to each other.

In particular, every finite-dimensional normed space is automatically a Banach space (complete), since completeness is preserved under isomorphism and $\mathbb{R}^n$ is complete.

Theorem 1.2.2 — Finite-Dimensional Subspaces Are Closed

Every finite-dimensional subspace $Y$ of a normed space $X$ is closed. Proof: pick a basis $\{y_1,\ldots,y_n\}$ for $Y$; the coordinate map $\mathbb{R}^n\to Y$ is an isomorphism, and since $Y$ is a finite-dimensional Banach space it is complete, hence closed in $X$.

Riesz's Lemma

Theorem 1.2.3 — Riesz's Lemma

Let $Y$ be a closed proper subspace of a normed space $X$ and let $0<\varepsilon<1$. Then there exists $x\in X$ with $\|x\|=1$ and $$d(x,Y) = \inf_{y\in Y}\|x-y\| \ge 1-\varepsilon.$$

Informally: there are unit vectors that are "almost orthogonal" to $Y$ (at distance nearly 1 from $Y$), even in the absence of an inner product.

Proof

Pick any $z\in X\setminus Y$. Let $\delta=d(z,Y)>0$ (positive since $Y$ is closed). For $\varepsilon>0$, choose $y_0\in Y$ with $\|z-y_0\|<\delta/(1-\varepsilon)$. Set $x=(z-y_0)/\|z-y_0\|$, so $\|x\|=1$.

For any $y\in Y$: $\|x-y\|=\|z-y_0-\|z-y_0\|y\|/\|z-y_0\|\ge\delta/\|z-y_0\|>(1-\varepsilon)$. $\square$

Compactness and Dimension

Theorem 1.2.4 — Compactness Characterises Finite Dimension

Let $X$ be a normed space. The following are equivalent:

  1. $\dim X < \infty$.
  2. The closed unit ball $\overline{B}(0,1)$ is compact.
  3. Every bounded sequence in $X$ has a convergent subsequence.

Remark — Contrast with Hilbert Spaces

In an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, the unit ball is weakly compact (Alaoglu) but not norm-compact. This is why compactness arguments in functional analysis usually involve weak or weak* topologies, or restrict to compact operators.
