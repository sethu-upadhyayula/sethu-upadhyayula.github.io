Chapter 1: Normed Spaces

Normed spaces are the natural setting for analysis in infinite dimensions. By equipping a vector space with a norm — a notion of length — we gain a metric, topology, and the vocabulary needed to talk about convergence, continuity, and boundedness for operators. The central objects of study are Banach spaces: complete normed spaces in which every Cauchy sequence converges.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Norms and Banach Spaces

We define norms and Banach spaces, establish key examples ($\mathbb{R}^n$, $\ell^p$, $L^p(\mu)$, $C[a,b]$), prove that all norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent via compactness of the unit sphere, and distinguish the sequence spaces $\ell^1$, $\ell^2$, $\ell^\infty$ as distinct Banach spaces.

1.2 — Finite-Dimensional Spaces

Every finite-dimensional normed space is isomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$. We prove that finite-dimensional subspaces are always closed and establish Riesz's lemma, leading to the fundamental characterisation: the closed unit ball is compact if and only if the space is finite-dimensional.

1.3 — Bounded Linear Operators

A linear operator is bounded if and only if it is continuous (equivalently, continuous at zero). We define the operator norm, show that $\mathcal{B}(X,Y)$ is Banach when $Y$ is Banach, and examine concrete examples including the differentiation operator and multiplication operators.
