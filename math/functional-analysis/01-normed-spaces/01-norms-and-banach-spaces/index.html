1.1 Norms and Banach Spaces

A norm on a vector space turns abstract algebraic structure into a metric geometry: we can measure lengths, define convergence, and study continuity. Banach spaces are the complete normed spaces — completeness ensures that Cauchy sequences converge, a prerequisite for most analytic arguments.

Norms

Definition 1.1.1 — Norm

A norm on a vector space $X$ over $\mathbb{F}$ ($\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$) is a function $\|\cdot\|:X\to[0,\infty)$ satisfying for all $x,y\in X$ and $\alpha\in\mathbb{F}$:

  • Positive definiteness: $\|x\|\ge0$, and $\|x\|=0\iff x=0$.
  • Homogeneity: $\|\alpha x\|=|\alpha|\|x\|$.
  • Triangle inequality: $\|x+y\|\le\|x\|+\|y\|$.

A vector space equipped with a norm is called a normed space. Every normed space is a metric space with $d(x,y)=\|x-y\|$.

Definition 1.1.2 — Banach Space

A normed space $(X,\|\cdot\|)$ is a Banach space if it is complete: every Cauchy sequence converges. That is, if $\|x_n-x_m\|\to0$ as $n,m\to\infty$, then there exists $x\in X$ with $\|x_n-x\|\to0$.

Key Examples

Example 1.1.3 — Euclidean and $\ell^p$ Norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$

On $\mathbb{R}^n$, for $1\le p<\infty$: $$\|x\|_p = \left(\sum_{k=1}^n |x_k|^p\right)^{1/p}, \qquad \|x\|_\infty = \max_{1\le k\le n}|x_k|.$$ All these norms make $\mathbb{R}^n$ a Banach space. By the equivalence theorem (below), they are all equivalent — sequences converge in one iff they converge in any other.

The $p=2$ case gives the familiar Euclidean norm arising from the standard inner product.

Example 1.1.4 — Sequence Spaces $\ell^p$

For $1\le p<\infty$, define $$\ell^p = \left\{(x_n)_{n\ge1} : \sum_{n=1}^\infty |x_n|^p < \infty\right\}, \quad \|x\|_p = \left(\sum_{n=1}^\infty|x_n|^p\right)^{1/p}.$$ Also $\ell^\infty = \{(x_n) : \sup_n|x_n|<\infty\}$ with $\|x\|_\infty=\sup_n|x_n|$.

Each $\ell^p$ ($1\le p\le\infty$) is a Banach space. Minkowski's inequality ensures the triangle inequality; completeness follows from a standard argument using dominated convergence or direct estimation. Note $\ell^1\subsetneq\ell^2\subsetneq\ell^\infty$ as sets, but they are distinct Banach spaces (non-isomorphic for $p\ne q$).

Example 1.1.5 — $L^p(\mu)$ Spaces

Let $(X,\mathcal{M},\mu)$ be a measure space. For $1\le p<\infty$: $$L^p(\mu) = \left\{f \text{ measurable} : \int|f|^p\,d\mu < \infty\right\}, \quad \|f\|_p = \left(\int|f|^p\,d\mu\right)^{1/p}.$$ Elements are equivalence classes (identify functions equal $\mu$-a.e.). $L^\infty(\mu)$ consists of essentially bounded functions with the essential supremum norm.

By the Riesz–Fischer theorem, each $L^p(\mu)$ ($1\le p\le\infty$) is a Banach space. Completeness is the key non-trivial fact.

Example 1.1.6 — $C[a,b]$ with the Sup Norm

The space of continuous functions $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ with norm $\|f\|_\infty=\sup_{t\in[a,b]}|f(t)|$ is a Banach space. Completeness follows because a uniform Cauchy sequence of continuous functions converges uniformly to a continuous limit.

Equivalence of Norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$

Theorem 1.1.7 — Equivalence of Norms on Finite-Dimensional Spaces

Any two norms $\|\cdot\|$ and $\|\cdot\|'$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent: there exist constants $c,C>0$ such that $$c\|x\|\le\|x\|'\le C\|x\| \quad \text{for all }x\in\mathbb{R}^n.$$

Consequently, convergence, continuity, and completeness are norm-independent on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Proof Sketch

It suffices to show every norm $\|\cdot\|'$ is equivalent to the Euclidean norm $\|\cdot\|_2$. Write $x=\sum x_k e_k$; by homogeneity and triangle inequality, $\|x\|'\le C\|x\|_2$ where $C=(\sum\|e_k\|'^2)^{1/2}$ (by Cauchy–Schwarz). This shows $\|\cdot\|'$ is continuous with respect to $\|\cdot\|_2$.

The unit sphere $S=\{x:\|x\|_2=1\}$ is compact in $(\mathbb{R}^n,\|\cdot\|_2)$. Since $\|\cdot\|'>0$ on $S$ and $\|\cdot\|'$ is continuous, it attains its minimum $c>0$ on $S$. Thus $\|x\|'\ge c\|x\|_2$ for all $x$. $\square$

Remark — Infinite Dimensions

Equivalence of all norms fails in infinite dimensions. On $C[0,1]$, the sup norm and the $L^1$ norm both make sense, but $\|\cdot\|_1\le\|\cdot\|_\infty$ while there is no constant $C$ with $\|f\|_\infty\le C\|f\|_1$ for all $f\in C[0,1]$. As a consequence, the spaces $\ell^1$, $\ell^2$, $\ell^\infty$ are genuinely different as Banach spaces.
