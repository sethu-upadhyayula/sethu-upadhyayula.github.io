6.1 Compact Operators

Compact operators occupy a special position between finite-rank operators and all bounded operators. They exhibit finite-dimensional behavior in many respects - their spectra are discrete, they satisfy a strong form of the spectral theorem - and arise naturally in the study of integral equations and PDE.

Definition and Basic Properties

Definition 6.1.1 - Compact Operator

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces. A bounded linear operator $T: X \to Y$ is compact if it maps bounded sets to precompact sets (sets with compact closure), i.e., for every bounded set $B \subseteq X$, the closure $\overline{T(B)}$ is compact in $Y$.

Equivalently, $T$ is compact if every bounded sequence $(x_n)$ in $X$ has a subsequence $(x_{n_k})$ such that $(Tx_{n_k})$ converges in $Y$.

Proposition 6.1.2 - Properties of Compact Operators
  1. Every finite-rank operator (image is finite-dimensional) is compact.
  2. The set $K(X,Y)$ of compact operators from $X$ to $Y$ is a closed subspace of $B(X,Y)$ (in operator norm).
  3. If $T: X \to Y$ is compact and $S: Y \to Z$ is bounded, then $ST$ is compact. Similarly $TS$ is compact if $S: Z \to X$ is bounded. (Compact operators form an ideal.)
  4. $T$ is compact iff $T^*$ is compact (Schauder's theorem).
Theorem 6.1.3 - Compact Operators as Limits of Finite-Rank Operators

In a Hilbert space $H$, a bounded operator $T: H \to H$ is compact if and only if it is the operator-norm limit of a sequence of finite-rank operators.

This holds for operators on $\ell^p$ and $L^p$ spaces as well. In general Banach spaces, limits of finite-rank operators are called approximable; the question whether all compact operators are approximable (the approximation problem) was open for decades and resolved negatively by Enflo in 1973.

Spectrum of Compact Operators

Theorem 6.1.4 - Riesz-Schauder Theorem

Let $T: X \to X$ be a compact operator on a Banach space $X$. Then:

  1. $0 \in \sigma(T)$ (if $X$ is infinite-dimensional).
  2. Every nonzero $\lambda \in \sigma(T)$ is an eigenvalue: $\sigma(T) \setminus \{0\} = \sigma_p(T) \setminus \{0\}$.
  3. Each nonzero eigenvalue has finite-dimensional eigenspace.
  4. The nonzero eigenvalues form a sequence (possibly finite or empty) with $|\lambda_n| \to 0$.

Hilbert-Schmidt Operators

Definition 6.1.5 - Hilbert-Schmidt Operator

Let $H$ be a separable Hilbert space with orthonormal basis $(e_n)$. A bounded operator $T: H \to H$ is Hilbert-Schmidt if

$$\|T\|_{HS}^2 = \sum_{n} \|Te_n\|^2 < \infty.$$

This definition is independent of the choice of orthonormal basis. Every Hilbert-Schmidt operator is compact (it is the limit in Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the truncations $T_N x = \sum_{n=1}^N \langle x, e_n\rangle Te_n$, which are finite-rank).

Example 6.1.6 - Integral Operators

Let $K \in L^2([0,1] \times [0,1])$ and define the integral operator

$$(Tf)(x) = \int_0^1 K(x,y) f(y)\, dy, \quad f \in L^2[0,1].$$

Then $T: L^2[0,1] \to L^2[0,1]$ is a Hilbert-Schmidt operator with $\|T\|_{HS} = \|K\|_{L^2}$, hence compact. This is the prototype example of a compact operator, and the theory of compact operators was originally developed to study such operators.

The kernel $K(x,y)$ is called the integral kernel. By Theorem 6.1.4, the nonzero eigenvalues of $T$ form a sequence $(\lambda_n)$ with $\lambda_n \to 0$, each of finite multiplicity - an infinite-dimensional analogue of the eigenvalue decomposition of a matrix.

Theorem 6.1.7 - Compact Self-Adjoint Operators Have Orthonormal Eigenbasis

Let $T: H \to H$ be compact and self-adjoint on a separable Hilbert space $H$. Then $H$ has an orthonormal basis $(e_n)$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$:

$$Te_n = \lambda_n e_n, \quad Tx = \sum_n \lambda_n \langle x, e_n\rangle e_n \quad \text{for all } x \in H,$$

where $(\lambda_n)$ are real, $|\lambda_n| \to 0$, and the sum converges in $H$. This is the spectral theorem (Theorem 5.2.1) specialized to compact self-adjoint operators.
