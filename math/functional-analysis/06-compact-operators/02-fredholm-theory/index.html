6.2 Fredholm Theory

Fredholm theory studies operators of the form $I - K$ where $K$ is compact, and more generally Fredholm operators — operators whose kernel and cokernel are both finite-dimensional. The Fredholm alternative gives a complete picture of the solvability of equations $(I - K)x = y$, directly generalizing the finite-dimensional Fredholm alternative for matrices.

Fredholm Operators

Definition 6.2.1 — Fredholm Operator

A bounded linear operator $T: X \to Y$ between Banach spaces is a Fredholm operator if:

  • $\ker T$ is finite-dimensional,
  • $\operatorname{ran}(T)$ is closed, and
  • $\operatorname{coker}(T) = Y / \operatorname{ran}(T)$ is finite-dimensional.

The Fredholm index of $T$ is $\operatorname{ind}(T) = \dim\ker T - \dim\operatorname{coker}(T)$.

Remark — Index

The Fredholm index is an integer (possibly negative) and is the key invariant. Surjective operators have $\operatorname{coker}(T) = 0$; injective operators have $\ker T = 0$. Bijections have index $0$.

For finite-dimensional operators ($\dim X = \dim Y < \infty$), every bounded operator is Fredholm of index $0$ by the rank-nullity theorem. Fredholm theory extends this to infinite dimensions for operators "close to invertible."

Theorem 6.2.2 — Atkinson's Theorem

A bounded operator $T: X \to Y$ is Fredholm if and only if it is invertible modulo compact operators: there exist bounded operators $S_1, S_2: Y \to X$ (called parametrices or approximate inverses) and compact operators $K_1: X \to X$, $K_2: Y \to Y$ such that

$$S_1 T = I_X + K_1, \qquad T S_2 = I_Y + K_2.$$

In other words, $T$ is invertible in the Calkin algebra $B(X)/K(X)$.

Proposition 6.2.3 — Stability of Index

The Fredholm index is stable under compact perturbations: if $T$ is Fredholm and $K$ is compact, then $T + K$ is Fredholm with $\operatorname{ind}(T + K) = \operatorname{ind}(T)$. Moreover, the index is locally constant on the set of Fredholm operators (with operator norm topology).

The Fredholm Alternative

Theorem 6.2.4 — Fredholm Alternative

Let $X$ be a Banach space and $K: X \to X$ a compact operator. For any $y \in X$, exactly one of the following holds:

  1. Unique solvability: The equation $(I - K)x = y$ has a unique solution $x \in X$.
  2. Homogeneous solutions: The equation $(I - K)x = 0$ has a nonzero solution.

Moreover, in case (2): the homogeneous equation $(I - K)x = 0$ and its adjoint $(I - K^*)x^* = 0$ have the same finite number of linearly independent solutions, and $(I - K)x = y$ is solvable if and only if $y$ is orthogonal to all solutions of the adjoint homogeneous equation.

In other words: $I - K$ has Fredholm index $0$.

Proof Sketch

Since $K$ is compact, $I - K$ is a Fredholm operator of index $0$. (One shows $I - K$ has closed range using the Riesz lemma, and that $\dim\ker(I-K) = \dim\ker(I-K^*)$ by a duality argument.) The Fredholm alternative then follows from $\operatorname{ind}(I-K) = 0$: injectivity and surjectivity are equivalent for operators of index $0$. $\square$

Application to Integral Equations

Example 6.2.5 — Fredholm Integral Equations

A Fredholm integral equation of the second kind is

$$f(x) - \int_0^1 K(x,y) f(y)\, dy = g(x), \quad x \in [0,1],$$

where $K \in L^2([0,1]^2)$ (the kernel) and $g \in L^2[0,1]$ are given, and $f$ is the unknown.

Writing $T_K f(x) = \int_0^1 K(x,y)f(y)\,dy$, the equation is $(I - T_K)f = g$. Since $T_K$ is a compact operator on $L^2[0,1]$ (Hilbert-Schmidt), the Fredholm alternative applies:

  • Either the equation has a unique solution $f$ for every $g \in L^2[0,1]$,
  • Or the homogeneous equation $f = T_K f$ has a nonzero solution, and then $(I - T_K)f = g$ is solvable iff $g \perp \ker(I - T_K^*)$.

This is a complete and concrete solvability criterion for a large class of integral equations in mathematical physics.

Example 6.2.6 — Elliptic PDE

Consider $-\Delta u + \lambda u = f$ on a bounded domain $\Omega$ with Dirichlet boundary conditions. Writing this as $(I + \lambda(-\Delta)^{-1})(-\Delta u) = f$ (formally), the operator $(-\Delta)^{-1}$ is compact on $L^2(\Omega)$ (by Sobolev embedding). The Fredholm alternative gives:

  • If $\lambda$ is not an eigenvalue of $-\Delta$, the equation has a unique solution for every $f$.
  • If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue, the equation is solvable iff $f$ is orthogonal to the corresponding eigenspace.
