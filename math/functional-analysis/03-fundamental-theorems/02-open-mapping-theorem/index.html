3.2 Open Mapping Theorem

The open mapping theorem is one of the three cornerstones of Banach space theory (alongside the closed graph theorem and the uniform boundedness principle). It gives a powerful automatic continuity result: any surjective bounded linear operator between Banach spaces must be an open map, and in particular any bounded bijection has a bounded inverse.

The Theorem

Theorem 3.2.1 — Open Mapping Theorem

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces, and let $T: X \to Y$ be a surjective bounded linear operator. Then $T$ is an open map: for every open set $U \subseteq X$, the image $T(U)$ is open in $Y$.

Equivalently, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $T(B_X(0,1)) \supseteq B_Y(0,\delta)$.

Proof Sketch

The proof proceeds in two steps.

Step 1 (Baire category). Since $T$ is surjective, $Y = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty T(\overline{B_X(0,n)})$. By the Baire category theorem (Banach spaces are complete, hence of second category), some $\overline{T(B_X(0,n))}$ has nonempty interior. Scaling, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\overline{T(B_X(0,1))} \supseteq B_Y(0,2\delta)$.

Step 2 (Completion). We show $T(B_X(0,1)) \supseteq B_Y(0,\delta)$. Given $y_0 \in B_Y(0,\delta)$, we inductively construct $x_n \in B_X(0, 2^{-n+1})$ such that

$$\left\|y_0 - T\!\left(\sum_{k=1}^n x_k\right)\right\| < 2^{-n}\delta.$$

At each step: since $\overline{T(B_X(0,2^{-n}))} \supseteq B_Y(0, 2^{-n}\delta)$, we can find $x_{n+1}$ with $\|x_{n+1}\| < 2^{-n}$ and $\|r_{n+1}\| < 2^{-n-1}\delta$ where $r_n$ is the residual. The partial sums $\sum x_k$ converge in $X$ (absolute convergence in a Banach space) to some $x$ with $\|x\| < 2$ and $Tx = y_0$. Hence $y_0 \in T(B_X(0,2))$, and rescaling gives the result. $\square$

Remark — Role of Completeness

Both $X$ and $Y$ must be complete. For example, the identity map from $(C[0,1], \|\cdot\|_1)$ to $(C[0,1], \|\cdot\|_\infty)$ is bounded (injective, surjective) but not open, because $C[0,1]$ under the $L^1$ norm is not complete.

The Bounded Inverse Theorem

Corollary 3.2.2 — Bounded Inverse Theorem

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces. If $T: X \to Y$ is a bounded linear bijection, then $T^{-1}: Y \to X$ is also bounded.

In other words, a bounded linear bijection between Banach spaces is automatically a topological isomorphism (homeomorphism of normed spaces).

Proof

Since $T$ is bijective and surjective, by the open mapping theorem $T$ is open. Thus $T^{-1}$ is continuous (the preimage of every open set under $T^{-1}$ is the $T$-image of that set, which is open). $\square$

Example — Why Surjectivity Is Necessary

Let $X = Y = \ell^2$ and define $T: \ell^2 \to \ell^2$ by $T(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots) = (0, x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots)$ (the right shift). Then $T$ is bounded (indeed isometric, $\|T\| = 1$) and injective, but not surjective: the vector $e_1 = (1,0,0,\ldots)$ is not in the range.

The image $T(\ell^2)$ is the closed subspace of sequences with first coordinate $0$, which is a proper closed subspace of $\ell^2$. So $T(B(0,1))$ is the unit ball of this subspace, which does not contain any ball $B(0,\varepsilon)$ in $\ell^2$. Hence $T$ is not open.

This shows that surjectivity cannot be dropped from the hypotheses of the open mapping theorem.

Application: Equivalent Norms

Proposition 3.2.3 — Automatic Equivalence of Norms

Suppose $(X, \|\cdot\|_1)$ and $(X, \|\cdot\|_2)$ are both Banach spaces on the same vector space $X$, and suppose $\|\cdot\|_2 \le C\|\cdot\|_1$ for some constant $C > 0$ (i.e., the identity $\mathrm{id}: (X,\|\cdot\|_1) \to (X,\|\cdot\|_2)$ is bounded). Then the two norms are equivalent:

$$\frac{1}{C'}\|x\|_1 \le \|x\|_2 \le C\|x\|_1 \quad \text{for all } x \in X.$$

In other words, there is no way to have two genuinely different Banach norms on the same space where one dominates the other.

Proof

The identity map $\mathrm{id}: (X,\|\cdot\|_1) \to (X,\|\cdot\|_2)$ is a bounded bijection between Banach spaces, so by the bounded inverse theorem its inverse is also bounded, giving the lower bound. $\square$
