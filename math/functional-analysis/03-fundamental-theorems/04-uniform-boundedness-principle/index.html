3.4 Uniform Boundedness Principle

The uniform boundedness principle (also called the Banach-Steinhaus theorem) is the third of the three fundamental theorems of functional analysis. It says that pointwise boundedness of a family of bounded operators on a Banach space forces uniform boundedness of the family — a striking "something for nothing" result that relies crucially on completeness.

The Theorem

Theorem 3.4.1 — Banach-Steinhaus / Uniform Boundedness Principle

Let $X$ be a Banach space, $Y$ a normed space, and $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ a family of bounded linear operators $T_\alpha: X \to Y$. If the family is pointwise bounded, i.e.,

$$\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha x\| < \infty \quad \text{for every } x \in X,$$

then the family is uniformly bounded:

$$\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha\| < \infty.$$
Proof via Baire Category

For each $n \ge 1$, define the closed set

$$E_n = \{x \in X : \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| \le n\} = \bigcap_{\alpha \in A} \{x : \|T_\alpha x\| \le n\}.$$

Each $E_n$ is closed (as an intersection of closed sets, since each $T_\alpha$ is continuous). By the pointwise boundedness assumption, $X = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n$.

Since $X$ is a Banach space (hence complete and of second category by the Baire category theorem), some $E_N$ has nonempty interior. Thus there exist $x_0 \in X$ and $r > 0$ such that $B(x_0, r) \subseteq E_N$, meaning $\|T_\alpha x\| \le N$ for all $\|x - x_0\| \le r$ and all $\alpha$.

Now for any $x$ with $\|x\| \le 1$ and any $\alpha$:

$$\|T_\alpha x\| = \frac{1}{r}\|T_\alpha(rx)\| = \frac{1}{r}\|T_\alpha((rx + x_0) - x_0)\| \le \frac{1}{r}(\|T_\alpha(rx+x_0)\| + \|T_\alpha x_0\|) \le \frac{2N}{r}.$$

Hence $\|T_\alpha\| \le 2N/r$ for all $\alpha$. $\square$

Corollary 3.4.2 — Pointwise Convergent Sequences of Operators

Let $X$ be a Banach space, $Y$ a normed space, and $(T_n)$ a sequence of bounded operators from $X$ to $Y$. If $T_n x$ converges in $Y$ for every $x \in X$, then:

  1. $\sup_n \|T_n\| < \infty$,
  2. The limit $Tx = \lim_{n\to\infty} T_n x$ defines a bounded linear operator $T: X \to Y$, and
  3. $\|T\| \le \liminf_{n\to\infty} \|T_n\|$.

Application: Divergence of Fourier Series

One of the most striking applications of the uniform boundedness principle is showing that there exist continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges at a point.

Example 3.4.3 — Divergent Fourier Series

Let $X = C[-\pi, \pi]$ with the sup norm, and for each $N \ge 1$ define the $N$-th partial sum operator

$$S_N f(x) = \sum_{n=-N}^{N} \hat{f}(n) e^{inx}, \quad \text{where } \hat{f}(n) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t) e^{-int}\,dt.$$

Evaluating at $x = 0$: $S_N f(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t) D_N(t)\,dt$, where $D_N(t) = \sum_{n=-N}^{N} e^{int} = \frac{\sin((N+\frac12)t)}{\sin(t/2)}$ is the Dirichlet kernel.

The operator norm $\|S_N\|_{(C, \mathbb{R})} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}|D_N(t)|\,dt = \|D_N\|_{L^1}$. One can show

$$\|D_N\|_{L^1} = \frac{4}{\pi^2}\log N + O(1) \to \infty.$$

By the uniform boundedness principle (contrapositive): if $S_N f(0)$ were bounded for every $f \in C[-\pi,\pi]$, the operators $S_N$ would be uniformly bounded in norm, contradiction. Hence there exists $f \in C[-\pi,\pi]$ such that $\sup_N |S_N f(0)| = \infty$, i.e., the Fourier series of $f$ diverges at $0$.

By a translation argument, there even exist continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges on a dense set of points.

Remark — Necessity of Completeness

The theorem fails without completeness. On a non-complete normed space one can construct a pointwise bounded but not uniformly bounded family. Indeed, the Baire category theorem is the engine of the proof, and it requires completeness.
