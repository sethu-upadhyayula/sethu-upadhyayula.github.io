3.3 Closed Graph Theorem

The closed graph theorem provides a powerful criterion for boundedness of linear operators: instead of directly estimating $\|Tx\|$, one need only check that the graph of $T$ is a closed subset of the product space. This is often easier to verify in practice, particularly for differential operators.

The Graph of an Operator

Definition 3.3.1 — Graph of a Linear Operator

Let $T: X \to Y$ be a linear operator between normed spaces. The graph of $T$ is the subset of $X \times Y$ defined by

$$\Gamma(T) = \{(x, Tx) : x \in X\} \subseteq X \times Y.$$

The product space $X \times Y$ is a normed space under $\|(x,y)\| = \|x\|_X + \|y\|_Y$ (or any equivalent product norm). We say $T$ has a closed graph if $\Gamma(T)$ is closed in $X \times Y$.

Proposition 3.3.2 — Sequential Characterization

A linear operator $T: X \to Y$ has closed graph if and only if: whenever $x_n \to x$ in $X$ and $Tx_n \to y$ in $Y$, it follows that $y = Tx$. In other words, $T$ cannot be "tricked" by sequences that converge both in domain and range to different limits.

Remark — Closed vs. Continuous

Every continuous (bounded) linear operator has closed graph: if $x_n \to x$ then $Tx_n \to Tx$ by continuity, so the limit must be $Tx$. The closed graph theorem says that for operators between Banach spaces, the converse holds too.

For incomplete spaces, the converse can fail. The classic example is the derivative operator $T: C^1[0,1] \to C[0,1]$, $Tf = f'$, under the $\sup$ norm on both spaces: $T$ has closed graph but is unbounded (the domain $C^1[0,1]$ is not complete under $\|\cdot\|_\infty$).

The Theorem

Theorem 3.3.3 — Closed Graph Theorem

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces, and let $T: X \to Y$ be a linear operator. Then $T$ is bounded if and only if its graph $\Gamma(T)$ is closed in $X \times Y$.

Proof

We already noted ($\Rightarrow$): bounded implies closed graph.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\Gamma(T)$ is closed in $X \times Y$. Since $X$ and $Y$ are Banach spaces, $X \times Y$ is also a Banach space. The subspace $\Gamma(T)$ is closed in a Banach space, hence itself a Banach space.

Define the projection maps $\pi_X: \Gamma(T) \to X$ by $\pi_X(x, Tx) = x$ and $\pi_Y: \Gamma(T) \to Y$ by $\pi_Y(x, Tx) = Tx$. Both are bounded (in fact, $\|\pi_X\| \le 1$ and $\|\pi_Y\| \le 1$ under the product norm $\|(x,y)\| = \|x\| + \|y\|$). Moreover, $\pi_X$ is a bounded bijection from the Banach space $\Gamma(T)$ onto the Banach space $X$.

By the bounded inverse theorem (Corollary 3.2.2), $\pi_X^{-1}: X \to \Gamma(T)$ is bounded. Now

$$T = \pi_Y \circ \pi_X^{-1}: X \to \Gamma(T) \to Y$$

is a composition of bounded operators, hence bounded. $\square$

Applications

Example 3.3.4 — Checking Boundedness via Sequences

Consider the multiplication operator $M_\phi: L^2[0,1] \to L^2[0,1]$ defined by $(M_\phi f)(x) = \phi(x) f(x)$ for a measurable function $\phi$. To check boundedness, suppose $f_n \to f$ in $L^2$ and $M_\phi f_n \to g$ in $L^2$. We need $g = \phi f$ a.e.

Passing to a subsequence, $f_{n_k} \to f$ a.e. and $\phi f_{n_k} \to g$ a.e. So $\phi(x) f(x) = g(x)$ a.e. The graph is closed. By the closed graph theorem, $M_\phi$ is bounded if and only if the graph is closed — and here we've verified closure, so boundedness follows provided $\phi \in L^\infty[0,1]$.

This argument is cleaner than directly estimating $\|M_\phi f\|_{L^2}$, though in this case both approaches work.

Example 3.3.5 — Symmetric Operators on Hilbert Spaces

Let $H$ be a Hilbert space and $T: H \to H$ a linear operator. If $T$ is everywhere defined and symmetric (i.e., $\langle Tx, y \rangle = \langle x, Ty \rangle$ for all $x, y$), then $T$ is bounded.

Proof sketch: Suppose $x_n \to x$ and $Tx_n \to y$. For any $z \in H$: $\langle y, z \rangle = \lim \langle Tx_n, z \rangle = \lim \langle x_n, Tz \rangle = \langle x, Tz \rangle = \langle Tx, z \rangle$. Since $z$ is arbitrary, $y = Tx$. So $T$ has closed graph, and by the closed graph theorem, $T$ is bounded.
