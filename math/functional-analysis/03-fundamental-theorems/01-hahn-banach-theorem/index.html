3.1 Hahn–Banach Theorem

The Hahn–Banach theorem guarantees that bounded linear functionals on subspaces can be extended to the whole space without increasing their norm. Its consequences — that $X^*$ is large enough to separate points and that $\|x\|$ can be computed from $X^*$ — are fundamental.

The Extension Theorem

Theorem 3.1.1 — Hahn–Banach (Real Version)

Let $X$ be a real vector space, $p:X\to\mathbb{R}$ a sublinear functional ($p(x+y)\le p(x)+p(y)$ and $p(tx)=tp(x)$ for $t\ge0$), and $Y\subseteq X$ a subspace. If $f:Y\to\mathbb{R}$ is linear with $f(y)\le p(y)$ for all $y\in Y$, then $f$ extends to a linear $F:X\to\mathbb{R}$ with $F(x)\le p(x)$ for all $x\in X$.

Theorem 3.1.2 — Hahn–Banach for Normed Spaces

Let $Y$ be a subspace of a normed space $X$ and $f\in Y^*$. There exists $F\in X^*$ with $F|_Y=f$ and $\|F\|=\|f\|$.

In particular: for any $x_0\ne0$ there exists $F\in X^*$ with $F(x_0)=\|x_0\|$ and $\|F\|=1$.

Proof

Apply the real version with $p(x)=\|f\|\|x\|$. Then $f(y)\le|f(y)|\le\|f\|\|y\|=p(y)$, so $f$ extends to $F:X\to\mathbb{R}$ with $F(x)\le p(x)=\|f\|\|x\|$. Replacing $x$ by $-x$ gives $|F(x)|\le\|f\|\|x\|$, so $\|F\|\le\|f\|$. Since $F|_Y=f$, $\|F\|\ge\|f\|$.

For the complex case: extend $\operatorname{Re}(f)$ and recover $F(x)=\operatorname{Re}(F(x))-i\operatorname{Re}(F(ix))$. $\square$

Consequences

Corollary 3.1.3 — $X^*$ Separates Points

For any $x\ne y$ in a normed space $X$, there exists $\phi\in X^*$ with $\phi(x)\ne\phi(y)$. Moreover: $$\|x\| = \sup\{|\phi(x)|:\phi\in X^*,\,\|\phi\|\le1\}.$$

Thus the norm of $x$ is determined by how $X^*$ evaluates $x$.

Remark — Role of Axiom of Choice

The proof of Hahn–Banach uses Zorn's lemma (a form of the Axiom of Choice): one considers the poset of all extensions dominated by $p$ and ordered by extension, and applies Zorn to obtain a maximal extension, which one then shows must be defined on all of $X$. Without Choice, the theorem can fail for general spaces.
