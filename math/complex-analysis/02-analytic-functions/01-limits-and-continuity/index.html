2.1 Limits and Continuity

Limits and continuity for complex functions mirror their real counterparts in definition, but the richness of $\mathbb{C}$ as a two-dimensional space means that a limit must be independent of the direction of approach — a constraint with far-reaching consequences for analytic function theory.

Limits of Complex Functions

Definition 2.1.1 — Limit

Let $f : D \to \mathbb{C}$ where $D \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ is open, and let $z_0$ be a limit point of $D$. We say $$\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = w_0$$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$0 < |z - z_0| < \delta \implies |f(z) - w_0| < \varepsilon.$$

Crucially, the limit must be the same regardless of how $z$ approaches $z_0$ — along any curve, spiral, or sequence converging to $z_0$.

Remark — Direction Independence

In $\mathbb{R}$, only two directions of approach exist (left and right). In $\mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2$, there are uncountably many directions. A limit $\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = w_0$ requires agreement along all paths. This makes complex limits far more restrictive than real limits, and underlies the exceptional rigidity of analytic functions.

Theorem 2.1.2 — Component-wise Characterisation

Write $f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)$ and $w_0 = a + ib$ where $z = x + iy$. Then $$\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = w_0$$ if and only if $$\lim_{(x,y) \to (x_0,y_0)} u(x,y) = a \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{(x,y) \to (x_0,y_0)} v(x,y) = b,$$ where both limits are the usual two-variable real limits.

Proof. The equivalence follows from $|f(z) - w_0|^2 = (u - a)^2 + (v - b)^2$ and the fact that $|u - a| \leq |f - w_0|$ and $|v - b| \leq |f - w_0| \leq |u - a| + |v - b|$. $\square$

Algebra of Limits

Theorem 2.1.3 — Limit Algebra

Suppose $\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = w_1$ and $\lim_{z \to z_0} g(z) = w_2$. Then:

  1. $\lim_{z \to z_0}(f(z) \pm g(z)) = w_1 \pm w_2$.
  2. $\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z)g(z) = w_1 w_2$.
  3. $\lim_{z \to z_0} \dfrac{f(z)}{g(z)} = \dfrac{w_1}{w_2}$, provided $w_2 \neq 0$.
  4. If $h$ is continuous at $w_1$, then $\lim_{z \to z_0} h(f(z)) = h(w_1)$ (composition rule).

Continuity

Definition 2.1.4 — Continuity

A function $f : D \to \mathbb{C}$ is continuous at $z_0 \in D$ if $$\lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = f(z_0).$$ Equivalently, for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ with $|z - z_0| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(z) - f(z_0)| < \varepsilon$. The function is continuous on $D$ if it is continuous at every point of $D$.

Proposition 2.1.5 — Component-wise Continuity

$f = u + iv$ is continuous at $z_0 = (x_0, y_0)$ if and only if both $u$ and $v$ are continuous at $(x_0, y_0)$ as real-valued functions of two variables.

Proposition 2.1.6 — Continuity Preserved Under Operations

Sums, differences, products, and (where the denominator is nonzero) quotients of continuous functions are continuous. Compositions of continuous functions are continuous. In particular, every polynomial $p(z) = a_n z^n + \cdots + a_0$ is continuous on all of $\mathbb{C}$, and every rational function is continuous on its domain.

Theorem 2.1.7 — Extreme Value Theorem

If $f : K \to \mathbb{C}$ is continuous and $K \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ is compact (closed and bounded), then $|f|$ attains its maximum and minimum on $K$. In particular, $f$ is bounded on $K$.

A Worked Example

Example 2.1.8 — Computing $\lim_{z \to i}(z^2 + 1)/(z - i)$

Note that $z^2 + 1 = (z - i)(z + i)$, so for $z \neq i$: $$\frac{z^2 + 1}{z - i} = \frac{(z-i)(z+i)}{z - i} = z + i.$$ Therefore $$\lim_{z \to i} \frac{z^2 + 1}{z - i} = \lim_{z \to i}(z + i) = i + i = 2i.$$

The apparent singularity at $z = i$ is removable: setting $f(i) = 2i$ makes $f$ continuous at $i$.

Example 2.1.9 — A Limit That Does Not Exist

Consider $f(z) = \bar{z}/z$ for $z \neq 0$. Along the real axis ($y = 0$, $z = x$): $f(x) = x/x = 1$. Along the imaginary axis ($z = iy$): $f(iy) = -iy/iy = -1$. Since the limits along these two paths differ, $\lim_{z \to 0} \bar{z}/z$ does not exist.

This illustrates why the direction-independence requirement is non-trivial in $\mathbb{C}$.
