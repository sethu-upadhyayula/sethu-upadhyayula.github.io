2.2 Differentiability and the Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Complex differentiability imposes a far stronger constraint than real differentiability. The requirement that the derivative limit be direction-independent forces the real and imaginary parts of $f$ to satisfy a coupled system of partial differential equations - the Cauchy-Riemann equations - which are the central object of study in this section.

Complex Differentiability

Definition 2.2.1 - Complex Derivative

Let $f : D \to \mathbb{C}$ with $D$ open. The complex derivative of $f$ at $z_0 \in D$ is $$f'(z_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h}, \quad h \in \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\},$$ provided this limit exists (independently of the manner in which $h \to 0$ in $\mathbb{C}$).

If $f'(z_0)$ exists, we say $f$ is complex differentiable (or holomorphic) at $z_0$.

Remark - Direction Independence is Essential

In one real variable, $h \to 0$ can only occur from left or right. In $\mathbb{C}$, $h \to 0$ along any ray $h = te^{i\theta}$ ($t \to 0^+$), any spiral, any curve. The limit $f'(z_0)$ must agree for all these approaches. This is why complex differentiability is so much stronger than real differentiability in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Theorem 2.2.2 - Necessary Condition (Cauchy-Riemann)

Let $f = u + iv$ be complex differentiable at $z_0 = x_0 + iy_0$. Then the partial derivatives $u_x, u_y, v_x, v_y$ all exist at $(x_0, y_0)$ and satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations: $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}, \qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}.$$

Proof sketch. Approach along the real axis ($h = \Delta x$): the difference quotient gives $f'(z_0) = u_x + iv_x$. Approach along the imaginary axis ($h = i\Delta y$): the quotient gives $f'(z_0) = v_y - iu_y$. Equating real and imaginary parts yields CR. $\square$

Theorem 2.2.3 - Sufficient Condition

Let $f = u + iv$ on an open set $D$. Suppose at $z_0 \in D$:

  1. The partial derivatives $u_x, u_y, v_x, v_y$ exist.
  2. These partials are continuous at $(x_0, y_0)$.
  3. The Cauchy-Riemann equations hold at $(x_0, y_0)$.
Then $f$ is complex differentiable at $z_0$, and $$f'(z_0) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x_0, y_0) + i\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}(x_0, y_0).$$

Proof. Under these hypotheses $u$ and $v$ are real-differentiable at $(x_0,y_0)$. Writing $h = \Delta x + i\Delta y$ and expanding $f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)$ using the total differentials of $u$ and $v$, one verifies that the remainder is $o(|h|)$, giving complex differentiability. $\square$

Remark - Equivalent Forms of CR

The Cauchy-Riemann equations can be written compactly as $\partial f / \partial \bar{z} = 0$, where $\partial/\partial \bar{z} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial/\partial x + i\,\partial/\partial y)$. A smooth function is holomorphic iff it has no dependence on $\bar{z}$ in this sense.

Analytic (Holomorphic) Functions

Definition 2.2.4 - Analytic / Holomorphic Function

A function $f$ is analytic (or holomorphic) on an open set $D$ if it is complex differentiable at every point of $D$. A function analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$ is called entire.

Example 2.2.5 - $f(z) = z^2$ is Entire

Write $f(z) = (x^2 - y^2) + i(2xy)$, so $u = x^2 - y^2$ and $v = 2xy$. Then: $$u_x = 2x = v_y, \qquad u_y = -2y = -v_x.$$ CR holds everywhere, with continuous partials. Hence $f$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C}$ with $f'(z) = u_x + iv_x = 2x + 2iy = 2z$. This matches the formal power rule.

Example 2.2.6 - $f(z) = \bar{z}$ is Nowhere Analytic

Here $f(x+iy) = x - iy$, so $u = x$, $v = -y$. Then $u_x = 1$ but $v_y = -1$. The first CR equation $u_x = v_y$ fails everywhere.

Alternatively: $\dfrac{f(z_0+h)-f(z_0)}{h} = \dfrac{\overline{h}}{h}$, which equals $1$ if $h$ is real and $-1$ if $h$ is purely imaginary. The limit does not exist at any point.

Proposition 2.2.7 - Derivative Formula

If $f = u + iv$ is holomorphic at $z_0$, the derivative can be computed as any of $$f'(z_0) = u_x + iv_x = v_y - iu_y = u_x - iu_y = v_y + iv_x,$$ where all partial derivatives are evaluated at $(x_0, y_0)$.
