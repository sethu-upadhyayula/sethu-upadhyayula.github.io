1.3 Complex Exponential and Euler's Formula

The complex exponential function is the cornerstone of complex analysis. Defined by extending the real Taylor series $e^x = \sum x^n/n!$ to complex arguments, it unifies exponential and trigonometric functions through Euler's formula and provides the polar form of complex numbers.

The Complex Exponential

Definition 1.3.1 - Complex Exponential

For $z = x + iy \in \mathbb{C}$, define $$e^z = e^x(\cos y + i \sin y).$$ Equivalently, via the absolutely convergent power series: $$e^z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^n}{n!}, \qquad z \in \mathbb{C}.$$

Theorem 1.3.2 - Euler's Formula

For any $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, $$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta.$$ This follows immediately from the definition with $x = 0$, $y = \theta$. Taking $\theta = \pi$ gives Euler's identity: $$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0.$$

Remark - Modulus of $e^{i\theta}$

For real $\theta$, $|e^{i\theta}|^2 = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$, so $|e^{i\theta}| = 1$. Every $e^{i\theta}$ lies on the unit circle $|z| = 1$. More generally, $|e^z| = e^{\mathrm{Re}(z)}$.

De Moivre's Theorem and Roots of Unity

Theorem 1.3.3 - De Moivre's Theorem

For $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, $$(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta).$$ Proof. By Euler's formula, $(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = (e^{i\theta})^n = e^{in\theta} = \cos(n\theta) + i\sin(n\theta)$. $\square$

Definition 1.3.4 - $n$-th Roots of Unity

The $n$ distinct $n$-th roots of unity are the solutions to $z^n = 1$: $$\omega_k = e^{2\pi i k/n} = \cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right) + i\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi k}{n}\right), \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1.$$ They form the vertices of a regular $n$-gon inscribed in the unit circle.

More generally, the $n$-th roots of any $w = r e^{i\theta}$ are $r^{1/n} e^{i(\theta + 2\pi k)/n}$ for $k = 0,\ldots,n-1$.

Complex Trigonometric and Hyperbolic Functions

Definition 1.3.5 - Complex Sine and Cosine

For $z \in \mathbb{C}$, define $$\cos z = \frac{e^{iz} + e^{-iz}}{2}, \qquad \sin z = \frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i}.$$ These reduce to the real trigonometric functions when $z$ is real, and satisfy $\cos^2 z + \sin^2 z = 1$ for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$.

Definition 1.3.6 - Complex Hyperbolic Functions

For $z \in \mathbb{C}$, define $$\cosh z = \frac{e^z + e^{-z}}{2}, \qquad \sinh z = \frac{e^z - e^{-z}}{2}.$$ The relationship between trigonometric and hyperbolic functions is: $$\sin(iz) = i\sinh z, \qquad \cos(iz) = \cosh z.$$

Proposition 1.3.7 - Periodicity

The complex exponential is periodic with period $2\pi i$: $$e^{z + 2\pi i} = e^z \quad \text{for all } z \in \mathbb{C}.$$ Consequently, $\cos z$ and $\sin z$ are periodic with period $2\pi$, and the exponential map $e^z : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}$ is many-to-one: $e^{z_1} = e^{z_2}$ iff $z_1 - z_2 \in 2\pi i \mathbb{Z}$.

Example 1.3.8 - Computing $e^{1+i\pi/4}$

With $z = 1 + i\pi/4$: $$e^{1+i\pi/4} = e^1 \cdot e^{i\pi/4} = e\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = e \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}(1 + i) = \frac{e\sqrt{2}}{2}(1+i).$$

Example 1.3.9 - Cube Roots of $-8$

Write $-8 = 8e^{i\pi}$. The three cube roots are $$8^{1/3} e^{i(\pi + 2\pi k)/3} = 2e^{i(\pi/3 + 2\pi k/3)}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2.$$

Explicitly: $2e^{i\pi/3} = 1 + i\sqrt{3}$, $\;2e^{i\pi} = -2$, $\;2e^{i5\pi/3} = 1 - i\sqrt{3}$.
