Chapter 1: Complex Numbers

The complex number system $\mathbb{C}$ extends $\mathbb{R}$ to a field where every polynomial has a root. This chapter establishes the algebraic and geometric foundations: the field structure and conjugate, the Argand plane, polar form and de Moivre's theorem, and the complex exponential.

A central insight is that $\mathbb{C}$ is not merely $\mathbb{R}^2$ equipped with coordinatewise operations — the multiplication rule $(a+bi)(c+di)=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i$ gives $\mathbb{C}$ the structure of a field, and Euler's formula $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$ ties this algebra to rotation in the plane.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — The Complex Field

We define $\mathbb{C}=\{a+bi:a,b\in\mathbb{R}\}$ with $i^2=-1$ and verify the field axioms. Every nonzero $z$ has inverse $z^{-1}=\bar{z}/|z|^2$. The modulus $|z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ satisfies the triangle inequality, and the conjugate $\bar{z}=a-bi$ satisfies $z\bar{z}=|z|^2$.

1.2 — Geometry of Complex Numbers

We identify $\mathbb{C}$ with the Argand plane, introduce polar form $z=re^{i\theta}$, and prove de Moivre's theorem $(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^n=\cos n\theta+i\sin n\theta$. The $n$-th roots of unity $e^{2\pi ik/n}$ form a regular $n$-gon.

1.3 — Complex Exponential and Euler's Formula

We define $e^z=e^a(\cos b+i\sin b)$ for $z=a+bi$, consistent with the Taylor series. Euler's formula $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$ and the identity $e^{i\pi}+1=0$ follow immediately. The exponential is periodic with period $2\pi i$.
