1.1 The Complex Field

We define the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ as the set of all expressions $a+bi$ where $a,b\in\mathbb{R}$ and $i$ is a formal symbol satisfying $i^2=-1$. Equipped with the natural addition and multiplication rules, $\mathbb{C}$ forms a field — and in fact the algebraically closed field extending $\mathbb{R}$.

Definition 1.1.1 — The Complex Numbers

The set of complex numbers is $$\mathbb{C} = \{a + bi : a, b \in \mathbb{R}\}$$ where $i$ satisfies $i^2 = -1$. For $z = a+bi$, the real part is $\operatorname{Re}(z) = a$ and the imaginary part is $\operatorname{Im}(z) = b$.

Addition: $(a+bi)+(c+di) = (a+c)+(b+d)i$.
Multiplication: $(a+bi)(c+di) = (ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i$.

Theorem 1.1.2 — $\mathbb{C}$ is a Field

Under the above operations, $\mathbb{C}$ is a field. The additive identity is $0=0+0i$, the multiplicative identity is $1=1+0i$, and every nonzero $z=a+bi$ has multiplicative inverse $$z^{-1} = \frac{a-bi}{a^2+b^2} = \frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^2}.$$

Complex Conjugate and Modulus

Definition 1.1.3 — Conjugate and Modulus

The complex conjugate of $z=a+bi$ is $\bar{z}=a-bi$. The modulus (absolute value) is $$|z| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2} = \sqrt{z\bar{z}}.$$

Real and imaginary parts recover as: $$\operatorname{Re}(z) = \frac{z+\bar{z}}{2}, \qquad \operatorname{Im}(z) = \frac{z-\bar{z}}{2i}.$$

Theorem 1.1.4 — Properties of Conjugate and Modulus

For all $z, w \in \mathbb{C}$:

  • $\overline{z+w} = \bar{z}+\bar{w}$ and $\overline{zw} = \bar{z}\bar{w}$.
  • $z\bar{z} = |z|^2$; in particular $|z|=0 \iff z=0$.
  • $|zw| = |z||w|$ (multiplicativity of modulus).
  • Triangle inequality: $|z+w| \le |z|+|w|$.
  • Reverse triangle inequality: $\bigl||z|-|w|\bigr| \le |z-w|$.
Proof of Triangle Inequality

We use $\operatorname{Re}(z\bar{w}) \le |z\bar{w}| = |z||w|$. Then: $$|z+w|^2 = (z+w)\overline{(z+w)} = |z|^2 + z\bar{w} + \bar{z}w + |w|^2 = |z|^2 + 2\operatorname{Re}(z\bar{w}) + |w|^2$$ $$\le |z|^2 + 2|z||w| + |w|^2 = (|z|+|w|)^2.$$

Taking square roots gives $|z+w| \le |z|+|w|$. $\square$

Division

Example 1.1.5 — Computing $z/w$

To compute $\dfrac{2+3i}{1-i}$, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator: $$\frac{2+3i}{1-i} \cdot \frac{1+i}{1+i} = \frac{(2+3i)(1+i)}{1+1} = \frac{2+2i+3i+3i^2}{2} = \frac{2+5i-3}{2} = \frac{-1+5i}{2}.$$

So $\operatorname{Re} = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $\operatorname{Im} = \tfrac{5}{2}$.

Remark — $\mathbb{C}$ vs $\mathbb{R}^2$

As a set, $\mathbb{C}$ is in bijection with $\mathbb{R}^2$, but $\mathbb{C}$ carries multiplicative structure that $\mathbb{R}^2$ (as a vector space) does not. The complex product encodes rotation and scaling, which is what makes $\mathbb{C}$ the natural setting for two-dimensional analytic geometry.
