1.2 Geometry of Complex Numbers

Every complex number $z=a+bi$ corresponds to the point $(a,b)$ in the Argand plane (or complex plane), with the real axis horizontal and imaginary axis vertical. This identification transforms algebraic operations on $\mathbb{C}$ into geometric operations on the plane.

Polar Form

Definition 1.2.1 - Polar Form and Argument

A nonzero complex number $z$ can be written in polar form: $$z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = re^{i\theta}$$ where $r = |z| > 0$ and $\theta$ is the argument $\arg z$ (angle from positive real axis, defined modulo $2\pi$).

The principal argument $\operatorname{Arg}(z) \in (-\pi, \pi]$ is the unique argument in that range.

Theorem 1.2.2 - Multiplication in Polar Form

If $z_1 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1}$ and $z_2 = r_2 e^{i\theta_2}$, then $$z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2\, e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}.$$ Multiplication multiplies moduli and adds arguments. Geometrically, multiplication by $z_2$ scales by $r_2$ and rotates by $\theta_2$.

De Moivre's Theorem

Theorem 1.2.3 - De Moivre's Theorem

For any $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$ and $n \in \mathbb{Z}$: $$(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^n = \cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta,$$ equivalently $(re^{i\theta})^n = r^n e^{in\theta}$.

Example 1.2.4 - Double Angle via De Moivre

Taking $n=2$: $(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)^2 = \cos2\theta+i\sin2\theta$. Expanding the left side: $$\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta + 2i\sin\theta\cos\theta = \cos2\theta + i\sin2\theta.$$

Equating real and imaginary parts recovers the double-angle formulas $\cos2\theta=\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta$ and $\sin2\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta$.

Roots of Unity and $n$-th Roots

Definition 1.2.5 - $n$-th Roots of Unity

The $n$-th roots of unity are the $n$ solutions to $z^n=1$: $$\omega_k = e^{2\pi ik/n}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1.$$ They form a regular $n$-gon inscribed in the unit circle, with $\omega_0 = 1$.

Theorem 1.2.6 - $n$-th Roots of a Complex Number

Given $z_0 = r_0 e^{i\theta_0} \neq 0$, the $n$ solutions to $z^n = z_0$ are $$z_k = r_0^{1/n}\, e^{i(\theta_0 + 2\pi k)/n}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1.$$

They are equally spaced around a circle of radius $r_0^{1/n}$, forming a regular $n$-gon.

Example 1.2.7 - Cube Roots of $-8$

Write $-8 = 8e^{i\pi}$. The three cube roots are: $$z_k = 2\,e^{i(\pi + 2\pi k)/3}, \quad k=0,1,2.$$ So $z_0 = 2e^{i\pi/3} = 1+i\sqrt{3}$, $z_1 = 2e^{i\pi} = -2$, $z_2 = 2e^{i5\pi/3} = 1-i\sqrt{3}$.

These three points form an equilateral triangle in $\mathbb{C}$.

Remark - Non-uniqueness of Argument

The argument $\arg z$ is only defined modulo $2\pi$, so $\arg(z_1 z_2) = \arg z_1 + \arg z_2 + 2\pi k$ for some integer $k$. The principal argument $\operatorname{Arg}$ is single-valued but discontinuous on the negative real axis - this is the first hint of the branch-cut phenomenon studied in Chapter 3.
