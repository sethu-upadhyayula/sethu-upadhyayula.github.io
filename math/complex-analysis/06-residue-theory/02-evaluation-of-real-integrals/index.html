6.2 Evaluation of Real Integrals

One of the most striking applications of residue theory is the evaluation of real definite integrals that resist all elementary techniques. The strategy is always the same: embed the real integral into a contour integral in $\mathbb{C}$, compute the contour integral by residues, and extract the real part.

Type 1: Rational Functions on $(-\infty, \infty)$

Theorem 6.2.1 — Semicircular Contour

Let $f(z) = P(z)/Q(z)$ be rational with $\deg Q \ge \deg P + 2$ and $Q$ having no real zeros. Let $\Gamma_R$ be the upper semicircle of radius $R$. Then $\int_{\Gamma_R} f(z)\,dz \to 0$ as $R\to\infty$, and $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\,dx = 2\pi i \sum_{\operatorname{Im}(z_j) > 0} \operatorname{Res}(f, z_j).$$

That is, the integral equals $2\pi i$ times the sum of residues of $f$ at poles in the upper half-plane.

Example 6.2.2 — Classic Integral $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx}{1+x^2}$

The function $f(z) = 1/(1+z^2) = 1/((z-i)(z+i))$ has poles at $z = \pm i$. Only $z = i$ lies in the upper half-plane. By the quotient residue formula: $$\operatorname{Res}(f, i) = \frac{1}{2i}.$$ Therefore: $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx}{1+x^2} = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{1}{2i} = \pi.$$

Example 6.2.3 — $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx}{(1+x^2)^2}$

$f(z) = 1/(1+z^2)^2$ has a pole of order $2$ at $z = i$. Compute: $$\operatorname{Res}(f, i) = \lim_{z\to i}\frac{d}{dz}\bigl[(z-i)^2 f(z)\bigr] = \lim_{z\to i}\frac{d}{dz}\frac{1}{(z+i)^2} = \lim_{z\to i}\frac{-2}{(z+i)^3} = \frac{-2}{(2i)^3} = \frac{-2}{-8i} = \frac{1}{4i}.$$

Therefore $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{dx}{(1+x^2)^2} = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{1}{4i} = \frac{\pi}{2}$.

Type 2: Trigonometric Integrals

Theorem 6.2.4 — Unit Circle Substitution

To evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi} R(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)\,d\theta$, set $z = e^{i\theta}$, so $d\theta = dz/(iz)$ and $$\cos\theta = \frac{z + z^{-1}}{2}, \qquad \sin\theta = \frac{z - z^{-1}}{2i}.$$ The integral becomes a contour integral over the unit circle $|z|=1$, evaluated by residues at poles inside $|z|=1$.

Example 6.2.5 — $\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\theta}{2 + \cos\theta}$

After substitution: $$\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\theta}{2+\cos\theta} = \oint_{|z|=1} \frac{1}{2 + \frac{z+z^{-1}}{2}}\cdot\frac{dz}{iz} = \oint_{|z|=1}\frac{2\,dz}{iz(z^2+4z+1)}.$$ The roots of $z^2+4z+1=0$ are $z = -2 \pm \sqrt{3}$. Only $z_0 = -2+\sqrt{3}$ lies inside $|z|=1$. Computing the residue: $$\operatorname{Res}\!\left(\frac{2}{iz(z^2+4z+1)}, z_0\right) = \frac{2}{iz_0 \cdot (2z_0+4)} = \frac{2}{iz_0 \cdot 2(z_0+2)} = \frac{1}{iz_0(z_0+2)}.$$ After simplification, the integral equals $\dfrac{2\pi}{\sqrt{3}}$.

Type 3: Fourier-Type Integrals

Theorem 6.2.6 — Jordan's Lemma

If $|f(z)| \to 0$ uniformly on the upper semicircle $|z| = R$ as $R \to \infty$, then for $a > 0$: $$\int_{\Gamma_R} f(z) e^{iaz}\,dz \to 0 \quad \text{as } R\to\infty.$$ This extends the semicircular contour method to integrals $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) e^{iax}\,dx$ where $f$ decays only like $1/|x|$ (rather than $1/|x|^2$).

Example 6.2.7 — $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{ix}}{1+x^2}\,dx$

Apply Jordan's lemma with $a=1$ and $f(z)=1/(1+z^2)$. The only pole in the upper half-plane is $z=i$: $$\operatorname{Res}\!\left(\frac{e^{iz}}{1+z^2}, i\right) = \frac{e^{i \cdot i}}{2i} = \frac{e^{-1}}{2i}.$$ Therefore: $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{ix}}{1+x^2}\,dx = 2\pi i \cdot \frac{e^{-1}}{2i} = \frac{\pi}{e}.$$

Taking real parts: $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\cos x}{1+x^2}\,dx = \frac{\pi}{e}$. Taking imaginary parts: $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\sin x}{1+x^2}\,dx = 0$ (odd integrand).

Remark — Other Contours

Other standard contours include: keyhole contours for integrals involving $x^\alpha$ ($0 < \alpha < 1$); rectangular contours for integrals involving $e^{ax}/(1+e^{bx})$; indented contours when $f$ has a singularity on the real axis. Each contour is designed so that all but one piece of the boundary contributes to the integral of interest.
