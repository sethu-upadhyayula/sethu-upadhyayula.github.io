Chapter 6: Residue Theory

The residue theorem is one of the most powerful computational tools in all of analysis. It converts a contour integral around a region to a finite sum of residues - one for each isolated singularity enclosed. This single theorem subsumes Cauchy's theorem (no singularities) and Cauchy's integral formula (simple poles) as special cases.

The real power of residue theory is its application to definite integrals over the real line. Many integrals that resist all elementary techniques yield immediately to the method of residues: one embeds the real integral into a contour integral in $\mathbb{C}$, identifies the enclosed poles, and reads off the answer.

Chapter Overview

6.1 - The Residue Theorem

The residue $\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = a_{-1}$ is the coefficient of $(z-z_0)^{-1}$ in the Laurent expansion. The residue theorem states $\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma f\,dz = \sum_j \operatorname{Res}(f,z_j)$ over all singularities $z_j$ inside $\gamma$. Practical formulas compute residues at simple poles and poles of higher order without expanding the full Laurent series.

6.2 - Evaluation of Real Integrals

Three main types: rational integrals $\int_{-\infty}^\infty P/Q\,dx$ via semicircular contours; trigonometric integrals $\int_0^{2\pi} R(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\,d\theta$ via $z=e^{i\theta}$; and Fourier-type integrals $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)e^{iax}\,dx$ via Jordan's lemma. Classic example: $\int_{-\infty}^\infty dx/(1+x^2) = \pi$.

6.3 - Argument Principle and Rouché's Theorem

The argument principle connects $\oint f'/f\,dz$ to the count of zeros minus poles. Rouché's theorem gives a purely topological criterion for counting zeros: if $|f| > |g|$ on $\gamma$, then $f$ and $f+g$ have the same number of zeros inside $\gamma$. Application: proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
