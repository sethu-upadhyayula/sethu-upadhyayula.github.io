6.1 The Residue Theorem

The residue theorem is the culmination of complex integration. It reduces the computation of any contour integral around isolated singularities to a sum of residues - a purely algebraic operation once the Laurent expansions are known.

Definition 6.1.1 - Residue

Let $f$ have an isolated singularity at $z_0$ with Laurent expansion $f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty a_n(z-z_0)^n$ on $0 < |z-z_0| < r$. The residue of $f$ at $z_0$ is $$\operatorname{Res}(f, z_0) = a_{-1},$$ the coefficient of $(z-z_0)^{-1}$. By the Laurent coefficient formula, $\operatorname{Res}(f,z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-z_0|=\rho} f(z)\,dz$ for small $\rho$.

Theorem 6.1.2 - The Residue Theorem

Let $f$ be analytic inside and on a simple closed positively-oriented contour $\gamma$, except at finitely many isolated singularities $z_1, \ldots, z_k$ inside $\gamma$. Then $$\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i \sum_{j=1}^k \operatorname{Res}(f, z_j).$$ Equivalently, $\dfrac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \sum_{j=1}^k \operatorname{Res}(f, z_j)$.

Proof idea: Surround each $z_j$ by a small circle $\gamma_j$. By Cauchy's theorem applied to the region between $\gamma$ and $\bigcup_j \gamma_j$, we get $\oint_\gamma f\,dz = \sum_j \oint_{\gamma_j} f\,dz = 2\pi i \sum_j \operatorname{Res}(f,z_j)$.

Computing Residues

Theorem 6.1.3 - Residue Formulas

Let $f$ have an isolated singularity at $z_0$.

  • Simple pole: $\operatorname{Res}(f, z_0) = \lim_{z \to z_0} (z - z_0) f(z)$.
  • Quotient $f = p/q$ with simple zero of $q$: If $p(z_0) \ne 0$ and $q(z_0) = 0$, $q'(z_0) \ne 0$, then $\operatorname{Res}(f, z_0) = \dfrac{p(z_0)}{q'(z_0)}$.
  • Pole of order $m$: $$\operatorname{Res}(f, z_0) = \frac{1}{(m-1)!} \lim_{z \to z_0} \frac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}\bigl[(z-z_0)^m f(z)\bigr].$$

For an essential singularity there is no shortcut; one must expand the Laurent series directly.

Examples

Example 6.1.4 - Simple Pole via Quotient Formula

Compute $\operatorname{Res}\!\left(\dfrac{e^z}{z^2+1}, i\right)$. Note $z^2+1 = (z-i)(z+i)$, so $z=i$ is a simple pole. With $p(z) = e^z$ and $q(z) = z^2+1$, $q'(z) = 2z$: $$\operatorname{Res} = \frac{e^i}{2i} = \frac{\cos 1 + i\sin 1}{2i}.$$

Example 6.1.5 - Pole of Order 2

Compute $\operatorname{Res}\!\left(\dfrac{e^z}{(z-1)^2}, 1\right)$. This is a pole of order $2$: $$\operatorname{Res} = \lim_{z\to 1} \frac{d}{dz}\bigl[(z-1)^2 \cdot \frac{e^z}{(z-1)^2}\bigr] = \lim_{z\to 1} \frac{d}{dz} e^z = e^1 = e.$$

Example 6.1.6 - Full Contour Integral

Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{dz}{z^2-1}$. The singularities $z = \pm 1$ both lie inside $|z|=2$. Write $f = 1/((z-1)(z+1))$. Then: $$\operatorname{Res}(f, 1) = \frac{1}{1+1} = \frac{1}{2}, \qquad \operatorname{Res}(f, -1) = \frac{1}{-1-1} = -\frac{1}{2}.$$

Sum of residues $= 0$, so $\oint f\,dz = 2\pi i \cdot 0 = 0$. (This also follows from Cauchy's theorem applied to $1/(z^2-1)$ on a slightly deformed contour, or from the partial fraction $f = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\tfrac{1}{z-1} - \tfrac{1}{z+1}\bigr)$.)

Remark - Generalizations

The residue theorem extends to multiply-connected domains and to meromorphic functions on compact Riemann surfaces, where it takes the form: sum of all residues on a compact surface is zero. This global statement has deep algebraic geometry implications.
