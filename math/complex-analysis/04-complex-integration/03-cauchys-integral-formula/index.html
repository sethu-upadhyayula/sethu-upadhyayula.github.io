4.3 Cauchy's Integral Formula

Cauchy's integral formula is one of the most remarkable results in mathematics: the values of an analytic function inside a contour are completely determined by its values on the boundary. This leads immediately to the infinite differentiability of analytic functions, Liouville's theorem, and the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

The Integral Formula

Theorem 4.3.1 — Cauchy's Integral Formula

Let $f$ be analytic on an open set containing a simple closed positively oriented contour $\gamma$ and its interior. For any $z_0$ in the interior of $\gamma$: $$f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0}\,dz.$$

Proof sketch. The function $g(z) = f(z)/(z-z_0)$ is analytic inside $\gamma$ except at $z_0$. By contour deformation, $\oint_\gamma g\,dz = \oint_{|z-z_0|=\varepsilon} g\,dz$. On the small circle $z = z_0 + \varepsilon e^{i\theta}$, the integral equals $\int_0^{2\pi} f(z_0 + \varepsilon e^{i\theta})\,d\theta \to 2\pi f(z_0)$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$ by continuity. $\square$

Derivatives via Contour Integrals

Theorem 4.3.2 — Cauchy's Integral Formula for Derivatives

Under the same hypotheses, $f$ is infinitely differentiable at every interior point $z_0$, with $$f^{(n)}(z_0) = \frac{n!}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{(z - z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

Proof. Differentiate the $n=0$ formula under the integral sign $n$ times with respect to $z_0$, justified by uniform convergence on compact sets. $\square$

Corollary 4.3.3 — Analyticity Implies $C^\infty$

Every analytic function on an open set $D$ is infinitely complex differentiable on $D$. In particular, once a function is complex differentiable once in an open set, it is automatically differentiable infinitely many times — a phenomenon with no real counterpart.

Major Consequences

Theorem 4.3.4 — Liouville's Theorem

Every bounded entire function is constant.

Proof. Let $|f(z)| \leq M$ for all $z$. By the $n=1$ CIF on $|z-z_0|=R$: $|f'(z_0)| \leq \frac{1!}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{M}{R^2} \cdot 2\pi R = \frac{M}{R} \to 0$ as $R \to \infty$. So $f' \equiv 0$, hence $f$ is constant. $\square$

Theorem 4.3.5 — Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Every non-constant polynomial $p(z) \in \mathbb{C}[z]$ has at least one root in $\mathbb{C}$.

Proof. Suppose $p$ has no zeros. Then $1/p$ is entire. Since $|p(z)| \to \infty$ as $|z| \to \infty$, we have $|1/p(z)| \to 0$, so $1/p$ is bounded. By Liouville, $1/p$ is constant, contradicting $p$ being non-constant. $\square$

Theorem 4.3.6 — Morera's Theorem (Converse to Cauchy)

Let $f : D \to \mathbb{C}$ be continuous on an open set $D$. If $\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0$ for every triangle $\gamma$ in $D$, then $f$ is analytic on $D$.

Proof. The hypothesis implies $f$ has a local antiderivative $F$ (constructed by integration along paths), and $F$ is analytic by definition. Since $F' = f$, and $F$ is analytic, so is $f$. $\square$

Example 4.3.7 — Computing $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{e^z}{z-1}\,dz$

The function $e^z$ is entire, and $z_0 = 1$ lies inside $|z| = 2$. By the CIF: $$\oint_{|z|=2} \frac{e^z}{z - 1}\,dz = 2\pi i \cdot e^1 = 2\pi i e.$$

Example 4.3.8 — Computing $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{\cos z}{z^3}\,dz$

By the higher derivative formula with $f(z) = \cos z$, $z_0 = 0$, $n = 2$: $$\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{\cos z}{z^3}\,dz = \frac{2\pi i}{2!} f''(0) = \pi i \cdot (-\cos 0) = -\pi i.$$
