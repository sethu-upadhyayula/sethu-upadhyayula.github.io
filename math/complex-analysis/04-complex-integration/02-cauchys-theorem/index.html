4.2 Cauchy's Theorem

Cauchy's theorem is the central result of complex integration: an analytic function integrated over any closed curve in a simply connected domain gives zero. This topological restriction on contour integrals has far-reaching consequences throughout the theory.

The Cauchy-Goursat Theorem

Theorem 4.2.1 — Cauchy-Goursat Theorem

Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be a simply connected domain and let $f : D \to \mathbb{C}$ be analytic on $D$. Then for every closed contour $\gamma$ in $D$, $$\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0.$$

Proof sketch (via Green's theorem). Writing $f = u + iv$ and $dz = dx + i\,dy$: $$\oint_\gamma f\,dz = \oint_\gamma (u\,dx - v\,dy) + i\oint_\gamma (v\,dx + u\,dy).$$ By Green's theorem, $\oint_\gamma u\,dx - v\,dy = \iint_D (-v_x - u_y)\,dA = 0$ (using $u_y = -v_x$ from CR), and similarly for the second integral. Goursat's refinement removes the assumption that $f'$ be continuous. $\square$

Definition 4.2.2 — Simply Connected Domain

A domain $D \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ is simply connected if it is connected and every closed curve in $D$ can be continuously deformed (while staying in $D$) to a point. Informally, $D$ has no holes. Examples: disks, half-planes, the full plane $\mathbb{C}$. Non-examples: $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$, annuli, punctured disks.

Homotopy Invariance and Deformation of Contours

Theorem 4.2.3 — Homotopy Version of Cauchy's Theorem

Let $D$ be a domain (not necessarily simply connected) and $f$ analytic on $D$. If $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ are closed curves in $D$ that are homotopic in $D$ (i.e., one can be continuously deformed into the other within $D$), then $$\oint_{\gamma_0} f(z)\,dz = \oint_{\gamma_1} f(z)\,dz.$$

In particular, if $f$ is analytic on an annulus $r_1 < |z - z_0| < r_2$, then the integral of $f$ over any simple closed curve encircling $z_0$ once (counterclockwise) equals $\oint_{|z-z_0|=r} f(z)\,dz$ for any $r \in (r_1, r_2)$.

Corollary 4.2.4 — Path Independence

If $f$ is analytic on a simply connected domain $D$, then $\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz$ depends only on the endpoints of $\gamma$ (not on the specific path). Consequently, $f$ has an antiderivative $F$ on $D$ (unique up to a constant), and $F' = f$.

Theorem 4.2.5 — Deformation of Contours

Let $f$ be analytic on $D$ except possibly at finitely many isolated singularities $z_1, \ldots, z_k$ inside a simple closed curve $\Gamma$. Let $C_1, \ldots, C_k$ be small circles around each $z_j$ lying entirely in $D$ and not surrounding each other. Then $$\oint_\Gamma f(z)\,dz = \sum_{j=1}^k \oint_{C_j} f(z)\,dz.$$

This reduces the computation of a complex integral to small local contributions near each singularity — the foundation of residue theory.

Example 4.2.6 — Consequence of Cauchy's Theorem

Since $f(z) = z^2$ is entire (analytic on all of $\mathbb{C}$), Cauchy's theorem implies $\oint_\gamma z^2\,dz = 0$ for any closed contour $\gamma$ in $\mathbb{C}$. More generally, $\oint_\gamma p(z)\,dz = 0$ for every polynomial $p$ and every closed contour.

On the other hand, $\oint_{|z|=1} z^{-1}\,dz = 2\pi i \neq 0$, consistent with $1/z$ not being analytic at $z = 0$, which lies inside the contour.
