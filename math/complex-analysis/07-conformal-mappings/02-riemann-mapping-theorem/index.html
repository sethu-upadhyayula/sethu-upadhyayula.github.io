7.2 Riemann Mapping Theorem

The Riemann mapping theorem is one of the most profound existence results in mathematics: every simply connected proper open subset of $\mathbb{C}$ is conformally equivalent to the unit disk. It reduces the study of harmonic functions on arbitrary simply connected domains to the study on the disk, and underpins the theory of conformal geometry.

Conformal Maps

Definition 7.2.1 — Conformal Map and Biholomorphism

An analytic function $f : D \to D'$ is conformal at $z_0$ if $f'(z_0) \neq 0$; it then preserves the angle and orientation between curves through $z_0$. A biholomorphism (conformal equivalence) from $D$ to $D'$ is a bijective analytic map $f : D \to D'$ whose inverse is also analytic (equivalently, $f$ is analytic, bijective, with $f' \neq 0$ on $D$).

Two domains $D$ and $D'$ are conformally equivalent if there exists a biholomorphism between them. This is an equivalence relation.

Remark — Angle Preservation

If $f'(z_0) \neq 0$, then $f$ acts locally as a rotation by $\arg f'(z_0)$ and a dilation by $|f'(z_0)|$. In particular, the angle between any two smooth curves at $z_0$ is preserved. This is the geometric meaning of conformality.

The Riemann Mapping Theorem

Theorem 7.2.2 — Riemann Mapping Theorem

Let $D \subsetneq \mathbb{C}$ be a simply connected domain (i.e., a simply connected open proper subset of $\mathbb{C}$). Then $D$ is conformally equivalent to the open unit disk $\mathbb{D} = \{z : |z| < 1\}$.

More precisely: for any $z_0 \in D$, there exists a unique biholomorphism $f : D \to \mathbb{D}$ satisfying $f(z_0) = 0$ and $f'(z_0) > 0$.

Remark — The Hypothesis $D \neq \mathbb{C}$ is Essential

The full complex plane $\mathbb{C}$ is simply connected but is not conformally equivalent to $\mathbb{D}$: by Liouville's theorem, any bounded entire function is constant, so any biholomorphism $\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{D}$ would be bounded and entire, hence constant — a contradiction. The Riemann mapping theorem thus draws a sharp dichotomy: simply connected domains are either $\mathbb{C}$, $\hat{\mathbb{C}}$, or conformally the disk.

Theorem 7.2.3 — Uniqueness

The biholomorphism $f : D \to \mathbb{D}$ with $f(z_0) = 0$ and $f'(z_0) > 0$ is unique. The conformal automorphisms of $\mathbb{D}$ (biholomorphisms $\mathbb{D} \to \mathbb{D}$) form a group, and each is a Möbius transformation of the form $z \mapsto e^{i\theta}(z - a)/(1 - \bar{a}z)$ with $|a| < 1$ and $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$.

The Upper Half-Plane and the Disk

Example 7.2.4 — Upper Half-Plane $\leftrightarrow$ Unit Disk

The map $f : \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{D}$ defined by $f(z) = (z-i)/(z+i)$ is a biholomorphism from the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{\mathrm{Im}(z) > 0\}$ to $\mathbb{D}$. Its inverse is $f^{-1}(w) = i(1+w)/(1-w)$.

Since $\mathbb{H}$ is simply connected and proper, this is consistent with the Riemann mapping theorem. The real axis (boundary of $\mathbb{H}$) maps to the unit circle (boundary of $\mathbb{D}$).

Schwarz-Christoffel Mapping

Theorem 7.2.5 — Schwarz-Christoffel Formula

A conformal map from $\mathbb{H}$ (or $\mathbb{D}$) to a polygon with interior angles $\alpha_k \pi$ at vertices $w_k$ is given by $$f(z) = A \int^z \prod_{k=1}^n (t - x_k)^{\alpha_k - 1}\,dt + B,$$ where $x_1 < x_2 < \cdots < x_n$ are the preimages of the vertices on the real axis, and $A, B \in \mathbb{C}$ are constants determined by normalisation.

This provides an explicit construction of the Riemann mapping for polygonal domains, with applications to electrostatics, fluid dynamics, and potential theory.

Example 7.2.6 — Mapping a Half-Strip

The map $f(z) = \sin z$ sends the half-strip $\{-\pi/2 < \mathrm{Re}(z) < \pi/2,\ \mathrm{Im}(z) > 0\}$ conformally onto $\mathbb{H}$. Combined with the Cayley map, this gives a conformal equivalence of the half-strip with $\mathbb{D}$.

Such explicit maps arise frequently in potential theory: solving Laplace's equation on $D$ reduces to solving it on $\mathbb{D}$ via the Riemann map, where the Poisson integral formula applies.
