7.1 Möbius Transformations

Möbius transformations (also called linear fractional transformations or homographic transformations) are the simplest non-trivial conformal maps of the Riemann sphere $\hat{\mathbb{C}} = \mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}$ to itself. Despite their elementary form, they are rich enough to act transitively on triples of points and to model non-Euclidean geometry.

Definition and Basic Properties

Definition 7.1.1 - Möbius Transformation

A Möbius transformation is a map $T : \hat{\mathbb{C}} \to \hat{\mathbb{C}}$ of the form $$T(z) = \frac{az + b}{cz + d}, \quad a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{C},\ ad - bc \neq 0.$$ The condition $ad - bc \neq 0$ ensures $T$ is non-constant and invertible. We set $T(-d/c) = \infty$ and $T(\infty) = a/c$ (if $c \neq 0$), and $T(\infty) = \infty$ (if $c = 0$).

The inverse is $T^{-1}(w) = (dw - b)/(-cw + a)$, again a Möbius transformation.

Proposition 7.1.2 - Composition and Group Structure

The composition of two Möbius transformations is again a Möbius transformation. The set of all Möbius transformations forms a group under composition, isomorphic to $\mathrm{PSL}(2,\mathbb{C}) = \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})/\{\pm I\}$, where the matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ corresponds to $T(z) = (az+b)/(cz+d)$.

Circles and Lines

Theorem 7.1.3 - Preservation of Circles and Lines

Every Möbius transformation maps the family of circles and lines in $\hat{\mathbb{C}}$ to itself (treating lines as circles through $\infty$).

Proof. It suffices to verify for the elementary transformations $z \mapsto az+b$, $z \mapsto 1/z$ that generate the group. The first is an affine map (rotation + dilation + translation) which clearly preserves circles and lines. For $1/z$: a circle $|z - z_0|^2 = r^2$ transforms under $w = 1/z$ (i.e., $z = 1/w$) to $|1/w - z_0|^2 = r^2$, which rearranges to a circle or line in $w$. $\square$

The Cross-Ratio and Three-Fold Transitivity

Definition 7.1.4 - Cross-Ratio

For distinct points $z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4 \in \hat{\mathbb{C}}$, the cross-ratio is $$(z_1, z_2; z_3, z_4) = \frac{(z_1 - z_3)(z_2 - z_4)}{(z_1 - z_4)(z_2 - z_3)}.$$ Möbius transformations preserve the cross-ratio: $(T(z_1), T(z_2); T(z_3), T(z_4)) = (z_1, z_2; z_3, z_4)$. Four points are concyclic (or collinear) if and only if their cross-ratio is real.

Theorem 7.1.5 - Three-Fold Transitivity

Given any two ordered triples of distinct points $(z_1, z_2, z_3)$ and $(w_1, w_2, w_3)$ in $\hat{\mathbb{C}}$, there exists a unique Möbius transformation $T$ with $T(z_j) = w_j$ for $j = 1, 2, 3$.

Construction. The unique $T$ mapping $(z_1, z_2, z_3) \mapsto (0, 1, \infty)$ is $T(z) = (z-z_1)(z_2 - z_3)/((z - z_3)(z_2 - z_1))$. Composing two such maps gives the unique $T$ mapping any triple to any other.

Classification of Möbius Transformations

Definition 7.1.6 - Classification by Fixed Points

A non-identity Möbius transformation $T$ (corresponding to matrix $M \in \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$) is classified by its trace $\tau = \mathrm{tr}(M) = a + d$:

  • Parabolic: $\tau^2 = 4$; exactly one fixed point; conjugate to $z \mapsto z + 1$.
  • Elliptic: $\tau^2 \in [0,4)$ (real); two fixed points; conjugate to $z \mapsto e^{i\theta}z$ (rotation).
  • Hyperbolic: $\tau^2 \in (4,\infty)$ (real); two fixed points; conjugate to $z \mapsto kz$ with $k > 0, k \neq 1$ (dilation).
  • Loxodromic: $\tau^2 \in \mathbb{C} \setminus [0,4]$; two fixed points; conjugate to $z \mapsto kz$ with $k \notin \mathbb{R}$ (spiral dilation).

Example 7.1.7 - Mapping the Upper Half-Plane to the Unit Disk

The Möbius transformation $$T(z) = \frac{z - i}{z + i}$$ maps the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{z : \mathrm{Im}(z) > 0\}$ conformally onto the unit disk $\mathbb{D} = \{|w| < 1\}$, sending $i \mapsto 0$, $0 \mapsto -1$, $\infty \mapsto 1$, and $-i \mapsto \infty$.

The inverse is $T^{-1}(w) = i(1+w)/(1-w)$, mapping $\mathbb{D}$ to $\mathbb{H}$.
