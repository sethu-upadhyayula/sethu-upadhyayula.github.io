3.1 Exponential and Trigonometric Functions

The complex exponential $e^z$ is the most fundamental entire function, and the complex trigonometric functions arise naturally from it via Euler's formula. In the complex plane these functions exhibit both familiar algebraic identities and new phenomena invisible over $¥mathbb{R}$, such as the unboundedness of $¥sin z$ and the non-real zeros of $¥cos z$.

The Complex Exponential

Theorem 3.1.1 - Properties of $e^z$

The function $e^z = e^x(¥cos y + i¥sin y)$ for $z = x + iy$ is entire (analytic on all of $¥mathbb{C}$) with $(e^z)' = e^z$. Further:

  • $e^{z_1+z_2} = e^{z_1}e^{z_2}$ for all $z_1, z_2 ¥in ¥mathbb{C}$.
  • $|e^z| = e^{¥mathrm{Re}(z)} = e^x$; in particular $|e^{iy}| = 1$.
  • $e^z$ is periodic with period $2¥pi i$: $e^{z+2¥pi i} = e^z$.
  • $e^z ¥neq 0$ for all $z ¥in ¥mathbb{C}$; the image of $e^z$ is $¥mathbb{C} ¥setminus ¥{0¥}$.

Complex Cosine and Sine

Definition 3.1.2 - Complex Cosine and Sine

For $z ¥in ¥mathbb{C}$: $$¥cos z = ¥frac{e^{iz} + e^{-iz}}{2}, ¥qquad ¥sin z = ¥frac{e^{iz} - e^{-iz}}{2i}.$$ Both functions are entire, reduce to the familiar real functions for real $z$, and satisfy $(¥cos z)' = -¥sin z$ and $(¥sin z)' = ¥cos z$.

Theorem 3.1.3 - Pythagorean Identity and Zeros

For all $z ¥in ¥mathbb{C}$: $$¥cos^2 z + ¥sin^2 z = 1.$$ The zeros of $¥sin z$ are exactly $z = n¥pi$ for $n ¥in ¥mathbb{Z}$; the zeros of $¥cos z$ are exactly $z = ¥tfrac{¥pi}{2} + n¥pi$ for $n ¥in ¥mathbb{Z}$. These are all real - unlike over $¥mathbb{C}$, $¥sin z$ has no non-real zeros.

Proof of identity. $¥cos^2 z + ¥sin^2 z = ¥tfrac{(e^{iz}+e^{-iz})^2}{4} - ¥tfrac{(e^{iz}-e^{-iz})^2}{4} = ¥tfrac{4e^0}{4} = 1$. The zero sets follow by writing $e^{iz} = e^{-iz}$ or $e^{iz} = -e^{-iz}$ and solving. $¥square$

Proposition 3.1.4 - Modulus of $¥sin z$

For $z = x + iy$: $$|¥sin z|^2 = ¥sin^2 x + ¥sinh^2 y, ¥qquad |¥cos z|^2 = ¥cos^2 x + ¥sinh^2 y.$$

Proof. Using $¥sin(x+iy) = ¥sin x¥cosh y + i¥cos x¥sinh y$ (from addition formulae and $¥sin(iy) = i¥sinh y$): $|¥sin z|^2 = ¥sin^2 x¥cosh^2 y + ¥cos^2 x¥sinh^2 y = ¥sin^2 x(1+¥sinh^2 y) + (1-¥sin^2 x)¥sinh^2 y = ¥sin^2 x + ¥sinh^2 y$. $¥square$

Remark - $¥sin z$ is Unbounded

Since $|¥sin z|^2 ¥geq ¥sinh^2 y ¥to ¥infty$ as $|y| ¥to ¥infty$, the complex sine function is unbounded on $¥mathbb{C}$. This stands in stark contrast to the real sine, which is bounded by $1$. Similarly $|¥cos z|$ is unbounded.

Hyperbolic Functions

Definition 3.1.5 - Complex Hyperbolic Functions

For $z ¥in ¥mathbb{C}$: $$¥cosh z = ¥frac{e^z + e^{-z}}{2}, ¥qquad ¥sinh z = ¥frac{e^z - e^{-z}}{2}.$$ These are entire with $(¥cosh z)' = ¥sinh z$ and $(¥sinh z)' = ¥cosh z$. The fundamental relations linking trigonometric and hyperbolic functions are: $$¥sin(iz) = i¥sinh z, ¥quad ¥cos(iz) = ¥cosh z, ¥quad ¥sinh(iz) = i¥sin z, ¥quad ¥cosh(iz) = ¥cos z.$$ The identity $¥cosh^2 z - ¥sinh^2 z = 1$ holds for all $z ¥in ¥mathbb{C}$.

Example 3.1.6 - Computing $¥sin(1+i)$

Using $¥sin(x+iy) = ¥sin x¥cosh y + i¥cos x¥sinh y$ with $x = y = 1$: $$¥sin(1+i) = ¥sin(1)¥cosh(1) + i¥cos(1)¥sinh(1).$$

Numerically: $¥sin 1 ¥approx 0.8415$, $¥cosh 1 ¥approx 1.5431$, $¥cos 1 ¥approx 0.5403$, $¥sinh 1 ¥approx 1.1752$, giving $¥sin(1+i) ¥approx 1.2985 + 0.6350i$. Note $|¥sin(1+i)|^2 = ¥sin^2(1) + ¥sinh^2(1) ¥approx 0.708 + 1.381 = 2.089 > 1$.
