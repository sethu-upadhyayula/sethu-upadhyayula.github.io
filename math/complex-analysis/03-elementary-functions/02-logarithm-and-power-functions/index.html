3.2 Logarithm and Power Functions

Since $e^z$ is $2\pi i$-periodic, its inverse — the complex logarithm — is inevitably multi-valued. Making rigorous sense of $\log z$ and $z^\alpha$ requires choosing a branch, and the resulting analytic functions depend on this choice.

The Multi-Valued Logarithm

Definition 3.2.1 — Complex Logarithm

For $z \neq 0$, we define $\log z$ as the set of all $w \in \mathbb{C}$ satisfying $e^w = z$. Writing $z = |z|e^{i\arg z}$: $$\log z = \ln|z| + i\arg(z) = \ln|z| + i(\theta + 2\pi k), \quad k \in \mathbb{Z},$$ where $\theta$ is any argument of $z$ and $\ln|z|$ is the real natural logarithm.

Thus $\log z$ is an infinite discrete set — a genuinely multi-valued function.

Definition 3.2.2 — Principal Branch $\operatorname{Log} z$

The principal branch of the logarithm is $$\operatorname{Log} z = \ln|z| + i\operatorname{Arg}(z), \quad z \in \mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty, 0],$$ where $\operatorname{Arg}(z) \in (-\pi, \pi]$ is the principal argument. The function $\operatorname{Log} z$ is analytic on $\mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty, 0]$ with derivative $$(\operatorname{Log} z)' = \frac{1}{z}.$$

Remark — Discontinuity on the Negative Real Axis

$\operatorname{Log} z$ fails to be continuous (let alone analytic) along the branch cut $(-\infty, 0]$. As $z \to -1$ from above, $\operatorname{Arg}(z) \to \pi$; from below, $\operatorname{Arg}(z) \to -\pi$. Different branches of $\log z$ differ by $2\pi i k$ for integer $k$; each branch is analytic once a branch cut is fixed.

Complex Powers

Definition 3.2.3 — General Power Function

For $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$ and $z \neq 0$, define $$z^\alpha = e^{\alpha \log z}.$$ Since $\log z$ is multi-valued, so is $z^\alpha$ in general, with different values differing by factors of $e^{2\pi i \alpha k}$ for $k \in \mathbb{Z}$. The principal value uses $\operatorname{Log} z$: $$z^\alpha = e^{\alpha \operatorname{Log} z}, \quad z \in \mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty, 0].$$ This branch is analytic on $\mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty, 0]$ with $(z^\alpha)' = \alpha z^{\alpha - 1}$.

Example 3.2.4 — $i^i$

$\operatorname{Log}(i) = \ln 1 + i\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2} = \tfrac{i\pi}{2}$. Hence the principal value is $$i^i = e^{i \cdot \operatorname{Log}(i)} = e^{i \cdot i\pi/2} = e^{-\pi/2} \approx 0.2079.$$

This is a real number! The other values are $e^{-\pi/2 + 2\pi k}$ for $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ — a discrete set of positive reals.

$n$-th Roots

Proposition 3.2.5 — $n$-th Roots

For $z = re^{i\theta} \neq 0$ and $n \in \mathbb{N}$, the $n$ distinct $n$-th roots of $z$ are $$z^{1/n} = r^{1/n} e^{i(\theta + 2\pi k)/n}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1,$$ where $r^{1/n} > 0$ is the positive real $n$-th root of $r$. These are the $n$ values of $e^{(\log z)/n}$. They form the vertices of a regular $n$-gon inscribed in the circle $|w| = r^{1/n}$.

Each $n$-th root defines an analytic branch on $\mathbb{C}$ minus a branch cut from $0$ to $\infty$.

Example 3.2.6 — Square Roots of $-4$

$-4 = 4e^{i\pi}$. The two square roots are $4^{1/2}e^{i\pi/2} = 2i$ and $4^{1/2}e^{i3\pi/2} = -2i$, confirming $(\pm 2i)^2 = -4$.

The principal square root of $z$ is $\sqrt{z} = e^{\frac{1}{2}\operatorname{Log} z}$, analytic on $\mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty, 0]$. For $-4$: $e^{\frac{1}{2}(\ln 4 + i\pi)} = e^{\ln 2 + i\pi/2} = 2e^{i\pi/2} = 2i$.
