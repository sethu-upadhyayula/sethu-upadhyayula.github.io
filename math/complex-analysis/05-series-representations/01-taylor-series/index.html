5.1 Taylor Series

One of the most remarkable consequences of analyticity is that every analytic function is locally equal to its Taylor series — a fact with no counterpart in real analysis, where smooth functions need not be real-analytic. The proof uses Cauchy's integral formula together with a geometric series expansion of the Cauchy kernel.

Theorem 5.1.1 — Taylor's Theorem for Analytic Functions

Let $f$ be analytic in the disk $D(z_0, R) = \{z : |z - z_0| < R\}$. Then $f$ equals its Taylor series on all of $D(z_0, R)$: $$f(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (z - z_0)^n,$$ where the coefficients are given by $$a_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(z_0)}{n!} = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{|z-z_0|=r} \frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz$$ for any $0 < r < R$. The series converges absolutely and uniformly on every compact subdisk $|z-z_0| \le \rho < R$.

Moreover, $R \ge \text{dist}(z_0, \partial D)$: the radius of convergence is at least the distance from $z_0$ to the nearest singularity of $f$.

Proof Sketch

Fix $w \in D(z_0, R)$ and choose $r$ with $|w - z_0| < r < R$. By Cauchy's integral formula: $$f(w) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{|z-z_0|=r} \frac{f(z)}{z - w}\,dz.$$ Write $z - w = (z - z_0)\bigl(1 - \tfrac{w-z_0}{z-z_0}\bigr)$ and expand via the geometric series, noting $\left|\tfrac{w-z_0}{z-z_0}\right| = \tfrac{|w-z_0|}{r} < 1$: $$\frac{1}{z - w} = \frac{1}{z - z_0} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{w - z_0}{z - z_0}\right)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(w-z_0)^n}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}.$$ Substituting and interchanging the (uniformly convergent) sum with the integral gives $$f(w) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-z_0|=r}\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz\right)(w-z_0)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(w-z_0)^n. \quad\square$$

Radius of Convergence

Theorem 5.1.2 — Radius of Convergence

The radius of convergence $R$ of the Taylor series of $f$ centered at $z_0$ equals the distance from $z_0$ to the nearest singularity of $f$ in $\mathbb{C}$ (or $+\infty$ if $f$ is entire). In particular, the series converges inside the largest disk centered at $z_0$ in which $f$ is analytic, and diverges outside it.

Remark — Contrast with Real Analysis

In real analysis, $f(x) = 1/(1+x^2)$ is $C^\infty$ on $\mathbb{R}$, yet its Taylor series at $x=0$ has radius of convergence $1$ — because the complex function $1/(1+z^2)$ has poles at $z = \pm i$. The real series "stops converging" because of complex singularities invisible on the real line.

Standard Taylor Series

Theorem 5.1.3 — Key Taylor Series

The following expansions hold for the indicated domains:

  • $\displaystyle e^z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^n}{n!}$,   $|z| < \infty$ (entire).
  • $\displaystyle \sin z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$,   $|z| < \infty$.
  • $\displaystyle \cos z = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n z^{2n}}{(2n)!}$,   $|z| < \infty$.
  • $\displaystyle \frac{1}{1-z} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n$,   $|z| < 1$.
  • $\displaystyle \log(1+z) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1} z^n}{n}$,   $|z| < 1$.

Examples

Example 5.1.4 — Series for $1/(1-z)^2$

Differentiating $\dfrac{1}{1-z} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n$ term by term (valid for $|z| < 1$): $$\frac{1}{(1-z)^2} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n z^{n-1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+1)z^n.$$

Differentiation of power series is valid inside the radius of convergence and preserves it.

Example 5.1.5 — Taylor Series at a Non-Zero Center

Expand $f(z) = e^z$ at $z_0 = 1$. Since $e^z = e^1 \cdot e^{z-1}$ and $e^w = \sum w^n/n!$: $$e^z = e \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(z-1)^n}{n!}.$$

The radius of convergence is $\infty$ since $e^z$ is entire.

Remark — Taylor Series and Zeros

If $f$ is analytic and not identically zero, its zeros are isolated. Near a zero $z_0$ of order $m$, we have $f(z) = (z-z_0)^m g(z)$ with $g$ analytic and $g(z_0) \ne 0$. This follows directly from the Taylor expansion: the first nonzero coefficient determines the order of the zero.
