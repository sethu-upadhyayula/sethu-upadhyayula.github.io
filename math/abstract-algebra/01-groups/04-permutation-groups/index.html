1.4 Permutation Groups

Permutation groups are both a rich source of examples and, by Cayley's theorem, representative of all groups. The symmetric group $S_n$ and its subgroup the alternating group $A_n$ are fundamental objects in algebra.

Definition 1.4.1 — Symmetric Group

The symmetric group $S_n$ is the group of all bijections $\sigma : \{1, 2, \ldots, n\} \to \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ under function composition. It has order $|S_n| = n!$.

Cycle Notation

Definition 1.4.2 — Cycle Notation

A $k$-cycle $(a_1\, a_2\, \cdots\, a_k)$ denotes the permutation sending $a_1 \mapsto a_2 \mapsto \cdots \mapsto a_k \mapsto a_1$ and fixing all other elements.

A transposition is a 2-cycle $(i\, j)$ that swaps $i$ and $j$. A 1-cycle is the identity.

Theorem 1.4.3 — Disjoint Cycle Decomposition

Every permutation $\sigma \in S_n$ can be written uniquely (up to the order of the cycles) as a product of disjoint cycles. The order of $\sigma$ equals the least common multiple of its cycle lengths.

Example

In $S_6$, let $\sigma = (1\;3\;5)(2\;4)$. This sends $1 \to 3 \to 5 \to 1$ and $2 \to 4 \to 2$, fixing 6. The order is $\text{lcm}(3, 2) = 6$.

As a two-line array: $\sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 3 & 4 & 5 & 2 & 1 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$.

Sign of a Permutation

Definition 1.4.4 — Transpositions and Sign

Every permutation can be written as a product of transpositions (not uniquely, but the parity — even or odd number of transpositions — is well-defined).

The sign (or signature) of $\sigma$ is: $$\text{sgn}(\sigma) = (-1)^{\text{number of transpositions in any decomposition of } \sigma}.$$ $\sigma$ is even if $\text{sgn}(\sigma) = +1$ and odd if $\text{sgn}(\sigma) = -1$.

A $k$-cycle has sign $(-1)^{k-1}$; so 2-cycles (transpositions) are odd, 3-cycles are even.

Theorem 1.4.5

The sign map $\text{sgn} : S_n \to \{+1, -1\}$ is a group homomorphism. In particular, $\text{sgn}(\sigma\tau) = \text{sgn}(\sigma)\text{sgn}(\tau)$.

The Alternating Group

Definition 1.4.6 — Alternating Group

The alternating group $A_n = \ker(\text{sgn}) = \{\sigma \in S_n : \text{sgn}(\sigma) = 1\}$ is the subgroup of even permutations. It has order $|A_n| = n!/2$ for $n \ge 2$.

Theorem 1.4.7 — Simplicity of $A_n$

For $n \ge 5$, the alternating group $A_n$ is simple: its only normal subgroups are $\{e\}$ and $A_n$ itself. This is a key step in proving that the general quintic polynomial is not solvable by radicals.

Theorem 1.4.8 — Cayley's Theorem

Every group $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_G$ (the symmetric group on the set $G$). If $|G| = n$, then $G$ embeds in $S_n$. This shows that $S_n$ contains, up to isomorphism, every group of order $\le n$.

Proof of Cayley's Theorem

For each $g \in G$, define $\lambda_g : G \to G$ by $\lambda_g(x) = gx$ (left multiplication). This is a bijection, so $\lambda_g \in S_G$. The map $\phi : G \to S_G$ given by $\phi(g) = \lambda_g$ is a group homomorphism: $\phi(gh)(x) = ghx = \lambda_g(\lambda_h(x)) = (\phi(g) \circ \phi(h))(x)$.

If $\phi(g) = \text{id}$, then $gx = x$ for all $x$, so $g = e$. Thus $\phi$ is injective, and $G \cong \text{im}(\phi) \le S_G$. $\square$
