Chapter 1: Groups

Groups are the simplest and most fundamental algebraic structures. A group captures the abstract notion of symmetry: the symmetries of any geometric object form a group, as do the integers under addition, invertible matrices under multiplication, and permutations of a set. Group theory unifies these diverse examples under a single axiomatic framework.

This chapter develops the basic theory from the axioms through Lagrange's theorem, one of the first deep results relating a subgroup to the full group via its index.

Chapter Overview

1.1 - Definition and Examples

We define a group $(G, \cdot)$ via four axioms - closure, associativity, identity, and inverses - and survey a rich collection of examples: $(\mathbb{Z},+)$, $(\mathbb{R}^*,\cdot)$, $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, $GL_n(\mathbb{R})$, symmetric groups $S_n$, and dihedral groups $D_n$. Basic consequences of the axioms (uniqueness of identity and inverses, cancellation laws) are derived.

1.2 - Subgroups

A subgroup $H \le G$ is a subset that is itself a group under the induced operation. We give the one-step subgroup test and study cosets. Lagrange's theorem states that $|H|$ divides $|G|$, with the cosets of $H$ partitioning $G$ into blocks of equal size $|H|$.

1.3 - Cyclic Groups

The cyclic group $\langle g \rangle$ generated by $g$ is the smallest subgroup containing $g$. Every cyclic group is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}$ or $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$. We count generators using Euler's totient function and connect cyclic groups to applications in cryptography.

1.4 - Permutation Groups

The symmetric group $S_n$ consists of all bijections on $\{1,\ldots,n\}$. Every permutation decomposes uniquely into disjoint cycles, and the sign (parity) of a permutation is well-defined. The alternating group $A_n$ of even permutations is simple for $n \ge 5$, a fact central to the unsolvability of the quintic.
