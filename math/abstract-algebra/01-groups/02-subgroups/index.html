1.2 Subgroups

A subgroup is a subset of a group that is itself a group under the same operation. Identifying subgroups is a key step in understanding a group's structure. Lagrange's theorem, the first major theorem of group theory, constrains which subgroup sizes are possible.

Definition 1.2.1 - Subgroup

Let $(G, \cdot)$ be a group. A nonempty subset $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup of $G$, written $H \le G$, if $H$ is itself a group under the operation inherited from $G$. Equivalently, $H$ must be closed under the operation and under taking inverses.

Theorem 1.2.2 - One-Step Subgroup Test

A nonempty subset $H \subseteq G$ is a subgroup if and only if for all $a, b \in H$, we have $ab^{-1} \in H$.

Proof

($\Rightarrow$) If $H \le G$ and $a, b \in H$, then $b^{-1} \in H$ (inverses) and $ab^{-1} \in H$ (closure).

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $ab^{-1} \in H$ for all $a, b \in H$. Taking $a = b$ gives $e = aa^{-1} \in H$. Taking $a = e$ gives $b^{-1} = eb^{-1} \in H$. For closure, if $a, c \in H$ then $c^{-1} \in H$ and $a(c^{-1})^{-1} = ac \in H$. Associativity is inherited from $G$. $\square$

Examples of Subgroups

Example 1.2.3
  • $n\mathbb{Z} = \{nk : k \in \mathbb{Z}\} \le (\mathbb{Z}, +)$ for any $n \ge 1$.
  • $SL_n(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in GL_n(\mathbb{R}) : \det A = 1\} \le GL_n(\mathbb{R})$.
  • $\{e\}$ and $G$ itself are always subgroups (trivial subgroups).
  • $A_n \le S_n$ (even permutations form a subgroup).

Cosets

Definition 1.2.4 - Cosets

Let $H \le G$ and $g \in G$. The left coset of $H$ by $g$ is $gH = \{gh : h \in H\}$. The right coset is $Hg = \{hg : h \in H\}$.

The index $[G:H]$ is the number of distinct left cosets of $H$ in $G$.

Theorem 1.2.5 - Cosets Partition $G$

The distinct left cosets of $H$ in $G$ partition $G$ into disjoint sets each of size $|H|$. Specifically:

  1. $g \in gH$ for all $g \in G$ (so the cosets cover $G$).
  2. Either $g_1H = g_2H$ or $g_1H \cap g_2H = \emptyset$.
  3. $|gH| = |H|$ for every $g \in G$.

Lagrange's Theorem

Theorem 1.2.6 - Lagrange's Theorem

If $G$ is a finite group and $H \le G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$. Moreover, $|G| = |H| \cdot [G:H]$.

Proof

By Theorem 1.2.5, the left cosets of $H$ partition $G$ into $[G:H]$ disjoint sets each of size $|H|$. Therefore $|G| = |H| \cdot [G:H]$, so $|H|$ divides $|G|$. $\square$

Corollary 1.2.7

Let $G$ be a finite group and $g \in G$. Then:

  1. $|g|$ divides $|G|$.
  2. $g^{|G|} = e$.
  3. If $|G| = p$ is prime, then $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ (every group of prime order is cyclic).
Proof of Corollary

The cyclic subgroup $\langle g \rangle = \{e, g, g^2, \ldots, g^{|g|-1}\}$ satisfies $|\langle g \rangle| = |g|$. By Lagrange, $|g|$ divides $|G|$. Writing $|G| = |g| \cdot k$, we get $g^{|G|} = g^{|g| \cdot k} = (g^{|g|})^k = e^k = e$.

For the last part: if $|G| = p$ prime, pick any $g \neq e$. Then $|g|$ divides $p$ and $|g| \neq 1$, so $|g| = p$ and $G = \langle g \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$. $\square$

Remark - Converse of Lagrange is False

Lagrange's theorem says that subgroup orders must divide $|G|$, but the converse is false in general: $A_4$ has order 12 but no subgroup of order 6. However, Cauchy's theorem guarantees that if a prime $p$ divides $|G|$, then $G$ has an element of order $p$.
