1.1 Definition and Examples

A group is the simplest algebraic structure that captures the notion of symmetry and invertible operations. From the integers under addition to invertible matrices under multiplication, groups appear throughout mathematics.

Definition 1.1.1 - Group

A group is a pair $(G, \cdot)$ where $G$ is a set and $\cdot : G \times G \to G$ is a binary operation satisfying:

  1. Closure: For all $a, b \in G$, $a \cdot b \in G$.
  2. Associativity: For all $a, b, c \in G$, $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$.
  3. Identity: There exists $e \in G$ such that $e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$ for all $a \in G$.
  4. Inverses: For each $a \in G$, there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ such that $a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$.

If additionally $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$ for all $a, b \in G$, the group is called abelian (or commutative).

Standard Examples

Example 1.1.2 - Integers Under Addition

$(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ is an abelian group. The identity is $0$, and the inverse of $n$ is $-n$. Similarly $(\mathbb{Q}, +)$, $(\mathbb{R}, +)$, and $(\mathbb{C}, +)$ are abelian groups.

Example 1.1.3 - Nonzero Reals Under Multiplication

$(\mathbb{R}^*, \cdot)$ is an abelian group where $\mathbb{R}^* = \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$. The identity is $1$ and the inverse of $x$ is $1/x$. Note: $(\mathbb{R}, \cdot)$ is not a group since $0$ has no multiplicative inverse.

Example 1.1.4 - Integers Modulo $n$

$(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)$ is an abelian group of order $n$. Elements are residue classes $[0], [1], \ldots, [n-1]$, with $[a] + [b] = [a+b \pmod n]$. Identity is $[0]$, inverse of $[a]$ is $[n-a]$.

Example 1.1.5 - General Linear Group

$GL_n(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in M_n(\mathbb{R}) : \det A \neq 0\}$ under matrix multiplication is a group (non-abelian for $n \ge 2$). Identity is $I_n$, inverse is the matrix inverse $A^{-1}$.

Example 1.1.6 - Symmetric Group $S_n$

The set of all bijections $\sigma : \{1, \ldots, n\} \to \{1, \ldots, n\}$ under function composition forms the symmetric group $S_n$ of order $n!$. It is non-abelian for $n \ge 3$.

Example 1.1.7 - Dihedral Group $D_n$

The symmetries of a regular $n$-gon (rotations and reflections) form the dihedral group $D_n$ of order $2n$. It is generated by a rotation $r$ of order $n$ and a reflection $s$ of order $2$, satisfying $srs^{-1} = r^{-1}$.

Non-Examples

Non-Examples

The following fail to be groups:

  • $(\mathbb{N}, +)$: no inverses (e.g., $2$ has no additive inverse in $\mathbb{N}$).
  • $(\mathbb{Z}, \cdot)$: most elements lack multiplicative inverses ($2 \cdot x = 1$ has no integer solution).
  • $(M_n(\mathbb{R}), \cdot)$: singular matrices are not invertible.

Basic Properties

Theorem 1.1.8 - Uniqueness of Identity and Inverses

In any group $G$:

  1. The identity element $e$ is unique.
  2. Each element $a \in G$ has a unique inverse $a^{-1}$.
  3. $(a^{-1})^{-1} = a$ for all $a \in G$.
  4. $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$ for all $a, b \in G$ (the sock-shoe property).

Cancellation: If $ac = bc$ then $a = b$ (right cancellation); if $ca = cb$ then $a = b$ (left cancellation).

Proof (Uniqueness of Identity)

Suppose $e$ and $e'$ are both identities. Then $e = e \cdot e' = e'$, where the first equality uses $e'$ as identity and the second uses $e$ as identity. $\square$

Proof of $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$

Compute $(ab)(b^{-1}a^{-1}) = a(bb^{-1})a^{-1} = a \cdot e \cdot a^{-1} = aa^{-1} = e$. By uniqueness of inverses, $(ab)^{-1} = b^{-1}a^{-1}$. $\square$

Order of a Group and Element

Definition 1.1.9 - Order

The order of a group $G$, denoted $|G|$, is the cardinality of the set $G$. If $|G| < \infty$, we say $G$ is a finite group.

The order of an element $g \in G$, denoted $|g|$ or $\text{ord}(g)$, is the smallest positive integer $n$ such that $g^n = e$. If no such $n$ exists, we say $g$ has infinite order.

Example

In $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$: $|[1]| = 6$, $|[2]| = 3$, $|[3]| = 2$, $|[0]| = 1$. In $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$: every nonzero element has infinite order since $n \cdot k \neq 0$ for $k \neq 0$, $n \ge 1$.
