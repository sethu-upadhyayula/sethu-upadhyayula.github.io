1.3 Cyclic Groups

Cyclic groups are the simplest groups: each is generated by a single element. Despite their simplicity, they play a central role in algebra and number theory, and appear throughout cryptography.

Definition 1.3.1 - Cyclic Group

Let $G$ be a group and $g \in G$. The cyclic subgroup generated by $g$ is $$\langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\} = \{\ldots, g^{-2}, g^{-1}, e, g, g^2, \ldots\}.$$ A group $G$ is cyclic if $G = \langle g \rangle$ for some $g \in G$; such a $g$ is called a generator of $G$.

Theorem 1.3.2 - Classification of Cyclic Groups

Every cyclic group is isomorphic to either $\mathbb{Z}$ (infinite cyclic) or $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (cyclic of order $n$). Specifically, if $G = \langle g \rangle$ then $G \cong \mathbb{Z}$ if $|g| = \infty$, and $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ if $|g| = n$.

Proof

Define $\phi : \mathbb{Z} \to G$ by $\phi(k) = g^k$. This is a surjective group homomorphism. Its kernel is a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$, hence of the form $n\mathbb{Z}$ for some $n \ge 0$. If $n = 0$, $\phi$ is injective and $G \cong \mathbb{Z}$. If $n > 0$, then $|g| = n$ and $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ by the first isomorphism theorem. $\square$

Subgroups of Cyclic Groups

Theorem 1.3.3

Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic. More precisely:

  • Every subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$ is of the form $n\mathbb{Z}$ for some $n \ge 0$.
  • The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ are exactly $\langle [d] \rangle$ for $d \mid n$, and there is exactly one subgroup of order $n/d$ for each divisor $d$ of $n$.

Generators and Euler's Totient

Theorem 1.3.4 - Number of Generators

The element $[k] \in \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ generates $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ if and only if $\gcd(k, n) = 1$. Therefore the number of generators of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is $\phi(n)$, the Euler totient of $n$.

In particular, $\phi(p) = p - 1$ for any prime $p$, so $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ has $p-1$ generators.

Example

In $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$: $\phi(12) = 12(1 - 1/2)(1 - 1/3) = 4$ generators. They are $[1], [5], [7], [11]$ - exactly the elements coprime to 12.

$[4]$ generates the subgroup $\{[0],[4],[8]\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$; $[6]$ generates $\{[0],[6]\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

Application: Discrete Logarithm and Cryptography

Application - Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

Many cryptographic protocols rely on the discrete logarithm problem: given a cyclic group $G = \langle g \rangle$, a generator $g$, and $h = g^k \in G$, find $k$.

In the group $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ for a large prime $p$, this is computationally hard. The Diffie-Hellman protocol uses this: Alice and Bob agree on a large prime $p$ and generator $g$, exchange $g^a \pmod p$ and $g^b \pmod p$, and each can compute the shared secret $g^{ab} \pmod p$ without the other's private exponent being revealed.

The group $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ is cyclic of order $p-1$ - a consequence of the classification theorem above applied to the multiplicative structure of a finite field.
