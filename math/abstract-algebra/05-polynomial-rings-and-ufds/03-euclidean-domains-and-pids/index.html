5.3 Euclidean Domains and PIDs

The hierarchy Euclidean domain $\Rightarrow$ PID $\Rightarrow$ UFD provides progressively weaker conditions under which unique factorisation holds. Euclidean domains admit a division algorithm, PIDs have every ideal principal, and UFDs have unique factorisation. Each implication is strict.

Definition 5.3.1 — Euclidean Domain

An integral domain $R$ is a Euclidean domain if there exists a function $\varphi : R \setminus \{0\} \to \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ (the Euclidean norm) such that for any $a \in R$ and $b \in R \setminus \{0\}$, there exist $q, r \in R$ with $a = qb + r$ and either $r = 0$ or $\varphi(r) < \varphi(b)$.

Example 5.3.2 — Standard Euclidean Domains
  • $\mathbb{Z}$ with $\varphi(n) = |n|$.
  • $F[x]$ for any field $F$, with $\varphi(f) = \deg f$.
  • The Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ with $\varphi(a+bi) = a^2 + b^2$ (the norm).
  • $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ (Eisenstein integers), with norm $\varphi(a+b\omega) = a^2 - ab + b^2$.
Definition 5.3.3 — Principal Ideal Domain

An integral domain $R$ is a principal ideal domain (PID) if every ideal of $R$ is principal, i.e., of the form $(a) = aR$ for some $a \in R$.

Theorem 5.3.4 — Euclidean $\Rightarrow$ PID $\Rightarrow$ UFD

Every Euclidean domain is a PID, and every PID is a UFD. The proofs proceed as follows:

  • ED $\Rightarrow$ PID: Given a non-zero ideal $I$, pick $b \in I$ with minimal $\varphi(b)$; then $I = (b)$ by the division algorithm.
  • PID $\Rightarrow$ UFD: Irreducibles are prime in a PID (since $(p)$ maximal $\Rightarrow$ $R/(p)$ field), and the ascending chain condition on ideals (PIDs are Noetherian) ensures factorisation terminates.
Theorem 5.3.5 — Bezout's Identity in PIDs

In a PID $R$, for any $a, b \in R$ not both zero, $\gcd(a,b)$ exists and there are $s, t \in R$ with $\gcd(a,b) = sa + tb$. This is because $(a,b) = (\gcd(a,b))$ in a PID.

Example 5.3.6 — PID that is not Euclidean

The ring $\mathbb{Z}\!\left[\frac{1+\sqrt{-19}}{2}\right]$ is a PID but not a Euclidean domain under any norm. This is the smallest example showing the two classes differ. On the other hand, $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a UFD that is not a PID (the ideal $(2, x)$ is not principal).

Remark — Summary of the Hierarchy

Fields $\subsetneq$ Euclidean domains $\subsetneq$ PIDs $\subsetneq$ UFDs $\subsetneq$ integral domains. Each class is strictly contained in the next: $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is Euclidean not a field; $\mathbb{Z}[(1+\sqrt{-19})/2]$ is a PID not Euclidean; $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a UFD not a PID; $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ is an integral domain not a UFD.
