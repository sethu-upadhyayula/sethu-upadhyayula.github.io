5.2 Unique Factorisation Domains

A unique factorisation domain formalises the fundamental theorem of arithmetic in the setting of arbitrary integral domains: every non-zero non-unit element factors into irreducibles, and this factorisation is unique up to order and units.

Definition 5.2.1 - Irreducible and Prime Elements

Let $R$ be an integral domain and $p \in R$ non-zero, non-unit.

  • $p$ is irreducible if $p = ab$ implies $a$ or $b$ is a unit.
  • $p$ is prime if $p \mid ab$ implies $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$.

In any integral domain, prime implies irreducible. The converse holds in UFDs.

Definition 5.2.2 - Unique Factorisation Domain

An integral domain $R$ is a unique factorisation domain (UFD) if every non-zero non-unit $a \in R$ can be written $a = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_n$ with each $p_i$ irreducible, and this factorisation is unique up to reordering and multiplication by units: if also $a = q_1 \cdots q_m$ with $q_j$ irreducible, then $m = n$ and there is a permutation $\sigma$ with $p_i$ and $q_{\sigma(i)}$ associates.

Example 5.2.3 - $\mathbb{Z}$ is a UFD

The fundamental theorem of arithmetic states that $\mathbb{Z}$ is a UFD: every integer $n > 1$ factors uniquely as $n = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_k^{e_k}$ with $p_i$ distinct primes and $e_i \geq 1$. The units of $\mathbb{Z}$ are $\pm 1$.

Example 5.2.4 - A Non-UFD

In $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$, we have $6 = 2 \cdot 3 = (1 + \sqrt{-5})(1 - \sqrt{-5})$. One can verify that $2, 3, 1\pm\sqrt{-5}$ are all irreducible using the norm $N(a+b\sqrt{-5}) = a^2 + 5b^2$, but these two factorisations are genuinely distinct, so $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ is not a UFD.

Theorem 5.2.5 - In a UFD, Irreducible iff Prime

Let $R$ be a UFD. Then $p \in R$ is irreducible if and only if $p$ is prime. This equivalence characterises UFDs among integral domains (along with the ascending chain condition on principal ideals).

Theorem 5.2.6 - GCDs Exist in UFDs

In a UFD, any two elements $a, b$ have a greatest common divisor $\gcd(a,b)$, defined as the element (unique up to units) dividing both $a$ and $b$ and divisible by every common divisor. If $a = u \prod p_i^{a_i}$ and $b = v \prod p_i^{b_i}$ (same primes), then $\gcd(a,b) = \prod p_i^{\min(a_i, b_i)}$.

Theorem 5.2.7 - $R[x]$ is a UFD if $R$ is

If $R$ is a UFD, then $R[x]$ is a UFD. In particular, $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a UFD, and by induction $\mathbb{Z}[x_1,\ldots,x_n]$ and $F[x_1,\ldots,x_n]$ (for $F$ a field) are UFDs. The proof uses Gauss's lemma to reduce factorisation in $R[x]$ to factorisation in $R$ and in the field of fractions $K[x]$.
