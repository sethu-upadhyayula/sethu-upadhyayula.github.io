5.1 Polynomial Rings

Given a commutative ring $R$, the polynomial ring $R[x]$ extends $R$ by a formal indeterminate $x$ and inherits an arithmetic structure that reflects the algebra of $R$. Over integral domains, polynomial rings possess a rich theory of divisibility driven by the degree function.

Definition 5.1.1 - Polynomial Ring

Let $R$ be a commutative ring. The polynomial ring $R[x]$ consists of formal sums $f = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_1 x + a_0$ with $a_i \in R$, finitely many non-zero, with coefficient-wise addition and the multiplication rule $x^i \cdot x^j = x^{i+j}$. If $a_n \ne 0$, then $\deg f = n$ and $a_n$ is the leading coefficient. A polynomial with leading coefficient $1$ is monic.

Theorem 5.1.2 - Division Algorithm

Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity, $f, g \in R[x]$ with $g \ne 0$ and leading coefficient of $g$ a unit in $R$. Then there exist unique $q, r \in R[x]$ with $$f = qg + r, \qquad \deg r < \deg g \text{ (or } r = 0\text{)}.$$ In particular, this holds when $R$ is a field.

Theorem 5.1.3 - Factor Theorem

Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity and $f \in R[x]$. Then $c \in R$ is a root of $f$ (i.e., $f(c) = 0$) if and only if $(x - c) \mid f(x)$ in $R[x]$. Moreover, a polynomial of degree $n$ over an integral domain has at most $n$ roots.

Theorem 5.1.4 - $F[x]$ is a PID

If $F$ is a field, then $F[x]$ is a principal ideal domain: every ideal of $F[x]$ is of the form $(f) = f \cdot F[x]$ for some $f \in F[x]$. The generator is the monic GCD of any generating set. Thus $F[x]$ is a Euclidean domain with the degree function as the norm.

Theorem 5.1.5 - Gauss's Lemma

Define the content $c(f)$ of $f \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ as the GCD of its coefficients. Then $c(fg) = c(f)c(g)$ for all $f, g \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$. A polynomial is primitive if $c(f) = 1$.

Corollary: If $f \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ is primitive and factors as $f = gh$ in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$, then it factors in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$: there exist $g_1, h_1 \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ with $f = g_1 h_1$ and $\deg g_1 = \deg g$, $\deg h_1 = \deg h$.

Theorem 5.1.6 - Eisenstein's Criterion

Let $f = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0 \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$. If there is a prime $p$ such that $p \nmid a_n$, $p \mid a_i$ for all $i < n$, and $p^2 \nmid a_0$, then $f$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$. Example: $x^4 + 2x + 4$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ (use $p = 2$).

Example 5.1.7 - Cyclotomic Polynomials

The $p$-th cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_p(x) = x^{p-1} + x^{p-2} + \cdots + 1 = (x^p - 1)/(x-1)$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ for any prime $p$. Proof via Eisenstein: substitute $x \mapsto x+1$ and apply the criterion with prime $p$, using that $\binom{p}{k}$ is divisible by $p$ for $0 < k < p$.
