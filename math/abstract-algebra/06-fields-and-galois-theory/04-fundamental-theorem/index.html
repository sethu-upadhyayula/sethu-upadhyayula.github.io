6.4 Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

The fundamental theorem establishes a perfect order-reversing bijection between the lattice of intermediate fields of a Galois extension and the lattice of subgroups of its Galois group. It is the cornerstone of Galois theory and has profound consequences for the solvability of polynomial equations.

Theorem 6.4.1 - Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

Let $K/F$ be a finite Galois extension with Galois group $G = \mathrm{Gal}(K/F)$. There is an order-reversing bijection: $$\left\{\text{intermediate fields } F \subseteq E \subseteq K\right\} \longleftrightarrow \left\{\text{subgroups } H \leq G\right\}$$ given by $E \mapsto \mathrm{Gal}(K/E)$ with inverse $H \mapsto K^H$. Under this correspondence:

  • $[E:F] = [G:\mathrm{Gal}(K/E)]$ and $[K:E] = |\mathrm{Gal}(K/E)|$.
  • $E/F$ is Galois $\iff$ $\mathrm{Gal}(K/E) \trianglelefteq G$, in which case $\mathrm{Gal}(E/F) \cong G/\mathrm{Gal}(K/E)$.
  • Larger subgroups correspond to smaller intermediate fields.
Example 6.4.2 - $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)/\mathbb{Q}$

With $G = S_3$, $|G| = 6$, the subgroup lattice has: $\{e\}$, three subgroups of order 2 ($\langle(12)\rangle$, $\langle(13)\rangle$, $\langle(23)\rangle$), one subgroup of order 3 ($A_3 = \langle(123)\rangle$), and $G$ itself. The correspondence gives:

  • $G \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Q}$ (fixed by all), $\{e\} \leftrightarrow K$.
  • $A_3 \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Q}(\omega)$ (degree 2 over $\mathbb{Q}$, normal since $A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$).
  • The three order-2 subgroups $\leftrightarrow$ $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$, $\mathbb{Q}(\omega\sqrt[3]{2})$, $\mathbb{Q}(\omega^2\sqrt[3]{2})$ (degree 3 over $\mathbb{Q}$, not normal).
Theorem 6.4.3 - Solvability by Radicals

A polynomial $f \in F[x]$ (char $F = 0$) is solvable by radicals if and only if its Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(K/F)$ (where $K$ is the splitting field) is a solvable group. Since $S_5$ is not solvable, the general quintic polynomial has no solution by radicals - a result first proved by Abel and Galois.

Corollary 6.4.4 - Quadratic, Cubic, Quartic Formulas

The Galois groups of generic polynomials of degrees 2, 3, 4 are $S_2 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$, $S_3$, and $S_4$ respectively. Since $S_2$, $S_3$, $S_4$ are all solvable (with composition factors $\mathbb{Z}/2$, $\mathbb{Z}/3$, $\mathbb{Z}/2$), the quadratic, cubic, and quartic formulas exist - consistent with classical results. The group $S_5$ is not solvable, hence no general quintic formula.

Remark - Constructibility

A real number $\alpha$ is constructible by straightedge and compass from $\mathbb{Q}$ if and only if $[\mathbb{Q}(\alpha):\mathbb{Q}]$ is a power of 2. This explains why trisecting an angle, doubling the cube ($\sqrt[3]{2}$ has degree 3), and constructing a regular 7-gon (requires degree 6) are impossible.
