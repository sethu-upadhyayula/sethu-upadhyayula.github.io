6.3 Galois Group

The Galois group of a field extension $K/F$ consists of all field automorphisms of $K$ that fix $F$ pointwise. It is the central object of Galois theory, connecting the structure of field extensions to group theory.

Definition 6.3.1 — Galois Group

Let $K/F$ be a Galois extension. The Galois group is $$\mathrm{Gal}(K/F) = \{\sigma : K \xrightarrow{\sim} K \mid \sigma(a) = a \text{ for all } a \in F\},$$ under composition. This is a finite group of order $[K:F]$.

Example 6.3.2 — $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q})$

Any automorphism fixing $\mathbb{Q}$ is determined by $\sigma(\sqrt{2})$, which must be another root of $x^2-2$, i.e., $\pm\sqrt{2}$. So $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q}) = \{\mathrm{id}, \sigma\}$ where $\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = -\sqrt{2}$. This is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

Example 6.3.3 — $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)/\mathbb{Q})$

With $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$, the splitting field $K$ of $x^3-2$ has $[K:\mathbb{Q}] = 6$. Any $\sigma \in \mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})$ is determined by $\sigma(\sqrt[3]{2}) \in \{\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega^2\sqrt[3]{2}\}$ and $\sigma(\omega) \in \{\omega, \omega^2\}$. This gives 6 automorphisms forming $S_3 \cong D_3$, the symmetric group on 3 elements.

Theorem 6.3.4 — Artin's Theorem

Let $G$ be a finite group of automorphisms of a field $K$, and let $F = K^G = \{x \in K : \sigma(x) = x \text{ for all } \sigma \in G\}$ be the fixed field. Then $K/F$ is a Galois extension with $\mathrm{Gal}(K/F) = G$ and $[K:F] = |G|$.

Definition 6.3.5 — Fixed Field

For a subgroup $H \leq \mathrm{Gal}(K/F)$, the fixed field is $K^H = \{x \in K : \sigma(x) = x \text{ for all } \sigma \in H\}$. This is an intermediate field $F \subseteq K^H \subseteq K$ with $[K:K^H] = |H|$ and $[K^H:F] = [G:H]$.

Example 6.3.6 — Cyclotomic Extensions

The splitting field of $x^n - 1$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ where $\zeta_n = e^{2\pi i/n}$. The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ via $\sigma_a(\zeta_n) = \zeta_n^a$ for $\gcd(a,n)=1$. The degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n):\mathbb{Q}] = \phi(n)$ (Euler's totient).
