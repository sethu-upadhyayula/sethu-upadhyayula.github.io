6.2 Splitting Fields

A splitting field is the smallest extension of a base field over which a given polynomial completely factors into linear factors. Splitting fields are canonical up to isomorphism and form the natural domain for studying roots of polynomials.

Definition 6.2.1 — Splitting Field

Let $F$ be a field and $f \in F[x]$ non-constant. A splitting field of $f$ over $F$ is an extension $K/F$ such that:

  1. $f$ splits completely in $K[x]$: $f(x) = a(x-\alpha_1)\cdots(x-\alpha_n)$ with all $\alpha_i \in K$.
  2. $K = F(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n)$ — $K$ is generated over $F$ by the roots of $f$.
Theorem 6.2.2 — Existence and Uniqueness

Every polynomial $f \in F[x]$ has a splitting field over $F$, and any two splitting fields of $f$ over $F$ are isomorphic via an isomorphism that fixes $F$ pointwise. The degree $[K:F]$ divides $(deg\,f)!$.

Example 6.2.3 — Splitting field of $x^2 - 2$

The splitting field of $f(x) = x^2 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$, since $f(x) = (x-\sqrt{2})(x+\sqrt{2})$ and both roots lie in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$. The degree is $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}] = 2$.

Example 6.2.4 — Splitting field of $x^3 - 2$

The roots of $x^3 - 2$ are $\sqrt[3]{2},\, \omega\sqrt[3]{2},\, \omega^2\sqrt[3]{2}$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. The splitting field over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$. Since $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}):\mathbb{Q}] = 3$ and $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2},\omega):\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})] = 2$ (as $\omega$ satisfies $x^2+x+1$ irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$), we get $[K:\mathbb{Q}] = 6$.

Definition 6.2.5 — Normal and Separable Extensions

An algebraic extension $K/F$ is:

  • Normal if $K$ is the splitting field of some polynomial over $F$, equivalently, if every irreducible polynomial in $F[x]$ with a root in $K$ splits completely in $K[x]$.
  • Separable if every element of $K$ has a separable minimal polynomial over $F$ (i.e., no repeated roots in $\overline{F}$). All extensions of characteristic 0 fields are separable.
  • A Galois extension is one that is both normal and separable.
Theorem 6.2.6 — Characterisation of Galois Extensions

A finite extension $K/F$ is Galois if and only if $|\text{Aut}(K/F)| = [K:F]$, where $\text{Aut}(K/F)$ denotes the group of field automorphisms of $K$ fixing $F$ pointwise. In this case $\text{Aut}(K/F)$ is called the Galois group $\text{Gal}(K/F)$.
