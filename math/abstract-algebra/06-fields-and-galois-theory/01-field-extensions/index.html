6.1 Field Extensions

A field extension $K/F$ enlarges $F$ into a bigger field $K$. The degree $[K:F]$ measures the size of the extension as a vector space, and algebraic extensions are those where every element satisfies a polynomial over the base field.

Definition 6.1.1 — Field Extension and Degree

A field extension is an inclusion of fields $F \hookrightarrow K$, written $K/F$, where $F$ is the base field and $K$ is the extension field. The degree is $[K:F] = \dim_F K$ (the dimension of $K$ as an $F$-vector space). If $[K:F]$ is finite, the extension is finite; otherwise infinite.

Theorem 6.1.2 — Tower Law

If $F \subseteq K \subseteq L$ are fields, then $[L:F] = [L:K][K:F]$. In particular, $[L:F]$ is finite if and only if both $[L:K]$ and $[K:F]$ are finite.

Definition 6.1.3 — Algebraic and Transcendental Elements

Let $K/F$ be a field extension and $\alpha \in K$. Then $\alpha$ is algebraic over $F$ if there exists a non-zero polynomial $f \in F[x]$ with $f(\alpha) = 0$. Otherwise $\alpha$ is transcendental over $F$. The extension $K/F$ is algebraic if every element of $K$ is algebraic over $F$.

Theorem 6.1.4 — Minimal Polynomial

If $\alpha$ is algebraic over $F$, there is a unique monic irreducible polynomial $m_\alpha \in F[x]$ with $m_\alpha(\alpha) = 0$, called the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $F$. It generates the kernel of the evaluation map $F[x] \to F(\alpha)$, $f \mapsto f(\alpha)$.

Moreover $F(\alpha) \cong F[x]/(m_\alpha)$ and $[F(\alpha):F] = \deg m_\alpha$.

Example 6.1.5 — $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$

The minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{2}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $x^2 - 2$ (irreducible by Eisenstein with $p=2$). Thus $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{a + b\sqrt{2} : a,b \in \mathbb{Q}\}$ and $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}] = 2$. More generally, $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[n]{p}):\mathbb{Q}] = n$ for any prime $p$.

Theorem 6.1.6 — Finite Extensions are Algebraic

Every finite extension is algebraic. The converse fails: $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}$ (the algebraic closure) is algebraic but infinite. However, a finitely generated algebraic extension is finite.

Definition 6.1.7 — Algebraic Closure

A field $F$ is algebraically closed if every non-constant polynomial in $F[x]$ has a root in $F$ (equivalently, splits into linear factors). The algebraic closure $\overline{F}$ is the smallest algebraically closed field containing $F$; it is unique up to isomorphism. The algebraic closure of $\mathbb{Q}$ is $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$; the algebraic closure of $\mathbb{R}$ is $\mathbb{C}$.
