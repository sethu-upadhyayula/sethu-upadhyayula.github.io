3.3 Sylow Theorems

Sylow's theorems (1872) are the most powerful general tool for determining the structure of finite groups. They provide sharp information about subgroups of prime-power order, going far beyond Lagrange's theorem.

Definition 3.3.1 — $p$-Sylow Subgroup

Let $p$ be prime and write $|G| = p^k m$ with $p \nmid m$. A $p$-Sylow subgroup (or Sylow $p$-subgroup) of $G$ is a subgroup of order $p^k$. Denote the set of all Sylow $p$-subgroups by $\text{Syl}_p(G)$ and their number by $n_p = |\text{Syl}_p(G)|$.

Theorem 3.3.2 — Sylow I (Existence)

For every prime $p$ dividing $|G|$, at least one Sylow $p$-subgroup exists: $\text{Syl}_p(G) \ne \emptyset$.

Proof Sketch

Let $G$ act by left multiplication on the set $\mathcal{X}$ of all $k$-element subsets of $G$, where $|\mathcal{X}| = \binom{p^k m}{p^k}$. By Lucas' theorem, $\binom{p^k m}{p^k} \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p}$. Since $|\mathcal{X}|$ is not divisible by $p$, some orbit has size not divisible by $p$. For a set $S$ in such an orbit, the stabiliser $G_S$ has order $|G|/|G \cdot S|$, which is divisible by $p^k$. One shows $|G_S| \le p^k$ (since $|gS \cap S|$ controls $G_S$), forcing $|G_S| = p^k$. $\square$

Theorem 3.3.3 — Sylow II (Conjugacy)

All Sylow $p$-subgroups of $G$ are conjugate to one another: if $P, Q \in \text{Syl}_p(G)$ then $Q = gPg^{-1}$ for some $g \in G$. In particular, $n_p = [G : N_G(P)]$ where $N_G(P) = \{g \in G : gPg^{-1} = P\}$ is the normaliser.

Theorem 3.3.4 — Sylow III (Count)

The number $n_p$ of Sylow $p$-subgroups satisfies: $$n_p \equiv 1 \pmod{p} \quad \text{and} \quad n_p \mid m$$ where $|G| = p^k m$.

Applications

Example 3.3.5 — Groups of Order 15

Let $|G| = 15 = 3 \cdot 5$. By Sylow III: $n_3 \mid 5$ and $n_3 \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, so $n_3 \in \{1, 5\}$; since $5 \not\equiv 1 \pmod 3$, we get $n_3 = 1$. Similarly $n_5 \mid 3$ and $n_5 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$, so $n_5 = 1$. The unique Sylow 3-subgroup $P_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ and Sylow 5-subgroup $P_5 \cong \mathbb{Z}_5$ are both normal in $G$, and $P_3 \cap P_5 = \{e\}$. Hence $G \cong P_3 \times P_5 \cong \mathbb{Z}_{15}$. Every group of order 15 is cyclic.

Example 3.3.6 — Simplicity of $A_5$

$|A_5| = 60 = 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5$. One checks: $n_5 \mid 12$, $n_5 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$, so $n_5 \in \{1, 6\}$. If $n_5 = 1$, the unique Sylow 5-subgroup would be normal; but $A_5$ has exactly 6 Sylow 5-subgroups (each contributing 4 elements of order 5, giving 24 elements). Similarly $n_3 = 10$. Counting elements leaves no room for a proper normal subgroup of any order, so $A_5$ is simple.

Remark 3.3.7 — Classification of Small Groups

Sylow theory, combined with the structure theorem for abelian groups, enables the complete classification of all groups up to order 15 (for example). For instance: order 12 groups are $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_6$, $A_4$, $D_6$, or $\text{Dic}_3$ (the dicyclic group). The Sylow subgroup analysis is the first step in each case.
