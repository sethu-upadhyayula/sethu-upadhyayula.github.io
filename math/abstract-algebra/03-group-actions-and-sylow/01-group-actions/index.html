3.1 Group Actions

A group action formalises the idea of a group acting as symmetries of a set. Rather than studying the group in isolation, we let it operate on external objects and read off structural information from the resulting orbits and stabilisers.

Definition 3.1.1 — Left Group Action

A left action of a group $G$ on a set $X$ is a map $G \times X \to X$, written $(g, x) \mapsto g \cdot x$, satisfying:

  1. Identity: $e \cdot x = x$ for all $x \in X$.
  2. Compatibility: $(gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)$ for all $g, h \in G$, $x \in X$.

Equivalently, the map $\phi: G \to \text{Sym}(X)$ defined by $\phi(g)(x) = g \cdot x$ is a group homomorphism. The action is faithful if $\ker\phi = \{e\}$, and transitive if there is only one orbit.

Standard Examples

Example 3.1.2 — Regular (Left Multiplication) Action

$G$ acts on itself by $g \cdot h = gh$. This action is faithful and transitive. It gives Cayley's theorem: every group $G$ embeds into $\text{Sym}(G) \cong S_{|G|}$.

Example 3.1.3 — Conjugation Action

$G$ acts on itself by conjugation: $g \cdot h = ghg^{-1}$. The orbit of $h$ is its conjugacy class $\{ghg^{-1} : g \in G\}$. The stabiliser of $h$ is the centraliser $C_G(h) = \{g \in G : gh = hg\}$.

Example 3.1.4 — Action on Cosets

For a subgroup $H \le G$, $G$ acts on the set of left cosets $G/H$ by $g \cdot (aH) = (ga)H$. This action is transitive and has stabiliser of the coset $aH$ equal to $aHa^{-1}$.

Example 3.1.5 — $S_n$ Acting on $\{1,\ldots,n\}$

The symmetric group $S_n$ acts on $X = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ by $\sigma \cdot i = \sigma(i)$. This action is faithful and transitive. The stabiliser of $i$ is $\{\sigma \in S_n : \sigma(i) = i\} \cong S_{n-1}$.

Example 3.1.6 — $D_n$ Acting on Vertices

The dihedral group $D_n$ acts on the $n$ vertices of a regular $n$-gon. The action is faithful and transitive. The stabiliser of any vertex has order 2 (generated by the reflection fixing that vertex).

Orbits and Stabilisers

Definition 3.1.7 — Orbit and Stabiliser

For $x \in X$:

  • The orbit of $x$ is $G \cdot x = \{g \cdot x : g \in G\} \subseteq X$.
  • The stabiliser of $x$ is $G_x = \{g \in G : g \cdot x = x\} \le G$.

The stabiliser $G_x$ is always a subgroup of $G$.

Theorem 3.1.8 — Orbits Partition $X$

The distinct orbits of a $G$-action on $X$ form a partition of $X$: every element of $X$ belongs to exactly one orbit. Specifically, define $x \sim y$ iff $y \in G \cdot x$; this is an equivalence relation, and the equivalence classes are the orbits.

Proof

Reflexivity: $e \cdot x = x$ so $x \sim x$. Symmetry: if $y = g \cdot x$ then $x = g^{-1} \cdot y$ so $x \sim y \Rightarrow y \sim x$. Transitivity: if $y = g \cdot x$ and $z = h \cdot y$ then $z = (hg) \cdot x$. Hence $\sim$ is an equivalence relation and its classes partition $X$. $\square$

Remark 3.1.9 — Fixed Points

An element $x \in X$ with $G \cdot x = \{x\}$ is called a fixed point; equivalently $G_x = G$. The set of fixed points of $g \in G$ is $X^g = \{x \in X : g \cdot x = x\}$, which plays a role in Burnside's lemma (Section 3.2).
