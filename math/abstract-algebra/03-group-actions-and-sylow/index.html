Chapter 3: Group Actions and Sylow Theory

Group actions provide a powerful tool for analysing group structure by letting groups act symmetrically on sets. Whenever a group $G$ acts on a set $X$, the set decomposes into orbits and the orbit-stabiliser theorem links the sizes of orbits to the order of the group.

Sylow's theorems are the sharpest general structural results about finite groups: they guarantee the existence, conjugacy, and count of subgroups of prime-power order, and form the backbone of the classification of groups of small order.

Chapter Overview

3.1 — Group Actions

We define a left action of $G$ on a set $X$ and study orbits, stabilisers, and the partition of $X$ into orbits. Key examples include the regular action (left multiplication), conjugation, action on cosets, $S_n$ acting on $\{1,\ldots,n\}$, and $D_n$ acting on the vertices of a regular $n$-gon.

3.2 — Orbit-Stabiliser Theorem

The orbit-stabiliser theorem states $|G \cdot x| \cdot |G_x| = |G|$, realised via a bijection $G/G_x \to G \cdot x$. Applications include the class equation, the fact that groups of order $p^2$ are abelian, and Burnside's lemma for counting orbits.

3.3 — Sylow Theorems

For $|G| = p^k m$ with $p \nmid m$, the three Sylow theorems assert existence, conjugacy, and a congruence-and-divisibility constraint on the number $n_p$ of $p$-Sylow subgroups. We apply them to prove that groups of order 15 are cyclic and that $A_5$ is simple.
