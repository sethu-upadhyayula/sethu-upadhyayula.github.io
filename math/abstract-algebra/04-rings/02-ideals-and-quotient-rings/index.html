4.2 Ideals and Quotient Rings

Ideals play the same role in ring theory that normal subgroups play in group theory: they are the kernels of ring homomorphisms and allow the construction of quotient rings.

Definition 4.2.1 — Ideal

Let $R$ be a ring. A non-empty subset $I \subseteq R$ is a left ideal if for all $a, b \in I$ and $r \in R$: (1) $a - b \in I$, and (2) $ra \in I$. It is a right ideal if $ar \in I$ instead. A (two-sided) ideal satisfies both conditions: $ra \in I$ and $ar \in I$ for all $r \in R$, $a \in I$.

In a commutative ring, left, right, and two-sided ideals coincide.

Example 4.2.2 — Principal Ideals

In $\mathbb{Z}$, for any $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, the set $n\mathbb{Z} = \{nk : k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is an ideal. These are all ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$: every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is of the form $n\mathbb{Z}$ for some $n \geq 0$. More generally, in a commutative ring $R$ with unity, the principal ideal generated by $a$ is $(a) = aR = \{ar : r \in R\}$.

Definition 4.2.3 — Quotient Ring

Let $I$ be an ideal of $R$. The quotient ring $R/I$ has underlying set $\{a + I : a \in R\}$ (the cosets of $I$ in $(R,+)$) with operations: $$(a+I) + (b+I) = (a+b)+I, \qquad (a+I)(b+I) = (ab)+I.$$ These are well-defined because $I$ is an ideal. The zero element is $0 + I = I$ and, if $R$ has unity $1$, then $1 + I$ is the unity of $R/I$.

Example 4.2.4 — $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$

Taking $R = \mathbb{Z}$ and $I = n\mathbb{Z}$, we obtain the familiar quotient ring $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ of integers modulo $n$. The elements are residue classes $[0], [1], \ldots, [n-1]$, with arithmetic modulo $n$. This ring is a field if and only if $n$ is prime.

Example 4.2.5 — $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2+1)$

In the polynomial ring $\mathbb{R}[x]$, the ideal $(x^2+1) = (x^2+1)\mathbb{R}[x]$ is maximal (since $x^2 + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{R}$). The quotient $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2+1) \cong \mathbb{C}$, where the image of $x$ plays the role of $i$.

Special Classes of Ideals

Definition 4.2.6 — Prime and Maximal Ideals

Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity and $I \subsetneq R$ a proper ideal.

  • $I$ is prime if $ab \in I$ implies $a \in I$ or $b \in I$.
  • $I$ is maximal if there is no ideal $J$ with $I \subsetneq J \subsetneq R$.
Theorem 4.2.7 — Characterisation via Quotients

Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unity and $I$ a proper ideal. Then:

  • $I$ is prime $\iff$ $R/I$ is an integral domain.
  • $I$ is maximal $\iff$ $R/I$ is a field.
In particular, every maximal ideal is prime.

Theorem 4.2.8 — Existence of Maximal Ideals

Every proper ideal of a commutative ring with unity is contained in a maximal ideal. (Proof uses Zorn's Lemma applied to the poset of proper ideals containing $I$, ordered by inclusion.)

Definition 4.2.9 — Generated Ideals

The ideal generated by a subset $S \subseteq R$ is the smallest ideal containing $S$: $$\langle S \rangle = \left\{\sum_{i} r_i s_i t_i : r_i, t_i \in R,\, s_i \in S\right\}.$$ For $S = \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ in a commutative ring with unity, $\langle a_1, \ldots, a_n \rangle = \{r_1 a_1 + \cdots + r_n a_n : r_i \in R\}$. An ideal generated by one element is called a principal ideal.
