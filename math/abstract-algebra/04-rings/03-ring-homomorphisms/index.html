4.3 Ring Homomorphisms

Ring homomorphisms are structure-preserving maps between rings. The First Isomorphism Theorem for rings precisely mirrors its group-theoretic counterpart, connecting kernels, ideals, and quotient rings.

Definition 4.3.1 — Ring Homomorphism

A map $\varphi : R \to S$ between rings is a ring homomorphism if for all $a, b \in R$: $$\varphi(a + b) = \varphi(a) + \varphi(b), \qquad \varphi(ab) = \varphi(a)\varphi(b).$$ If $R$ and $S$ have unity we additionally require $\varphi(1_R) = 1_S$ (unity-preserving). A bijective ring homomorphism is a ring isomorphism.

Definition 4.3.2 — Kernel and Image

For a ring homomorphism $\varphi : R \to S$: $$\ker \varphi = \{a \in R : \varphi(a) = 0_S\}, \qquad \operatorname{Im} \varphi = \{\varphi(a) : a \in R\}.$$ The kernel $\ker\varphi$ is an ideal of $R$, and $\operatorname{Im}\varphi$ is a subring of $S$.

Theorem 4.3.3 — First Isomorphism Theorem for Rings

Let $\varphi : R \to S$ be a ring homomorphism. Then $\ker\varphi$ is an ideal of $R$ and $$R/\ker\varphi \cong \operatorname{Im}\varphi$$ via the isomorphism $\bar{\varphi}(a + \ker\varphi) = \varphi(a)$. In particular, $\varphi$ is injective if and only if $\ker\varphi = \{0\}$.

Example 4.3.4 — Reduction Modulo $n$

The map $\varphi : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, $\varphi(k) = [k]$, is a surjective ring homomorphism with $\ker\varphi = n\mathbb{Z}$. By the First Isomorphism Theorem, $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (tautologically), but more usefully: any ring homomorphism $\mathbb{Z} \to S$ is determined by the image of $1$.

Example 4.3.5 — Evaluation Homomorphism

Fix $c \in R$ where $R$ is a commutative ring. The evaluation map $\text{ev}_c : R[x] \to R$, $\text{ev}_c(f) = f(c)$, is a surjective ring homomorphism with kernel $(x - c) = \{(x-c)g(x) : g \in R[x]\}$ (when $R$ is an integral domain). Thus $R[x]/(x-c) \cong R$.

Theorem 4.3.6 — Second and Third Isomorphism Theorems

Let $R$ be a ring with ideals $I$ and $J$.

  • Second: If $I \subseteq J$, then $J/I$ is an ideal of $R/I$ and $(R/I)/(J/I) \cong R/J$.
  • Third: $(I + J)/J \cong I/(I \cap J)$ as rings.
Remark — Correspondence Theorem

There is a bijection between ideals of $R$ containing $I$ and ideals of $R/I$, given by $J \mapsto J/I$. This is the lattice isomorphism theorem for rings and is crucial for understanding the ideal structure of quotient rings.
