Chapter 4: Rings

Ring theory abstracts the arithmetic of integers and polynomials, distinguishing those algebraic structures that admit both addition and multiplication. Whereas a group has one operation, a ring has two, connected by the distributive law.

The hierarchy of rings - from general rings to integral domains to fields - mirrors the arithmetic properties one requires: commutativity, absence of zero divisors, and invertibility of nonzero elements.

Chapter Overview

4.1 - Definition and Examples

We define rings, commutative rings, integral domains, and fields through their axioms. Key examples include $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, matrix rings $M_n(\mathbb{R})$, the Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, and $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$.

4.2 - Ideals and Quotient Rings

Two-sided ideals are the kernels of ring homomorphisms; quotient rings $R/I$ are defined precisely when $I$ is two-sided. We study principal ideals, prime ideals (where $R/P$ is an integral domain), and maximal ideals (where $R/M$ is a field). In $\mathbb{Z}$, these reduce to the ideals $n\mathbb{Z}$.

4.3 - Ring Homomorphisms

Ring homomorphisms preserve both addition and multiplication. The first isomorphism theorem $R/\ker\phi \cong \phi(R)$ holds, as does the Chinese remainder theorem $R/(I \cap J) \cong R/I \times R/J$ when $I + J = R$. We examine the key example $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2+1) \cong \mathbb{C}$.
