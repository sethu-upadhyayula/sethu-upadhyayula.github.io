Chapter 2: Second-Order Linear ODEs

Second-order linear ODEs are central to classical physics: the harmonic oscillator, beam bending, wave propagation, and quantum mechanics all lead to equations of this form. The theory is rich: the solution space is a two-dimensional vector space, spanned by any two linearly independent solutions, and the Wronskian provides a computable criterion for independence.

The general second-order linear ODE is $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x)$. The associated homogeneous equation has $g \equiv 0$, and any particular solution plus the general homogeneous solution gives the complete general solution.

Chapter Overview

2.1 â€” Constant Coefficient Equations

For $ay'' + by' + cy = 0$, the characteristic equation $ar^2 + br + c = 0$ determines the solution type: two distinct real roots give $e^{r_1 x}$ and $e^{r_2 x}$; a repeated root $r$ gives $e^{rx}$ and $xe^{rx}$; complex conjugate roots $\alpha \pm \beta i$ give $e^{\alpha x}\cos\beta x$ and $e^{\alpha x}\sin\beta x$. Non-homogeneous equations are handled by undetermined coefficients.

2.2 â€” Reduction of Order

Given one solution $y_1$, the substitution $y = y_1 v(x)$ reduces the order by one. The resulting first-order equation for $v'$ can be solved by integration. Abel's formula gives the Wronskian $W[y_1,y_2](x) = C e^{-\int p\,dx}$ without knowing $y_2$ explicitly.

2.3 â€” Variation of Parameters

For the nonhomogeneous equation $y'' + py' + qy = g$, we seek a particular solution $y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2$ where $u_1' = -y_2 g/W$ and $u_2' = y_1 g/W$. This method works for any continuous $g$ and requires only the fundamental solutions $y_1, y_2$.
