4.3 Bessel and Legendre Equations

The Bessel and Legendre equations are the two most important special ODEs in mathematical physics, arising from the separation of the Laplacian in cylindrical and spherical coordinates respectively. Both are Frobenius-type problems at their singular points, and both yield complete orthogonal systems of functions on the relevant domains.

Bessel's Equation

Definition 4.3.1 — Bessel's Equation

For $\nu \geq 0$, Bessel's equation of order $\nu$ is $$x^2 y'' + x y' + (x^2 - \nu^2) y = 0.$$ The point $x = 0$ is a regular singular point with $p_0 = 1$, $q_0 = -\nu^2$, giving the indicial equation $r^2 - \nu^2 = 0$, so $r = \pm \nu$.

Bessel Functions of the First Kind

Taking $r = \nu$ (assuming $\nu \geq 0$) and applying the Frobenius method with $a_0 = 1/(2^\nu \Gamma(\nu+1))$ yields the Bessel function of the first kind:

$$J_\nu(x) = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{m!\,\Gamma(m+\nu+1)}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^{2m+\nu}.$$
Proposition 4.3.2 — Recurrence Relations for $J_\nu$

The following identities hold for Bessel functions:

  • $\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[x^\nu J_\nu(x)\bigr] = x^\nu J_{\nu-1}(x)$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[x^{-\nu}J_\nu(x)\bigr] = -x^{-\nu}J_{\nu+1}(x)$
  • $J_{\nu-1}(x) + J_{\nu+1}(x) = \frac{2\nu}{x}J_\nu(x)$ (three-term recurrence)
  • $J_{\nu-1}(x) - J_{\nu+1}(x) = 2J_\nu'(x)$

Bessel Functions of the Second Kind

When $2\nu \notin \mathbb{Z}$, the second independent solution is $J_{-\nu}(x)$. When $\nu = n$ is a non-negative integer, $J_{-n} = (-1)^n J_n$ and a second solution must involve $\ln x$. The standard second solution is the Bessel function of the second kind (Neumann function):

$$Y_\nu(x) = \frac{J_\nu(x)\cos(\nu\pi) - J_{-\nu}(x)}{\sin(\nu\pi)},$$

with the limit taken when $\nu = n \in \mathbb{Z}$. $Y_\nu(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to 0^+$, so it is excluded from solutions regular at the origin.

Orthogonality of Bessel Functions

Theorem 4.3.3 — Bessel Orthogonality

Let $\lambda_m, \lambda_n > 0$ be distinct zeros of $J_\nu$ (i.e.\ $J_\nu(\lambda_m) = J_\nu(\lambda_n) = 0$). Then $$\int_0^1 x\, J_\nu(\lambda_m x)\, J_\nu(\lambda_n x)\, dx = 0 \quad (m \neq n).$$ The normalisation is $$\int_0^1 x\, [J_\nu(\lambda_n x)]^2\, dx = \frac{1}{2}[J_{\nu+1}(\lambda_n)]^2.$$

Consequently any $f \in L^2(0,1;x\,dx)$ has a Bessel–Fourier (Fourier–Bessel) series: $$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n J_\nu(\lambda_n x), \quad c_n = \frac{2}{[J_{\nu+1}(\lambda_n)]^2}\int_0^1 x f(x) J_\nu(\lambda_n x)\,dx.$$

Legendre's Equation

Definition 4.3.4 — Legendre's Equation

Legendre's equation of order $n$ is $$(1-x^2)y'' - 2xy' + n(n+1)y = 0, \quad x \in (-1,1),$$ or equivalently $\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[(1-x^2)y'\bigr] + n(n+1)y = 0$. The points $x = \pm 1$ are regular singular points. The origin $x = 0$ is an ordinary point.

Legendre Polynomials

Power series about $x=0$ with the indicial equation analysis (ordinary point) gives two independent series. When $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, one of the series terminates, yielding the Legendre polynomial $P_n(x)$, which is the unique polynomial solution bounded at $x = \pm 1$, normalised by $P_n(1) = 1$.

Definition 4.3.5 — Rodrigues' Formula

The Legendre polynomials are given by $$P_n(x) = \frac{1}{2^n n!}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}(x^2-1)^n.$$ The first several are $P_0 = 1$, $P_1 = x$, $P_2 = \frac{1}{2}(3x^2-1)$, $P_3 = \frac{1}{2}(5x^3 - 3x)$, $P_4 = \frac{1}{8}(35x^4 - 30x^2 + 3)$.

Theorem 4.3.6 — Legendre Orthogonality

The Legendre polynomials are orthogonal on $[-1,1]$: $$\int_{-1}^1 P_m(x)\, P_n(x)\, dx = \frac{2}{2n+1}\,\delta_{mn}.$$

Any $f \in L^2(-1,1)$ has a Legendre series: $$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n P_n(x), \quad c_n = \frac{2n+1}{2}\int_{-1}^1 f(x)P_n(x)\,dx.$$ The series converges in $L^2(-1,1)$ and pointwise where $f$ is Lipschitz.

Proposition 4.3.7 — Three-Term Recurrence for Legendre Polynomials

$$(n+1)P_{n+1}(x) = (2n+1)xP_n(x) - nP_{n-1}(x), \quad n \geq 1.$$ This provides an efficient way to compute $P_n$ for higher $n$ without Rodrigues' formula.

Example 4.3.8 — Gravitational Potential Expansion

The gravitational (or electrostatic) potential due to a point source at distance $r'$ from the origin, evaluated at a field point at distance $r > r'$, expands as $$\frac{1}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|} = \frac{1}{r}\sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{r'}{r}\right)^n P_n(\cos\theta),$$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{r}'$. This is the generating function for the Legendre polynomials: $\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 2xt + t^2}} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty P_n(x) t^n$ for $|t| < 1$.
