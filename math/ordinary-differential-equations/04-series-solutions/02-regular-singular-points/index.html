4.2 Regular Singular Points

At a singular point the coefficient functions $P(x)$ or $Q(x)$ fail to be analytic. However, if the singularity is "mild enough" — classified as regular — the Frobenius method guarantees at least one solution of the form $x^r \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n$. The exponent $r$ is determined by the indicial equation, and the relationship between the two roots dictates the form of a second independent solution.

Classification of Singular Points

Definition 4.2.1 — Regular and Irregular Singular Points

For $y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0$, a singular point $x_0$ is called regular if $$\tilde{P}(x) := (x-x_0)P(x) \quad\text{and}\quad \tilde{Q}(x) := (x-x_0)^2 Q(x)$$ are both analytic at $x_0$. Equivalently, $P(x)$ has at most a simple pole and $Q(x)$ at most a double pole at $x_0$.

A singular point that is not regular is called irregular. The Frobenius method does not apply to irregular singular points.

The Frobenius Method

At a regular singular point $x_0 = 0$ (translate if needed), assume a Frobenius series:

$$y = x^r \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^{n+r}, \quad a_0 \neq 0.$$

Substituting into the ODE and extracting the lowest power of $x$ yields the indicial equation:

$$r(r-1) + p_0 r + q_0 = 0,$$

where $p_0 = \lim_{x\to 0} xP(x)$ and $q_0 = \lim_{x\to 0} x^2 Q(x)$. Let $r_1, r_2$ be the roots with $\operatorname{Re}(r_1) \geq \operatorname{Re}(r_2)$.

Theorem 4.2.2 — Frobenius Existence (Two Independent Solutions)

Let $x_0 = 0$ be a regular singular point. Let $r_1, r_2$ be roots of the indicial equation with $r_1 - r_2 \notin \mathbb{Z}_{\leq 0}$ (or more precisely, $r_1 \geq r_2$ as above). Then:

  • Case 1 ($r_1 - r_2 \notin \mathbb{Z}$): Two independent Frobenius solutions $y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum a_n x^n$ and $y_2 = x^{r_2}\sum b_n x^n$.
  • Case 2 ($r_1 = r_2$): One Frobenius solution $y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum a_n x^n$; second solution $y_2 = y_1 \ln x + x^{r_1}\sum b_n x^n$.
  • Case 3 ($r_1 - r_2 = N \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$): $y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum a_n x^n$; second solution $y_2 = c\, y_1 \ln x + x^{r_2}\sum b_n x^n$ where $c$ may be zero.

All series converge for $0 < |x| < R$ where $R$ is the distance from $x_0$ to the next nearest singular point in $\mathbb{C}$.

Example: Euler Equation

Example 4.2.3 — Euler (Cauchy–Euler) Equation

The Euler equation $x^2 y'' + \alpha x y' + \beta y = 0$ has $x=0$ as a regular singular point with $p_0 = \alpha$ and $q_0 = \beta$. The indicial equation is $$r(r-1) + \alpha r + \beta = 0 \iff r^2 + (\alpha-1)r + \beta = 0.$$ The general solution depends on the discriminant $\Delta = (\alpha-1)^2 - 4\beta$:

  • $\Delta > 0$, distinct real roots $r_1 > r_2$: $y = c_1 x^{r_1} + c_2 x^{r_2}$.
  • $\Delta = 0$, repeated root $r_1 = r_2 = r$: $y = x^r(c_1 + c_2 \ln x)$.
  • $\Delta < 0$, complex roots $r = \mu \pm i\nu$: $y = x^\mu\bigl[c_1 \cos(\nu\ln x) + c_2 \sin(\nu\ln x)\bigr]$.

The Euler equation is the "simplest" regular singular point problem because the Frobenius series terminates at $n=0$ — the solution is a pure power $x^r$. For general regular singular points the recursion generates infinitely many terms.

Example 4.2.4 — Logarithmic Second Solution

Consider $x y'' + y' = 0$, i.e.\ $(xy')' = 0$. Here $P = 1/x$, $Q = 0$, so $p_0 = 1$, $q_0 = 0$. Indicial equation: $r(r-1) + r = r^2 = 0$, giving a double root $r = 0$.

The first Frobenius solution with $r=0$: substituting $y_1 = \sum a_n x^n$ gives $y_1 = a_0$ (constant), i.e.\ $y_1 = 1$. (This can also be verified directly: $xy_1'' + y_1' = 0$ trivially.)

The second solution (Case 2) is $y_2 = \ln x + \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n x^n$. Substituting: $x\bigl(\frac{-1}{x^2} + \sum n(n-1)b_n x^{n-2}\bigr) + \bigl(\frac{1}{x} + \sum nb_n x^{n-1}\bigr) = 0$ simplifies to $\sum_{n=1}^\infty n^2 b_n x^{n-1} = 0$, so $b_n = 0$ for all $n \geq 1$. Thus $y_2 = \ln x$, and the general solution is $y = c_1 + c_2 \ln x$, consistent with direct integration of $(xy')' = 0$.

Remark — Irregular Singular Points

At an irregular singular point, Frobenius series generally diverge. Solutions may involve essential singularities and are typically studied via asymptotic (WKB) methods or Stokes phenomena. For example, $y'' - y/x^3 = 0$ has an irregular singular point at $x=0$ and solutions that are not even of Frobenius type.
