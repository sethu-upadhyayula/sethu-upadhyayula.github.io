5.2 Solving ODEs via Laplace

The general strategy for solving a linear constant-coefficient IVP via the Laplace transform is: (1) transform the ODE to an algebraic equation in $Y(s)$; (2) solve for $Y(s)$ in closed form; (3) decompose by partial fractions; (4) apply the inverse transform term by term. Initial conditions enter at step (1) and require no further adjustment.

The Method

Definition 5.2.1 — Inverse Laplace Transform

The inverse Laplace transform $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F\}(t)$ is defined by the Bromwich inversion integral $$f(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty} e^{st}F(s)\,ds, \quad t > 0,$$ where the vertical contour $\text{Re}(s) = c$ lies to the right of all singularities of $F$. In practice, inversion is performed by matching $F(s)$ (after partial fractions) to entries in the standard table.

Heaviside Step Function and Dirac Delta

Definition 5.2.2 — Heaviside Function and Dirac Delta

The Heaviside unit step is $$H(t-a) = \begin{cases}0 & t < a,\\ 1 & t \geq a,\end{cases} \qquad a \geq 0.$$ Its Laplace transform is $\mathcal{L}\{H(t-a)\}(s) = e^{-as}/s$.

The Dirac delta $\delta(t-a)$ is the distributional derivative of $H(t-a)$. It satisfies $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \varphi(t)\delta(t-a)\,dt = \varphi(a)$ for any continuous test function $\varphi$. Its transform is $$\mathcal{L}\{\delta(t-a)\}(s) = e^{-as}, \qquad a \geq 0.$$ For $a=0$: $\mathcal{L}\{\delta(t)\}(s) = 1$.

Physically, $\delta(t-a)$ models an impulsive force applied instantaneously at $t=a$; $H(t-a)$ switches on a forcing term at $t=a$.

Worked Example: Resonance

Example 5.2.3 — $y'' + 4y = \sin 2t$, $y(0) = 0$, $y'(0) = 1$

Taking the Laplace transform of both sides: $$(s^2 Y - sy(0) - y'(0)) + 4Y = \frac{2}{s^2+4}.$$ Inserting $y(0)=0$, $y'(0)=1$: $$(s^2+4)Y = 1 + \frac{2}{s^2+4},$$ $$Y(s) = \frac{1}{s^2+4} + \frac{2}{(s^2+4)^2}.$$ From the table, $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{1}{s^2+4}\right\} = \dfrac{1}{2}\sin 2t$. For the second term, use the identity $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{2\omega s}{(s^2+\omega^2)^2}\right\} = t\sin\omega t$ (differentiate $\mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega t\}$ with respect to $\omega$, then adjust). Here $$\frac{2}{(s^2+4)^2} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{4}{(s^2+4)^2}.$$ Using $\mathcal{L}\!\left\{\dfrac{\sin\omega t - \omega t\cos\omega t}{2\omega^3}\right\}(s) = \dfrac{1}{(s^2+\omega^2)^2}$ with $\omega=2$: $$\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\frac{2}{(s^2+4)^2}\right\} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\sin 2t - 2t\cos 2t}{4} = \frac{\sin 2t - 2t\cos 2t}{8}.$$ Therefore $$y(t) = \frac{1}{2}\sin 2t + \frac{\sin 2t - 2t\cos 2t}{8} = \frac{5\sin 2t}{8} - \frac{t\cos 2t}{4}.$$ The term $-\tfrac{t}{4}\cos 2t$ is the resonant response — it grows linearly in amplitude because the forcing frequency $\omega=2$ matches the natural frequency $\sqrt{4}=2$.

Verification: $y(0) = 0$ ✓; $y'(t) = \tfrac{5}{4}\cos 2t - \tfrac{1}{4}\cos 2t + \tfrac{1}{2}t\sin 2t$, so $y'(0) = 1$ ✓.

Piecewise Forcing with the Heaviside Function

Example 5.2.4 — Switch-on Forcing

Solve $y'' + y = f(t)$, $y(0)=0$, $y'(0)=0$, where $$f(t) = \begin{cases}1 & 0 \leq t < \pi,\\ 0 & t \geq \pi.\end{cases}$$ Write $f(t) = 1 - H(t-\pi)$. Then $$F(s) = \frac{1}{s} - \frac{e^{-\pi s}}{s}.$$ Transforming the ODE: $(s^2+1)Y = F(s)$, so $$Y(s) = \frac{1}{s(s^2+1)} - \frac{e^{-\pi s}}{s(s^2+1)}.$$ Partial fractions: $\dfrac{1}{s(s^2+1)} = \dfrac{1}{s} - \dfrac{s}{s^2+1}$. Hence $$y_1(t) = \mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\frac{1}{s} - \frac{s}{s^2+1}\right\} = 1 - \cos t.$$ By the second shift theorem, $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{e^{-\pi s}}{s(s^2+1)}\right\} = H(t-\pi)[1-\cos(t-\pi)] = H(t-\pi)[1+\cos t].$ Therefore $$y(t) = (1-\cos t) - H(t-\pi)(1+\cos t) = \begin{cases}1-\cos t, & 0 \leq t < \pi,\\ -2\cos t, & t \geq \pi.\end{cases}$$

The solution transitions smoothly at $t=\pi$: $y(\pi^-)=1-(-1)=2=y(\pi^+)$ ✓ and $y'(\pi^-)=\sin\pi=0=y'(\pi^+)$ ✓.

Remark — Partial Fractions Strategy

After solving $(a_n s^n + \cdots + a_0)Y(s) = G(s)$, the rational function $Y(s)$ is decomposed by partial fractions: real poles $s=r$ contribute $A/(s-r)$ terms (giving $Ae^{rt}$); repeated poles $s=r$ of multiplicity $k$ contribute $A_j/(s-r)^j$ terms (giving $A_j t^{j-1}e^{rt}/(j-1)!$); complex conjugate pairs $s = \alpha\pm i\beta$ contribute damped sinusoids $e^{\alpha t}(B\cos\beta t + C\sin\beta t)$.
