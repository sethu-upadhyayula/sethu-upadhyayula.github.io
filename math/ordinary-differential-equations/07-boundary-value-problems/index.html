7. Boundary Value Problems

Boundary value problems (BVPs) differ fundamentally from initial value problems: conditions are imposed at two (or more) separate points rather than all at one point in time. This changes the mathematical character of the problem - existence and uniqueness are no longer guaranteed by a direct analogue of the Picard-Lindelöf theorem, and the problem may have no solution, a unique solution, or infinitely many solutions depending on the boundary conditions and the ODE. BVPs arise naturally in steady-state problems - equilibrium temperatures, deflection of beams, quantum mechanics (Schrödinger equation with box boundary conditions), and normal-mode analysis.

This chapter covers three progressively more abstract levels: (1) direct solution of two-point BVPs via the shooting method and finite differences; (2) the Sturm-Liouville eigenvalue theory that underlies the spectral structure of self-adjoint differential operators; and (3) eigenfunction expansions that represent arbitrary functions as infinite series of the eigenfunctions - unifying Fourier series, Bessel series, and Legendre series under a single framework.

Chapter Contents

  • 7.1 Two-Point Boundary Problems - Shooting method; finite difference discretisation; Green's function for linear BVPs.
  • 7.2 Sturm-Liouville Theory - Self-adjoint operators; real eigenvalues; orthogonal eigenfunctions; Sturm comparison theorem; regular and singular problems.
  • 7.3 Eigenfunction Expansions - Generalised Fourier series; $L^2$ convergence; Parseval identity; Fourier sine, Fourier-Bessel, and Fourier-Legendre series.
