7.2 Sturm–Liouville Theory

Sturm–Liouville theory provides the spectral theory of a broad class of second-order linear differential operators. The key results — real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenfunctions, completeness — generalise the spectral theorem for symmetric matrices to infinite dimensions and underlie the convergence of all classical eigenfunction expansions.

The Sturm–Liouville Operator

Definition 7.2.1 — Sturm–Liouville Problem

A Sturm–Liouville (SL) problem consists of the equation $$\mathcal{L}[y] \equiv -\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[p(x)\frac{dy}{dx}\right] + q(x)y = \lambda\,w(x)\,y, \quad a < x < b,$$ together with boundary conditions at $x=a$ and $x=b$. Here:

  • $p, p', q, w$ are continuous on $[a,b]$;
  • $p(x) > 0$ and $w(x) > 0$ on $(a,b)$ (weight function);
  • $\lambda$ is the eigenvalue parameter.

The problem is regular if $p > 0$ and $w > 0$ on the closed interval $[a,b]$; it is singular if $p$ or $w$ vanish at an endpoint (as in the Legendre or Bessel equations). Standard separated BCs: $\alpha_1 y(a) + \alpha_2 y'(a) = 0$ and $\beta_1 y(b) + \beta_2 y'(b) = 0$ (not both coefficients zero at each end).

Self-Adjointness

Proposition 7.2.2 — Self-Adjointness

Define the weighted inner product $\langle u, v\rangle_w = \int_a^b u(x)\overline{v(x)}\,w(x)\,dx$. The SL operator $\mathcal{L}$ is self-adjoint with respect to this inner product (for functions satisfying the BCs): $$\langle \mathcal{L}u, v\rangle_w = \langle u, \mathcal{L}v\rangle_w.$$

Proof via Green's identity. Integrate by parts twice: $$\int_a^b \mathcal{L}[u]\cdot \overline{v}\,dx = \int_a^b \mathcal{L}[\overline{v}]\cdot u\,dx + \bigl[p(u'\overline{v} - u\overline{v}')\bigr]_a^b.$$

The boundary term $[p(u'\overline{v}-u\overline{v}')]_a^b$ vanishes when $u$ and $v$ satisfy the separated BCs, establishing self-adjointness. (This is the Lagrange identity.)

Main Spectral Theorem

Theorem 7.2.3 — Sturm–Liouville Spectral Theorem

For a regular SL problem:

  1. All eigenvalues are real.
  2. Eigenfunctions corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal with respect to $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_w$: $$\lambda_m \neq \lambda_n \implies \langle \phi_m, \phi_n\rangle_w = 0.$$
  3. Each eigenvalue has geometric multiplicity 1 (simple eigenvalues).
  4. Eigenvalues form a countably infinite sequence $\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 < \lambda_3 < \cdots \to +\infty$.
  5. The eigenfunctions $\{\phi_n\}$ form a complete orthogonal set in $L^2_w(a,b)$.

Proof sketch (reality). Suppose $\mathcal{L}[\phi] = \lambda w\phi$. Then $\langle\mathcal{L}\phi,\phi\rangle_w = \lambda\|\phi\|^2_w$. By self-adjointness, the left side equals $\langle\phi,\mathcal{L}\phi\rangle_w = \overline{\lambda}\|\phi\|^2_w$. Since $\|\phi\|^2_w > 0$, $\lambda = \overline{\lambda}$, so $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.

Oscillation and Comparison

Theorem 7.2.4 — Sturm Comparison Theorem

Let $y_1$ and $y_2$ be non-trivial solutions of $(p_1 y')' + q_1 y = 0$ and $(p_2 y')' + q_2 y = 0$ respectively on $(a,b)$, where $0 < p_2 \leq p_1$ and $q_1 \leq q_2$. Then between any two consecutive zeros of $y_1$ there is at least one zero of $y_2$. In particular, as $\lambda$ increases, the $n$-th eigenfunction $\phi_n$ has exactly $n-1$ interior zeros on $(a,b)$.

Examples as Sturm–Liouville Problems

Example 7.2.5 — Fourier Problem as SL

The problem $-y'' = \lambda y$, $y(0)=0$, $y(L)=0$ is SL with $p=1$, $q=0$, $w=1$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2$; eigenfunctions: $\phi_n(x) = \sin(n\pi x/L)$, $n=1,2,3,\ldots$ These are orthogonal: $\int_0^L \sin(m\pi x/L)\sin(n\pi x/L)\,dx = \frac{L}{2}\delta_{mn}$.

The eigenfunction expansion is the Fourier sine series (Section 7.3).

Example 7.2.6 — Legendre Equation as SL

The Legendre equation $(1-x^2)y'' - 2xy' + \lambda y = 0$ on $[-1,1]$ is written as $$-\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[(1-x^2)\frac{dy}{dx}\right] = \lambda y.$$ This is SL with $p(x) = 1-x^2$, $q=0$, $w=1$. The endpoint $x=\pm1$ are singular (since $p(\pm1)=0$), but with the natural boundary condition of boundedness the eigenvalues are $\lambda_n = n(n+1)$, $n=0,1,2,\ldots$, and the eigenfunctions are the Legendre polynomials $P_n(x)$: $$\int_{-1}^1 P_m(x)P_n(x)\,dx = \frac{2}{2n+1}\delta_{mn}.$$

The weight is $w=1$ here; the orthogonality is with respect to the ordinary $L^2(-1,1)$ inner product.

Example 7.2.7 — Bessel Equation as SL

The Bessel equation of order $\nu$: $x^2 y'' + xy' + (\mu^2 x^2 - \nu^2)y = 0$ can be rewritten as $$-\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[x\frac{dy}{dx}\right] + \frac{\nu^2}{x}y = \mu^2 x\,y.$$ This is SL with $p(x)=x$, $q(x)=\nu^2/x$, $w(x)=x$ on $(0,R)$. The endpoint $x=0$ is singular. With BCs $y(0)$ bounded and $y(R)=0$ (or $y'(R)=0$), the eigenvalues are $\mu^2 = j_{\nu,n}^2/R^2$ where $j_{\nu,n}$ are the positive zeros of $J_\nu$. Eigenfunctions $J_\nu(\mu_n x)$ are orthogonal with weight $w=x$: $\int_0^R J_\nu(\mu_m x)J_\nu(\mu_n x)\,x\,dx = \frac{R^2}{2}[J_{\nu+1}(\mu_n R)]^2\,\delta_{mn}$.
