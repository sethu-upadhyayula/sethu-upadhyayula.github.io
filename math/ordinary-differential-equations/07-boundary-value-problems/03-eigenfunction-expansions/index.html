7.3 Eigenfunction Expansions

The completeness of Sturm-Liouville eigenfunctions guarantees that arbitrary functions in the appropriate $L^2$ space can be expanded as infinite series. This section develops the general theory of such expansions and illustrates it with the three classical cases: Fourier sine series, Fourier-Bessel series, and Fourier-Legendre series.

Generalised Fourier Series

Definition 7.3.1 - Eigenfunction Expansion

Let $\{\phi_n\}_{n=1}^\infty$ be the orthogonal eigenfunctions of a regular SL problem on $(a,b)$ with weight $w$. The eigenfunction expansion (generalised Fourier series) of $f \in L^2_w(a,b)$ is $$f(x) \sim \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n\,\phi_n(x),$$ where the coefficients are $$c_n = \frac{\langle f, \phi_n\rangle_w}{\|\phi_n\|^2_w} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,\phi_n(x)\,w(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_a^b [\phi_n(x)]^2\,w(x)\,dx}.$$

The $\sim$ becomes equality in $L^2_w(a,b)$: the partial sums converge to $f$ in the mean-square sense, and under mild smoothness conditions (piecewise $C^1$), pointwise convergence holds at continuity points.

Theorem 7.3.2 - $L^2$ Convergence and Parseval's Identity

If $\{\phi_n\}$ is a complete orthogonal set in $L^2_w(a,b)$, then for every $f \in L^2_w(a,b)$: $$\left\|f - \sum_{n=1}^N c_n\phi_n\right\|^2_w \to 0 \quad \text{as } N\to\infty.$$ Moreover, Parseval's identity (Bessel's equality in the limit) holds: $$\|f\|^2_w = \sum_{n=1}^\infty |c_n|^2\,\|\phi_n\|^2_w, \quad \text{i.e., } \int_a^b [f(x)]^2 w(x)\,dx = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n^2\,\|\phi_n\|^2_w.$$

Parseval's identity is the analogue of the Pythagorean theorem in infinite dimensions: the "squared length" of $f$ equals the sum of squared "components" in each eigenfunction direction.

Theorem 7.3.3 - Completeness of SL Eigenfunctions

The eigenfunctions of a regular SL problem form a complete orthogonal basis of $L^2_w(a,b)$: the only function in $L^2_w$ orthogonal to every eigenfunction is the zero function. Equivalently, $\sum_{n=1}^\infty |c_n|^2\|\phi_n\|^2_w = \|f\|^2_w$ for all $f \in L^2_w$. Completeness is proved via the spectral theory of compact self-adjoint operators (the resolvent of $\mathcal{L}$ is compact on $L^2_w$).

Fourier Sine Series

Example 7.3.4 - Fourier Sine Series

SL problem: $-y'' = \lambda y$, $y(0)=0$, $y(L)=0$ (i.e., $p=w=1$, $q=0$). Eigenfunctions: $\phi_n(x) = \sin(n\pi x/L)$, eigenvalues $\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2$. Norms: $\|\phi_n\|^2 = L/2$. The expansion of $f \in L^2(0,L)$ is the Fourier sine series: $$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \sin\!\frac{n\pi x}{L}, \qquad b_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin\!\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx.$$

For $f(x) = 1$ on $(0,L)$: $b_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx = \frac{2}{n\pi}(1-\cos n\pi) = \begin{cases}4/(n\pi) & n\text{ odd},\\0 & n\text{ even}.\end{cases}$ So $1 = \frac{4}{\pi}\!\left(\sin\frac{\pi x}{L} + \frac{1}{3}\sin\frac{3\pi x}{L} + \cdots\right)$ on $(0,L)$. Parseval: $\int_0^L 1\,dx = L = \sum_{n\text{ odd}}\frac{16}{n^2\pi^2}\cdot\frac{L}{2}$, giving $\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{1}{(2n+1)^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{8}$.

Fourier-Bessel Series

Example 7.3.5 - Fourier-Bessel Series

SL problem: Bessel equation of order $\nu \geq 0$ on $(0,R)$ with $p(x)=x$, $q=\nu^2/x$, $w=x$, BCs: boundedness at $0$ and $y(R)=0$. Eigenfunctions: $\phi_n(x) = J_\nu(\mu_n x)$ where $\mu_n = j_{\nu,n}/R$ and $j_{\nu,n}$ is the $n$-th positive zero of $J_\nu$. The squared norm is $\|\phi_n\|^2_w = \frac{R^2}{2}[J_{\nu+1}(j_{\nu,n})]^2$.

The Fourier-Bessel expansion is $$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n\,J_\nu(\mu_n x), \qquad c_n = \frac{2}{R^2[J_{\nu+1}(j_{\nu,n})]^2}\int_0^R f(x)\,J_\nu(\mu_n x)\,x\,dx.$$

This arises when solving the heat equation or wave equation on a disk by separation of variables in polar coordinates: the radial part leads to the Bessel equation, and the angular-independent solution is expanded in this series.

Fourier-Legendre Series

Example 7.3.6 - Fourier-Legendre Series

SL problem: Legendre equation on $(-1,1)$ with $p=1-x^2$, $q=0$, $w=1$, bounded solutions. Eigenfunctions: $P_n(x)$ (Legendre polynomials), eigenvalues $\lambda_n = n(n+1)$. Norms: $\|P_n\|^2 = \frac{2}{2n+1}$.

The Fourier-Legendre expansion is $$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n\,P_n(x), \qquad c_n = \frac{2n+1}{2}\int_{-1}^1 f(x)\,P_n(x)\,dx.$$

The Legendre polynomials are given by Rodrigues' formula: $$P_n(x) = \frac{1}{2^n n!}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}(x^2-1)^n.$$ Thus $c_n = \frac{2n+1}{2}\int_{-1}^1 f(x)\,\frac{1}{2^n n!}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}(x^2-1)^n\,dx$. Integrating by parts $n$ times to shift derivatives from $P_n$ to $f$ is the standard technique to evaluate these integrals for smooth $f$.

Example: $f(x) = x^2$. Since $P_0=1$, $P_1=x$, $P_2=\frac{1}{2}(3x^2-1)$, we write $x^2 = \frac{1}{3}P_0 + \frac{2}{3}P_2$ (exact two-term expansion since $x^2$ is a degree-2 polynomial).

Remark - Completeness and PDE Applications

The completeness of SL eigenfunctions is the mathematical foundation for the method of separation of variables applied to PDEs. When solving $u_t = u_{xx}$ on $[0,L]$ with $u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0$, the ansatz $u = X(x)T(t)$ leads to the SL problem $-X'' = \lambda X$ with the same BCs. The general solution is $u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n e^{-\lambda_n t}\sin(n\pi x/L)$, and completeness ensures every $L^2$ initial datum $u(x,0)=f(x)$ can be matched by an appropriate choice of $\{b_n\}$.
