6.1 Lyapunov Stability

We consider the autonomous system $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, where $\mathbf{f}: \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is $C^1$. An equilibrium (or fixed point) is a point $\mathbf{x}^*$ with $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$; without loss of generality we shift coordinates so $\mathbf{x}^* = \mathbf{0}$.

Definitions of Stability

Definition 6.1.1 - Lyapunov Stability

The equilibrium $\mathbf{0}$ is Lyapunov stable (stable in the sense of Lyapunov) if: $$\forall\,\varepsilon > 0,\;\exists\,\delta > 0 \text{ such that } \|\mathbf{x}(0)\| < \delta \implies \|\mathbf{x}(t)\| < \varepsilon \quad \forall\, t \geq 0.$$ In words: solutions starting sufficiently close to $\mathbf{0}$ remain close for all future time.

The equilibrium is asymptotically stable if it is Lyapunov stable and additionally $$\exists\,\delta_0 > 0 \text{ such that } \|\mathbf{x}(0)\| < \delta_0 \implies \lim_{t\to\infty}\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{0}.$$

The equilibrium is globally asymptotically stable (GAS) if it is asymptotically stable with $\delta_0 = \infty$, i.e., $\lim_{t\to\infty}\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{0}$ for every initial condition.

The equilibrium is unstable if it is not Lyapunov stable: there exist $\varepsilon > 0$ and a sequence of initial conditions $\mathbf{x}_n(0) \to \mathbf{0}$ whose orbits exit the ball $\|\mathbf{x}\| < \varepsilon$.

Remark - Stability vs Asymptotic Stability

Lyapunov stability alone does not require attraction to the equilibrium - a stable spiral is also asymptotically stable, but a centre (pure oscillation) is Lyapunov stable without being asymptotically stable. Asymptotic stability is the stronger, more useful property for engineering applications.

Linearisation

Definition 6.1.2 - Linearisation at an Equilibrium

Let $\mathbf{f}$ be $C^1$ near $\mathbf{x}^* = \mathbf{0}$. Write $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{u}$ (perturbation). Taylor expanding: $$\mathbf{u}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{u}) = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{0})\,\mathbf{u} + O(\|\mathbf{u}\|^2),$$ where $D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{0})$ is the Jacobian matrix (the matrix of partial derivatives $\partial f_i/\partial x_j$ evaluated at $\mathbf{0}$). The linearisation is $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$, $A = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{0})$.

Theorem 6.1.3 - Hartman-Grobman Theorem

Suppose all eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$ of $A = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{0})$ satisfy $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) \neq 0$. Then in a neighbourhood of $\mathbf{0}$, the nonlinear flow of $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ is topologically conjugate (homeomorphic) to the linear flow of $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$. In particular:

  • If all $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0$: $\mathbf{0}$ is asymptotically stable (nonlinearly).
  • If some $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) > 0$: $\mathbf{0}$ is unstable (nonlinearly).

Equilibria satisfying $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) \neq 0$ for all $i$ are called hyperbolic. When eigenvalues lie on the imaginary axis (non-hyperbolic case, e.g. a centre), linearisation alone cannot determine stability and Lyapunov methods (Section 6.2) are required.

Examples

Example 6.1.4 - Simple Pendulum

The equation of motion for an undamped pendulum is $\theta'' + \sin\theta = 0$. Writing $x_1 = \theta$, $x_2 = \theta'$: $$x_1' = x_2, \qquad x_2' = -\sin x_1.$$ Equilibria: $(0,0)$ (hanging down) and $(\pi, 0)$ (inverted). Jacobian: $$A = D\mathbf{f} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\-\cos x_1 & 0\end{pmatrix}.$$ At $(0,0)$: $A = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, eigenvalues $\pm i$. Non-hyperbolic: linearisation gives a centre; the nonlinear system also has a centre (conserved energy $E = \frac{1}{2}x_2^2 + (1-\cos x_1)$), so $(0,0)$ is Lyapunov stable but not asymptotically stable.

At $(\pi, 0)$: $A = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, eigenvalues $\pm 1$. Hyperbolic saddle: $(\pi,0)$ is unstable.

Example 6.1.5 - Van der Pol Oscillator

The Van der Pol equation $\ddot{x} - \mu(1-x^2)\dot{x} + x = 0$ ($\mu > 0$) in phase-plane form: $$x_1' = x_2, \qquad x_2' = \mu(1-x_1^2)x_2 - x_1.$$ The only equilibrium is $(0,0)$. Jacobian there: $$A = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ -1 & \mu\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \text{tr}(A) = \mu > 0, \quad \det(A) = 1 > 0.$$ Both eigenvalues have positive real part (since trace $> 0$ and determinant $> 0$). By Hartman-Grobman, the origin is an unstable focus. Trajectories starting near the origin spiral outward and are attracted to the unique stable limit cycle (Poincaré-Bendixson + Bendixson-Dulac).

This illustrates that an unstable equilibrium can coexist with a globally attracting limit cycle.

Proposition 6.1.6 - Stability via Eigenvalues (Linear Systems)

For the linear system $\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}$: the origin is asymptotically stable if and only if all eigenvalues of $A$ have strictly negative real part; it is Lyapunov stable (but not asymptotically stable) if all eigenvalues have non-positive real part and every purely imaginary eigenvalue is semisimple (i.e., its geometric multiplicity equals its algebraic multiplicity); otherwise it is unstable.
