3.3 Equilibria and Stability

The qualitative behaviour of an autonomous system $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ is largely governed by its equilibria and the nature of the flow nearby. Linearisation converts this nonlinear question into a linear eigenvalue problem, and the Hartman–Grobman theorem tells us when the linearisation faithfully represents the local dynamics.

Equilibrium Points

Definition 3.3.1 — Equilibrium (Fixed Point)

A point $\mathbf{x}^* \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an equilibrium (or fixed point) of the autonomous system $$\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}), \quad \mathbf{f}: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n,$$ if $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$. The constant function $\mathbf{x}(t) \equiv \mathbf{x}^*$ is then a solution for all $t$.

Linearisation at an Equilibrium

Suppose $\mathbf{f}$ is $C^1$ in a neighbourhood of $\mathbf{x}^*$. Write $\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{x}^* + \boldsymbol{\xi}(t)$ where $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ is a small perturbation. Taylor-expanding:

$$\boldsymbol{\xi}' = \mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^* + \boldsymbol{\xi}) = \underbrace{\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*)}_{=\mathbf{0}} + J\boldsymbol{\xi} + O(\|\boldsymbol{\xi}\|^2),$$

where $J = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*)$ is the Jacobian matrix evaluated at the equilibrium:

$$J_{ij} = \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\bigg|_{\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^*}.$$

The linearised system is $\boldsymbol{\xi}' = J\boldsymbol{\xi}$. Its behaviour is determined entirely by the eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n$ of $J$.

Classification of Planar Equilibria

For a two-dimensional system $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ with $\mathbf{x} = (x,y)^T$, let $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ be the eigenvalues of $J$. The trace $\tau = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \operatorname{tr}(J)$ and determinant $\Delta = \lambda_1\lambda_2 = \det(J)$ give a concise classification.

Definition 3.3.2 — Classification of Hyperbolic Equilibria (Planar)

Assume $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ are the eigenvalues of $J$ at an equilibrium $\mathbf{x}^*$.

  • Stable node: both eigenvalues real with $\lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 < 0$. Trajectories converge to $\mathbf{x}^*$ along the eigendirections.
  • Unstable node: both eigenvalues real with $0 < \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2$. Trajectories diverge from $\mathbf{x}^*$.
  • Saddle point: real eigenvalues of opposite sign, $\lambda_1 < 0 < \lambda_2$. Stable manifold (one dimension) and unstable manifold (one dimension) meet at $\mathbf{x}^*$.
  • Stable spiral: complex conjugate eigenvalues $\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta$ with $\alpha < 0$. Trajectories spiral inward to $\mathbf{x}^*$.
  • Unstable spiral: complex conjugate eigenvalues with $\alpha > 0$. Trajectories spiral outward.
  • Centre: purely imaginary eigenvalues $\lambda = \pm i\beta$, $\beta \neq 0$. Closed orbits surround $\mathbf{x}^*$.
Remark — Discriminant Criterion

With $\tau = \operatorname{tr}(J)$ and $\Delta = \det(J)$: if $\Delta < 0$ the equilibrium is a saddle; if $\Delta > 0$ and $\tau^2 - 4\Delta > 0$ it is a node; if $\Delta > 0$ and $\tau^2 - 4\Delta < 0$ it is a spiral; if $\tau = 0$ and $\Delta > 0$ it is a centre (for the linear system).

The Hartman–Grobman Theorem

Definition 3.3.3 — Hyperbolic Equilibrium

An equilibrium $\mathbf{x}^*$ is called hyperbolic if no eigenvalue of $J = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*)$ has zero real part, i.e.\ $\operatorname{Re}(\lambda_i) \neq 0$ for all $i$.

Theorem 3.3.4 — Hartman–Grobman

Let $\mathbf{x}^*$ be a hyperbolic equilibrium of $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ with $\mathbf{f} \in C^1$. Then there exist neighbourhoods $U$ of $\mathbf{x}^*$ and $V$ of the origin, and a homeomorphism $h: U \to V$, such that $h$ maps trajectories of $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ in $U$ to trajectories of the linearised system $\boldsymbol{\xi}' = J\boldsymbol{\xi}$ in $V$, preserving the direction of time. In particular, the qualitative local phase portrait of the nonlinear system is topologically equivalent to that of its linearisation.

Remark — Non-Hyperbolic Equilibria

When $\mathbf{x}^*$ is non-hyperbolic (e.g.\ a centre of the linearisation), the Hartman–Grobman theorem does not apply and higher-order terms determine the actual stability. A centre of the linearisation may be a stable spiral, unstable spiral, or true centre in the full nonlinear system.

Example: The Nonlinear Pendulum

Example 3.3.5 — Pendulum

The undamped pendulum equation $\theta'' + \frac{g}{L}\sin\theta = 0$ is written as the system

$$\begin{pmatrix}x \\ y\end{pmatrix}' = \begin{pmatrix}y \\ -\frac{g}{L}\sin x\end{pmatrix}, \quad x = \theta,\; y = \theta'.$$

Equilibria satisfy $y = 0$ and $\sin x = 0$, giving $\mathbf{x}^* = (n\pi, 0)$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

The Jacobian is $$J = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ -\frac{g}{L}\cos x & 0\end{pmatrix}.$$

At $(0,0)$ (hanging position): $J = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-g/L&0\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvalues $\lambda = \pm i\sqrt{g/L}$. Purely imaginary — linear system is a centre. The full nonlinear system is also a centre (energy conservation can be used to verify closed orbits).

At $(\pi,0)$ (inverted position): $J = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\g/L&0\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvalues $\lambda = \pm\sqrt{g/L}$. Real with opposite signs — this is a saddle point. By Hartman–Grobman (the equilibrium is hyperbolic), the local phase portrait is topologically a saddle.

Example: Predator–Prey (Lotka–Volterra)

Example 3.3.6 — Lotka–Volterra System

The classical predator–prey model is

$$\begin{cases}x' = ax - bxy \\ y' = -cy + dxy\end{cases}, \quad a,b,c,d > 0,$$

where $x(t)$ is prey population and $y(t)$ is predator population.

Equilibria:

  • $\mathbf{x}^* = (0,0)$: trivial equilibrium.
  • $\mathbf{x}^* = (c/d,\, a/b)$: coexistence equilibrium.

The Jacobian is $$J = \begin{pmatrix}a - by & -bx \\ dy & -c + dx\end{pmatrix}.$$

At $(0,0)$: $J = \begin{pmatrix}a&0\\0&-c\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvalues $a > 0$ and $-c < 0$. Saddle point.

At $(c/d, a/b)$: $$J = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -bc/d \\ da/b & 0\end{pmatrix},$$ eigenvalues $\lambda = \pm i\sqrt{ac}$. Purely imaginary — the linearisation is a centre. The full nonlinear system is also a centre: the conserved quantity $$H(x,y) = d x - c\ln x + b y - a\ln y = \text{const}$$ gives closed orbits around the coexistence equilibrium.

This non-hyperbolic equilibrium cannot be analysed by Hartman–Grobman; the conservation law must be invoked directly.

Remark — Stable vs Asymptotically Stable

A centre is Lyapunov stable (nearby trajectories stay nearby) but not asymptotically stable (they do not converge to the equilibrium). Both the pendulum equilibrium at the origin and the Lotka–Volterra coexistence equilibrium are stable but not asymptotically stable. For the formal $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definitions, see Chapter 6.
