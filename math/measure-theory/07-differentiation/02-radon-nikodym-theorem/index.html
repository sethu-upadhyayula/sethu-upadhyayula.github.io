7.2 Radon-Nikodym Theorem

The Radon-Nikodym theorem is the abstract counterpart of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, but for measures. It asks: when can one measure be "differentiated" with respect to another? The answer involves the concept of absolute continuity for measures.

Absolute Continuity of Measures

Definition 7.2.1 - Absolute Continuity of Measures

Let $\mu, \nu$ be measures on $(X, \mathcal{M})$. We say $\nu$ is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mu$, written $\nu \ll \mu$, if: $$\mu(E) = 0 \implies \nu(E) = 0 \quad \text{for all } E \in \mathcal{M}.$$

Informally: "$\mu$-null sets are also $\nu$-null." Equivalently (when $\mu, \nu$ are $\sigma$-finite): for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\mu(E) < \delta \Rightarrow \nu(E) < \varepsilon$.

Example 7.2.2 - Examples of Absolute Continuity
  • If $g \geq 0$ is measurable and $\nu(E) = \int_E g\,d\mu$, then $\nu \ll \mu$ (if $\mu(E)=0$ then $\int_E g = 0$).
  • The normal distribution $\nu = N(0,1)$ satisfies $\nu \ll \lambda$ (Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}$) with $d\nu/d\lambda = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$.
  • The Dirac delta $\delta_0$ satisfies $\delta_0 \not\ll \lambda$ since $\lambda(\{0\}) = 0$ but $\delta_0(\{0\}) = 1$.

The Radon-Nikodym Theorem

Theorem 7.2.3 - Radon-Nikodym Theorem

Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ be a $\sigma$-finite measure space and $\nu$ a $\sigma$-finite measure with $\nu \ll \mu$. Then there exists a unique (up to $\mu$-a.e. equality) non-negative measurable function $h \in L^1(\mu)$ such that: $$\nu(E) = \int_E h \, d\mu \quad \text{for all } E \in \mathcal{M}.$$

The function $h$ is called the Radon-Nikodym derivative (or density) of $\nu$ with respect to $\mu$, written $h = \frac{d\nu}{d\mu}$.

More generally, for $f \in L^1(\nu)$: $\int_X f \, d\nu = \int_X f \cdot \frac{d\nu}{d\mu} \, d\mu$.

Proof Sketch (via Hilbert space methods)

Assume $\mu$ and $\nu$ are both finite. Consider $L^2(X, \mu + \nu)$ and the linear functional $T(f) = \int f\,d\nu$. By the Riesz representation theorem for Hilbert spaces, there exists $g \in L^2(\mu+\nu)$ with $T(f) = \int fg\,d(\mu+\nu)$, i.e., $\int f\,d\nu = \int fg\,d(\mu+\nu) = \int fg\,d\mu + \int fg\,d\nu$.

Rearranging: $\int f(1-g)\,d\nu = \int fg\,d\mu$. Setting $h = g/(1-g)$ on the set $\{g < 1\}$ (which has $\nu$-full measure, using $\nu \ll \mu$), one obtains $\nu(E) = \int_E h\,d\mu$. $\square$

Properties and Consequences

Proposition 7.2.4 - Chain Rule for Radon-Nikodym Derivatives

If $\nu \ll \mu \ll \lambda$ (all $\sigma$-finite), then $\nu \ll \lambda$ and: $$\frac{d\nu}{d\lambda} = \frac{d\nu}{d\mu} \cdot \frac{d\mu}{d\lambda} \quad \mu\text{-a.e.}$$

If additionally $\mu \ll \nu$, then $\frac{d\mu}{d\nu} = \left(\frac{d\nu}{d\mu}\right)^{-1}$ $\mu$-a.e.

Theorem 7.2.5 - Lebesgue Decomposition

For $\sigma$-finite measures $\mu, \nu$, there is a unique decomposition $\nu = \nu_{\text{ac}} + \nu_s$ where $\nu_{\text{ac}} \ll \mu$ (absolutely continuous part) and $\nu_s \perp \mu$ (singular part: there exists $S$ with $\mu(S) = 0$ and $\nu_s(S^c) = 0$).

The Radon-Nikodym theorem gives the density for $\nu_{\text{ac}}$. For example, on $\mathbb{R}$: any Borel measure decomposes into an absolutely continuous part (density with respect to $\lambda$), a singular continuous part (like the Cantor measure), and a pure point part (countable sum of point masses).

Example 7.2.6 - Conditional Expectation as Radon-Nikodym Derivative

In probability theory, if $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a probability space, $X \in L^1(P)$, and $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ is a sub-$\sigma$-algebra, the conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the Radon-Nikodym derivative of the measure $A \mapsto \int_A X\,dP$ with respect to $P|_\mathcal{G}$. This abstract definition captures all the properties of conditional expectation and works for arbitrary $\sigma$-algebras.
