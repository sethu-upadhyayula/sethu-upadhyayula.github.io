Chapter 7: Differentiation

The final chapter ties together integration and differentiation in the measure-theoretic framework. The classical Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says that differentiation and integration are inverse operations; here we make this precise and general.

Three major results structure this chapter:

  • Absolute continuity: characterises which functions arise as integrals, giving a complete form of the FTC for the Lebesgue integral.
  • Radon-Nikodym theorem: solves the abstract problem of "differentiating" one measure with respect to another, yielding the Radon-Nikodym derivative $d\nu/d\mu$.
  • Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem: shows that a locally integrable function equals the limit of its averages over shrinking balls, a.e. — a powerful pointwise result.

Sections

Absolute continuity, the FTC for Lebesgue integrals, and characterisation as integrals of $L^1$ functions.

7.3
Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem

$f(x) = \lim_{r\to 0}\frac{1}{|B_r|}\int_{B_r} f$ a.e., Hardy-Littlewood maximal inequality.
