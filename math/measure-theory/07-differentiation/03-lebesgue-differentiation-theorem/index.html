7.3 Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem

The Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem is a remarkable result: it says that for almost every point $x$, the average of a locally integrable function over a ball $B_r(x)$ converges to $f(x)$ as $r \to 0$. In other words, a locally integrable function is determined almost everywhere by its local averages.

The Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Function

The key technical tool is the Hardy-Littlewood maximal function, which controls the supremum of all averages.

Definition 7.3.1 - Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Function

For $f \in L^1_{\text{loc}}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ (locally integrable), the Hardy-Littlewood maximal function is: $$(Mf)(x) = \sup_{r > 0} \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} |f(y)| \, dy,$$ where $B_r(x)$ is the open ball of radius $r$ centred at $x$ and $|B_r(x)| = \lambda^n(B_r(x))$.

Theorem 7.3.2 - Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Inequality

There exists a constant $C_n > 0$ (depending only on dimension $n$) such that for all $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and all $\alpha > 0$: $$\lambda^n(\{x : Mf(x) > \alpha\}) \leq \frac{C_n}{\alpha} \|f\|_{L^1}.$$

That is, $Mf$ is of weak type $(1,1)$. One may take $C_n = 3^n$. For $p > 1$, one has the stronger bound $\|Mf\|_{L^p} \leq C_{n,p} \|f\|_{L^p}$ (strong type $(p,p)$).

Proof Sketch (Vitali Covering Lemma)

Let $E_\alpha = \{Mf > \alpha\}$. For each $x \in E_\alpha$, pick $r_x > 0$ with $\int_{B_{r_x}(x)} |f| > \alpha |B_{r_x}(x)|$. The collection $\{B_{r_x}(x)\}$ covers $E_\alpha$. The Vitali covering lemma extracts a disjoint subcollection $\{B_{r_{x_i}}(x_i)\}$ such that $E_\alpha \subset \bigcup_i B_{3r_{x_i}}(x_i)$. Then: $$\lambda^n(E_\alpha) \leq \sum_i \lambda^n(B_{3r_{x_i}}) = 3^n \sum_i \lambda^n(B_{r_{x_i}}) \leq \frac{3^n}{\alpha} \sum_i \int_{B_{r_{x_i}}}|f| \leq \frac{3^n}{\alpha}\|f\|_1. \quad\square$$

The Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem

Theorem 7.3.3 - Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem

For $f \in L^1_{\text{loc}}(\mathbb{R}^n)$: $$\lim_{r \to 0} \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} f(y) \, dy = f(x) \quad \text{for } \lambda^n\text{-a.e. } x \in \mathbb{R}^n.$$

Points where this limit holds and equals $f(x)$ are called Lebesgue points of $f$. The theorem says $\lambda^n$-a.e. $x$ is a Lebesgue point.

Proof Sketch

Define the oscillation $A_r f(x) = \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|}\int_{B_r} f$. It suffices to show $A_r f(x) \to f(x)$ a.e. For continuous $g$ with compact support, $A_r g(x) \to g(x)$ everywhere (easy). For general $f \in L^1_{\text{loc}}$, approximate $f \approx g + h$ where $g$ is continuous with compact support and $\|h\|_{L^1}$ is small. Then: $$|A_r f(x) - f(x)| \leq |A_r g(x) - g(x)| + 2Mh(x) + |g(x) - f(x)|.$$ The first term $\to 0$ for all $x$; the last two are controlled by the maximal inequality and go to 0 a.e. $\square$

Consequences

Corollary 7.3.4 - $f$ Determined A.E. by Its Averages

If $f \in L^1_{\text{loc}}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and $\int_{B_r(x)} f = 0$ for all balls $B_r(x)$ (or for a.e. $x$ and all small $r$), then $f = 0$ a.e. More generally, two locally integrable functions with the same local averages a.e. must agree a.e.

Corollary 7.3.5 - Density of Sets

For a measurable set $E \subset \mathbb{R}^n$, define the density of $E$ at $x$ as $d(x, E) = \lim_{r\to 0} \frac{\lambda(E \cap B_r(x))}{\lambda(B_r(x))}$. By the Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem applied to $f = \mathbf{1}_E$:

  • $d(x, E) = 1$ for a.e. $x \in E$ (density-1 points of $E$),
  • $d(x, E) = 0$ for a.e. $x \notin E$ (density-0 points).
Remark 7.3.6 - Extensions and Limitations

The theorem extends to all $L^p_{\text{loc}}$ spaces ($p \geq 1$) and to averages over cubes or other "regular" sets. It fails for general sequences of sets shrinking to $x$ if they are not "regular." For example, if $B_r$ is replaced by very thin rectangles not containing $x$ in their interior relative to their longer axis, the averages may not converge to $f(x)$.

The theorem is one of the cornerstones connecting the abstract integration theory developed in this course to the concrete structure of functions in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
