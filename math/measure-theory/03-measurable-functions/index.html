Chapter 3: Measurable Functions

Measurable functions are the correct morphisms between measure spaces — they are the functions for which preimages of measurable sets are measurable. Just as continuous functions are the natural maps between topological spaces, measurable functions are the natural maps between measurable spaces.

This chapter defines measurability, studies simple functions (the building blocks of integration), and proves the fundamental approximation theorem: every non-negative measurable function is a pointwise limit of increasing simple functions.

Chapter Overview

3.1 — Measurability

A function $f : (X, \mathcal{M}) \to (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B})$ is measurable if $f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{M}$ for all Borel $B$, equivalently if $\{f > c\} \in \mathcal{M}$ for all $c \in \mathbb{R}$. Sums, products, compositions, and pointwise limits of measurable functions are measurable. Continuous functions are Borel measurable.

3.2 — Simple Functions

A simple function $s = \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \mathbf{1}_{E_i}$ takes finitely many non-negative values on measurable sets. The integral $\int s\,d\mu = \sum c_i \mu(E_i)$ is defined by linearity, and satisfies monotonicity and linearity properties that foreshadow the general integral.

3.3 — Approximation by Simple Functions

Every non-negative measurable function $f$ is the pointwise limit of an increasing sequence of simple functions $s_n \nearrow f$. For bounded $f$, convergence is uniform on sets of finite measure. This approximation theorem is the engine behind the definition and properties of the Lebesgue integral.
