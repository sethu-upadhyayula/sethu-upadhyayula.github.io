5.3 Completeness of $L^p$

A normed vector space is complete if every Cauchy sequence converges. Complete normed spaces are called Banach spaces - they are the correct setting for functional analysis, operator theory, and applications. The Riesz-Fischer theorem establishes that $L^p$ is always complete.

The Riesz-Fischer Theorem

Theorem 5.3.1 - Riesz-Fischer Theorem

For $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, the space $L^p(\mu)$ is complete: every Cauchy sequence in $L^p$ converges in $L^p$ to an element of $L^p$. That is, $L^p(\mu)$ is a Banach space.

Proof for $1 \leq p < \infty$

Step 1: Extract a fast Cauchy subsequence. Let $\{f_n\}$ be Cauchy in $L^p$. Choose a subsequence $\{f_{n_k}\}$ with $\|f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k}\|_p < 2^{-k}$.

Step 2: Absolute convergence implies convergence. Define $g_N(x) = \sum_{k=1}^N |f_{n_{k+1}}(x) - f_{n_k}(x)|$ and $g = \lim_N g_N$. By Minkowski: $$\|g_N\|_p \leq \sum_{k=1}^N \|f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k}\|_p < \sum_{k=1}^\infty 2^{-k} = 1.$$ By MCT, $\|g\|_p \leq 1 < \infty$, so $g \in L^p$ and $g < \infty$ a.e.

Step 3: The subsequence converges a.e. On the set where $g < \infty$ (i.e., a.e.), the telescoping series $f_{n_k}(x) = f_{n_1}(x) + \sum_{j=1}^{k-1}(f_{n_{j+1}}(x) - f_{n_j}(x))$ converges absolutely. Define $f(x)$ as this limit where $g < \infty$, and $f(x) = 0$ elsewhere.

Step 4: $f_{n_k} \to f$ in $L^p$. We have $|f_{n_k} - f|^p \leq (2g)^p \in L^1$ and $f_{n_k} \to f$ a.e. By DCT: $\|f_{n_k} - f\|_p \to 0$.

Step 5: The full sequence converges. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N$ so $\|f_m - f_n\|_p < \varepsilon/2$ for $m,n \geq N$, and $k$ so $n_k \geq N$ and $\|f_{n_k} - f\|_p < \varepsilon/2$. Then $\|f_n - f\|_p \leq \|f_n - f_{n_k}\|_p + \|f_{n_k} - f\|_p < \varepsilon$. $\square$

Remark 5.3.2 - Completeness of $L^\infty$

For $p = \infty$: a Cauchy sequence $\{f_n\}$ in $L^\infty$ satisfies $\|f_m - f_n\|_\infty < \varepsilon$ for large $m,n$, meaning $|f_m(x) - f_n(x)| < \varepsilon$ for a.e. $x$. On the null set complement, $\{f_n(x)\}$ is a pointwise Cauchy sequence in $\mathbb{R}$, converging to some $f(x)$. One then verifies $f \in L^\infty$ and $\|f_n - f\|_\infty \to 0$.

$L^2$ as a Hilbert Space

Theorem 5.3.3 - $L^2$ is a Hilbert Space

The space $L^2(\mu)$ is a Hilbert space with inner product: $$\langle f, g \rangle = \int fg \, d\mu \quad (\text{real case}), \qquad \langle f, g \rangle = \int f \overline{g} \, d\mu \quad (\text{complex case}).$$

This is well-defined by Hölder's inequality (Cauchy-Schwarz). The induced norm is $\|f\|_2 = \sqrt{\langle f,f\rangle} = \left(\int |f|^2\,d\mu\right)^{1/2}$. Since $L^2$ is also complete, it is a Hilbert space.

The parallelogram law $\|f+g\|_2^2 + \|f-g\|_2^2 = 2\|f\|_2^2 + 2\|g\|_2^2$ characterises Hilbert spaces among Banach spaces (it holds in $L^2$ but fails in $L^p$ for $p \neq 2$).

Remark 5.3.4 - Separability and Orthonormal Bases

When $\mu$ is $\sigma$-finite and the underlying space is second-countable (e.g., $\mu = \lambda$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$), $L^2(\mu)$ is separable. By the theory of Hilbert spaces, every separable Hilbert space is isometrically isomorphic to $\ell^2(\mathbb{N})$, the space of square-summable sequences.

In $L^2([0,1], \lambda)$, the trigonometric functions $\{e^{2\pi i n x}\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ form a complete orthonormal system - the foundation of Fourier analysis.

Corollary 5.3.5 - Convergence in $L^p$ Has Subsequence Converging A.E.

If $f_n \to f$ in $L^p$ ($1 \leq p \leq \infty$), then there exists a subsequence $f_{n_k} \to f$ pointwise $\mu$-a.e. Note that $L^p$ convergence alone does not imply pointwise a.e. convergence (only along a subsequence).
