6.1 Product Sigma-Algebras

To integrate functions of two variables $(x, y) \in X \times Y$, we need a suitable $\sigma$-algebra on the product space and a measure on it. This section constructs the product $\sigma$-algebra and product measure.

The Product $\sigma$-Algebra

Definition 6.1.1 — Product $\sigma$-Algebra

Let $(X, \mathcal{A})$ and $(Y, \mathcal{B})$ be measurable spaces. The product $\sigma$-algebra is: $$\mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B} = \sigma(\{A \times B : A \in \mathcal{A},\, B \in \mathcal{B}\}),$$ the $\sigma$-algebra on $X \times Y$ generated by all measurable rectangles $A \times B$.

The product measurable space is $(X \times Y, \mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B})$.

Proposition 6.1.2 — Sections are Measurable

If $E \in \mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B}$, then for every $x \in X$ and $y \in Y$, the sections: $$E_x = \{y \in Y : (x, y) \in E\} \in \mathcal{B}, \qquad E^y = \{x \in X : (x,y) \in E\} \in \mathcal{A}.$$

Similarly, if $f : X \times Y \to \mathbb{R}$ is $(\mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B})$-measurable, then the sections $f_x(y) = f(x,y)$ and $f^y(x) = f(x,y)$ are $\mathcal{B}$- and $\mathcal{A}$-measurable respectively.

Example 6.1.3 — Borel Sets on $\mathbb{R}^2$

The product $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) \otimes \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ equals $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^2)$, the Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}^2$. This extends to $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^m) \otimes \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^{m+n})$.

The Product Measure

Theorem 6.1.4 — Existence and Uniqueness of Product Measure

Let $(X, \mathcal{A}, \mu)$ and $(Y, \mathcal{B}, \nu)$ be $\sigma$-finite measure spaces. There exists a unique measure $\mu \otimes \nu$ on $(X \times Y, \mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B})$ such that: $$(\mu \otimes \nu)(A \times B) = \mu(A) \cdot \nu(B) \quad \text{for all } A \in \mathcal{A},\, B \in \mathcal{B}.$$

The product measure satisfies: $$(\mu \otimes \nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x) \, d\mu(x) = \int_Y \mu(E^y) \, d\nu(y) \quad \text{for all } E \in \mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B}.$$

Uniqueness follows from the $\sigma$-finiteness assumption and Carathéodory's extension theorem.

Proof Sketch

Define a pre-measure on the algebra of finite disjoint unions of measurable rectangles by $\pi(A \times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B)$. One checks $\pi$ is $\sigma$-additive on this algebra. By Carathéodory's extension theorem (Chapter 2), $\pi$ extends uniquely to a measure on $\mathcal{A} \otimes \mathcal{B}$, thanks to $\sigma$-finiteness ensuring uniqueness.

The formula $(\mu\otimes\nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu$ is verified first for measurable rectangles, then extended to $\mathcal{A}\otimes\mathcal{B}$ via the monotone class theorem. $\square$

Remark 6.1.5 — $\sigma$-Finiteness is Necessary

Without $\sigma$-finiteness, the product measure may not be unique. For example, take $\mu$ = Lebesgue measure and $\nu$ = counting measure on $[0,1]$. The diagonal $D = \{(x,x)\}$ has $\int \nu(D_x)\,d\mu = \int 1 \,d\mu = 1$ but $\int \mu(D^y)\,d\nu = \int 0 \,d\nu = 0$. Here $\nu$ is not $\sigma$-finite, and Fubini fails.

Example 6.1.6 — Lebesgue Measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$

The $n$-dimensional Lebesgue measure $\lambda^n$ equals the $n$-fold product measure $\lambda \otimes \cdots \otimes \lambda$. The product $\sigma$-algebra of $n$ copies of $\mathcal{L}$ (Lebesgue $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{R}$) is contained in the $n$-dimensional Lebesgue $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{L}^n$, with agreement on $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)$.
