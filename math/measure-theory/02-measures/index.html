Chapter 2: Measures

A measure assigns a non-negative "size" to each measurable set, generalising length, area, volume, probability, and counting. The key axiom is countable additivity: the measure of a countable disjoint union equals the sum of the individual measures.

This chapter develops the abstract theory of measures, introduces outer measures as a preliminary tool defined on all subsets, and constructs Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}$ as the canonical example.

Chapter Overview

2.1 — Measures and Outer Measures

We define measures via countable additivity and derive monotonicity, countable subadditivity, and continuity from below and above. Outer measures are defined on all subsets and serve as a stepping stone to genuine measures.

2.2 — Carathéodory's Extension Theorem

Carathéodory's criterion identifies which sets are "split cleanly" by the outer measure. These Carathéodory-measurable sets form a $\sigma$-algebra, and the restriction of the outer measure to them is a complete measure. This yields the fundamental extension theorem: a premeasure on an algebra extends to a measure on the generated $\sigma$-algebra.

2.3 — Lebesgue Measure

Applying the Carathéodory construction to the length function on intervals yields Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $\mathbb{R}$. We verify $\lambda([a,b]) = b - a$, prove translation invariance, and examine null sets including the uncountable Cantor set. The Vitali construction shows non-measurable sets exist.
