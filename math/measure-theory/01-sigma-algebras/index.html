Chapter 1: Sigma-Algebras

Before we can assign sizes to sets, we must decide which sets are eligible to be measured. The answer is not "all sets" - the Vitali construction shows that trying to measure every subset of $\mathbb{R}$ leads to contradictions. Instead, we work with a distinguished collection of sets closed under the operations that arise naturally in analysis.

$\sigma$-algebras are exactly the right notion of "measurable subsets", enabling a rigorous and self-consistent theory of size, integration, and probability.

Chapter Overview

1.1 - Sigma-Algebras and Generated Sigma-Algebras

We define $\sigma$-algebras as collections closed under complements and countable unions, prove they are also closed under countable intersections, and construct the smallest $\sigma$-algebra generated by any class $\mathcal{C}$ via intersection. We introduce measurable spaces and discuss the trivial, discrete, and non-measurable (Vitali) examples.

1.2 - Borel Sets

The Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is generated by the open sets of $\mathbb{R}$, or equivalently by all intervals. We describe the Borel hierarchy: open sets, closed sets, $G_\delta$ sets, $F_\sigma$ sets, and beyond. Every open set in $\mathbb{R}$ is a countable union of open intervals. Non-Borel analytic sets exist, showing the Borel hierarchy is strict.
