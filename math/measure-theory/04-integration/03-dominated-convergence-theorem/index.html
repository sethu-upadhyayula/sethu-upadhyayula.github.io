4.3 Dominated Convergence Theorem

The Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT) is the workhorse of Lebesgue integration. It allows one to pass the limit inside the integral whenever the sequence is pointwise convergent and controlled by a single integrable function. It is used constantly in analysis, probability, and PDEs.

Statement and Proof

Theorem 4.3.1 - Lebesgue's Dominated Convergence Theorem

Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ be a measure space. Suppose $\{f_n\}$ is a sequence of measurable functions such that:

  1. $f_n(x) \to f(x)$ for $\mu$-a.e. $x$ (pointwise convergence a.e.),
  2. There exists $g \in L^1(\mu)$ with $|f_n(x)| \leq g(x)$ for $\mu$-a.e. $x$ and all $n$ (domination).

Then $f \in L^1(\mu)$ and: $$\lim_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu.$$

Moreover, $\|f_n - f\|_{L^1} = \int |f_n - f| \, d\mu \to 0$.

Proof via Fatou's Lemma

Since $|f| = \lim_n |f_n| \leq g$ a.e. and $g \in L^1$, we have $f \in L^1$. Consider the sequences $g + f_n \geq 0$ and $g - f_n \geq 0$. By Fatou's lemma:

$$\int (g + f) \, d\mu = \int \liminf_n (g + f_n) \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int (g + f_n) \, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu + \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu.$$

Since $\int g$ is finite, subtracting gives $\int f \leq \liminf_n \int f_n$. Similarly from $g - f_n \geq 0$: $$\int (g - f) \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int (g - f_n) \, d\mu = \int g \, d\mu - \limsup_n \int f_n \, d\mu,$$ so $\limsup_n \int f_n \leq \int f$. Combining: $\int f \leq \liminf_n \int f_n \leq \limsup_n \int f_n \leq \int f$.

Hence $\lim_n \int f_n = \int f$. For $L^1$ convergence, apply the same argument to $|f_n - f| \leq 2g$ with $|f_n - f| \to 0$ a.e. $\square$

When Domination Fails

The domination condition is not merely a technical assumption - it is genuinely necessary. The following examples show what can go wrong without it.

Example 4.3.2 - Escaping Mass

Let $f_n = \mathbf{1}_{[n, n+1]}$ on $(\mathbb{R}, \lambda)$. Then $f_n \to 0$ pointwise but $\int f_n = 1 \not\to 0 = \int 0$. No integrable dominating function exists since $\sup_n f_n = \mathbf{1}_{\bigcup [n,n+1]}$, whose integral diverges.

Example 4.3.3 - Concentrating Spike

Let $f_n = n \mathbf{1}_{(0, 1/n]}$ on $([0,1], \lambda)$. Then $f_n \to 0$ pointwise on $(0,1]$ but $\int f_n = 1$ for all $n$. Any dominating function $g$ must satisfy $g(x) \geq n$ for $x \in (0, 1/n]$, i.e., $g(x) \geq 1/x$ near 0. But $\int_0^1 \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \infty$, so no $L^1$ dominator exists.

Generalisation: Vitali Convergence

Theorem 4.3.4 - Vitali Convergence Theorem

On a finite measure space, $f_n \to f$ in $L^1$ if and only if:

  1. $f_n \to f$ in measure,
  2. $\{f_n\}$ is uniformly integrable: for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\mu(E) < \delta \Rightarrow \int_E |f_n| \, d\mu < \varepsilon$ for all $n$.

The DCT is a special case: domination by $g \in L^1$ implies uniform integrability, since $\int_E |f_n| \leq \int_E g$, which is small when $\mu(E)$ is small (by absolute continuity of the integral).

Applications

Example 4.3.5 - Differentiation Under the Integral Sign

Suppose $F(\theta) = \int_0^\infty f(x, \theta) \, dx$ where $\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$ exists and $\left|\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\right| \leq g(x)$ with $g \in L^1$. Then: $$F'(\theta) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}(x, \theta) \, dx.$$

This follows by writing $F'(\theta) = \lim_{h\to 0} \int \frac{f(x,\theta+h)-f(x,\theta)}{h}\,dx$, applying DCT with dominator $g$ (via the mean value theorem). This technique, sometimes called "Leibniz's rule," is one of the most practical uses of DCT.

Example 4.3.6 - A Classic Limit

Compute $\lim_{n\to\infty} \int_0^1 \frac{n x^{n-1}}{1+x} \, dx$. Let $f_n(x) = \frac{n x^{n-1}}{1+x}$. Note $f_n \to 0$ on $[0,1)$ and $f_n(1) = n/2$. The dominator approach fails directly. Instead, integrate by parts: $\int_0^1 f_n \, dx = \frac{n}{n+1}\int_0^1 \frac{x^n}{(1+x)^2}\,dx + 1/2$... Alternatively, write $f_n = \frac{n x^{n-1}}{1+x} \leq n x^{n-1}$ and check: $\int_0^1 n x^{n-1}\,dx = 1$. The functions $f_n$ are dominated by $g = 1/(1-x)$ near 1, which is not integrable. A careful computation gives limit $= 0$ via the substitution $x = t^{1/n}$ and DCT on $[0,\infty)$.
