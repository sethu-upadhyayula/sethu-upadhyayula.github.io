4.2 Monotone Convergence Theorem

The Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT) is the first and most fundamental interchange-of-limits result in Lebesgue integration. It says that for an increasing sequence of non-negative measurable functions, the integral of the limit equals the limit of the integrals - no domination condition is needed, only monotonicity and non-negativity.

Statement and Proof

Theorem 4.2.1 - Monotone Convergence Theorem (Beppo Levi)

Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ be a measure space and let $\{f_n\}_{n=1}^\infty$ be a sequence of measurable functions satisfying:

  1. $f_n \geq 0$ for all $n$ (non-negativity),
  2. $f_n \leq f_{n+1}$ $\mu$-a.e. for all $n$ (monotone non-decreasing).

Define $f(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x)$ (which exists in $[0,\infty]$ a.e.). Then $f$ is measurable and $$\lim_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu.$$

Equivalently, $\int \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n \, d\mu = \lim_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu$.

Proof Sketch

Since $f_n \leq f_{n+1} \leq f$, monotonicity of the integral gives $\int f_n \, d\mu \leq \int f_{n+1} \, d\mu \leq \int f \, d\mu$. Hence $\lim_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu \leq \int f \, d\mu$.

For the reverse inequality, let $0 < c < 1$ and let $s$ be any simple function with $0 \leq s \leq f$. Define $A_n = \{x : f_n(x) \geq c \cdot s(x)\}$. Then $A_n \nearrow X$ as $n\to\infty$ (since $f_n \nearrow f \geq s$). Thus: $$\int f_n \, d\mu \geq \int_{A_n} f_n \, d\mu \geq c \int_{A_n} s \, d\mu.$$ Taking $n\to\infty$ and using continuity from below of measures, $\int_{A_n} s \, d\mu \to \int s \, d\mu$. So $\lim_n \int f_n \geq c \int s \, d\mu$. Since $c < 1$ and $s \leq f$ are arbitrary, taking $c\to 1$ and the supremum over $s$ gives $\lim_n \int f_n \geq \int f \, d\mu$.

Combining both inequalities yields $\lim_n \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu$. $\square$

Remark - Necessity of Hypotheses

Both hypotheses are essential. Without non-negativity: $f_n = -\mathbf{1}_{[n, n+1]}$ is decreasing to 0 yet $\int f_n = -1$ for all $n$, and $\int 0 = 0 \neq -1$. Without monotonicity: $f_n = n \mathbf{1}_{(0, 1/n]}$ converges to 0 pointwise but $\int f_n = 1$ for all $n$.

The theorem allows $\int f \, d\mu = +\infty$; it is valid throughout $[0,\infty]$.

Fatou's Lemma

Fatou's lemma is a companion result that requires no monotonicity assumption, giving instead a one-sided inequality.

Theorem 4.2.2 - Fatou's Lemma

Let $\{f_n\}$ be a sequence of non-negative measurable functions. Then: $$\int \liminf_{n\to\infty} f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu.$$

In particular, if $f_n \to f$ pointwise a.e., then $\int f \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu$.

Proof

Let $g_n(x) = \inf_{k \geq n} f_k(x)$. Then $g_n \nearrow \liminf_n f_n$ and $g_n \leq f_n$ for all $n$. By MCT: $$\int \liminf_n f_n \, d\mu = \lim_{n\to\infty} \int g_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu. \quad \square$$

Example 4.2.3 - Strict Inequality in Fatou

Let $f_n = \mathbf{1}_{[n, n+1]}$ on $(\mathbb{R}, \lambda)$. Then $f_n \to 0$ pointwise, so $\int \liminf f_n = 0$. But $\int f_n = 1$ for all $n$, so $\liminf \int f_n = 1 > 0$. This shows the inequality in Fatou's lemma can be strict: "mass can escape to infinity."

Interchange of Sum and Integral

MCT immediately yields one of its most useful corollaries: for non-negative series, summation and integration commute.

Corollary 4.2.4 - Series and Integrals

Let $\{f_n\}$ be a sequence of non-negative measurable functions. Then: $$\int \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n \, d\mu = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int f_n \, d\mu.$$

This follows by applying MCT to the partial sums $S_N = \sum_{n=1}^N f_n \nearrow \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n$ and using linearity of the integral for finite sums.

Example 4.2.5 - Power Series Integration

Let $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n$ for $x \in [0,1)$. Each term $x^n$ is non-negative and measurable. By Corollary 4.2.4: $$\int_0^1 \frac{1}{1-x} \, dx = \int_0^1 \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n \, dx = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \int_0^1 x^n \, dx = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{n+1} = +\infty,$$ consistently with the divergence of the harmonic series and the fact that $\int_0^1 \frac{dx}{1-x} = +\infty$.

For a finite example: $\int_0^\infty e^{-x} \, dx = \int_0^\infty \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^n}{n!} \, dx$. Here the terms are not all non-negative, so MCT does not directly apply; DCT would be needed. But for $\int_0^\infty \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n e^{-2x}}{n!} \, dx = \int_0^\infty e^{-x} \, dx = 1$, MCT applies since all terms are non-negative.
