Chapter 4: Integration

The Lebesgue integral extends the Riemann integral to a much wider class of functions while providing far more powerful convergence theorems. The key innovation is to integrate by partitioning the range of $f$ (via level sets) rather than the domain. The result is an integral that interacts perfectly with pointwise limits of functions.

Chapter Overview

4.1 - The Lebesgue Integral

For $f \geq 0$, $\int f\,d\mu = \sup\{\int s\,d\mu : s \text{ simple}, 0 \leq s \leq f\}$. For general $f = f^+ - f^-$, $\int f = \int f^+ - \int f^-$ when not $\infty - \infty$. Key properties: linearity, monotonicity, and $\int f = 0 \Leftrightarrow f = 0$ a.e.

4.2 - Monotone Convergence Theorem

If $0 \leq f_n \nearrow f$ pointwise a.e., then $\int f_n \nearrow \int f$. This is the fundamental tool for interchanging limits and integrals of non-negative functions, and implies both the series interchange theorem and Fatou's lemma.

4.3 - Dominated Convergence Theorem

If $f_n \to f$ a.e. and $|f_n| \leq g$ with $\int g < \infty$, then $\int f_n \to \int f$. This is the main tool for signed functions and allows limit-integral interchange without monotonicity, merely requiring a dominating integrable function.

4.4 - Comparison with Riemann Integral

Every Riemann integrable function is Lebesgue integrable with the same value. Lebesgue's criterion: bounded $f$ is Riemann integrable iff its discontinuities form a null set. The Lebesgue integral handles many functions the Riemann integral cannot.
