5.2 Latin Squares

A Latin square is a combinatorial object that has been studied since Euler's time. It generalises the notion of a multiplication table for a group and has deep connections to experimental design, finite geometry, and algebra.

Definition — Latin Square

A Latin square of order $n$ is an $n \times n$ array filled with $n$ different symbols $\{1,2,\ldots,n\}$ such that each symbol occurs exactly once in each row and exactly once in each column.

Example — Order 3

The following is a Latin square of order 3 with symbols $\{1,2,3\}$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the addition table of $\mathbb{Z}_3$.

Definition — Orthogonal Latin Squares

Two Latin squares $L_1$ and $L_2$ of order $n$ are orthogonal if, when superimposed, every ordered pair $(a,b)$ with $a,b \in \{1,\ldots,n\}$ appears exactly once. Formally, the $n^2$ pairs $(L_1(i,j), L_2(i,j))$ are all distinct.

A set of $k$ mutually orthogonal Latin squares of order $n$ is called a set of $k$ MOLS$(n)$.

Example — MOLS(3)

The two squares

$$L_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1&2&3\\2&3&1\\3&1&2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad L_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1&2&3\\3&1&2\\2&3&1 \end{pmatrix}$$

are orthogonal: superimposing them gives each of the 9 pairs $(1,1),(1,2),\ldots,(3,3)$ exactly once.

Theorem — MOLS from Finite Fields

If $q = p^k$ is a prime power, then there exist $q-1$ mutually orthogonal Latin squares of order $q$ (i.e., $N(q) \ge q-1$). In particular, $N(q) \ge 2$ for all prime powers $q \ge 3$.

Construction

Label rows and columns with elements of $\mathbb{F}_q$. For each $a \in \mathbb{F}_q^*$, define $L_a(i,j) = ai + j$ (operations in $\mathbb{F}_q$). Then $L_a$ is a Latin square for each $a$, and $L_a, L_b$ are orthogonal for $a \ne b$: if $(L_a(i,j), L_b(i,j)) = (L_a(i',j'), L_b(i',j'))$ then $ai+j = ai'+j'$ and $bi+j = bi'+j'$. Subtracting: $(a-b)i = (a-b)i'$, so $i=i'$ (since $a \ne b$), then $j=j'$.

Theorem — Euler's Conjecture (Disproved)

Euler conjectured in 1782 that no two orthogonal Latin squares of order $n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ exist. This is true for $n=2$ and $n=6$ (Tarry, 1901, by exhaustive check). However, Bose, Shrikhande, and Parker (1960) proved that MOLS$(n)$ exist for all $n \ge 3$ except $n=6$.

Connection to Affine Planes

A set of $n-1$ MOLS$(n)$ is equivalent to an affine plane of order $n$: a structure with $n^2$ points and $n^2+n$ lines such that any two points determine a unique line. Affine planes exist for all prime-power orders; their existence for non-prime-powers is a major open problem.

Definition — Quasigroup

A quasigroup $(Q, \cdot)$ is a set $Q$ with a binary operation such that for every $a,b \in Q$ the equations $ax = b$ and $ya = b$ each have unique solutions. The multiplication table of a quasigroup of order $n$ is exactly a Latin square of order $n$.

Applications

Latin squares appear in:

  • Statistics: A Latin square of order $n$ gives a design for $n$ treatments in an $n\times n$ field, controlling for two nuisance variables (rows and columns).
  • Cryptography: Used in S-boxes and in the design of block ciphers.
  • Sudoku: A $9\times 9$ Sudoku is a Latin square with the additional constraint that each $3\times 3$ sub-square also contains each symbol.
