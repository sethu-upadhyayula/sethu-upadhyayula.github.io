1.2 Permutations and Combinations

Permutations count ordered selections; combinations count unordered selections. These are the two basic ways to choose $k$ items from $n$ distinct items, and they appear throughout combinatorics and probability.

Definition — Permutations

A $k$-permutation of an $n$-element set $[n] = \{1,\ldots,n\}$ is an ordered selection of $k$ distinct elements. The number of $k$-permutations is: $$P(n,k) = n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}.$$ In particular, $P(n,n) = n!$ counts all orderings (permutations) of $n$ elements.

Definition — Combinations (Binomial Coefficient)

A $k$-combination of $[n]$ is an unordered selection of $k$ distinct elements — equivalently, a $k$-element subset. The number of $k$-combinations is: $$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!} = \frac{P(n,k)}{k!}.$$ We define $\binom{n}{k} = 0$ for $k < 0$ or $k > n$.

Theorem — Basic Properties of $\binom{n}{k}$

Symmetry: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$. (Choosing $k$ to include is the same as choosing $n-k$ to exclude.)

Pascal's Identity: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$.

Proof — Pascal's Identity

Consider element $n \in [n]$. A $k$-subset of $[n]$ either contains $n$ (choose the remaining $k-1$ from $[n-1]$: $\binom{n-1}{k-1}$ ways) or does not contain $n$ (choose all $k$ from $[n-1]$: $\binom{n-1}{k}$ ways). By the addition principle, $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$. $\square$

Definition — Permutations with Repetition

The number of ordered selections of $k$ items from $n$ types with repetition allowed is $n^k$ (each of $k$ positions independently has $n$ choices).

Theorem — Combinations with Repetition (Stars and Bars)

The number of ways to choose $k$ items from $n$ types with repetition (order does not matter) equals the number of non-negative integer solutions to $x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n = k$: $$\binom{n+k-1}{k} = \binom{n+k-1}{n-1}.$$

Proof — Stars and Bars

Represent a solution as a sequence of $k$ stars ($\star$) and $n-1$ bars ($|$) separating the $n$ types. Any arrangement of $k$ stars and $n-1$ bars in $n+k-1$ positions gives a unique solution. Hence there are $\binom{n+k-1}{k}$ solutions. $\square$

Definition — Multinomial Coefficient

The number of ways to arrange $n$ objects where $k_1$ are of type 1, $k_2$ of type 2, …, $k_r$ of type $r$ (with $k_1 + \cdots + k_r = n$) is the multinomial coefficient: $$\binom{n}{k_1, k_2, \ldots, k_r} = \frac{n!}{k_1!\, k_2!\, \cdots\, k_r!}.$$

Example

How many ways can 10 people be split into groups of 3, 3, and 4?

$\dfrac{10!}{3!\,3!\,4!} = \dfrac{3{,}628{,}800}{6 \cdot 6 \cdot 24} = 4{,}200$.
