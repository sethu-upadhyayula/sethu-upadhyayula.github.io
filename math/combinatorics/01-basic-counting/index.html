Chapter 1: Basic Counting

Counting is the foundation of combinatorics. Before we can study complex combinatorial structures, we must master the fundamental principles governing how many ways a process can occur. This chapter develops the core tools: the addition and multiplication principles, permutations and combinations, the binomial theorem, and the inclusion-exclusion principle.

These seemingly simple ideas underpin vast areas of mathematics, computer science, and probability. The binomial theorem connects counting to algebra; inclusion-exclusion solves problems where direct counting overcounts or undercounts.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Addition and Multiplication Principles

If $A$ and $B$ are disjoint finite sets, $|A\cup B|=|A|+|B|$. If a procedure has $m$ independent steps with $n_i$ choices at step $i$, the total number of outcomes is $n_1\cdot n_2\cdots n_m$. Applications to passwords, functions $A\to B$, and subsets of $[n]$.

1.2 — Permutations and Combinations

Ordered selections: $P(n,k)=n!/(n-k)!$. Unordered selections: $\binom{n}{k}=n!/(k!(n-k)!)$. Pascal's identity, combinations with repetition (stars and bars $\binom{n+k-1}{k}$), and the multinomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k_1,\ldots,k_r}=n!/(k_1!\cdots k_r!)$.

1.3 — Binomial Theorem

$(x+y)^n=\sum_{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}x^ky^{n-k}$. Corollaries: $\sum_{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}=2^n$, $\sum_{k=0}^n(-1)^k\binom{n}{k}=0$, and Vandermonde's identity $\sum_k\binom{n}{k}^2=\binom{2n}{n}$. The multinomial theorem generalises to more variables.

1.4 — Inclusion-Exclusion

$|A_1\cup\cdots\cup A_n|=\sum|A_i|-\sum|A_i\cap A_j|+\cdots+(-1)^{n+1}|A_1\cap\cdots\cap A_n|$. Applications: derangements $D_n\approx n!/e$, Euler's totient $\phi(n)$, and counting surjections from $[n]$ to $[k]$.
