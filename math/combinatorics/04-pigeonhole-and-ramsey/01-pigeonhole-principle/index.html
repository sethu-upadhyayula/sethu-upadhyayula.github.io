4.1 Pigeonhole Principle

The pigeonhole principle (PHP) states: if $n+1$ objects are distributed among $n$ boxes, some box contains at least two objects. Simple as it is, this principle yields deep results in number theory, geometry, and combinatorics.

Theorem — Basic Pigeonhole Principle

If $m$ objects are placed into $n$ boxes with $m > n$, then at least one box contains $\ge 2$ objects.

Theorem — Generalised Pigeonhole Principle

If $m$ objects are placed into $n$ boxes, then at least one box contains $\ge \lceil m/n \rceil$ objects.

Proof

Suppose for contradiction that every box contains $\le \lceil m/n \rceil - 1 < m/n$ objects. Then the total number of objects is at most $n(\lceil m/n \rceil - 1) < n \cdot (m/n) = m$, contradicting the assumption that $m$ objects are placed. $\square$

Example — Shared Birth Month

Among any 13 people, at least two share a birth month (12 months = 12 boxes, 13 people).

Example — Geometry

Any 5 points inside a unit square include two points within distance $\le \sqrt{2}/2$ of each other.

Proof: Divide the unit square into 4 subsquares of side $1/2$. By PHP, two of the 5 points are in the same subsquare, which has diagonal $\sqrt{(1/2)^2 + (1/2)^2} = \sqrt{2}/2$. $\square$

Theorem — Erdős-Szekeres Theorem

Any sequence of more than $mn$ distinct real numbers has either an increasing subsequence of length $> m$ or a decreasing subsequence of length $> n$.

Equivalently, any sequence of $mn+1$ distinct reals has a monotone subsequence of length $\ge \max(m,n)+1$.

Proof

For each element $a_i$ in the sequence, let $\text{inc}(i)$ be the length of the longest increasing subsequence starting at $i$. If any $\text{inc}(i) > m$, we are done. Otherwise, $\text{inc}(i) \in \{1,\ldots,m\}$ for all $i$. With $mn+1$ elements and only $m$ possible values, by PHP some value occurs $\ge n+1$ times. The subsequence of elements with that value forms an increasing subsequence... (otherwise, it gives a decreasing one). $\square$

Example — Consecutive Integers

Among any $n+1$ integers chosen from $\{1, 2, \ldots, 2n\}$, at least two are consecutive.

Proof: Pair the integers as $\{1,2\}, \{3,4\}, \ldots, \{2n-1, 2n\}$ — these are $n$ pairs (boxes). Any $n+1$ chosen integers must have two from the same pair, hence consecutive. $\square$
