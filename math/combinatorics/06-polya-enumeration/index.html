6. Pólya Enumeration

Many combinatorial counting problems ask not just how many objects exist, but how many are distinct up to symmetry. How many ways are there to colour the faces of a cube with two colours, where two colourings are considered the same if one can be rotated into the other? Pólya enumeration theory answers such questions systematically, using the algebraic structure of symmetry groups.

Chapter Overview
  • 6.1 Group Actions on Sets: actions of groups on sets; orbits and stabilisers; the orbit-stabiliser theorem; examples from combinatorics.
  • 6.2 Burnside's Lemma: counting orbits via fixed-point sets; proof by double-counting; necklace enumeration examples.
  • 6.3 Pólya's Theorem: the cycle index of a permutation group; Pólya's enumeration theorem; weighted enumeration; applications to graph and chemical structure counting.
Historical Note

Burnside's lemma was actually known earlier to Frobenius. Pólya's theorem, published in 1937, gives a far-reaching generalisation that subsumes Burnside's result. These tools are central to combinatorics, chemistry (counting molecular isomers), and graph theory (counting non-isomorphic graphs).

Key Idea

The central insight is that counting objects up to symmetry is equivalent to counting orbits of a group action. The orbit-stabiliser theorem, Burnside's lemma, and Pólya's cycle index machinery are successive refinements of this idea, each more powerful than the last.
