6.2 Burnside's Lemma

Burnside's lemma (also known as the Cauchy–Frobenius lemma) gives a formula for the number of orbits of a group action in terms of fixed-point sets. It is the fundamental tool for counting objects up to symmetry.

Definition — Fixed-Point Set

Let $G$ act on $X$. For $g \in G$, the fixed-point set of $g$ is

$$\mathrm{Fix}(g) = \{x \in X : g \cdot x = x\}$$

the set of elements of $X$ left unchanged by $g$.

Theorem — Burnside's Lemma

Let $G$ be a finite group acting on a finite set $X$. The number of orbits is

$$|X/G| = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |\mathrm{Fix}(g)|$$
Proof

Count the set of pairs $S = \{(g,x) \in G \times X : g \cdot x = x\}$ in two ways.

By rows (fixing $g$): $|S| = \sum_{g \in G} |\mathrm{Fix}(g)|$.

By columns (fixing $x$): $|S| = \sum_{x \in X} |\mathrm{Stab}(x)|$. By the orbit-stabiliser theorem, $|\mathrm{Stab}(x)| = |G|/|\mathrm{Orb}(x)|$. Grouping $x$ by orbits, if orbit $O$ has size $|O|$, then $\sum_{x \in O} |G|/|O| = |G|$. Summing over all orbits: $|S| = |G| \cdot (\text{number of orbits})$.

Equating the two counts: $\text{number of orbits} = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |\mathrm{Fix}(g)|$.

Example — Colouring a Square's Corners

Count 2-colourings of the 4 corners of a square, up to rotation by the cyclic group $G = \mathbb{Z}_4 = \{r^0, r^1, r^2, r^3\}$ (rotations by $0^\circ, 90^\circ, 180^\circ, 270^\circ$). Here $X = \{0,1\}^4$, so $|X| = 16$.

  • $r^0$ (identity): fixes all $2^4 = 16$ colourings. $|\mathrm{Fix}(r^0)| = 16$.
  • $r^1$ ($90^\circ$): fixes colouring iff all 4 corners have the same colour. $|\mathrm{Fix}(r^1)| = 2$.
  • $r^2$ ($180^\circ$): fixes colouring iff opposite corners match: positions $(1,3)$ match and $(2,4)$ match. $|\mathrm{Fix}(r^2)| = 2^2 = 4$.
  • $r^3$ ($270^\circ$): same as $90^\circ$. $|\mathrm{Fix}(r^3)| = 2$.
$$|X/G| = \frac{1}{4}(16 + 2 + 4 + 2) = \frac{24}{4} = 6$$

There are 6 distinct colourings of the corners up to rotation.

Example — Necklaces with 6 Beads and 2 Colours

Count binary necklaces with 6 beads, where two necklaces are equivalent if one is a rotation of the other. Here $G = \mathbb{Z}_6$ acts on $X = \{0,1\}^6$.

For a rotation $r^k$ (by $k$ positions), the fixed colourings are those periodic with period $\gcd(k,6)$: there are $2^{\gcd(k,6)}$ such colourings.

$$|X/G| = \frac{1}{6}\bigl(2^6 + 2^1 + 2^2 + 2^3 + 2^2 + 2^1\bigr) = \frac{64+2+4+8+4+2}{6} = \frac{84}{6} = 14$$

There are 14 distinct binary necklaces with 6 beads.

Remark — Dihedral Symmetry

For necklaces where flips (reflections) are also considered equivalent, the symmetry group is the dihedral group $D_n$ of order $2n$. Burnside's lemma still applies; one must also compute $|\mathrm{Fix}|$ for each reflection. For $n=6$, this gives $(84 + 6 \cdot 8)/12 = 132/12 = 11$ bracelets (since reflections contribute $3 \cdot 2^3 + 3 \cdot 2^4 = 24 + 48 = 72$... careful calculation needed).
