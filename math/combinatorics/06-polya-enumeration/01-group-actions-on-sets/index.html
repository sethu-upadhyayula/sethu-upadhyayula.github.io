6.1 Group Actions on Sets

A group action formalises the idea of a symmetry group permuting a set of objects. Counting distinct objects under symmetry reduces to counting orbits of such an action.

Definition — Group Action

A (left) action of a group $G$ on a set $X$ is a function $\cdot: G \times X \to X$, written $(g,x) \mapsto g \cdot x$, satisfying:

  1. Identity: $e \cdot x = x$ for all $x \in X$, where $e$ is the identity of $G$.
  2. Compatibility: $(gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)$ for all $g,h \in G$ and $x \in X$.

We say $G$ acts on $X$, or that $X$ is a $G$-set.

Definition — Orbit and Stabiliser

Let $G$ act on $X$ and let $x \in X$.

  • The orbit of $x$ is $\mathrm{Orb}(x) = G \cdot x = \{g \cdot x : g \in G\} \subseteq X$.
  • The stabiliser (or isotropy subgroup) of $x$ is $\mathrm{Stab}(x) = \{g \in G : g \cdot x = x\} \le G$.
Proposition — Orbits Partition $X$

The orbits of a $G$-action on $X$ form a partition of $X$: every element of $X$ belongs to exactly one orbit.

Proof

Define the relation $x \sim y$ iff $y = g \cdot x$ for some $g \in G$. This is an equivalence relation: reflexivity follows from $e \cdot x = x$; symmetry from $y = g \cdot x \Rightarrow x = g^{-1} \cdot y$; transitivity from $(gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)$. The orbits are exactly the equivalence classes.

Theorem — Orbit-Stabiliser

For any $x \in X$:

$$|\mathrm{Orb}(x)| \cdot |\mathrm{Stab}(x)| = |G|$$

In other words, the size of an orbit times the size of its stabiliser equals the order of $G$.

Proof

Define a bijection $\mathrm{Orb}(x) \to G/\mathrm{Stab}(x)$ (left cosets) by $g \cdot x \mapsto g\,\mathrm{Stab}(x)$. This is well-defined: $g \cdot x = h \cdot x$ iff $h^{-1}g \cdot x = x$ iff $h^{-1}g \in \mathrm{Stab}(x)$ iff $g\,\mathrm{Stab}(x) = h\,\mathrm{Stab}(x)$. Since the map is a bijection, $|\mathrm{Orb}(x)| = |G:\mathrm{Stab}(x)| = |G|/|\mathrm{Stab}(x)|$ by Lagrange's theorem.

Example — Rotations Acting on Colourings

Let $G = \mathbb{Z}_4$ (rotations of a square by $0^\circ, 90^\circ, 180^\circ, 270^\circ$) act on $X = \{0,1\}^4$ (2-colourings of the 4 corners). There are $2^4 = 16$ colourings in $X$.

The orbit of the all-red colouring $(1,1,1,1)$ has size 1 (it is fixed by all rotations), with stabiliser $\mathbb{Z}_4$ of order 4. The orbit-stabiliser theorem confirms: $1 \cdot 4 = 4 = |G|$.

The colouring $(1,0,1,0)$ (alternating) has orbit $\{(1,0,1,0)\}$ of size 1 (fixed by $180^\circ$ and $0^\circ$, but wait — fixed by all even rotations), stabiliser $\{0^\circ, 180^\circ\}$ of order 2. Orbit size: $4/2 = 2$. (Actually $\{(1,0,1,0)\}$ is fixed by all rotations of a square if the alternating pattern is symmetric — one needs to check carefully.)

Definition — Transitive Action

An action is transitive if it has only one orbit: for any $x,y \in X$, there exists $g \in G$ with $g \cdot x = y$. An action is free if all stabilisers are trivial: $\mathrm{Stab}(x) = \{e\}$ for all $x$.

Example — Symmetric Group

The symmetric group $S_n$ acts on $X = \{1,\ldots,n\}$ by $\sigma \cdot i = \sigma(i)$. This action is transitive (any element can be sent to any other) and has $\mathrm{Stab}(i) \cong S_{n-1}$. The orbit-stabiliser theorem gives $n \cdot (n-1)! = n!$, confirming $|S_n| = n!$.
