Chapter 7: Computational Complexity

Complexity theory classifies computational problems by their inherent difficulty - the minimum resources (time, space) any algorithm must use, regardless of ingenuity. The P vs NP question is the central unsolved problem of theoretical computer science and one of the Millennium Prize Problems.

For many natural optimisation problems - scheduling, packing, colouring, routing - no polynomial-time algorithm is known. Complexity theory explains why: these problems are NP-complete, and a polynomial-time algorithm for any one would imply $P = NP$. When exact solutions are intractable, approximation algorithms provide guaranteed near-optimal solutions efficiently.

Chapter Overview

7.1 - P and NP

$P$ is the class of decision problems solvable in polynomial time. $NP$ is the class verifiable in polynomial time given a certificate. $P \subseteq NP$; whether $P = NP$ is open. NP-hard problems are at least as hard as the hardest NP problems; NP-complete problems are in NP and NP-hard.

7.2 - NP-Completeness

The Cook-Levin theorem establishes SAT as NP-complete. By polynomial-time reductions, hundreds of problems (3-SAT, independent set, vertex cover, Hamiltonian cycle, TSP, subset sum) are NP-complete. Reductions form a rich combinatorial theory.

7.3 - Approximation Algorithms

When exact solutions are NP-hard, approximation algorithms with provable approximation ratios provide the best polynomial-time guarantee. Vertex cover has a 2-approximation; metric TSP has a 1.5-approximation (Christofides); set cover has an $O(\log n)$-approximation. Inapproximability results show some problems cannot be approximated well unless $P = NP$.
