1.3 Subspaces

Given a topological space $(X, \tau)$ and a subset $A \subseteq X$, there is a canonical way to make $A$ into a topological space in its own right by "restricting" the topology of $X$ to $A$. The resulting space is called the subspace (or induced or relative space) of $X$ on $A$.

Definition — Subspace Topology

Let $(X, \tau)$ be a topological space and $A \subseteq X$. The subspace topology on $A$ is $$\tau_A = \{ U \cap A : U \in \tau \}.$$ The pair $(A, \tau_A)$ is called a topological subspace of $(X, \tau)$.

One readily verifies the topology axioms: $\emptyset = \emptyset \cap A \in \tau_A$ and $A = X \cap A \in \tau_A$; arbitrary unions $\bigcup_\alpha (U_\alpha \cap A) = (\bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha) \cap A \in \tau_A$; and finite intersections $(U_1 \cap A) \cap (U_2 \cap A) = (U_1 \cap U_2) \cap A \in \tau_A$.

Remark — Inclusion Map

The subspace topology is precisely the coarsest topology on $A$ making the inclusion map $\iota: A \hookrightarrow X$, $\iota(a) = a$, continuous. Equivalently, $\tau_A$ is characterised by the universal property: a map $f: Z \to A$ is continuous if and only if $\iota \circ f: Z \to X$ is continuous.

Open and Closed Sets in Subspaces

Proposition — Open Sets in Subspaces

Let $A \subseteq X$. A set $V \subseteq A$ is open in $(A, \tau_A)$ if and only if $V = U \cap A$ for some $U$ open in $X$.

In particular, if $A$ is itself open in $X$, then $V \subseteq A$ is open in $A$ if and only if $V$ is open in $X$: the subspace topology on an open subspace coincides with the restriction of the ambient topology.

Proposition — Closed Sets in Subspaces

A set $C \subseteq A$ is closed in $(A, \tau_A)$ if and only if $C = F \cap A$ for some closed set $F$ in $X$.

Proof. $C$ is closed in $A$ iff $A \setminus C$ is open in $A$ iff $A \setminus C = U \cap A$ for some open $U \subseteq X$ iff $C = A \setminus (U \cap A) = A \cap (X \setminus U) = (X \setminus U) \cap A$, and $X \setminus U$ is closed in $X$. $\square$

Example — $(0,1)$ as a Subspace of $\mathbb{R}$

Consider $A = (0,1) \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ with the subspace topology inherited from the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$. The open sets of $(0,1)$ are all sets of the form $U \cap (0,1)$ for $U$ open in $\mathbb{R}$. For instance:

  • $(0, \tfrac{1}{2})$ is open in $(0,1)$ (it equals $(-1, \tfrac{1}{2}) \cap (0,1)$).
  • $(0, \tfrac{1}{2}]$ is not open in $\mathbb{R}$, but is it open in $(0,1)$? No: any open set $U \ni \tfrac{1}{2}$ contains an interval $(\tfrac{1}{2}-\varepsilon, \tfrac{1}{2}+\varepsilon)$, so $U \cap (0,1)$ contains $(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}+\varepsilon)$, which is not a subset of $(0, \tfrac{1}{2}]$.
  • However, $(0, \tfrac{1}{2}]$ is closed in $(0,1)$ because $[\tfrac{1}{2}, 1]$ is closed in $\mathbb{R}$ and $[\tfrac{1}{2},1] \cap (0,1) = [\tfrac{1}{2},1)$... wait — more precisely, $(0,\tfrac{1}{2}] = (-\infty, \tfrac{1}{2}] \cap (0,1)$, and $(-\infty, \tfrac{1}{2}]$ is closed in $\mathbb{R}$, so $(0,\tfrac{1}{2}]$ is closed in $(0,1)$.

This illustrates that a set may be closed in a subspace without being closed in the ambient space.

Closure and Interior in Subspaces

Proposition — Closure in Subspaces

If $B \subseteq A \subseteq X$, the closure of $B$ in $(A, \tau_A)$ is $\overline{B}^A = \overline{B}^X \cap A$, where $\overline{B}^X$ denotes closure in $X$. In other words, the closure is computed in $X$ and then intersected with $A$.

Example — Closure Depends on Ambient Space

Let $A = (0,2)$ and $B = (0,1)$, both subsets of $\mathbb{R}$. The closure of $B$ in $\mathbb{R}$ is $[0,1]$. The closure of $B$ in $(0,2)$ is $[0,1] \cap (0,2) = (0,1]$. Thus $0$ is a limit point of $B$ in $\mathbb{R}$ but is "invisible" in the subspace $(0,2)$.

Hereditary Properties

Definition — Hereditary Property

A topological property $P$ is hereditary if every subspace of a space with property $P$ also has property $P$. It is closed-hereditary if every closed subspace inherits $P$, and open-hereditary if every open subspace inherits $P$.

Proposition — Examples of Hereditary Properties

The following properties are hereditary:

  • $T_0$, $T_1$, $T_2$ (Hausdorff), $T_3$ (regular), $T_{3\frac{1}{2}}$ (Tychonoff).
  • First countability and second countability.
  • Metrisability.

Compactness is not hereditary (e.g., $(0,1)$ is not compact even though $[0,1]$ is), but it is closed-hereditary: every closed subspace of a compact space is compact. Connectedness is not hereditary: $[0,1]$ is connected but $[0, \tfrac{1}{3}) \cup (\tfrac{2}{3}, 1]$, as a subspace, is not.

Transitivity of Subspaces

Proposition — Subspace of a Subspace

If $B \subseteq A \subseteq X$, then the subspace topology on $B$ inherited from $(A, \tau_A)$ equals the subspace topology on $B$ inherited directly from $(X, \tau)$. Indeed, $\{V \cap B : V \in \tau_A\} = \{(U \cap A) \cap B : U \in \tau\} = \{U \cap B : U \in \tau\}$ since $B \subseteq A$ implies $(U \cap A) \cap B = U \cap B$.
