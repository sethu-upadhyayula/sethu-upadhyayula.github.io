1.2 Basis and Subbasis

Specifying a topology by listing all open sets is usually impractical. A basis is a smaller, more natural collection from which all open sets can be recovered by taking unions. A subbasis is even more economical: one takes all finite intersections, then all unions.

Definition — Basis for a Topology

A collection $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)$ is a basis for a topology on $X$ if:

  1. Covering: For every $x \in X$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B$.
  2. Intersection condition: For every $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$ and every $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$, there exists $B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$.

The topology generated by $\mathcal{B}$ is $\tau(\mathcal{B}) = \{ U \subseteq X : \forall\, x \in U,\, \exists\, B \in \mathcal{B},\, x \in B \subseteq U\}$. Equivalently, the open sets are precisely the arbitrary unions of elements of $\mathcal{B}$.

Theorem — Characterisation of the Generated Topology

If $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for a topology on $X$, then $\tau(\mathcal{B})$ is indeed a topology, and $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for it in the sense that every $U \in \tau(\mathcal{B})$ is a union of elements of $\mathcal{B}$. Moreover, $\tau(\mathcal{B})$ is the unique topology for which $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis.

Example — Open Intervals on $\mathbb{R}$

The collection $\mathcal{B} = \{(a, b) : a < b,\, a, b \in \mathbb{R}\}$ is a basis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$. The covering condition is clear. For the intersection condition: $(a_1, b_1) \cap (a_2, b_2) = (\max(a_1,a_2), \min(b_1,b_2))$, which is itself an open interval (or empty) and hence in $\mathcal{B}$. The rational-endpoint intervals $\{(a,b) : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\}$ also form a countable basis for the same topology.

Example — Open Balls on $\mathbb{R}^n$

The open balls $\mathcal{B} = \{B(x, r) : x \in \mathbb{R}^n,\, r > 0\}$ form a basis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}^n$. A countable basis is given by balls with rational centres and rational radii: $\{B(q, r) : q \in \mathbb{Q}^n,\, r \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}\}$, demonstrating that $\mathbb{R}^n$ is second-countable.

Theorem — Comparing Topologies via Bases

Let $\mathcal{B}_1$ and $\mathcal{B}_2$ be bases generating topologies $\tau_1$ and $\tau_2$ on $X$. Then $\tau_1 \subseteq \tau_2$ (i.e., $\tau_2$ is finer) if and only if for every $B_1 \in \mathcal{B}_1$ and every $x \in B_1$, there exists $B_2 \in \mathcal{B}_2$ with $x \in B_2 \subseteq B_1$.

In particular, $\tau_1 = \tau_2$ if and only if both bases are refinements of each other in this sense.

Subbasis

Definition — Subbasis

A subbasis for a topology on $X$ is any collection $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)$ such that $\bigcup_{S \in \mathcal{S}} S = X$. The topology generated by $\mathcal{S}$ is:

$$\tau(\mathcal{S}) = \text{all unions of finite intersections of elements of } \mathcal{S}.$$

The collection of all finite intersections of $\mathcal{S}$-elements forms a basis for $\tau(\mathcal{S})$. Note that $\mathcal{S}$ need not satisfy the basis intersection condition — the passage through finite intersections compensates for this.

Example — Subbasis for the Standard Topology on $\mathbb{R}$

The collection $\mathcal{S} = \{(-\infty, b) : b \in \mathbb{R}\} \cup \{(a, \infty) : a \in \mathbb{R}\}$ is a subbasis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$. Indeed, finite intersections give exactly the open intervals: $(-\infty, b) \cap (a, \infty) = (a, b)$ for $a < b$, and arbitrary unions of these generate all open sets.

Example — Sorgenfrey Line

The Sorgenfrey line $\mathbb{R}_\ell$ is $\mathbb{R}$ with the basis $\mathcal{B} = \{[a, b) : a < b\}$. This topology is strictly finer than the standard topology (every standard open set is $\mathbb{R}_\ell$-open, but $[0,1)$ is $\mathbb{R}_\ell$-open and not standard-open). The Sorgenfrey line is $T_{3\frac{1}{2}}$ but not second-countable, and the Sorgenfrey plane $\mathbb{R}_\ell \times \mathbb{R}_\ell$ is not normal — a famous counterexample.

Proposition — Neighbourhood Basis

A neighbourhood basis (or local basis) at a point $x \in X$ is a collection $\mathcal{B}_x$ of open neighbourhoods of $x$ such that every open neighbourhood of $x$ contains some element of $\mathcal{B}_x$. A space is first-countable if every point has a countable neighbourhood basis; it is second-countable if the entire topology has a countable basis. Second-countable implies first-countable.
