7.3 van Kampen's Theorem

Van Kampen's theorem is the main computational tool for fundamental groups. It expresses the fundamental group of a space assembled from two path-connected open pieces in terms of the groups of those pieces and their intersection.

Theorem - van Kampen's Theorem

Let $X = U \cup V$ where $U$, $V$, and $U \cap V$ are path-connected open subsets of $X$, and choose a basepoint $x_0 \in U \cap V$. Let $i_U: \pi_1(U \cap V) \to \pi_1(U)$ and $i_V: \pi_1(U \cap V) \to \pi_1(V)$ be the homomorphisms induced by the inclusions. Then $$\pi_1(X, x_0) \cong \pi_1(U, x_0) *_{\pi_1(U \cap V, x_0)} \pi_1(V, x_0),$$ the amalgamated free product: the free product $\pi_1(U) * \pi_1(V)$ modulo the normal subgroup generated by $\{i_U(\gamma) i_V(\gamma)^{-1} : \gamma \in \pi_1(U \cap V)\}$.

Equivalently, $\pi_1(X)$ is the pushout of $\pi_1(U) \leftarrow \pi_1(U \cap V) \rightarrow \pi_1(V)$ in the category of groups.

Applications

Example - $\pi_1(S^2) = 0$

Write $S^2 = U \cup V$ where $U = S^2 \setminus \{N\}$ (the sphere minus the north pole) and $V = S^2 \setminus \{S\}$ (minus the south pole). Both $U$ and $V$ are homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^2$, hence simply connected: $\pi_1(U) = \pi_1(V) = 0$. The intersection $U \cap V = S^2 \setminus \{N,S\}$ is homeomorphic to $S^1 \times \mathbb{R}$, which is path-connected. By van Kampen: $$\pi_1(S^2) \cong 0 *_{\pi_1(U \cap V)} 0 = 0. \quad \square$$

Example - $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$

The torus $T^2 = S^1 \times S^1$. One can apply van Kampen by writing $T^2$ as the union of two open sets each homotopy equivalent to $S^1$, with intersection homotopy equivalent to $S^1 \times S^1$ minus a disk - but the quickest argument uses the product formula: for path-connected spaces, $$\pi_1(X \times Y, (x_0, y_0)) \cong \pi_1(X, x_0) \times \pi_1(Y, y_0).$$ Thus $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \pi_1(S^1) \times \pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}^2$. The two generators correspond to the two independent cycles on the torus.

Example - $\pi_1(\text{Klein bottle})$

The Klein bottle $K$ can be decomposed as $U \cup V$ where each of $U, V$ is a Möbius band (open, homotopy equivalent to $S^1$) and $U \cap V \simeq S^1$. Van Kampen gives: $$\pi_1(K) \cong \langle a, b \mid abab^{-1} = 1 \rangle,$$ where the relation comes from the identification of the boundary circle in the intersection. This is a non-abelian group; the abelianisation is $\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, distinguishing $K$ from the torus (whose abelianisation is $\mathbb{Z}^2$).

Wedge Sum

Example - Wedge Sum $X \vee Y$

The wedge sum $X \vee Y$ is the quotient of $X \sqcup Y$ identifying a chosen basepoint $x_0 \in X$ with $y_0 \in Y$. When $X$ and $Y$ are path-connected with "nice" basepoints (e.g., manifolds or CW-complexes), van Kampen gives: $$\pi_1(X \vee Y) \cong \pi_1(X) * \pi_1(Y) \quad \text{(free product)},$$ since the intersection $U \cap V$ can be taken to be contractible (a small neighbourhood of the wedge point). For example, $\pi_1(S^1 \vee S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z}$, the free group on two generators.

This illustrates that the fundamental group detects the number and arrangement of "one-dimensional holes" in a space.
