7.2 The Fundamental Group

The fundamental group captures the first-order homotopic structure of a space by classifying loops up to continuous deformation. It is the simplest algebraic invariant in algebraic topology.

Definition - Loop and Based Loop Space

A loop based at $x_0 \in X$ is a path $\gamma: [0,1] \to X$ with $\gamma(0) = \gamma(1) = x_0$. Two loops $\gamma, \delta$ based at $x_0$ are homotopic rel $\{0,1\}$ (or homotopic as loops) if there is a continuous $H: [0,1] \times [0,1] \to X$ with $H(s,0) = \gamma(s)$, $H(s,1) = \delta(s)$, and $H(0,t) = H(1,t) = x_0$ for all $s, t$.

This is an equivalence relation; the equivalence class of $\gamma$ is written $[\gamma]$.

Definition - Fundamental Group

The fundamental group $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ is the set of homotopy classes of loops based at $x_0$, with group operation given by path concatenation: $$(\gamma * \delta)(s) = \begin{cases} \gamma(2s) & 0 \leq s \leq \tfrac{1}{2} \\ \delta(2s-1) & \tfrac{1}{2} \leq s \leq 1. \end{cases}$$ The group operation on homotopy classes is $[\gamma][\delta] = [\gamma * \delta]$.

Proposition - Group Axioms

$(\pi_1(X, x_0), *)$ is a group:

  • Associativity: $[\gamma * (\delta * \eta)] = [(\gamma * \delta) * \eta]$ (reparametrisation homotopy).
  • Identity: $[e_{x_0}]$, where $e_{x_0}(s) = x_0$ is the constant loop.
  • Inverse: $[\gamma]^{-1} = [\bar{\gamma}]$, where $\bar{\gamma}(s) = \gamma(1-s)$ is the reversed loop.

In general $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ need not be abelian.

Fundamental Groups of Key Spaces

Theorem - Simply Connected Spaces

$\pi_1(\mathbb{R}^n, 0) = 0$ (trivial group) for all $n \geq 0$, since $\mathbb{R}^n$ is contractible (§7.1). More generally, $\pi_1(X, x_0) = 0$ for any contractible space. A path-connected space with trivial fundamental group is called simply connected. Examples: $S^n$ for $n \geq 2$, $\mathbb{R}^n$, convex sets.

Theorem - $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$

This is one of the most important results in algebraic topology. The isomorphism sends the homotopy class of a loop to its winding number: the loop $\gamma_n(t) = e^{2\pi i n t}$ wraps around the circle $n$ times and corresponds to $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

The proof uses the theory of covering spaces: the exponential map $p: \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $p(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$, is the universal cover of $S^1$, and $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathrm{Deck}(\mathbb{R}/S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (the group of deck transformations, which are integer translations of $\mathbb{R}$).

Change of Basepoint

Proposition - Basepoint Independence

If $X$ is path-connected and $x_0, x_1 \in X$, then $\pi_1(X, x_0) \cong \pi_1(X, x_1)$. Given any path $\alpha$ from $x_0$ to $x_1$, the map $[\gamma] \mapsto [\bar{\alpha} * \gamma * \alpha]$ defines an isomorphism $\pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(X, x_1)$. The isomorphism depends on the choice of $\alpha$, but the groups are abstractly isomorphic. For a path-connected space, one therefore speaks of "the" fundamental group $\pi_1(X)$ without specifying basepoint.
