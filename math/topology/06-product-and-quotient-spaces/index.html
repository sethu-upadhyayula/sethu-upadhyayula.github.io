Chapter 6: Product and Quotient Spaces

Product and quotient topologies are fundamental constructions for building new topological spaces from known ones. Products take Cartesian products and equip them with the coarsest topology making all projections continuous. Quotients identify points according to an equivalence relation, inheriting the finest topology making the identification map continuous.

Together these constructions produce the torus, Klein bottle, real projective space, and many other spaces central to geometry and topology. Tychonoff's theorem — that arbitrary products of compact spaces are compact — is one of the deepest results in point-set topology.

Chapter Overview

6.1 — Product Topology

The product topology on $\prod_\alpha X_\alpha$ is characterised by a universal property: a map into the product is continuous if and only if each component map is continuous. For finite products, a basis consists of products of open sets. For infinite products (Tychonoff topology), only finitely many factors may be restricted at once. Tychonoff's theorem states that the product of compact spaces is compact — a result equivalent to the Axiom of Choice.

6.2 — Quotient Topology

Given an equivalence relation $\sim$ on $X$, the quotient space $X/{\sim}$ carries the finest topology making the quotient map $q: X \to X/{\sim}$ continuous. A map out of a quotient is continuous precisely when its composition with $q$ is continuous. Saturated sets, open maps, and closed maps play key roles in identifying when quotient maps have good properties.

6.3 — Examples of Quotient Spaces

Concrete constructions: the torus $T^2 = [0,1]^2/{\sim}$ (identify opposite edges), the Klein bottle (one pair reversed), the Möbius band, real projective space $\mathbb{RP}^2 = S^2/{\{\mathbf{x}\sim-\mathbf{x}\}}$, and the quotient $D^2/S^1 \cong S^2$. These spaces are the building blocks for the CW complexes used throughout algebraic topology.
