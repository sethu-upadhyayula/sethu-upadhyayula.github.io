6.2 Quotient Topology

The quotient topology is the finest topology on a set of equivalence classes that makes the identification map continuous. It is the dual construction to the subspace topology (which uses the coarsest topology) and to the product topology (defined by a universal property for maps in).

Definition — Quotient Map

A surjective continuous map $q: X \to Y$ is a quotient map if $V \subseteq Y$ is open if and only if $q^{-1}(V)$ is open in $X$.

Equivalently: $q$ is a quotient map if it is surjective, continuous, and satisfies the "if" direction: whenever $q^{-1}(V)$ is open, so is $V$. Open surjections and closed surjections are quotient maps, but not all quotient maps are open or closed.

Definition — Quotient Space

Let $X$ be a topological space and $\sim$ an equivalence relation on $X$. Let $X/{\sim}$ denote the set of equivalence classes $[x] = \{y \in X : y \sim x\}$, and let $q: X \to X/{\sim}$ be the quotient map $q(x) = [x]$.

The quotient topology on $X/{\sim}$ is

$$\tau_{X/\sim} = \{V \subseteq X/{\sim} : q^{-1}(V) \text{ is open in } X\}.$$

This is the finest topology on $X/{\sim}$ making $q$ continuous.

Theorem — Universal Property of Quotient Spaces

Let $q: X \to X/{\sim}$ be the quotient map. A function $f: X/{\sim} \to Z$ is continuous if and only if $f \circ q: X \to Z$ is continuous.

$$\begin{array}{ccc} X & \xrightarrow{f \circ q} & Z \\ \downarrow^q & \nearrow^f \\ X/{\sim} \end{array}$$

This universal property characterises the quotient topology uniquely.

Definition — Saturated Sets

A subset $A \subseteq X$ is saturated (with respect to $\sim$) if $A = q^{-1}(q(A))$, i.e., $A$ is a union of equivalence classes.

The quotient map $q$ sends open saturated sets to open sets and closed saturated sets to closed sets. Understanding saturation is key to determining the topology of $X/{\sim}$.

Remark — Open and Closed Maps

A continuous map $f: X \to Y$ is open if images of open sets are open, and closed if images of closed sets are closed. Not every quotient map is open or closed, and quotient maps that are neither can be difficult to analyse directly.

A key criterion: if $q: X \to Y$ is a surjective continuous open map (or surjective continuous closed map), it is a quotient map. Conversely, if $q$ is a quotient map and $f \circ q$ is open/closed, it does not follow that $f$ is.

Example — Circle as Quotient of $[0,1]$

Define $\sim$ on $[0,1]$ by $0 \sim 1$ and $x \sim x$ otherwise. Then $[0,1]/{\sim} \cong S^1$.

Proof: The map $f: [0,1] \to S^1$, $f(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$, is continuous and satisfies $f(0) = f(1)$, so it factors through $q$ to give $\tilde{f}: [0,1]/{\sim} \to S^1$. This map is a continuous bijection from a compact space to a Hausdorff space, hence a homeomorphism.

Similarly, $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z} \cong S^1$ via $t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$.

Example — Torus and Klein Bottle as Quotients of $[0,1]^2$

Torus $T^2$: Identify $(x,0) \sim (x,1)$ and $(0,y) \sim (1,y)$ (opposite edges, same direction). Schematically: $abab^{-1}$ becomes $aba^{-1}b^{-1}$ in the quotient? No — same direction on both pairs gives the torus with $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$.

Klein bottle: Identify $(x,0) \sim (x,1)$ (same direction) and $(0,y) \sim (1,1-y)$ (opposite direction). The result is non-orientable and cannot be embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$ without self-intersection.

These identifications are encoded in the word for the polygon: $aba^{-1}b^{-1}$ (torus), $abab^{-1}$ (Klein bottle).
