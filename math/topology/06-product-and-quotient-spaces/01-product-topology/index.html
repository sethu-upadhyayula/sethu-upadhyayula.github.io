6.1 Product Topology

The product topology is the canonical way to combine topological spaces into a Cartesian product while preserving continuity of projections. It is defined by a universal property that characterises it uniquely up to homeomorphism.

Definition — Finite Product Topology

Let $(X, \tau_X)$ and $(Y, \tau_Y)$ be topological spaces. The product topology on $X \times Y$ has as a basis the collection

$$\mathcal{B} = \{U \times V : U \in \tau_X,\; V \in \tau_Y\}.$$

An open set in $X \times Y$ is an arbitrary union of such products. The projection maps $\pi_X: X \times Y \to X$ and $\pi_Y: X \times Y \to Y$ are continuous, open, and surjective.

Theorem — Universal Property of Finite Products

A function $f: Z \to X \times Y$ is continuous if and only if both component maps $\pi_X \circ f: Z \to X$ and $\pi_Y \circ f: Z \to Y$ are continuous.

This universal property characterises the product topology: it is the coarsest topology on $X \times Y$ making both projections continuous.

Definition — Infinite Product (Tychonoff Topology)

Let $\{X_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ be a family of topological spaces. The Tychonoff (product) topology on $\prod_{\alpha \in A} X_\alpha$ is the topology generated by the subbasis

$$\mathcal{S} = \{\pi_\alpha^{-1}(U_\alpha) : \alpha \in A,\; U_\alpha \subseteq X_\alpha \text{ open}\}.$$

A basis element is thus a product $\prod_\alpha V_\alpha$ where $V_\alpha = X_\alpha$ for all but finitely many $\alpha$, and $V_\alpha$ is open in $X_\alpha$ for the remaining finitely many.

Note: The box topology (where all factors may be restricted) is strictly finer than the Tychonoff topology for infinite products, and lacks good properties (e.g., the product of countably many copies of $\mathbb{R}$ in the box topology is not separable nor first countable in expected ways).

Theorem — Tychonoff's Theorem

The product $\prod_{\alpha \in A} X_\alpha$ (with the Tychonoff topology) is compact if and only if each $X_\alpha$ is compact.

Proof (sketch, using Alexander's subbasis lemma): It suffices to show every cover by subbasis elements has a finite subcover. If no finite subcover exists, one finds a consistent choice of points in each factor using Zorn's lemma, constructing a point with no neighbourhood in any finite subcollection — a contradiction.

Equivalence with AC: Tychonoff's theorem for Hausdorff spaces is equivalent to the Axiom of Choice; for compact Hausdorff spaces it is equivalent to the Boolean prime ideal theorem (a weaker choice principle).

Theorem — Product Preserves Separation and Connectedness

Let $\{X_\alpha\}$ be a family of topological spaces.

  • If each $X_\alpha$ is $T_2$ (Hausdorff), then $\prod X_\alpha$ is Hausdorff.
  • If each $X_\alpha$ is connected, then $\prod X_\alpha$ is connected.
  • If each $X_\alpha$ is completely regular ($T_{3\frac{1}{2}}$), then $\prod X_\alpha$ is completely regular.

However, products need not preserve normality: the Sorgenfrey plane $\mathbb{R}_\ell \times \mathbb{R}_\ell$ is a counterexample.

Example — Cantor Set and $\{0,1\}^\mathbb{N}$

The countable product $\{0,1\}^\mathbb{N}$ of the discrete two-point space is homeomorphic to the Cantor set $C \subseteq [0,1]$ (the middle-thirds Cantor set). It is compact, totally disconnected, and perfect (every point is a limit point). By Brouwer's theorem, every compact metrisable totally disconnected perfect space is homeomorphic to the Cantor set.

This shows infinite products can be far more exotic than their factors suggest.

Example — Hilbert Cube $[0,1]^\mathbb{N}$

The countably infinite product $[0,1]^\mathbb{N}$ (the Hilbert cube) is compact (by Tychonoff), metrisable (by $d((x_n),(y_n)) = \sum_n 2^{-n}|x_n - y_n|$), and by Urysohn's metrisation theorem it contains a homeomorphic copy of every second-countable $T_3$ space.
