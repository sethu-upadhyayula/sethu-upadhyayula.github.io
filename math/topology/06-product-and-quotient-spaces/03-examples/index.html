6.3 Examples of Surfaces via Quotients

Quotient constructions allow us to build fundamental examples of compact surfaces by identifying edges of polygons. These examples are central to the classification of compact surfaces and to algebraic topology.

Example - Torus $T^2 = S^1 \times S^1$

The torus $T^2$ is the product $S^1 \times S^1$, where $S^1 = \{e^{2\pi i t} : t \in [0,1]\}$. Concretely, $T^2$ can be realised as the quotient of the unit square $[0,1]^2$ by identifying opposite edges: $$(x,0) \sim (x,1) \quad \text{and} \quad (0,y) \sim (1,y).$$ The identification space carries the quotient topology, and the resulting space is homeomorphic to $S^1 \times S^1$. The torus can be embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$ as a surface of revolution, but its intrinsic topology is best captured by the square with identifications. Its fundamental group is $\pi_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}^2$.

Example - Cylinder $S^1 \times [0,1]$

The cylinder is $S^1 \times [0,1]$, which can also be described as the quotient of $[0,1]^2$ by identifying only one pair of parallel edges: $(0,y) \sim (1,y)$ for $y \in [0,1]$. The cylinder has two boundary circles $S^1 \times \{0\}$ and $S^1 \times \{1\}$ and deformation retracts onto $S^1$, so $\pi_1(\text{cylinder}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

Example - Möbius Band

The Möbius band is the quotient of $[0,1]^2$ by the identification $(0,y) \sim (1, 1-y)$ - that is, one pair of edges is identified with a twist (reversal). The resulting space is a non-orientable surface with boundary. Its boundary is a single circle. The Möbius band also deformation retracts onto $S^1$, so its fundamental group is $\mathbb{Z}$, but it is not homeomorphic to the cylinder (the boundary of the Möbius band is connected, while that of the cylinder is not).

Example - Klein Bottle

The Klein bottle $K$ is the quotient of $[0,1]^2$ by the identifications: $$(x, 0) \sim (x, 1) \quad \text{and} \quad (0, y) \sim (1, 1-y).$$ One pair of edges is identified as in the torus, and the other pair is identified with a reversal (as in the Möbius band). The Klein bottle cannot be embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$ without self-intersection, but it embeds in $\mathbb{R}^4$. It is compact, non-orientable, and closed (no boundary). Its fundamental group is $$\pi_1(K) = \langle a, b \mid abab^{-1} = 1 \rangle.$$

By van Kampen's theorem, $K$ can be seen as two Möbius bands glued along their boundaries.

Example - Real Projective Plane $\mathbb{R}P^2$

The real projective plane $\mathbb{R}P^2$ is the quotient of $S^2$ by the antipodal map $x \mapsto -x$: identify antipodal points. Equivalently, $\mathbb{R}P^2$ is the quotient of the closed disk $D^2$ by identifying antipodal boundary points: $(x,y) \sim (-x,-y)$ for $(x,y) \in S^1 = \partial D^2$. It can also be described as the square $[0,1]^2$ with identification $(x,0) \sim (1-x, 1)$ and $(0,y) \sim (1, 1-y)$. The projective plane is compact, non-orientable, and closed with $$\pi_1(\mathbb{R}P^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.$$

Classification of Compact Surfaces

Theorem - Classification of Compact Surfaces (Statement)

Every compact, connected, closed surface (2-manifold without boundary) is homeomorphic to exactly one of the following:

  • The sphere $S^2$.
  • A connected sum $T^2 \# \cdots \# T^2$ of $g \geq 1$ tori (orientable surfaces of genus $g$).
  • A connected sum $\mathbb{R}P^2 \# \cdots \# \mathbb{R}P^2$ of $k \geq 1$ projective planes (non-orientable surfaces).

These families are pairwise non-homeomorphic (distinguished, e.g., by their Euler characteristic and orientability). The classification is one of the great theorems of 19th-20th century topology.
