5.3 Tietze Extension Theorem

The Tietze–Urysohn extension theorem generalises Urysohn's lemma: rather than constructing a single separating function, it extends an arbitrary continuous function defined on a closed subspace to the whole normal space. This is a foundational result in analysis with far-reaching applications in PDE and differential geometry via partitions of unity.

Theorem — Tietze–Urysohn Extension Theorem

Let $X$ be a normal topological space, $A \subseteq X$ a closed subspace, and $f: A \to [a, b]$ a continuous function. Then there exists a continuous function $F: X \to [a, b]$ such that $F|_A = f$ (i.e., $F(a') = f(a')$ for all $a' \in A$).

Moreover, if $f: A \to \mathbb{R}$ is any continuous function (unbounded), there still exists a continuous extension $F: X \to \mathbb{R}$.

Proof Sketch — Iterative Urysohn Construction

Without loss of generality take $[a,b] = [-1,1]$. We construct $F$ as a uniformly convergent series $F = \sum_{n=0}^\infty g_n$ of continuous functions on $X$.

Base step: The sets $A_0 = f^{-1}([-1, -\tfrac{1}{3}])$ and $B_0 = f^{-1}([\tfrac{1}{3}, 1])$ are disjoint closed subsets of $A$, hence of $X$. By normality, Urysohn's lemma supplies $g_0: X \to [-\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3}]$ with $g_0|_{A_0} = -\tfrac{1}{3}$ and $g_0|_{B_0} = \tfrac{1}{3}$.

Then $|f(x) - g_0(x)| \le \tfrac{2}{3}$ for all $x \in A$, so $f_1 := f - g_0|_A: A \to [-\tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3}]$.

Inductive step: Apply the same argument to $f_n: A \to [-(\tfrac{2}{3})^n, (\tfrac{2}{3})^n]$, obtaining $g_n: X \to [-\tfrac{1}{3}(\tfrac{2}{3})^n, \tfrac{1}{3}(\tfrac{2}{3})^n]$ with $|f_n(x) - g_n(x)| \le (\tfrac{2}{3})^{n+1}$ for $x \in A$.

Convergence: $\sum |g_n| \le \sum \tfrac{1}{3}(\tfrac{2}{3})^n = 1$, so $F = \sum g_n$ converges uniformly and hence $F$ is continuous. One verifies $F|_A = f$.

For the unbounded case $f: A \to \mathbb{R}$, compose with a homeomorphism $\mathbb{R} \cong (-1,1)$, extend, then compose back. $\square$

Remark — Sharpness

Normality is necessary: if $X$ is not normal, there exist continuous $f: A \to [0,1]$ (with $A$ closed) that cannot be extended. The Tietze theorem is in fact equivalent to normality: a $T_1$ space in which every bounded continuous function on a closed subspace extends to $X$ is normal.

Definition — Partition of Unity

Let $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ be an open cover of a topological space $X$. A partition of unity subordinate to the cover is a collection of continuous functions $\{\phi_\alpha: X \to [0,1]\}_{\alpha \in A}$ such that:

  • $\mathrm{supp}(\phi_\alpha) \subseteq U_\alpha$ for all $\alpha$,
  • the supports are locally finite (every point has a neighbourhood meeting only finitely many supports),
  • $\sum_\alpha \phi_\alpha(x) = 1$ for all $x \in X$.

Partitions of unity allow local constructions to be patched together globally.

Theorem — Existence on Paracompact Spaces

If $X$ is paracompact and Hausdorff (in particular, if $X$ is a manifold or a compact Hausdorff space), then every open cover admits a partition of unity subordinate to it.

Proof idea: Use paracompactness to find a locally finite refinement; apply Urysohn's lemma on the normal space to build bump functions for each refined open set; normalise to sum to 1.

Applications

1. Smooth manifolds. Partitions of unity on smooth manifolds allow one to define integration of differential forms, construct Riemannian metrics, and prove the existence of smooth bump functions. They underlie the definition of sheaf cohomology and the construction of connections.

2. PDE theory. To solve a PDE on a manifold, one often solves it locally in coordinate charts and patches the solutions together using a partition of unity. This technique appears throughout elliptic PDE theory and the study of Sobolev spaces.

3. Extending functions in analysis. If $A$ is a closed subset of a metric space $X$ and $f: A \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, the McShane–Whitney extension $F(x) = \inf_{a \in A}(f(a) + L \cdot d(x,a))$ (for Lipschitz $f$) is an explicit extension without needing the full Tietze machinery.

4. Algebraic topology. Extensions of maps are a central concern of obstruction theory; the Tietze theorem is the "degree-0" case of a general obstruction theory for extending maps into $K(\pi, n)$-spaces.
