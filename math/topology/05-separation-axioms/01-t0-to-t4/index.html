5.1 Separation Axioms T₀ to T₄

Separation axioms measure how richly a topological space can distinguish its points and closed sets by means of open sets. They form a strict hierarchy, with each higher axiom implying all lower ones. Most spaces arising in analysis and geometry sit at the Hausdorff level or above.

Definition — $T_0$ (Kolmogorov)

A space $X$ is $T_0$ if for any two distinct points $x \neq y$, there exists an open set containing one but not the other. Formally: $\exists\, U$ open with $x\in U,\, y\notin U$, or $\exists\, V$ open with $y\in V,\, x\notin V$ (but not necessarily both).

The Sierpiński space $\{0,1\}$ with topology $\{\emptyset, \{1\}, \{0,1\}\}$ is $T_0$ but not $T_1$: the point $0$ has no open neighbourhood excluding $1$.

Definition — $T_1$

A space $X$ is $T_1$ if for any two distinct points $x \neq y$, each has an open neighbourhood not containing the other. Equivalently, every singleton $\{x\}$ is a closed set in $X$.

In a $T_1$ space, finite sets are closed. The cofinite topology on any infinite set is $T_1$ but generally not $T_2$.

Definition — $T_2$ (Hausdorff)

A space $X$ is Hausdorff ($T_2$) if for any two distinct points $x \neq y$, there exist disjoint open sets $U \ni x$ and $V \ni y$ (i.e., $U \cap V = \emptyset$).

The Hausdorff condition is extremely natural: in a $T_2$ space, limits of sequences (and nets) are unique. Every metric space is Hausdorff. Every subspace and every product of Hausdorff spaces is Hausdorff.

Theorem — Limits are unique in Hausdorff spaces

If $X$ is Hausdorff and a net $(x_\alpha)$ converges to both $x$ and $y$, then $x = y$. Proof: If $x \neq y$, choose disjoint open $U \ni x$, $V \ni y$. Eventually $x_\alpha \in U$ and eventually $x_\alpha \in V$, so eventually $x_\alpha \in U \cap V = \emptyset$, a contradiction.

Definition — $T_3$ (Regular)

A space $X$ is regular if it is $T_1$ and for every point $x$ and every closed set $C$ with $x \notin C$, there exist disjoint open sets $U \ni x$ and $V \supseteq C$.

Regularity strengthens $T_2$ by separating a point from a closed set, not just from another point. Metric spaces are regular ($T_3$). A subspace of a regular space is regular.

Definition — $T_{3\frac{1}{2}}$ (Completely Regular / Tychonoff)

A space $X$ is completely regular (or Tychonoff) if it is $T_1$ and for every point $x$ and every closed set $C$ with $x\notin C$, there exists a continuous function $f:X\to[0,1]$ with $f(x)=0$ and $f|_C\equiv 1$.

Complete regularity lies strictly between $T_3$ and $T_4$. Every subspace of a completely regular space is completely regular; arbitrary products of completely regular spaces are completely regular (by the universal property of products).

Definition — $T_4$ (Normal)

A space $X$ is normal if it is $T_1$ and for any two disjoint closed sets $A$ and $B$, there exist disjoint open sets $U \supseteq A$ and $V \supseteq B$.

Normality separates closed sets by open sets. The next section (Urysohn's lemma) characterises normal spaces in terms of continuous functions.

Theorem — Metric spaces are $T_4$

Every metric space $(X,d)$ is normal. Proof sketch: Given disjoint closed sets $A, B$, define $f(x) = d(x,A)/(d(x,A)+d(x,B))$; then $f$ is continuous, $f|_A = 0$, $f|_B = 1$, and $U=f^{-1}([0,\tfrac12))$, $V=f^{-1}((\tfrac12,1])$ are disjoint open neighbourhoods of $A$ and $B$.

Hence metric spaces satisfy: $T_4 \Rightarrow T_{3\frac{1}{2}} \Rightarrow T_3 \Rightarrow T_2 \Rightarrow T_1 \Rightarrow T_0$.

Theorem — Compact Hausdorff spaces are $T_4$

If $X$ is compact and Hausdorff, then $X$ is normal. Proof: First show every compact subset of a Hausdorff space can be separated from any disjoint closed set by open sets (using the Hausdorff condition finitely many times by compactness). In a compact space, closed subsets are compact, so disjoint closed sets can be so separated.

The Separation Hierarchy

The implications between separation axioms are summarised as:

$$T_4 \;\Rightarrow\; T_{3\frac{1}{2}} \;\Rightarrow\; T_3 \;\Rightarrow\; T_2 \;\Rightarrow\; T_1 \;\Rightarrow\; T_0$$

None of these implications reverses in general. Counterexamples include:

  • Sierpiński space: $T_0$ but not $T_1$.
  • Cofinite topology on $\mathbb{N}$: $T_1$ but not $T_2$.
  • The Sorgenfrey plane $\mathbb{R}_\ell \times \mathbb{R}_\ell$: $T_{3\frac{1}{2}}$ but not $T_4$ (the antidiagonal is a closed discrete subspace whose complement is not normal).
Example — $T_2$ does not imply $T_3$

The Knaster–Kuratowski fan (or certain subsets of $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the induced topology) can be constructed to be Hausdorff but not regular. More concretely: start with $\mathbb{R}$ in the usual topology and add the integers as isolated points — certain modifications yield $T_2$ non-$T_3$ spaces.
