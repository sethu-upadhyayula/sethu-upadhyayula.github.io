2.3 Topological Properties

Countability axioms measure how "big" the topology of a space is, while separability and the Lindelöf property relate to density and coverings. These properties form a hierarchy linking metric spaces to more general topological spaces.

First and Second Countability

Definition — First Countable

A topological space $X$ is first countable if every point $x \in X$ has a countable neighbourhood basis: a countable collection $\{U_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ of open sets containing $x$ such that every open neighbourhood of $x$ contains some $U_n$.

Definition — Second Countable

A topological space $X$ is second countable if it has a countable basis: a countable collection $\mathcal{B} = \{B_n\}$ of open sets such that every open set is a union of elements of $\mathcal{B}$.

Proposition — Metric Spaces are First Countable

Every metric space $(X, d)$ is first countable. For each $x \in X$, the balls $\{B(x, 1/n) : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ form a countable neighbourhood basis. In a first countable space, sequences suffice to detect limits and continuity: $x \in \overline{A}$ iff some sequence in $A$ converges to $x$, and $f$ is continuous at $x$ iff $f(x_n) \to f(x)$ for every sequence $x_n \to x$.

Proposition — $\mathbb{R}^n$ is Second Countable

$\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard topology is second countable. A countable basis is given by $\{B(q, r) : q \in \mathbb{Q}^n,\, r \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}\}$, the open balls with rational centres and rational radii. This is countable (a countable union of countable sets) and is a basis since every open ball $B(x, \varepsilon)$ contains a rational ball $B(q, r) \ni x$ with $r < \varepsilon$.

Separability

Definition — Separable Space

A topological space $X$ is separable if it contains a countable dense subset: a countable $D \subseteq X$ with $\overline{D} = X$.

Proposition — Second Countable Implies Separable

Every second countable space is separable. If $\{B_n\}$ is a countable basis, choose $x_n \in B_n$ for each $n$; then $D = \{x_n\}$ is a countable dense subset (every non-empty open set contains some $B_n$, hence some $x_n$). The converse fails in general: a separable space need not be second countable (e.g., the lower-limit topology $\mathbb{R}_\ell$, which is separable but not second countable).

Proposition — Second Countable $\Rightarrow$ Separable $\Rightarrow$ (in metric spaces) Second Countable

For metric spaces, second countability, separability, and the Lindelöf property are all equivalent. Thus $\mathbb{R}^n$ (separable: $\mathbb{Q}^n$ is dense) is also second countable and Lindelöf. Non-metrisable spaces can break these implications.

The Lindelöf Property

Definition — Lindelöf Space

A topological space $X$ is Lindelöf if every open cover of $X$ has a countable subcover. Every compact space is Lindelöf. Every second countable space is Lindelöf (given an open cover, refine to the countable basis and select corresponding cover elements).

Proposition — Summary of Implications

Among general topological spaces:

$$\text{second countable} \Rightarrow \text{separable} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{second countable} \Rightarrow \text{Lindelöf}$$

but neither implication reverses in general. In metric spaces:

$$\text{second countable} \Leftrightarrow \text{separable} \Leftrightarrow \text{Lindelöf}.$$

Also: second countable $\Rightarrow$ first countable (take local basis at $x$ from the global basis), but the converse fails.
