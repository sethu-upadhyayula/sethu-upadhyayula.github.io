2.2 Homeomorphisms

A homeomorphism is a continuous bijection with a continuous inverse - the "isomorphism" of topology. Two homeomorphic spaces are topologically indistinguishable: any topological property of one holds for the other.

Definition - Homeomorphism

A function $f: X \to Y$ is a homeomorphism if:

  1. $f$ is a bijection.
  2. $f$ is continuous.
  3. $f^{-1}: Y \to X$ is continuous.

If such an $f$ exists, $X$ and $Y$ are said to be homeomorphic, written $X \cong Y$. Homeomorphism is an equivalence relation on the class of topological spaces.

Remark - Bijective Continuous Maps Need Not Be Homeomorphisms

A bijective continuous map need not have a continuous inverse. For example, let $X = [0, 1) \cup \{2\}$ with the subspace topology from $\mathbb{R}$ and $Y = [0,1]$. Define $f: [0,1] \to S^1$ by $f(t) = (\cos 2\pi t, \sin 2\pi t)$. This is a continuous bijection but its inverse is not continuous at $f(0) = (1,0)$. The key additional condition is that $f^{-1}$ be continuous, or equivalently that $f$ be an open map (images of open sets are open).

Key Examples

Example - $(0,1) \cong \mathbb{R}$

The open interval $(0,1)$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}$. One homeomorphism is $f: (0,1) \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $$f(t) = \tan\!\left(\pi t - \frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \cot(\pi(1-t)).$$ This is a bijection; $f$ and $f^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\arctan(x) + \frac{1}{2}$ are both continuous. More generally, any open interval $(a,b)$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}$, so boundedness is not a topological invariant among non-compact spaces.

However, $(0,1)$ is not homeomorphic to $[0,1]$: removing any interior point from $[0,1]$ leaves a disconnected space, but $(0,1)$ has points whose removal leaves a disconnected space too - in fact these spaces differ in compactness: $[0,1]$ is compact and $(0,1)$ is not.

Example - $S^1$ Is Not Homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}$

The circle $S^1 = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x^2 + y^2 = 1\}$ is not homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}$. One argument: $S^1$ is compact (closed and bounded in $\mathbb{R}^2$) while $\mathbb{R}$ is not. Another: removing any point from $S^1$ leaves a connected space (homeomorphic to $(0,1)$), whereas removing any point from $\mathbb{R}$ leaves a disconnected space.

Example - $\mathbb{R}^m \not\cong \mathbb{R}^n$ for $m \neq n$

The spaces $\mathbb{R}^m$ and $\mathbb{R}^n$ are not homeomorphic for $m \neq n$. This is the invariance of domain theorem, whose proof requires algebraic topology (specifically, homology groups distinguish them). As a simpler special case, $\mathbb{R} \not\cong \mathbb{R}^2$: removing a point from $\mathbb{R}$ disconnects it, while removing a point from $\mathbb{R}^2$ does not.

Topological Invariants

Definition - Topological Invariant

A property $P$ of topological spaces is a topological invariant if, whenever $X \cong Y$ and $X$ has property $P$, then $Y$ also has property $P$. Topological invariants are used to distinguish non-homeomorphic spaces.

Example - Standard Topological Invariants

The following are topological invariants:

  • Compactness: continuous images of compact spaces are compact; in particular, if $f: X \to Y$ is a homeomorphism and $X$ is compact, so is $Y$.
  • Connectedness and path-connectedness.
  • Separation axioms $T_0, T_1, T_2, \ldots$
  • Countability properties: first and second countability, separability.
  • Fundamental group $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ (up to isomorphism).

These invariants give necessary conditions for homeomorphism. The art of topology is finding invariants powerful enough to distinguish spaces of interest.

Homeomorphism Groups

Definition - Homeomorphism Group

The homeomorphism group $\mathrm{Homeo}(X)$ of a topological space $X$ is the group of all homeomorphisms $f: X \to X$ under composition. The group operation is composition, the identity is $\mathrm{id}_X$, and inverses are the inverse homeomorphisms. For example, $\mathrm{Homeo}(S^1)$ contains all rotations and reflections of the circle; it is related to the orthogonal group $O(2)$ but is strictly larger.
