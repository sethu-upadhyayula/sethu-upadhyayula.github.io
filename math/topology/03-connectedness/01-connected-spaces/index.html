3.1 Connected Spaces

A space is connected if it cannot be split into two disjoint non-empty open pieces. This seemingly simple condition has profound consequences and is preserved by continuous maps.

Definition — Connected Space

A topological space $X$ is disconnected if there exist non-empty open sets $U, V \subseteq X$ such that $U \cap V = \emptyset$ and $U \cup V = X$. The pair $(U, V)$ is called a separation of $X$.

$X$ is connected if no such separation exists.

Proposition — Clopen Characterisation

$X$ is connected if and only if the only subsets of $X$ that are both open and closed (clopen) are $\emptyset$ and $X$ itself. Indeed, if $U$ is clopen and $U \neq \emptyset, X$, then $(U, X \setminus U)$ is a separation.

Connectedness of $\mathbb{R}$ and $[a,b]$

Theorem — $\mathbb{R}$ is Connected

Proof. Suppose $\mathbb{R} = U \cup V$ with $U, V$ open, disjoint, non-empty. Pick $u \in U$ and $v \in V$; WLOG $u < v$. Let $s = \sup(U \cap [u,v])$. Since $U$ is open, $s \notin U$ (otherwise an interval around $s$ would be in $U$, contradicting $s = \sup$). So $s \in V$. But $V$ is open, so some $(s-\varepsilon, s+\varepsilon) \subseteq V$, meaning $(s-\varepsilon, s) \cap U = \emptyset$, contradicting $s = \sup(U \cap [u,v])$. $\square$

The same argument shows any interval (open, closed, or half-open) in $\mathbb{R}$ is connected. More generally, the connected subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are exactly the intervals (including singletons and $\mathbb{R}$ itself).

Theorem — Intermediate Value Theorem

Let $f: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous and $f(a) < c < f(b)$. Then there exists $x \in (a,b)$ with $f(x) = c$. This follows immediately from connectedness: $f([a,b])$ is a connected subset of $\mathbb{R}$ containing $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, so it contains the entire interval $[f(a), f(b)]$.

Continuous Images of Connected Spaces

Theorem — Continuous Image of Connected is Connected

Proof. Let $f: X \to Y$ be continuous, $X$ connected. Suppose $f(X) = U \cup V$ with $U, V$ open in $f(X)$, disjoint, non-empty. Then $X = f^{-1}(U) \cup f^{-1}(V)$ is a separation of $X$ by open sets (since $f$ is continuous), contradicting connectedness of $X$. $\square$

Connectedness is therefore a topological invariant: homeomorphic spaces are either both connected or both disconnected.

$\mathbb{Q}$ is Disconnected

Example — $\mathbb{Q}$ is Totally Disconnected

The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ (with the subspace topology from $\mathbb{R}$) are totally disconnected: every connected subset has at most one point. For any $p < q$ in $\mathbb{Q}$, choose an irrational $\alpha \in (p,q)$; then $U = (-\infty, \alpha) \cap \mathbb{Q}$ and $V = (\alpha, \infty) \cap \mathbb{Q}$ separate $\mathbb{Q}$.

Further Properties

Proposition — Union of Connected Sets

If $\{A_\alpha\}$ is a family of connected subsets of $X$ with $\bigcap_\alpha A_\alpha \neq \emptyset$, then $\bigcup_\alpha A_\alpha$ is connected. Also, the closure of a connected set is connected: if $A$ is connected, so is $\overline{A}$.
