4.2 Heine–Borel Theorem

The Heine–Borel theorem gives a concrete, metric-space characterisation of compactness in $\mathbb{R}^n$, connecting the abstract open-cover definition to the familiar notions of being closed and bounded.

Theorem — Heine–Borel

A subset $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is compact (with the standard metric topology) if and only if $A$ is closed and bounded.

Proof: Closed and Bounded Implies Compact

Step 1 — $[a,b]$ is Compact

Let $\{U_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $[a,b]$. Define $s = \sup\{x \in [a,b] : [a,x] \text{ has a finite subcover}\}$. Then $s \in U_\beta$ for some $\beta$, so $[a, s+\varepsilon] \cap [a,b]$ is also finitely covered, contradicting maximality of $s$ unless $s = b$. Hence $s = b$ and $[a,b]$ is compact. $\square$

Step 2 — Products and Closed Subsets

By Tychonoff's theorem (or the tube lemma for finite products), $[a_1,b_1] \times \cdots \times [a_n, b_n]$ is compact in $\mathbb{R}^n$. If $A$ is closed and bounded, choose a box $[-M,M]^n \supseteq A$. Then $A$ is a closed subset of a compact space, hence compact (§4.1). $\square$

Proof: Compact Implies Closed and Bounded

Compact Subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$ are Closed and Bounded

Since $\mathbb{R}^n$ is Hausdorff, every compact $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is closed (§4.1). For boundedness, the open cover $\{B(0,n) : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ of $A$ has a finite subcover $B(0,n_1), \ldots, B(0, n_k)$; taking $M = \max n_i$, we get $A \subseteq B(0, M)$. $\square$

Sequential Compactness

Definition — Sequentially Compact

A metric space is sequentially compact if every sequence has a convergent subsequence.

Theorem — Equivalence in Metric Spaces

For metric spaces, compactness, sequential compactness, and completeness plus total boundedness are all equivalent. Thus a subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is compact iff it is sequentially compact iff every sequence in it has a convergent subsequence with limit in the set.

Theorem — Bolzano–Weierstrass

Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}^n$ has a convergent subsequence. This is a restatement of the sequential compactness of $[-M,M]^n$: a bounded sequence lies in some closed box, which is compact, hence sequentially compact.
