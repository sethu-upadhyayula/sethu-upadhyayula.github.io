4.1 Compact Spaces

Compactness is defined via open covers and is the topological generalisation of finiteness. Many key theorems of analysis rely on compactness in an essential way.

Definition — Compact Space

A topological space $X$ is compact if every open cover of $X$ has a finite subcover: whenever $X = \bigcup_{\alpha \in A} U_\alpha$ with each $U_\alpha$ open, there exist finitely many indices $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ such that $X = U_{\alpha_1} \cup \cdots \cup U_{\alpha_n}$.

A subset $K \subseteq X$ is compact if it is compact as a topological space with the subspace topology, equivalently if every cover of $K$ by open sets of $X$ has a finite subcover.

Fundamental Theorems

Theorem — Closed Subsets of Compact Spaces are Compact

Proof. Let $K \subseteq X$ compact and $C \subseteq K$ closed in $K$ (or equivalently, $C$ closed in $X$ and $C \subseteq K$). Let $\{U_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $C$. Then $\{U_\alpha\} \cup \{X \setminus C\}$ is an open cover of $K$. By compactness of $K$, finitely many sets cover $K$, and removing $X \setminus C$ gives a finite cover of $C$. $\square$

Theorem — Compact Subsets of Hausdorff Spaces are Closed

Proof. Let $K \subseteq X$ be compact with $X$ Hausdorff. We show $X \setminus K$ is open. Fix $x \in X \setminus K$. For each $k \in K$, find disjoint open $U_k \ni x$ and $V_k \ni k$. Then $\{V_k\}$ covers $K$; extract a finite subcover $V_{k_1}, \ldots, V_{k_n}$. Then $U = U_{k_1} \cap \cdots \cap U_{k_n}$ is an open neighbourhood of $x$ disjoint from $V_{k_1} \cup \cdots \cup V_{k_n} \supseteq K$. $\square$

Theorem — Continuous Image of Compact is Compact

Proof. Let $f: X \to Y$ continuous, $X$ compact. Any open cover $\{V_\alpha\}$ of $f(X)$ gives an open cover $\{f^{-1}(V_\alpha)\}$ of $X$. Extract a finite subcover $f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_1}), \ldots, f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_n})$; then $V_{\alpha_1}, \ldots, V_{\alpha_n}$ cover $f(X)$. $\square$

Corollary: a continuous bijection from a compact space to a Hausdorff space is a homeomorphism (the image is compact, hence closed, so the inverse is continuous).

Tychonoff's Theorem

Theorem — Tychonoff's Theorem

An arbitrary product of compact spaces is compact: if $\{X_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ are compact topological spaces, then $\prod_{\alpha \in A} X_\alpha$ with the product topology is compact. For finite products this follows by induction from the tube lemma (§4.3). The general case requires the Axiom of Choice (specifically Zorn's Lemma) and is one of the most important theorems in topology. Tychonoff's theorem is in fact equivalent to the Axiom of Choice.
