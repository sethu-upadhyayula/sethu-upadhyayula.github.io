6.3 Stability and Stiffness

For non-stiff problems, accuracy governs the choice of step size. For stiff problems, stability imposes a far more restrictive constraint on $h$ when using explicit methods, making implicit schemes essential. Understanding stability regions and A-stability is the key to selecting the right solver.

Stability Regions

Definition - Stability Region

The stability region $\mathcal{S}$ of a one-step method is the set of $z = h\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ for which the method, applied to $y' = \lambda y$, produces $|y_{n+1}/y_n| = |R(z)| \le 1$. For the test equation the exact solution satisfies $|y(t_n)| = e^{(\text{Re}\,\lambda)t_n} \to 0$ iff $\text{Re}\,\lambda < 0$.

Proposition - Stability Functions

The stability functions of the key explicit methods are Taylor polynomials of $e^z$:

  • Forward Euler: $R(z) = 1+z$. Stability region: disk $|1+z|\le 1$ (radius 1, centred at $-1$). On the negative real axis: $z \in [-2,0]$, so $h \le 2/|\lambda|$.
  • RK4: $R(z) = 1 + z + z^2/2 + z^3/6 + z^4/24$. Stability region on negative real axis: $z \in [-2.785, 0]$, so $h \le 2.785/|\lambda|$.
  • Backward Euler: $R(z) = 1/(1-z)$. $|R(z)| \le 1$ for all $\text{Re}\,z \le 0$: the entire left half-plane.

A-Stability

Definition - A-Stability

A method is A-stable if its stability region contains the entire open left half-plane $\mathbb{C}^- = \{z : \text{Re}\,z < 0\}$. An A-stable method can take any step size $h>0$ and still produce bounded numerical solutions whenever $\text{Re}\,\lambda < 0$.

Theorem - Dahlquist Barrier

No explicit linear multistep method is A-stable. Among implicit linear multistep methods, A-stable methods have order at most 2. The most accurate A-stable linear multistep method is the trapezoidal rule (Crank-Nicolson), which is second-order and A-stable.

Stiff Equations

Definition - Stiffness

A system $\mathbf{y}' = J\mathbf{y}$ (or its linearisation) is stiff if its Jacobian $J$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m$ with widely separated magnitudes: $\max_i |\text{Re}\,\lambda_i| \gg \min_i |\text{Re}\,\lambda_i|$ (all with $\text{Re}\,\lambda_i < 0$).

The stiffness ratio $S = \max|\text{Re}\,\lambda_i|/\min|\text{Re}\,\lambda_i|$ measures severity. For an explicit method with stability boundary at $|z_{\max}|$, stability requires $h \le |z_{\max}|/\max|\lambda_i|$, forcing tiny steps even when accuracy would permit large $h$ (governed by $\min|\lambda_i|$).

Example - Chemical Kinetics

Robertson's equations ($y_1' = -0.04y_1 + 10^4 y_2 y_3$, etc.) have eigenvalues ranging from $\sim -0.04$ to $\sim -10^{10}$, a stiffness ratio of $\sim 10^{11}$. An explicit method would require $h \lesssim 10^{-10}$ throughout, while the solution evolves on a time scale of $10^{10}$: $\sim 10^{20}$ steps. Implicit methods reduce this to a few thousand steps.

Other stiff problems: electrical circuits with fast and slow RC time constants, atmospheric chemistry, structural mechanics with high-frequency vibrations.

Implicit Methods for Stiff Problems

Definition - Crank-Nicolson and DIRK Methods

Crank-Nicolson (trapezoidal rule): $$y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{2}\bigl(f(t_n,y_n)+f(t_{n+1},y_{n+1})\bigr).$$ Second-order accurate and A-stable.

DIRK (Diagonally Implicit RK) methods: implicit RK with lower-triangular $A$ (diagonal nonzero). Each stage requires solving one nonlinear equation rather than a coupled system, reducing cost. L-stable variants also damp high-frequency error at each step (useful for parabolic PDEs).

Definition - BDF Methods

The Backward Differentiation Formulae (BDF) of order $k$ use a $k$-step interpolating polynomial to approximate $y'$: $$\sum_{j=0}^k \alpha_j y_{n+j} = h\,\beta_0 f(t_{n+k},y_{n+k}).$$ Only the new value $y_{n+k}$ appears on the right, making these implicit one-derivative methods.

OrderA-stabilityStability
BDF1A-stable(= Backward Euler)
BDF2A-stableRecommended for stiff problems
BDF3-6A($\alpha$)-stableLarge stability angle, widely used

BDF methods are the workhorse of stiff ODE solvers (MATLAB's ode15s, SUNDIALS' CVODE).
