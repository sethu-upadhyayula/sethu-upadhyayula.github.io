4.1 Polynomial Interpolation

Given $n+1$ data points, there is a unique polynomial of degree at most $n$ passing through all of them. The Lagrange form gives an explicit formula; the error formula shows how the interpolation error depends on the smoothness of $f$ and the placement of the nodes.

Lagrange Interpolating Polynomial

Theorem — Existence and Uniqueness

Given $n+1$ distinct nodes $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n$ and values $y_0, \ldots, y_n$, there exists a unique polynomial $L_n \in \mathcal{P}_n$ (polynomials of degree $\le n$) satisfying $L_n(x_i) = y_i$ for all $i$.

Definition — Lagrange Basis Polynomials

The Lagrange basis polynomial associated with node $x_i$ is:

$$\ell_i(x) = \prod_{j=0,\, j \ne i}^n \frac{x - x_j}{x_i - x_j}.$$

It satisfies $\ell_i(x_j) = \delta_{ij}$ (Kronecker delta). The interpolating polynomial is:

$$L_n(x) = \sum_{i=0}^n y_i \,\ell_i(x).$$

Interpolation Error

Theorem — Interpolation Error

If $f \in C^{n+1}[a,b]$ and $L_n$ is its interpolant at nodes $x_0, \ldots, x_n \in [a,b]$, then for each $x \in [a,b]$ there exists $\xi \in (a,b)$ such that:

$$f(x) - L_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(\xi)}{(n+1)!} \,\omega_{n+1}(x),$$

where $\omega_{n+1}(x) = \prod_{i=0}^n (x - x_i)$ is the node polynomial. Thus $\|f - L_n\|_\infty \le \tfrac{\|f^{(n+1)}\|_\infty}{(n+1)!}\|\omega_{n+1}\|_\infty$.

Runge's Phenomenon

Example — Runge's Phenomenon

Consider $f(x) = 1/(1+25x^2)$ on $[-1,1]$ with $n+1 = 11$ equally-spaced nodes. The interpolant $L_{10}$ oscillates wildly near $x = \pm 1$, with maximum error growing as $n \to \infty$. This is Runge's phenomenon: for uniformly-spaced nodes, $\|\omega_{n+1}\|_\infty$ grows super-exponentially even though $f$ is analytic, making high-degree polynomial interpolation numerically useless for this class of functions and node placements.

The fundamental issue is that $\max_x |\omega_{n+1}(x)|$ for uniform nodes on $[-1,1]$ grows like $4^{-n}(n+1)!/n^n$, which dominates any fixed number of derivatives of $f$.

Chebyshev Nodes

Definition — Chebyshev Nodes

The Chebyshev nodes of the first kind on $[-1,1]$ are:

$$x_k = \cos\!\left(\frac{(2k+1)\pi}{2(n+1)}\right), \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n.$$

These are the zeros of the Chebyshev polynomial $T_{n+1}(x) = \cos((n+1)\arccos x)$.

Theorem — Optimality of Chebyshev Nodes

Among all choices of $n+1$ nodes in $[-1,1]$, the Chebyshev nodes minimise $\|\omega_{n+1}\|_\infty$. The minimum value is:

$$\min_{x_0,\ldots,x_n} \left\|\prod_{i=0}^n(x-x_i)\right\|_\infty = \frac{1}{2^n},$$

achieved precisely by the Chebyshev nodes. In particular, $\|\omega_{n+1}\|_\infty = 2^{-n}$ for Chebyshev nodes versus $O(n! / (4n)^n)$ for uniform nodes (exponentially worse). For analytic $f$, Chebyshev interpolation converges geometrically.

Remark — Barycentric Interpolation

The barycentric form of Lagrange interpolation rewrites $L_n(x) = \bigl(\sum_i w_i y_i / (x-x_i)\bigr) / \bigl(\sum_i w_i/(x-x_i)\bigr)$ where $w_i = 1/\prod_{j\ne i}(x_i - x_j)$. For Chebyshev nodes, $w_k = (-1)^k \sin((2k+1)\pi/(2n+2))$. Evaluation costs $O(n)$ and the formula is numerically stable — the preferred algorithm in practice.
