7.2 QR Algorithm

The QR algorithm, discovered independently by Francis and Kublanovskaya in 1961, is the standard method for computing all eigenvalues of a dense matrix. In its practical form it runs in $O(n^3)$ operations and is numerically backward stable.

Basic QR Iteration

Definition — Basic QR Iteration

Set $A_0 = A$. At each step, compute the QR factorisation $A_k = Q_k R_k$ and form the reversed product: $$A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k = Q_k^T A_k Q_k.$$

Since $A_{k+1}$ is orthogonally similar to $A_k$, all iterates share the same eigenvalues. The sequence $A_k$ converges (under mild conditions) to the real Schur form — an upper quasi-triangular matrix with 1×1 and 2×2 diagonal blocks whose eigenvalues are the eigenvalues of $A$.

Theorem — Convergence of Basic QR

If $A$ is diagonalisable with $|\lambda_1| > |\lambda_2| > \cdots > |\lambda_n|$, then the subdiagonal entries satisfy $(A_k)_{ij} \to 0$ for $i > j$ as $k\to\infty$, and the convergence rate of $(A_k)_{j+1,j}$ is $|\lambda_{j+1}/\lambda_j|^k$. Thus well-separated eigenvalues give fast convergence.

Hessenberg Reduction

Proposition — Reduction to Hessenberg Form

Before running QR iteration, reduce $A$ to upper Hessenberg form $H = Q^T A Q$ (zeros below the first subdiagonal) using $n-2$ Householder reflections. This costs $O(n^3)$ once and is exact (up to rounding). The QR factorisation of a Hessenberg matrix costs only $O(n^2)$ per step (instead of $O(n^3)$), so the full algorithm runs in $O(n^3)$ total for $O(n)$ QR steps. Hessenberg form is preserved by QR iteration.

Shifts and the Francis Double-Shift

Definition — Shifted QR and Francis Double Shift

A shift $\sigma$ accelerates convergence: perform QR on $A_k - \sigma I$ and add back: $A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k + \sigma I$. The bottom-right eigenvalue converges fastest when $\sigma \approx \lambda_n$.

The Francis double-shift (implicit QR) works with the complex conjugate pair of shifts $\sigma, \bar{\sigma}$ simultaneously without leaving the reals. It applies a bulge-chasing algorithm entirely in real arithmetic, making one implicit double-step equivalent to two shifted QR steps. This is the algorithm implemented in LAPACK's DHSEQR.

Convergence and Complexity

Theorem — Complexity of Practical QR

The practical QR algorithm (Hessenberg reduction + implicit double-shift QR) computes all eigenvalues of an $n\times n$ real matrix in $O(n^3)$ flops. Computing the full Schur decomposition (eigenvectors) costs approximately $10n^3$ flops. The algorithm is backward stable: the computed Schur form is the exact Schur form of $A + E$ where $\|E\|_2 \lesssim n\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}\|A\|_2$.
