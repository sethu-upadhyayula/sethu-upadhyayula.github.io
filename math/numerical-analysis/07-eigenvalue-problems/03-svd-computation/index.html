7.3 SVD Computation

The singular value decomposition is perhaps the most important matrix factorisation in computational mathematics, underpinning least-squares solving, dimensionality reduction, image compression, and the definition of numerical rank.

The SVD

Theorem — Singular Value Decomposition

Every $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ (with $m \ge n$) has a factorisation $A = U \Sigma V^T$ where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ are orthogonal, and $\Sigma = \text{diag}(\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_n)$ with $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sigma_n \ge 0$. The values $\sigma_i$ are the singular values; they satisfy $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)}$.

Golub-Reinsch Algorithm

Definition — Golub-Reinsch Algorithm

Phase 1: Bidiagonalisation. Use Householder reflections alternately from the left and right to reduce $A$ to upper bidiagonal form: $$U_1^T A V_1 = B = \begin{pmatrix} d_1 & e_1 & & \\ & d_2 & e_2 & \\ & & \ddots & e_{n-1} \\ & & & d_n \end{pmatrix}.$$ This requires $O(mn^2)$ flops.

Phase 2: Bidiagonal SVD. Apply QR-type iterations with implicit shifts to the bidiagonal matrix $B$ until the off-diagonal entries vanish. The singular values $\sigma_i$ then appear on the diagonal. Total: $O(mn^2 + n^3)$ flops.

Thin SVD and Numerical Rank

Definition — Thin SVD and Numerical Rank

The thin (economy) SVD retains only the first $n$ columns of $U$: $A = U_n \Sigma V^T$ with $U_n \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$.

The numerical rank of $A$ is $r = \#\{i : \sigma_i > \delta\}$ for a threshold $\delta$ typically taken as $n\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}\sigma_1$. Singular values below $\delta$ are numerically indistinguishable from zero given the precision of the data.

Best Low-Rank Approximation: Eckart-Young Theorem

Theorem — Eckart-Young-Mirsky

Let $A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ (truncated SVD). Then $$\min_{\text{rank}(B) \le k} \|A - B\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1} \quad \text{achieved at } B = A_k,$$ and similarly for the Frobenius norm: $\min_{\text{rank}(B)\le k}\|A-B\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_n^2}$, also achieved at $B = A_k$.

This gives the theoretical basis for image compression: represent an $m\times n$ image matrix by its rank-$k$ approximation using $k(m+n+1)$ numbers instead of $mn$.

Applications

Definition — Pseudoinverse

The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse is $A^\dagger = V \Sigma^\dagger U^T$ where $\Sigma^\dagger = \text{diag}(\sigma_1^{-1},\ldots,\sigma_r^{-1},0,\ldots,0)$ (inverting only the nonzero singular values). For an overdetermined consistent system $Ax=b$, $x^* = A^\dagger b$ is the minimum-norm least-squares solution.

Definition — Condition Number

The condition number of $A$ in the 2-norm is $\kappa(A) = \sigma_1/\sigma_n$. The relative error in the least-squares solution due to perturbations $\delta b$ in the right-hand side satisfies $\|\delta x\|/\|x\| \le \kappa(A)\cdot \|\delta b\|/\|b\|$. Large $\kappa$ signals a nearly rank-deficient problem.

Remark — PCA via SVD

In principal component analysis, form the centred data matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ (rows = observations, columns = variables). The right singular vectors $V$ are the principal directions; the singular values relate to variance explained. Computing the thin SVD of $X$ directly is numerically preferable to forming and diagonalising the sample covariance $X^TX/n$, since squaring the condition number would double the floating-point errors.
