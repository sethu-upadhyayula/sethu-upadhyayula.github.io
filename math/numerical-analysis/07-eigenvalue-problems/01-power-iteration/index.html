7.1 Power Iteration

Power iteration is the simplest method for finding the dominant (largest in magnitude) eigenvalue and its eigenvector. Despite its simplicity, it is the conceptual foundation for the QR algorithm, the Lanczos method, and Google's PageRank.

The Power Method

Definition — Power Method

Given $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$, choose an initial vector $x_0$ with $\|x_0\|=1$. Iterate: $$z_{k+1} = A x_k, \qquad x_{k+1} = \frac{z_{k+1}}{\|z_{k+1}\|}.$$ The Rayleigh quotient $\rho_k = x_k^T A x_k$ converges to the dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1$, and $x_k$ converges to the corresponding eigenvector $v_1$ (up to sign).

Convergence Analysis

Theorem — Convergence Rate of Power Method

Assume $A$ has eigenvalues $|\lambda_1| > |\lambda_2| \ge \cdots \ge |\lambda_n|$ with eigenvectors $v_1,\ldots,v_n$. Write $x_0 = \sum_{j=1}^n c_j v_j$ with $c_1 \ne 0$. Then: $$A^k x_0 = \lambda_1^k\left(c_1 v_1 + \sum_{j=2}^n c_j \left(\frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda_1}\right)^k v_j\right).$$

The dominant term grows as $\lambda_1^k$; after normalisation, $x_k \to v_1$ as $k\to\infty$. The convergence rate is $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|^k$: the method is fast when $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1| \ll 1$ (well-separated dominant eigenvalue) and slow when $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1| \approx 1$.

Rayleigh Quotient

Definition — Rayleigh Quotient

For a vector $x \ne 0$, the Rayleigh quotient is $$\rho(x) = \frac{x^T A x}{x^T x}.$$ At an eigenvector $v_j$, $\rho(v_j) = \lambda_j$. Near $v_1$, $\rho(x)$ converges to $\lambda_1$ at rate $|\lambda_2/\lambda_1|^{2k}$ — one order faster than $x_k$ itself, because the Rayleigh quotient is the optimal linear estimator of $\lambda$ given $x$.

Inverse Iteration and Shift-and-Invert

Definition — Inverse Iteration with Shift

To find the eigenvalue of $A$ nearest a given shift $\mu$, apply power iteration to $(A - \mu I)^{-1}$: $$z_{k+1} = (A-\mu I)^{-1} x_k, \qquad x_{k+1} = z_{k+1}/\|z_{k+1}\|.$$ The eigenvalues of $(A-\mu I)^{-1}$ are $(\lambda_j - \mu)^{-1}$, so the dominant eigenvalue is $(\lambda_j-\mu)^{-1}$ for the $\lambda_j$ nearest $\mu$. Convergence rate: $|(\lambda_j-\mu)/(\lambda_{j'}-\mu)|$ where $\lambda_{j'}$ is the second nearest eigenvalue to $\mu$. Each iteration requires solving $(A-\mu I)x = z$ (factorize once, apply repeatedly).

Rayleigh Quotient Iteration

Theorem — Cubic Convergence of Rayleigh Quotient Iteration

Update the shift at each step: $\mu_k = \rho(x_k) = x_k^T A x_k$. Apply inverse iteration with shift $\mu_k$. For a symmetric matrix, this method converges cubically once near an eigenvector: if $\sin\angle(x_k,v_j) = O(\varepsilon)$, then $\sin\angle(x_{k+1},v_j) = O(\varepsilon^3)$. The cost per step is one linear solve (which changes because $\mu_k$ changes).

Deflation

Definition — Deflation

Once $\lambda_1$ and $v_1$ have been found, deflation removes the dominant mode. Replace $A$ by $A - \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^T$ (Hotelling deflation for symmetric matrices). The new matrix has eigenvalues $0, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n$; power iteration on the deflated matrix finds $\lambda_2$. Successive deflations yield all eigenvalues in decreasing order of magnitude, though numerical errors accumulate and the approach is not recommended for more than a few eigenvalues (the QR algorithm is preferred).
