3.1 Gaussian Elimination

Gaussian elimination reduces a linear system $Ax = b$ to upper-triangular form through a sequence of elementary row operations, after which back substitution yields the solution. With partial pivoting it is the standard direct solver for dense systems, requiring $\tfrac{2}{3}n^3 + O(n^2)$ floating-point operations.

Forward Elimination

Definition — Forward Elimination

Forward elimination proceeds in $n-1$ stages. At stage $k$ ($k = 1, \ldots, n-1$), for each row $i = k+1, \ldots, n$:

  1. Compute the multiplier $\ell_{ik} = a_{ik}^{(k)} / a_{kk}^{(k)}$ (assuming $a_{kk}^{(k)} \ne 0$).
  2. Subtract $\ell_{ik}$ times row $k$ from row $i$: $a_{ij}^{(k+1)} = a_{ij}^{(k)} - \ell_{ik}\,a_{kj}^{(k)}$ for $j = k, \ldots, n$, and $b_i^{(k+1)} = b_i^{(k)} - \ell_{ik}\,b_k^{(k)}$.

After $n-1$ stages the augmented matrix $[A|b]$ is in upper-triangular form $[U|\tilde{b}]$.

Operation Count

Proposition — Flop Count

Forward elimination requires $\tfrac{2}{3}n^3 + O(n^2)$ floating-point operations (additions and multiplications), and back substitution requires $n^2 + O(n)$ operations. The total cost is dominated by $\tfrac{2}{3}n^3$ flops. Doubling $n$ increases cost eightfold.

Partial Pivoting

Definition — Partial Pivoting

At stage $k$, partial pivoting selects the pivot row as the index $p \ge k$ that maximises $|a_{pk}^{(k)}|$: $$p = \arg\max_{k \le i \le n} |a_{ik}^{(k)}|.$$ Row $p$ is swapped with row $k$ before elimination. This is recorded in a permutation matrix $P$ so that the factorisation takes the form $PA = LU$. Partial pivoting ensures $|\ell_{ik}| \le 1$, which controls error growth.

Theorem — Growth Factor

Define the growth factor $g = \max_{i,j,k}|u_{ij}^{(k)}| / \max_{i,j}|a_{ij}|$. With partial pivoting, $g \le 2^{n-1}$ in the worst case. However, growth factors this large essentially never occur in practice; for random matrices, $g$ is typically $O(n^{2/3})$. The backward error of Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting satisfies:

$$\frac{\|r\|}{\|A\|\|x\|} = O(g\,\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}),$$

where $r = b - A\hat{x}$ is the residual of the computed solution $\hat{x}$.

Worked 3×3 Example

Example — Gaussian Elimination with Partial Pivoting

Solve the system with augmented matrix:

$$[A|b] = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 & 4 \\ 3 & 8 & 1 & 12 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}.$$

Stage 1: Pivot column 1. Max in column: $|3| > |1|$, so swap rows 1 and 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 & 1 & 12 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}.$$

Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = 1/3$, $\ell_{31} = 0$. Eliminate:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 & 1 & 12 \\ 0 & -2/3 & 2/3 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}.$$

Stage 2: Pivot column 2. Max: $|4| > |-2/3|$, swap rows 2 and 3:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 & 1 & 12 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & -2/3 & 2/3 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.$$

Multiplier: $\ell_{32} = (-2/3)/4 = -1/6$. Eliminate:

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 5/6 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \tilde{b} = \begin{bmatrix}12 \\ 3 \\ 1/2\end{bmatrix}.$$

Back substitution: $x_3 = (1/2)/(5/6) = 3/5$, $x_2 = (3 - x_3)/4 = 3/5$, $x_1 = (12 - 8x_2 - x_3)/3 = 2$.

Solution: $x = (2,\, 3/5,\, 3/5)^T$.
