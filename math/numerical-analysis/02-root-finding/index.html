Chapter 2: Root Finding

Root-finding algorithms solve the nonlinear equation $f(x) = 0$ for a scalar unknown $x$. The three classical methods — bisection, Newton-Raphson, and the secant method — illustrate the fundamental trade-off between robustness and speed of convergence. Bisection guarantees convergence but converges slowly; Newton-Raphson converges quadratically but can fail; the secant method is a practical compromise.

Overview

Definition — Root of a Function

A root (or zero) of a function $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a point $r \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $f(r) = 0$. The root has multiplicity $m$ if $f(r) = f'(r) = \cdots = f^{(m-1)}(r) = 0$ but $f^{(m)}(r) \ne 0$. Simple roots ($m=1$) are the easiest to compute and most amenable to fast convergence.

The three sections of this chapter cover:

  • 2.1 Bisection Method — bracket-based, linear convergence, guaranteed for continuous functions.
  • 2.2 Newton-Raphson Method — tangent-line iteration, quadratic convergence, requires $f'$.
  • 2.3 Secant Method — finite-difference Newton, superlinear convergence order $(1+\sqrt{5})/2$, no derivatives needed.
