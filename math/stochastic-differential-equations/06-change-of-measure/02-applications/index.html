6.2 Applications

The main application of Girsanov's theorem is in mathematical finance, where changing from the physical measure $P$ (under which assets grow at rate $\mu$) to the risk-neutral measure $Q$ (under which they grow at the risk-free rate $r$) simplifies pricing to a discounted expectation under $Q$. The martingale representation theorem then guarantees that every claim can be replicated.

Risk-Neutral Pricing

Example 6.2.1 — From Physical to Risk-Neutral Measure

Under the physical measure $P$, a stock satisfies $dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$. The discounted price $\widetilde{S}_t = e^{-rt}S_t$ satisfies

$$d\widetilde{S}_t = (\mu - r)\widetilde{S}_t\,dt + \sigma\widetilde{S}_t\,dW_t.$$

This is not a $P$-martingale if $\mu \neq r$. Choose the market price of risk $\theta = (\mu - r)/\sigma$ (assumed constant). By Girsanov's theorem with this $\theta$, under $Q$ (with $dQ/dP = Z_T$, $Z_T = e^{-\theta W_T - \frac{1}{2}\theta^2 T}$) the process $\widetilde{W}_t = W_t + \theta t$ is a $Q$-Brownian motion. Then

$$d\widetilde{S}_t = \sigma\widetilde{S}_t\,d\widetilde{W}_t,$$

a $Q$-martingale. Novikov's condition is satisfied since $\theta$ is constant: $E^P[e^{\frac{1}{2}\theta^2 T}] = e^{\frac{1}{2}\theta^2 T} < \infty$.

Theorem 6.2.2 — Risk-Neutral Pricing Formula

In the Black-Scholes market (one stock, one bond), under the risk-neutral measure $Q$: $S_t = S_0 e^{(r-\sigma^2/2)t + \sigma\widetilde{W}_t}$. The fair price at time $0$ of a European option with payoff $g(S_T)$ is

$$V_0 = e^{-rT} E^Q[g(S_T)].$$

For a call option $g(S) = (S-K)^+$, explicit computation gives the Black-Scholes formula:

$$V_0 = S_0\Phi(d_+) - Ke^{-rT}\Phi(d_-),$$ $$d_\pm = \frac{\ln(S_0/K) + (r \pm \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}},$$

where $\Phi$ denotes the standard normal CDF.

Derivation of Black-Scholes Formula

Under $Q$, $S_T = S_0 e^{(r-\sigma^2/2)T + \sigma\sqrt{T}\,Z}$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$. Thus

$$V_0 = e^{-rT}\int_{-\infty}^\infty (S_0 e^{(r-\sigma^2/2)T+\sigma\sqrt{T}z} - K)^+ \frac{e^{-z^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,dz.$$

The call is in the money when $S_0 e^{(r-\sigma^2/2)T+\sigma\sqrt{T}z} > K$, i.e., $z > -d_-$. Splitting the integral and completing the square in the exponent for the $S_0$ term gives the formula. $\square$

Martingale Representation Theorem

Theorem 6.2.3 — Martingale Representation Theorem

Let $(\widetilde{W}_t)_{0 \leq t \leq T}$ be a $Q$-Brownian motion on a filtered probability space with the $\widetilde{W}$-generated filtration. Let $M$ be a square-integrable $Q$-martingale. Then there exists a unique progressively measurable process $H$ with $E^Q\!\left[\int_0^T H_s^2\,ds\right] < \infty$ such that

$$M_t = M_0 + \int_0^t H_s\,d\widetilde{W}_s, \quad 0 \leq t \leq T.$$
Corollary 6.2.4 — Replicating Portfolio and Market Completeness

Let $C = g(S_T)$ be a contingent claim with $E^Q[e^{-rT}C]^2 < \infty$. Define $V_t = e^{-r(T-t)}E^Q[C \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$. Then the discounted value process $e^{-rt}V_t$ is a $Q$-martingale, and by the martingale representation theorem

$$e^{-rt}V_t = V_0 + \int_0^t \Delta_s\,d\widetilde{S}_s,$$

for some $\Delta_s$. In terms of the original (un-discounted) quantities, the portfolio holding $\Delta_t$ shares and a cash position $V_t - \Delta_t S_t$ in the bond replicates $C$ at time $T$. This proves market completeness: every contingent claim in the Black-Scholes model can be perfectly hedged.

For the European call, $\Delta_t = \partial V/\partial S = \Phi(d_+(S_t, T-t))$, the Black-Scholes delta.

Change of Numeraire

Remark 6.2.5 — Change of Numeraire as a Special Case

A numeraire is a positive asset $N_t$ used to deflate prices. If $Q^N$ is the measure under which $S_t/N_t$ is a martingale (the forward measure when $N_t = P(t,T)$, the zero-coupon bond price), then Girsanov's theorem applied with $\theta = \sigma_N$ (the volatility of $N$) converts the risk-neutral $Q$ to $Q^N$. The change-of-numeraire formula is

$$\frac{dQ^N}{dQ}\Big|_{\mathcal{F}_T} = \frac{N_T/N_0}{B_T},$$

where $B_T = e^{rT}$ is the money-market account. This technique simplifies the pricing of instruments such as swaptions and bond options where the natural numeraire is not the money-market account.
