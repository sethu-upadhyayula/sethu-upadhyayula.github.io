Chapter 6: Change of Measure

Changing the probability measure — replacing the physical measure $P$ with an equivalent measure $Q$ — is a technique of fundamental importance in stochastic analysis and mathematical finance. Girsanov's theorem characterises exactly how Brownian motion transforms under such a change, converting a process with drift into a martingale under the new measure.

Chapter Overview

6.1 — Girsanov's Theorem

Given an adapted process $\theta_t$, the Doléans-Dade exponential $Z_t = \exp\!\left(-\int_0^t \theta_s\,dW_s - \frac{1}{2}\int_0^t \theta_s^2\,ds\right)$ defines a change of measure from $P$ to $Q$ via $dQ/dP = Z_T$. Under $Q$, the process $\widetilde{W}_t = W_t + \int_0^t \theta_s\,ds$ is a standard Brownian motion. Novikov's condition $E^P[e^{\frac{1}{2}\int_0^T \theta_s^2\,ds}] < \infty$ ensures $Z$ is a true martingale.

6.2 — Applications

The canonical application is risk-neutral pricing: choosing $\theta = (\mu - r)/\sigma$ removes the excess drift from the stock price, making the discounted price $e^{-rt}S_t$ a $Q$-martingale. The martingale representation theorem guarantees that every contingent claim can be replicated, establishing market completeness and giving the Black-Scholes pricing formula as a special case.
