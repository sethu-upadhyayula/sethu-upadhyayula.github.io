Chapter 1: Brownian Motion and Filtrations

Brownian motion and filtrations form the foundational layer of stochastic calculus. Before one can integrate against a noise process or write down a stochastic differential equation, one must have a rigorous probability space on which the process lives, a precise definition of the process itself, and a way to encode the information available up to each time. This chapter builds these foundations.

We begin with the standard Brownian motion — its definition through independent Gaussian increments, its almost-sure path properties, and its quadratic variation — then introduce the language of filtrations and adapted processes that make the Itô integral well-defined.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Brownian Motion

We define standard Brownian motion $(W_t)_{t \ge 0}$ through its four defining properties: $W_0 = 0$, continuous paths, independent increments, and $W_t - W_s \sim N(0, t-s)$. We compute the quadratic variation $[W]_t = t$ almost surely and show that paths are nowhere differentiable.

1.2 — Filtrations and Adaptedness

A filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}$ is an increasing family of $\sigma$-algebras encoding the flow of information. A process $X_t$ is adapted if $X_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable for all $t$. We discuss predictable processes and why adaptedness is the crucial hypothesis for the Itô integral to be a martingale.
