1.1 Brownian Motion

Brownian motion - named after botanist Robert Brown who observed the erratic motion of pollen grains in 1827, and made rigorous by Norbert Wiener in 1923 - is the central building block of stochastic calculus. It is simultaneously a martingale, a Markov process, and a Gaussian process, and its paths are continuous but nowhere differentiable.

Definition 1.1.1 - Standard Brownian Motion

A stochastic process $(W_t)_{t \ge 0}$ defined on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a standard Brownian motion (or Wiener process) if:

  1. $W_0 = 0$ almost surely.
  2. Continuous paths: $t \mapsto W_t(\omega)$ is continuous for almost every $\omega$.
  3. Independent increments: For $0 \le s < t$, the increment $W_t - W_s$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_s^W = \sigma(W_u : u \le s)$.
  4. Gaussian increments: $W_t - W_s \sim N(0, t-s)$ for all $0 \le s < t$.
Remark - Existence

The existence of such a process is non-trivial. Wiener's construction uses the Daniell-Kolmogorov extension theorem and a Hölder continuity argument (Kolmogorov's continuity criterion) to pass from finite-dimensional distributions to a continuous version. One may also construct Brownian motion via the Lévy-Ciesielski series $W_t = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \xi_n \psi_n(t)$ where $\xi_n \sim N(0,1)$ i.i.d. and $\psi_n$ are Haar wavelets.

Basic Properties

Theorem 1.1.2 - Moments and Covariance

For standard Brownian motion:

  • $E[W_t] = 0$ for all $t \ge 0$.
  • $\text{Var}(W_t) = t$.
  • $\text{Cov}(W_s, W_t) = \min(s,t)$ for all $s,t \ge 0$.

In particular, $(W_{t_1}, \ldots, W_{t_n})$ is a multivariate Gaussian for any $0 \le t_1 < \cdots < t_n$.

Proof - Covariance Formula

For $s \le t$: $\text{Cov}(W_s, W_t) = E[W_s W_t] = E[W_s(W_t - W_s + W_s)] = E[W_s(W_t-W_s)] + E[W_s^2]$. By independence of increments, $E[W_s(W_t-W_s)] = E[W_s]E[W_t-W_s] = 0$. Hence $\text{Cov}(W_s,W_t) = E[W_s^2] = s = \min(s,t)$.

By symmetry the formula holds for $t \le s$ as well.

Quadratic Variation

One of the most striking properties of Brownian motion - and the key that drives Itô's formula - is that its paths have non-zero quadratic variation, unlike any differentiable function.

Definition 1.1.3 - Quadratic Variation

For a partition $\Pi = \{0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = T\}$ with mesh $|\Pi| = \max_k(t_{k+1}-t_k)$, the quadratic variation of $W$ on $[0,T]$ is:

$$[W]_T = \lim_{|\Pi| \to 0} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k})^2$$
Theorem 1.1.4 - Quadratic Variation of Brownian Motion

For standard Brownian motion, $[W]_t = t$ almost surely for all $t \ge 0$. Symbolically, $dW_t^2 = dt$.

Proof Sketch

Let $Q_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(W_{t_{k+1}}-W_{t_k})^2$ for the uniform partition $t_k = kT/n$. Each increment $\Delta W_k = W_{t_{k+1}}-W_{t_k} \sim N(0, T/n)$, so $E[(\Delta W_k)^2] = T/n$ and $E[Q_n] = T$.

For the variance: $\text{Var}((\Delta W_k)^2) = 2(T/n)^2$ (since $(\Delta W_k)^2/(T/n) \sim \chi^2_1$). By independence:

$$\text{Var}(Q_n) = \sum_k 2(T/n)^2 = 2T^2/n \to 0.$$

Hence $Q_n \to T$ in $L^2$, and a subsequence converges a.s. A refinement shows convergence for all sequences of partitions with $|\Pi| \to 0$.

Remark - Itô's Table

The quadratic variation rule encodes the Itô multiplication table used in deriving Itô's formula:

$$dW_t \cdot dW_t = dt, \qquad dW_t \cdot dt = 0, \qquad dt \cdot dt = 0.$$

Any term of order $(dt)^\alpha$ with $\alpha > 1$ vanishes in the limit.

Path Properties

Theorem 1.1.5 - Nowhere Differentiability

Almost surely, the sample path $t \mapsto W_t$ is nowhere differentiable. In fact, almost surely, $\limsup_{h \to 0} |W_{t+h} - W_t|/\sqrt{h \log \log(1/h)} = \sqrt{2}$ (the law of the iterated logarithm), which shows the paths oscillate far too wildly for any derivative to exist.

Theorem 1.1.6 - Hölder Continuity

Almost surely, sample paths of Brownian motion are Hölder continuous with exponent $\alpha$ for every $\alpha < 1/2$, but not for any $\alpha \ge 1/2$. Precisely, for each $T > 0$ and $\alpha < 1/2$, there exists a finite random variable $C$ such that $|W_t - W_s| \le C|t-s|^\alpha$ for all $s,t \in [0,T]$ a.s.

The Natural Filtration

Definition 1.1.7 - Natural Filtration of Brownian Motion

The natural filtration of Brownian motion is $\mathcal{F}_t^W = \sigma(W_s : s \le t)$, the smallest $\sigma$-algebra making all $W_s$ (for $s \le t$) measurable. It represents the information accumulated by observing the Brownian path up to time $t$.

Example 1.1.8 - Brownian Motion is a Martingale

With respect to its natural filtration, Brownian motion is a martingale: for $s \le t$,

$$E[W_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s^W] = E[W_t - W_s + W_s \mid \mathcal{F}_s^W] = E[W_t - W_s] + W_s = 0 + W_s = W_s.$$

Similarly, $W_t^2 - t$ is a martingale, which is essentially the statement that $[W]_t = t$.
