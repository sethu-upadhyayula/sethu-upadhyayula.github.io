1.2 Filtrations and Adaptedness

A filtration formalises the idea of information accumulating over time. In stochastic calculus, we integrate processes against Brownian motion, and we must ensure that the integrand at time $t$ only uses information available up to time $t$ — the adaptedness condition. This section makes these concepts precise.

Definition 1.2.1 — Filtration

A filtration on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a family $(\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}$ of sub-$\sigma$-algebras of $\mathcal{F}$ satisfying:

$$s \le t \implies \mathcal{F}_s \subseteq \mathcal{F}_t.$$

The tuple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, (\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \ge 0}, P)$ is called a filtered probability space.

Remark — Usual Conditions

A filtration satisfies the usual conditions if:

  • Completeness: $\mathcal{F}_0$ (hence every $\mathcal{F}_t$) contains all $P$-null sets.
  • Right-continuity: $\mathcal{F}_t = \mathcal{F}_{t+} := \bigcap_{s > t} \mathcal{F}_s$ for all $t \ge 0$.

The usual conditions are assumed throughout stochastic calculus. They ensure that stopping times are well-behaved and that stochastic processes have right-continuous modifications.

Definition 1.2.2 — Adapted Process

A stochastic process $(X_t)_{t \ge 0}$ is adapted to the filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ if, for every $t \ge 0$, the random variable $X_t$ is $\mathcal{F}_t$-measurable. Intuitively, the value $X_t$ is determined by the information available at time $t$.

Example 1.2.3 — Adapted Processes
  • Brownian motion $(W_t)$ is adapted to its natural filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t^W)$.
  • Any deterministic function $f(t)$ is adapted to any filtration.
  • The process $X_t = \int_0^t f(W_s)\,ds$ is adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_t^W)$ for measurable $f$.
  • The "future-looking" process $X_t = W_{t+1}$ is not adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_t^W)$, since $W_{t+1}$ uses information after time $t$.

Predictable Processes

Definition 1.2.4 — Predictable Process

A process $(X_t)_{t \ge 0}$ is predictable (or previsible) if it is measurable with respect to the predictable $\sigma$-algebra, generated by all left-continuous adapted processes.

Predictable processes include all deterministic functions, all left-continuous adapted processes, and all simple processes of the form $\sum_k f_k \mathbf{1}_{(t_k, t_{k+1}]}$ where $f_k \in \mathcal{F}_{t_k}$.

Remark — Predictable vs Adapted

Every predictable process is adapted, but not vice versa. A right-continuous adapted process need not be predictable (its left limit is predictable, but the process itself may "jump using information from the future"). For continuous processes, the distinction disappears: a continuous adapted process is predictable.

The Itô integral requires the integrand to be adapted (and square-integrable); in the continuous setting this is equivalent to predictability.

Why Adaptedness Matters for the Itô Integral

Theorem 1.2.5 — Martingale Property Requires Adaptedness

If $f = (f_t)_{t \in [0,T]}$ is a simple adapted process, the stochastic integral $M_t = \int_0^t f_s\,dW_s$ is a martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$.

If instead $f$ were not adapted — for example $f_t = W_{t+\epsilon}$ — the stochastic integral would not be a martingale, and the Itô isometry would fail. Adaptedness is the correct hypothesis for both the martingale property and the $L^2$ theory.

Example 1.2.6 — The Class $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$

The domain of the Itô integral is the Hilbert space:

$$\mathcal{L}^2([0,T]) = \left\{ f : [0,T] \times \Omega \to \mathbb{R} : f \text{ is adapted and } E\!\left[\int_0^T f_t^2\,dt\right] < \infty \right\}.$$

The two conditions — adaptedness and square-integrability — work together: adaptedness ensures the integral is a martingale, and square-integrability ensures the $L^2$ convergence of the approximating sums.
