3.1 One-Dimensional Itô Formula

Itô's formula is the change-of-variables formula for stochastic calculus. It generalises the classical chain rule $df(X_t) = f'(X_t)\,dX_t$ to the stochastic setting, where the extra quadratic variation term is unavoidable.

Theorem 3.1.1 - Itô's Formula (One Dimension)

Let $X_t$ be an Itô process satisfying $dX_t = \mu_t\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t$, and let $f(t,x) \in C^{1,2}([0,T] \times \mathbb{R})$. Then:

$$df(t,X_t) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}\,dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,dX_t + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\,d[X]_t$$

Expanding $dX_t$ and $d[X]_t = \sigma_t^2\,dt$:

$$df(t,X_t) = \left(f_t + \mu_t f_x + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_t^2 f_{xx}\right)dt + \sigma_t f_x\,dW_t.$$
Derivation via Taylor Expansion

Apply the Taylor expansion to $f(t+dt, X_t+dX_t)$:

$$df = f_t\,dt + f_x\,dX + \frac{1}{2}f_{tt}(dt)^2 + f_{tx}\,dt\,dX + \frac{1}{2}f_{xx}(dX)^2 + \cdots$$

Using the Itô multiplication table: $dW_t \cdot dW_t = dt$, $dW_t \cdot dt = 0$, $dt \cdot dt = 0$:

$$(dX)^2 = (\mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW)^2 = \mu^2(dt)^2 + 2\mu\sigma\,dt\,dW + \sigma^2(dW)^2 = \sigma^2\,dt$$

All other higher-order terms are $o(dt)$ and vanish. Therefore:

$$df = f_t\,dt + f_x(\mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW) + \frac{1}{2}f_{xx}\sigma^2\,dt = \left(f_t + \mu f_x + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 f_{xx}\right)dt + \sigma f_x\,dW.$$

The Product Rule (Integration by Parts)

Theorem 3.1.2 - Itô Product Rule

If $X_t$ and $Y_t$ are Itô processes, then:

$$d(X_t Y_t) = X_t\,dY_t + Y_t\,dX_t + d[X,Y]_t$$

where $[X,Y]_t$ is the cross-quadratic-variation. For $dX = \mu^X\,dt + \sigma^X\,dW$ and $dY = \mu^Y\,dt + \sigma^Y\,dW$: $d[X,Y] = \sigma^X\sigma^Y\,dt$.

Proof via $f(x,y) = xy$

Apply Itô's formula (two-variable version) to $f(x,y)=xy$: $f_x = y$, $f_y = x$, $f_{xx} = f_{yy} = 0$, $f_{xy} = 1$. Then:

$$d(XY) = Y\,dX + X\,dY + d[X,Y].$$

Compare with the classical product rule $d(XY) = X\,dY + Y\,dX$: the correction $d[X,Y] = \sigma^X\sigma^Y\,dt$ is the purely stochastic contribution.

Example 3.1.3 - $f(W_t) = W_t^2$

Apply Itô's formula with $f(x) = x^2$, $X_t = W_t$ ($\mu = 0$, $\sigma = 1$):

$$d(W_t^2) = 2W_t\,dW_t + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 1\,dt = 2W_t\,dW_t + dt.$$

Integrating: $W_T^2 = 2\int_0^T W_t\,dW_t + T$, so $\int_0^T W_t\,dW_t = \frac{1}{2}(W_T^2 - T)$, confirming our earlier calculation.

The term $T$ is the Itô correction; it would be absent in the classical integral or in the Stratonovich convention.
