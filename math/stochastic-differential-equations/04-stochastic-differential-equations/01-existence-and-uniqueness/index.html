4.1 Existence and Uniqueness

A stochastic differential equation (SDE) is written in differential notation as

$$dX_t = b(t, X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t, X_t)\,dW_t, \quad X_0 = x_0,$$

but is interpreted as the integral equation

$$X_t = x_0 + \int_0^t b(s, X_s)\,ds + \int_0^t \sigma(s, X_s)\,dW_s.$$

The central question is whether this integral equation has a solution, and whether that solution is unique. The standard answer is given by the Itô existence and uniqueness theorem under Lipschitz and linear growth conditions.

Definition 4.1.1 — Strong Solution

Given a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ with a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ and a Brownian motion $W_t$ defined on it, a strong solution of the SDE $dX = b(t,X)\,dt + \sigma(t,X)\,dW$ with initial condition $X_0 = x_0$ is an $(\mathcal{F}_t)$-adapted process $X_t$ such that:

  1. $X$ has continuous sample paths almost surely.
  2. $\int_0^T \bigl(|b(t,X_t)| + \sigma(t,X_t)^2\bigr)\,dt < \infty$ a.s. for all $T > 0$.
  3. $X_t = x_0 + \int_0^t b(s,X_s)\,ds + \int_0^t \sigma(s,X_s)\,dW_s$ a.s. for all $t \geq 0$.
Definition 4.1.2 — Lipschitz and Linear Growth Conditions

The coefficients $b, \sigma: [0,T] \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfy the standard conditions if there exists a constant $K > 0$ such that for all $t \in [0,T]$ and all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$:

Lipschitz condition:

$$|b(t,x) - b(t,y)| + |\sigma(t,x) - \sigma(t,y)| \leq K|x - y|.$$

Linear growth condition:

$$|b(t,x)|^2 + |\sigma(t,x)|^2 \leq K^2(1 + |x|^2).$$
Theorem 4.1.3 — Itô Existence and Uniqueness

Suppose $b$ and $\sigma$ satisfy the Lipschitz and linear growth conditions, and $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}$ (or $x_0$ is $\mathcal{F}_0$-measurable with $\mathbb{E}[x_0^2] < \infty$). Then:

  1. Existence: There exists a strong solution $X_t$ of $dX = b(t,X)\,dt + \sigma(t,X)\,dW$ on $[0,T]$.
  2. Pathwise uniqueness: If $X_t$ and $\tilde{X}_t$ are two strong solutions on the same probability space with the same Brownian motion, then $\mathbb{P}(X_t = \tilde{X}_t \text{ for all } t \in [0,T]) = 1$.
  3. Moment bound: $\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sup_{0 \leq t \leq T} |X_t|^2\right] \leq C(1 + \mathbb{E}[x_0^2])e^{CT}$ for a constant $C$ depending only on $K$ and $T$.
Proof Sketch — Picard Iteration

Define the Picard iterates $X^{(0)}_t = x_0$ and

$$X^{(n+1)}_t = x_0 + \int_0^t b(s, X^{(n)}_s)\,ds + \int_0^t \sigma(s, X^{(n)}_s)\,dW_s.$$

Set $e_n(t) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sup_{0 \leq s \leq t}|X^{(n+1)}_s - X^{(n)}_s|^2\right]$. Using the Lipschitz condition, the BDG inequality, and Gronwall's lemma, one shows

$$e_n(t) \leq \frac{(CKt)^n}{n!} e_0(T),$$

so $\sum_n e_n(T)^{1/2} < \infty$, and the iterates converge uniformly in $L^2$. The limit $X_t$ solves the SDE and inherits continuity. Pathwise uniqueness follows from applying Gronwall's lemma directly to $\mathbb{E}[\sup|X_t - \tilde{X}_t|^2]$.

Weak Solutions

Definition 4.1.4 — Weak Solution

A weak solution of $dX = b(t,X)\,dt + \sigma(t,X)\,dW$ consists of a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$, a filtration, a Brownian motion $W_t$, and an adapted process $X_t$ satisfying the integral equation. The Brownian motion is part of the solution data, not given in advance.

Weak solutions are more general: they need not be adapted to the filtration generated by a pre-specified Brownian motion. By the Yamada–Watanabe theorem, pathwise uniqueness combined with weak existence implies strong existence and strong uniqueness.

Remark 4.1.5 — Necessity of the Conditions

The linear growth condition prevents finite-time blowup of solutions. Without it, a solution can explode in finite time; a classical example is $dX = X^2\,dW$, which does not satisfy linear growth and can blow up. The Lipschitz condition ensures pathwise uniqueness; without it, multiple solutions may coexist (e.g., $dX = |X|^{1/2}\,dW$ with $X_0 = 0$ has the trivial solution and non-trivial ones).
