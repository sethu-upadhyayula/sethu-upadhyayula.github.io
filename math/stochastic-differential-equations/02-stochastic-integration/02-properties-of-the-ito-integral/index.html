2.2 Properties of the Itô Integral

The Itô integral inherits several key properties from its definition as an $L^2$ limit. We collect the most important ones here, and conclude with a comparison to the Stratonovich integral.

Theorem 2.2.1 — Linearity

For $f, g \in \mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$ and constants $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\int_0^T (\alpha f_t + \beta g_t)\,dW_t = \alpha \int_0^T f_t\,dW_t + \beta \int_0^T g_t\,dW_t \quad \text{a.s.}$$
Theorem 2.2.2 — Zero Mean

For $f \in \mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$:

$$E\!\left[\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t\right] = 0.$$

This follows from the martingale property: $E[M_T] = E[M_0] = 0$.

Theorem 2.2.3 — Quadratic Variation of the Stochastic Integral

If $M_t = \int_0^t f_s\,dW_s$, then the quadratic variation of $M$ is:

$$[M]_t = \int_0^t f_s^2\,ds.$$

Equivalently, $d[M]_t = f_t^2\,dt$, or in differential notation: $(f_t\,dW_t)^2 = f_t^2\,dt$.

Theorem 2.2.4 — Continuity in $t$

For $f \in \mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$, the process $M_t = \int_0^t f_s\,dW_s$ has a continuous modification. That is, there exists a process $\tilde{M}$ with $P(\tilde{M}_t = M_t) = 1$ for all $t$, and $t \mapsto \tilde{M}_t(\omega)$ is continuous for almost every $\omega$.

Itô vs Stratonovich

Definition 2.2.5 — Stratonovich Integral

The Stratonovich integral is defined using midpoint evaluation:

$$\int_0^T f_t \circ dW_t = \lim_{|\Pi| \to 0} \sum_k \frac{f_{t_k} + f_{t_{k+1}}}{2}(W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k}).$$

When $f$ is itself a semimartingale, the two integrals are related by the Itô-Stratonovich correction:

$$\int_0^T f_t \circ dW_t = \int_0^T f_t\,dW_t + \frac{1}{2}[f, W]_T$$ where $[f, W]_T$ is the cross-quadratic-variation. If $f_t = g(W_t)$, then $[f,W]_T = \int_0^T g'(W_t)\,dt$, giving: $$\int_0^T g(W_t) \circ dW_t = \int_0^T g(W_t)\,dW_t + \frac{1}{2}\int_0^T g'(W_t)\,dt.$$
Remark — When to Use Each Convention

The Itô integral is preferred in probability and mathematical finance: it is a martingale, the Itô isometry holds, and it is well-suited to conditioning arguments.

The Stratonovich integral is preferred in physics and differential geometry: it satisfies the classical chain rule (no correction term), making it natural when studying SDEs on manifolds or when the SDE arises from a physical limit of smooth noise.

The two conventions are always equivalent — every Stratonovich SDE can be rewritten as an Itô SDE and vice versa — so the choice is one of convenience.

Example 2.2.6 — The Correction Term

For $g(x) = x$: $\int_0^T W_t \circ dW_t = \int_0^T W_t\,dW_t + \frac{1}{2}T = \frac{1}{2}W_T^2 - \frac{1}{2}T + \frac{1}{2}T = \frac{1}{2}W_T^2$.

The Stratonovich result matches the classical calculus formula $\int_0^T x\,dx = \frac{1}{2}x^2$, confirming that Stratonovich satisfies the classical chain rule.
