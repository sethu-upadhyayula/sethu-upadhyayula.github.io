2.3 Itô Isometry

The Itô isometry is the fundamental norm-preservation property of the stochastic integral. It says that the map $f \mapsto \int f\,dW$ is an isometry from $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$ (with the norm $\|f\|^2 = E[\int f^2\,dt]$) into $L^2(\Omega)$ (with the norm $\|X\|^2 = E[X^2]$). This is what allows the integral to be extended from simple to general processes.

Theorem 2.3.1 - Itô Isometry

For $f \in \mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$:

$$E\!\left[\left(\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t\right)^2\right] = E\!\left[\int_0^T f_t^2\,dt\right].$$
Proof - Simple Processes

Let $f$ be simple with $f_t = f_{t_k}$ on $[t_k, t_{k+1})$. Write $\Delta W_k = W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k}$ and $I = \int_0^T f_t\,dW_t = \sum_k f_{t_k}\Delta W_k$. Then:

$$E[I^2] = \sum_k E[f_{t_k}^2 (\Delta W_k)^2] + 2\sum_{j < k} E[f_{t_j}\Delta W_j \cdot f_{t_k}\Delta W_k].$$

For the cross terms with $j < k$: since $f_{t_j}, \Delta W_j, f_{t_k}$ are all $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$-measurable while $\Delta W_k$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$ with $E[\Delta W_k] = 0$:

$$E[f_{t_j}\Delta W_j \cdot f_{t_k}\Delta W_k] = E[f_{t_j}\Delta W_j \cdot f_{t_k}] \cdot E[\Delta W_k] = 0.$$

For the diagonal terms: since $f_{t_k} \in \mathcal{F}_{t_k}$ and $\Delta W_k$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$ with $E[(\Delta W_k)^2] = t_{k+1} - t_k$:

$$E[f_{t_k}^2(\Delta W_k)^2] = E[f_{t_k}^2] \cdot (t_{k+1} - t_k) = E\!\left[\int_{t_k}^{t_{k+1}} f_{t_k}^2\,dt\right] = E\!\left[\int_{t_k}^{t_{k+1}} f_t^2\,dt\right].$$

Summing over $k$ gives $E[I^2] = E[\int_0^T f_t^2\,dt]$. The general case follows by density of simple processes in $\mathcal{L}^2$.

Generalisation: Cross-Product Formula

Theorem 2.3.2 - Polarisation Identity

For $f, g \in \mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$:

$$E\!\left[\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t \cdot \int_0^T g_t\,dW_t\right] = E\!\left[\int_0^T f_t g_t\,dt\right].$$

This follows immediately by polarisation: $4\langle u, v\rangle = \|u+v\|^2 - \|u-v\|^2$, applying the isometry to $f+g$ and $f-g$.

Remark - Isometry and Hilbert Space Structure

The Itô isometry says the map $f \mapsto \int_0^T f_t\,dW_t$ is an inner-product-preserving map from $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$ to $L^2(\Omega)$. In particular, it is an isometric embedding: it preserves all distances and angles. This is what allows the extension from simple processes (a dense subspace) to all of $\mathcal{L}^2$.

Concretely: if $f^{(n)} \to f$ in $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$, then by isometry $\int f^{(n)}\,dW \to \int f\,dW$ in $L^2(\Omega)$. The limit is well-defined and independent of the approximating sequence.

Example 2.3.3 - Computing Second Moments

Let $M_t = \int_0^t e^{-s}\,dW_s$. By the isometry:

$$E[M_t^2] = E\!\left[\int_0^t e^{-2s}\,ds\right] = \int_0^t e^{-2s}\,ds = \frac{1 - e^{-2t}}{2}.$$

As $t \to \infty$, $E[M_\infty^2] = 1/2$, so $M_\infty$ is a well-defined $L^2$ random variable.

For the cross-product: $E[M_s M_t] = E[\int_0^{\min(s,t)} e^{-2u}\,du] = (1 - e^{-2\min(s,t)})/2$, which gives the covariance structure of this Gaussian process.
