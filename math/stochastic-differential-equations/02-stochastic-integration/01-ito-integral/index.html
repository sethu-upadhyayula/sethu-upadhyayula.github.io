2.1 The Itô Integral

The Itô integral is the cornerstone of stochastic calculus. We want to define $\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t$ for adapted processes $f$. Because Brownian motion has unbounded variation on every interval, the Stieltjes integral $\int f\,dW$ does not exist pathwise. Instead, we define the integral as an $L^2$ limit of simple approximations.

Simple Processes

Definition 2.1.1 - Simple Adapted Process

A process $f$ is simple if it has the form:

$$f_t = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f_{t_k} \mathbf{1}_{[t_k, t_{k+1})}(t)$$

for a partition $0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = T$, where each $f_{t_k}$ is $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$-measurable and square-integrable. The collection of simple adapted processes is denoted $\mathcal{S}$.

Definition 2.1.2 - Itô Integral for Simple Processes

For a simple adapted process $f \in \mathcal{S}$, the Itô integral is defined as:

$$\int_0^T f_t\,dW_t = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f_{t_k}(W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k}).$$

The critical feature is that $f_{t_k}$ is evaluated at the left endpoint $t_k$, using information from $\mathcal{F}_{t_k}$, before the increment $W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k}$ is observed.

Remark - Why Left Endpoints?

The left-endpoint rule is not a technicality - it is the crucial choice that makes the integral a martingale. Economically, it corresponds to choosing a portfolio $f_{t_k}$ shares at the beginning of the interval $[t_k, t_{k+1}]$ before seeing the price move. This is the only causal (non-anticipating) choice.

If we used midpoints instead, we would obtain the Stratonovich integral $\int f \circ dW$, which satisfies the classical chain rule but is not a martingale and is harder to work with probabilistically.

Extension to $\mathcal{L}^2$

Definition 2.1.3 - The Space $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$

Let $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$ denote the space of processes $f : [0,T] \times \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ that are adapted to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ and satisfy:

$$\|f\|_{\mathcal{L}^2}^2 := E\!\left[\int_0^T f_t^2\,dt\right] < \infty.$$

Simple processes are dense in $\mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$: for any $f \in \mathcal{L}^2$, there exist simple $f^{(n)} \in \mathcal{S}$ with $\|f^{(n)} - f\|_{\mathcal{L}^2} \to 0$.

Theorem 2.1.4 - Extension of the Itô Integral

There is a unique linear map $\mathcal{I}: \mathcal{L}^2([0,T]) \to L^2(\Omega)$, called the Itô integral, such that:

  1. $\mathcal{I}(f) = \sum_k f_{t_k}(W_{t_{k+1}}-W_{t_k})$ for simple $f$.
  2. $\|\mathcal{I}(f)\|_{L^2(\Omega)}^2 = \|f\|_{\mathcal{L}^2}^2$ (Itô isometry).
  3. $\mathcal{I}(f) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \mathcal{I}(f^{(n)})$ in $L^2(\Omega)$ for any approximating sequence $f^{(n)} \to f$ in $\mathcal{L}^2$.

We write $\mathcal{I}(f) = \int_0^T f_t\,dW_t$.

The Itô Integral as a Martingale

Theorem 2.1.5 - Martingale Property

For $f \in \mathcal{L}^2([0,T])$, the process $M_t = \int_0^t f_s\,dW_s$ (for $t \in [0,T]$) is a continuous square-integrable martingale with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$. In particular:

  • $M_0 = 0$.
  • $E[M_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = M_s$ for $s \le t$.
  • $E[M_t^2] = E\!\left[\int_0^t f_s^2\,ds\right]$.
Proof - Martingale Property for Simple Processes

For simple $f$, with $s \in [t_j, t_{j+1})$ and $t \in [t_k, t_{k+1})$ with $j \le k$:

$$E[M_t - M_s \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = \sum_{i=j}^{k-1} E[f_{t_i}(W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i}) \mid \mathcal{F}_s].$$

Since $f_{t_i} \in \mathcal{F}_{t_i} \subseteq \mathcal{F}_{t_{i+1}}$ and $W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i}$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}_{t_i}$ with mean zero:

$$E[f_{t_i}(W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i}) \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = E[f_{t_i} \mid \mathcal{F}_s] \cdot E[W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i}] = 0.$$

Hence $E[M_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = M_s$. The general case follows by taking $L^2$ limits.

Example 2.1.6 - Itô Integral of Brownian Motion

Compute $\int_0^T W_t\,dW_t$. Since $W_t \in \mathcal{F}_t$, the integrand is adapted. Using the formula $W_{t_k}(W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k}) = \frac{1}{2}(W_{t_{k+1}}^2 - W_{t_k}^2) - \frac{1}{2}(W_{t_{k+1}} - W_{t_k})^2$, summing and taking limits:

$$\int_0^T W_t\,dW_t = \frac{1}{2}W_T^2 - \frac{1}{2}T.$$

This differs from the classical result $\int_0^T W_t\,dW_t = \frac{1}{2}W_T^2$ by the Itô correction $-T/2$, which comes from the quadratic variation term $[W]_T = T$.
