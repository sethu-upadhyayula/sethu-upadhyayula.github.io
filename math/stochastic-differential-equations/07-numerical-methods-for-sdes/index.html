Chapter 7: Numerical Methods for SDEs

Most stochastic differential equations encountered in practice — beyond a handful of solvable cases such as linear SDEs and geometric Brownian motion — admit no closed-form solution. To compute quantities like $E[f(X_T)]$ or to simulate sample paths of $X_t$, we must discretize the SDE in time and approximate the solution numerically. This chapter introduces the two foundational discretization schemes, Euler-Maruyama and Milstein, and the notions of strong and weak convergence used to assess their accuracy.

Strong vs. Weak Convergence

Given an SDE $dX_t = \mu(X_t,t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t$ on $[0,T]$, a numerical scheme produces an approximation $Y^{\Delta t}_n \approx X_{t_n}$ on a time grid $t_n = n\Delta t$. There are two natural ways to measure how good this approximation is.

Definition — Strong Convergence

A scheme converges strongly with order $\gamma$ if there is a constant $C$, independent of $\Delta t$, such that

$$E\left[\left|X_T - Y^{\Delta t}_N\right|\right] \leq C(\Delta t)^\gamma$$

for sufficiently small $\Delta t$. Strong convergence measures pathwise accuracy — how close individual simulated trajectories are to the true solution driven by the same Brownian path. It matters for applications like hedging simulations and pathwise sensitivity analysis.

Definition — Weak Convergence

A scheme converges weakly with order $\beta$ if for every sufficiently smooth function $g$ there is a constant $C$, independent of $\Delta t$, such that

$$\left|E[g(X_T)] - E[g(Y^{\Delta t}_N)]\right| \leq C(\Delta t)^\beta.$$

Weak convergence measures accuracy of distributional quantities — expectations, moments, option prices — and does not require matching individual sample paths. It is typically achievable to higher order than strong convergence, since errors in the noise term can partially cancel in expectation.

Chapter Overview

7.1 — Euler-Maruyama Method

The simplest discretization, directly analogous to the Euler method for ODEs: $X_{n+1} = X_n + \mu(X_n,t_n)\Delta t + \sigma(X_n,t_n)\Delta W_n$. It achieves strong order $0.5$ and weak order $1.0$, and is the workhorse scheme for Monte Carlo simulation of SDEs due to its simplicity.

7.2 — Milstein Scheme

By including the next term in the stochastic Taylor (Itô-Taylor) expansion, the Milstein scheme adds a correction term involving $\sigma\sigma'$ and $(\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t$, raising the strong order of convergence to $1.0$. This improves pathwise accuracy at the cost of requiring the derivative $\sigma'$ of the diffusion coefficient.
