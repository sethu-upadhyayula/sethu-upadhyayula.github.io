7.1 Euler–Maruyama Method

Consider an SDE on $[0,T]$

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t,t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t, \qquad X_0 = x_0,$$

where $\mu$ is the drift coefficient and $\sigma$ the diffusion coefficient. Except in special cases (linear SDEs, geometric Brownian motion, Ornstein–Uhlenbeck), no closed-form expression for $X_t$ exists. The Euler–Maruyama method is the most direct way to approximate $X_t$ by discretizing time and replacing $dW_t$ with the actual Brownian increments.

The Scheme

Definition 7.1.1 — Euler–Maruyama Scheme

Partition $[0,T]$ into $N$ subintervals with grid points $t_n = n\Delta t$, $\Delta t = T/N$, $n = 0,\dots,N$. Let $\Delta W_n = W_{t_{n+1}} - W_{t_n} \sim N(0,\Delta t)$ be independent Gaussian increments. The Euler–Maruyama approximation $Y_n \approx X_{t_n}$ is defined recursively by

$$Y_0 = x_0, \qquad Y_{n+1} = Y_n + \mu(Y_n,t_n)\,\Delta t + \sigma(Y_n,t_n)\,\Delta W_n.$$

In simulation, $\Delta W_n = \sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z_n$ where $Z_n \sim N(0,1)$ are i.i.d. standard normals. The scheme is the direct stochastic analogue of the explicit Euler method for ODEs, obtained by freezing the coefficients $\mu, \sigma$ at the left endpoint $t_n$ of each subinterval.

Remark 7.1.2 — Origin of the Scheme

The scheme follows from the integral form of the SDE,

$$X_{t_{n+1}} = X_{t_n} + \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} \mu(X_s,s)\,ds + \int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}} \sigma(X_s,s)\,dW_s,$$

by approximating both integrands as constant and equal to their values at $t_n$: $\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}}\mu(X_s,s)\,ds \approx \mu(X_{t_n},t_n)\Delta t$ and $\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}}\sigma(X_s,s)\,dW_s \approx \sigma(X_{t_n},t_n)\Delta W_n$, exploiting the Itô isometry to control the error of the second approximation.

Convergence

Theorem 7.1.3 — Strong Convergence of Euler–Maruyama

Suppose $\mu,\sigma$ satisfy global Lipschitz and linear growth conditions. Then the Euler–Maruyama approximation converges strongly with order $\gamma = 0.5$:

$$E\left[\max_{0\leq n\leq N}\left|X_{t_n} - Y_n\right|\right] \leq C(\Delta t)^{1/2},$$

for a constant $C$ independent of $\Delta t$. Halving the step size reduces the pathwise error by a factor of only $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$.

Theorem 7.1.4 — Weak Convergence of Euler–Maruyama

Under the same conditions, plus sufficient smoothness of $\mu,\sigma$ and of the test function $g$, the Euler–Maruyama approximation converges weakly with order $\beta = 1.0$:

$$\left|E[g(X_T)] - E[g(Y_N)]\right| \leq C(\Delta t).$$

The weak order exceeds the strong order because the dominant pathwise error term, arising from the difference between $\Delta W_n$ and the higher-order Itô–Taylor corrections, has mean zero and so does not contribute to the error in expectations at leading order.

Heuristic for the Strong Order $1/2$

Over one step, the local error in approximating $\sigma(X_s,s)$ by $\sigma(X_{t_n},t_n)$ for $s \in [t_n,t_{n+1}]$ is itself $O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ in the $L^2$ sense, since $X_s - X_{t_n} = O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ pathwise (by the scaling of Brownian increments). Multiplying this $O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ coefficient error by the increment $\Delta W_n = O(\sqrt{\Delta t})$ gives a local strong error of order $\Delta t$. Accumulating $N = T/\Delta t$ such independent-ish errors in an $L^2$ sense (square-root accumulation) yields a global strong error of order $\sqrt{\Delta t \cdot N \cdot (\Delta t)^2}\big/\Delta t \sim \sqrt{\Delta t}$, i.e. order $1/2$. $\square$

Worked Example: Geometric Brownian Motion

Example 7.1.5 — Simulating $dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$

For geometric Brownian motion, $\mu(S,t) = \mu S$ and $\sigma(S,t) = \sigma S$. The Euler–Maruyama recursion is

$$S_{n+1} = S_n + \mu S_n \Delta t + \sigma S_n \Delta W_n = S_n\left(1 + \mu\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n\right).$$

The exact solution is $S_T = S_0\exp\left((\mu - \sigma^2/2)T + \sigma W_T\right)$. Comparing the two: the exact solution involves $\exp(\sigma\Delta W_n - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\Delta t)$ over a step, which expands as $1 + \sigma\Delta W_n + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\left((\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right) + O((\Delta t)^{3/2})$.

The Euler–Maruyama step $1 + \mu\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n$ is missing exactly the $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\left((\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right)$ term — an $O(\Delta t)$ quantity with zero mean. This is precisely the term that the Milstein scheme (Section 7.2) adds back in, improving the strong order from $0.5$ to $1.0$.

Stability Remarks

Remark 7.1.6 — Step Size and Stability

As with deterministic ODE solvers, the explicit Euler–Maruyama scheme can be numerically unstable for stiff SDEs (e.g., mean-reverting processes with large reversion speed) unless $\Delta t$ is chosen sufficiently small relative to the relevant time scale of the drift. Implicit variants (e.g., the drift-implicit or "backward" Euler–Maruyama scheme, $Y_{n+1} = Y_n + \mu(Y_{n+1},t_{n+1})\Delta t + \sigma(Y_n,t_n)\Delta W_n$) trade extra computational cost per step for improved stability, and are preferred for stiff systems. Additionally, for processes with non-negativity constraints (e.g., interest rate or volatility models such as CIR), the additive Gaussian noise in Euler–Maruyama can produce negative values; truncation or reflection schemes are commonly used to address this in practice.
