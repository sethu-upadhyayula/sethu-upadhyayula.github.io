7.2 Milstein Scheme

The Euler-Maruyama scheme achieves only strong order $0.5$ because it omits an $O(\Delta t)$ term arising from the diffusion coefficient's own variation over a time step. The Milstein scheme recovers this term using one additional level of the stochastic Taylor (Itô-Taylor) expansion, raising the strong order of convergence to $1.0$.

Motivation: the Itô-Taylor Expansion

Remark 7.2.1 - Where the Missing Term Comes From

Apply Itô's formula to $\sigma(X_s,s)$ along the solution of $dX_t = \mu(X_t,t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t$. For $s \in [t_n,t_{n+1}]$,

$$\sigma(X_s,s) = \sigma(X_{t_n},t_n) + \int_{t_n}^{s}\left(\mu\sigma_x + \sigma_t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\sigma_{xx}\right)du + \int_{t_n}^{s}\sigma(X_u,u)\sigma_x(X_u,u)\,dW_u,$$

where subscripts denote partial derivatives, all evaluated along $X$. Substituting this into $\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}}\sigma(X_s,s)\,dW_s$, the leading correction beyond the Euler-Maruyama term $\sigma(X_{t_n},t_n)\Delta W_n$ comes from the double Itô integral

$$\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}}\!\!\int_{t_n}^{s}\sigma\sigma_x\,dW_u\,dW_s \approx \sigma(X_{t_n},t_n)\sigma_x(X_{t_n},t_n)\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}}\!\!\int_{t_n}^{s}dW_u\,dW_s = \sigma\sigma_x \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\left((\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right),$$

using the Itô isometry-type identity $\int_{t_n}^{t_{n+1}}\!\!\int_{t_n}^{s}dW_u\,dW_s = \frac{1}{2}\left((\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right)$. This $O(\Delta t)$ term, with zero mean but nonzero variance, is exactly what is missing from Euler-Maruyama.

The Scheme

Definition 7.2.2 - Milstein Scheme (one dimension)

For the SDE $dX_t = \mu(X_t,t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t,t)\,dW_t$, write $\sigma' = \partial\sigma/\partial x$. The Milstein approximation $Y_n \approx X_{t_n}$ is

$$Y_{n+1} = Y_n + \mu(Y_n,t_n)\,\Delta t + \sigma(Y_n,t_n)\,\Delta W_n + \frac{1}{2}\sigma(Y_n,t_n)\sigma'(Y_n,t_n)\left((\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right),$$

with $Y_0 = x_0$, $\Delta t = T/N$, $\Delta W_n = W_{t_{n+1}} - W_{t_n} \sim N(0,\Delta t)$. The scheme reduces to Euler-Maruyama whenever $\sigma$ does not depend on $x$ (i.e., $\sigma' \equiv 0$, additive noise), since in that case the correction term vanishes identically.

Convergence

Theorem 7.2.3 - Strong Convergence of the Milstein Scheme

Under standard regularity conditions on $\mu, \sigma$ (Lipschitz, linear growth, and $\sigma \in C^2$ in $x$), the Milstein scheme converges strongly with order $\gamma = 1.0$:

$$E\left[\max_{0\leq n \leq N}\left|X_{t_n} - Y_n\right|\right] \leq C(\Delta t).$$

This is twice the strong order of Euler-Maruyama: halving $\Delta t$ halves the pathwise error, rather than reducing it by only a factor of $\sqrt{2}$. The weak order remains $\beta = 1.0$, unchanged from Euler-Maruyama, so Milstein is primarily advantageous when pathwise (strong) accuracy matters.

Derivation Sketch

Subtracting the Euler-Maruyama increment from the true increment of $X$ over $[t_n,t_{n+1}]$, the Itô-Taylor expansion shows the leading-order discrepancy is precisely $\frac{1}{2}\sigma\sigma'\left((\Delta W_n)^2-\Delta t\right) + R_n$, where the remainder $R_n$ collects terms of order $(\Delta t)^{3/2}$ and higher (involving double and triple iterated integrals such as $\int\int\int dW\,dW\,dW$ and $\int\int dW\,ds$). The Milstein scheme explicitly includes the leading term and absorbs $R_n$ into the local truncation error, which is $O((\Delta t)^{3/2})$ per step in the strong sense. Summing the variances of $N = T/\Delta t$ such local errors (each $O((\Delta t)^3)$ in $L^2$) gives a global strong error of order $\sqrt{N \cdot (\Delta t)^3} = \sqrt{(\Delta t)^2} = \Delta t$, i.e. strong order $1$. $\square$

Worked Example: Geometric Brownian Motion

Example 7.2.4 - Milstein is Exact for GBM

For $dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$, we have $\sigma(S,t) = \sigma S$ and $\sigma'(S,t) = \sigma$, so $\sigma\sigma' = \sigma^2 S$. The Milstein recursion becomes

$$S_{n+1} = S_n + \mu S_n\Delta t + \sigma S_n \Delta W_n + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S_n\left((\Delta W_n)^2 - \Delta t\right) = S_n\left(1 + \mu\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\left((\Delta W_n)^2-\Delta t\right)\right).$$

Recall from Example 7.1.5 that the exact one-step multiplicative factor is $\exp\left((\mu-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n\right)$, whose Taylor expansion to second order is exactly $1 + \mu\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\left((\Delta W_n)^2-\Delta t\right) + O((\Delta t)^{3/2})$.

In fact, for GBM one can do better than Milstein: simulating $S_{n+1} = S_n\exp\left((\mu-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\Delta t + \sigma\Delta W_n\right)$ directly reproduces the exact transition law of $S$ at the grid points, with zero discretization error. This "exact simulation" is possible whenever the SDE can be transformed (e.g., via Itô's formula applied to $\log S_t$) into one with constant coefficients.

Beyond Milstein

Remark 7.2.5 - Higher-Order Schemes

In multiple dimensions with non-commuting noise terms, the Milstein correction involves Lévy areas - iterated stochastic integrals $\int\int dW^i\,dW^j$ for $i \neq j$ - which generally have no closed-form distribution and must themselves be simulated or approximated, complicating implementation. To push beyond strong order $1$ without this difficulty, derivative-free stochastic Runge-Kutta schemes (e.g., the Platen scheme) approximate the Milstein correction term using extra evaluations of $\sigma$ at perturbed points, achieving the same strong order $1.0$ (or higher, for higher-stage variants) without requiring explicit derivatives of $\mu$ or $\sigma$. These methods are widely used in practice when the coefficients are not analytically differentiable or when working in high dimensions.
