5.1 Connections to PDEs

Every Itô diffusion has an associated second-order differential operator — its infinitesimal generator — and the evolution of conditional expectations is governed by a parabolic PDE. This section makes the SDE–PDE dictionary precise, covering the Kolmogorov backward and forward equations and the exit-time problem.

Definition 5.1.1 — Infinitesimal Generator

Let $X$ satisfy the Itô SDE in $\mathbb{R}^n$:

$$dX_t = b(t, X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t, X_t)\,dW_t,$$

where $b: [0,T]\times\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ and $\sigma: [0,T]\times\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$. The infinitesimal generator of $X$ at time $t$ is the operator

$$\mathcal{L}_t u = b(t,x)\cdot\nabla u + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^n (\sigma\sigma^\top)_{ij}(t,x)\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} = b \cdot \nabla u + \frac{1}{2}a:\nabla^2 u,$$

where $a = \sigma\sigma^\top$ is the diffusion matrix. For smooth $f$, Itô's formula gives $df(X_t) = (\partial_t f + \mathcal{L}_t f)(t,X_t)\,dt + (\nabla f \cdot \sigma)\,dW_t$, so $\mathcal{L}_t$ captures the drift of $f(X_t)$.

Kolmogorov Backward Equation

Theorem 5.1.2 — Kolmogorov Backward Equation

Let $X$ be the diffusion above (with $b, \sigma$ smooth and satisfying standard growth conditions), and define

$$u(t,x) = E[g(X_T) \mid X_t = x]$$

for a bounded measurable $g$. Then $u \in C^{1,2}([0,T)\times\mathbb{R}^n)$ and satisfies

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L}_t u = 0, \quad (t,x) \in [0,T)\times\mathbb{R}^n,$$ $$u(T,x) = g(x).$$
Proof Sketch

By the Markov property, $u(t,X_t) = E[g(X_T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$ is a martingale. Apply Itô's formula:

$$du(t,X_t) = \left(\partial_t u + \mathcal{L}_t u\right)dt + \nabla u \cdot \sigma\,dW_t.$$

For $u(t,X_t)$ to be a martingale, the $dt$ term must vanish: $\partial_t u + \mathcal{L}_t u = 0$. Making this argument rigorous (via mollification and a priori estimates) gives the result. $\square$

Fokker–Planck Equation

Theorem 5.1.3 — Fokker–Planck (Kolmogorov Forward) Equation

Suppose $X_0$ has density $p_0$ and let $p(t,x)$ denote the density of $X_t$ (assuming it exists). Then $p$ satisfies the Fokker–Planck equation

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \mathcal{L}_t^* p := -\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(b_i p) + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}(a_{ij} p),$$

with initial condition $p(0,x) = p_0(x)$. Here $\mathcal{L}^*$ is the formal $L^2$-adjoint of $\mathcal{L}$. The backward equation governs expected values; the forward equation governs the probability density of the process.

Example 5.1.4 — SDE–PDE Dictionary

The correspondence between SDEs and PDEs can be summarised as follows:

SDE objectPDE object
Diffusion $dX = b\,dt + \sigma\,dW$Operator $\mathcal{L} = b\cdot\nabla + \tfrac{1}{2}a:\nabla^2$
$E[g(X_T)\mid X_t = x]$Solution of Kolmogorov backward PDE
Density of $X_t$Solution of Fokker–Planck PDE
Brownian motion $dX = dW$Heat equation $\partial_t u = \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta u$

Exit Time and Elliptic Problems

Theorem 5.1.5 — Exit Time and Elliptic BVP

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be bounded, $\tau = \inf\{t \geq 0 : X_t \notin \Omega\}$ the first exit time from $\Omega$, and $f \in C(\overline\Omega)$ a boundary datum. Define $u(x) = E^x[f(X_\tau)]$ (where $E^x$ denotes expectation starting from $X_0 = x$). Then $u$ solves the elliptic boundary value problem

$$\mathcal{L} u = 0 \text{ in } \Omega, \quad u = f \text{ on } \partial\Omega.$$

More generally, if $r \geq 0$ and $u(x) = E^x\!\left[e^{-\int_0^\tau r(X_s)\,ds} f(X_\tau)\right]$, then $\mathcal{L}u - ru = 0$ in $\Omega$ with $u = f$ on $\partial\Omega$.
