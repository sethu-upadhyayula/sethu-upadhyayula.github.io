5.2 Feynman-Kac Formula

The Feynman-Kac formula gives a probabilistic representation of the solution to a broad class of linear parabolic PDEs in terms of expectations over diffusion paths. It is both a theoretical tool - providing existence results via probabilistic arguments - and a computational tool - enabling Monte Carlo simulation of PDE solutions.

Theorem 5.2.1 - Feynman-Kac Formula

Let $X$ satisfy $dX_s = b(s,X_s)\,ds + \sigma(s,X_s)\,dW_s$ with $X_t = x$. Let $c, f, g$ be smooth with $c \geq 0$ bounded. Define

$$u(t,x) = E\!\left[e^{-\int_t^T c(s,X_s)\,ds}\,g(X_T) + \int_t^T e^{-\int_t^s c(r,X_r)\,dr}\,f(s,X_s)\,ds\;\Big|\;X_t = x\right].$$

Then $u \in C^{1,2}([0,T)\times\mathbb{R}^n)$ and satisfies

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mathcal{L}_t u - c(t,x)\,u + f(t,x) = 0, \quad (t,x) \in [0,T)\times\mathbb{R}^n,$$ $$u(T,x) = g(x).$$
Proof via Itô's Formula

Define the discounted process $M_s = e^{-\int_t^s c\,dr}\,u(s, X_s) + \int_t^s e^{-\int_t^r c\,dv}\,f(r,X_r)\,dr$. Apply Itô's formula to $e^{-\int_t^s c\,dr}u(s,X_s)$:

$$d\!\left[e^{-\int_t^s c\,dr}u\right] = e^{-\int_t^s c\,dr}\left[(\partial_s u + \mathcal{L}_s u - cu)\,ds + \nabla u \cdot \sigma\,dW_s\right].$$

If $u$ satisfies $\partial_s u + \mathcal{L}_s u - cu + f = 0$, then the $ds$ terms in $dM_s$ cancel, leaving only the stochastic integral $e^{-\int_t^s c\,dr}\nabla u \cdot \sigma\,dW_s$. Hence $M_s$ is a local martingale. Under appropriate integrability, it is a true martingale, so $M_t = E[M_T \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$. At $s = t$: $M_t = u(t,x)$. At $s = T$: $M_T = e^{-\int_t^T c\,dr}g(X_T) + \int_t^T e^{-\int_t^s c\,dr}f\,ds$. Taking expectations gives the Feynman-Kac representation. $\square$

Application 1: Black-Scholes PDE

Example 5.2.2 - Black-Scholes PDE via Feynman-Kac

Under the risk-neutral measure $Q$, the stock price satisfies $dS = rS\,dt + \sigma S\,d\widetilde{W}$ (geometric Brownian motion with drift $r$). The price of a European call option with payoff $g(S_T) = (S_T - K)^+$ and discount rate $c = r$ is

$$V(t,S) = E^Q\!\left[e^{-r(T-t)}(S_T - K)^+\;\Big|\;S_t = S\right].$$

By Feynman-Kac with $b = rS$, $\sigma(S) = \sigma S$, $c = r$, $f = 0$, $g(S) = (S-K)^+$, the function $V$ satisfies the Black-Scholes PDE:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} - rV = 0, \quad V(T,S) = (S-K)^+.$$

The closed-form solution is the Black-Scholes formula: $V(t,S) = S\Phi(d_+) - Ke^{-r(T-t)}\Phi(d_-)$ where $d_\pm = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r \pm \sigma^2/2)(T-t)}{\sigma\sqrt{T-t}}$ and $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

Application 2: Monte Carlo Simulation

Remark 5.2.3 - Monte Carlo via Feynman-Kac

The Feynman-Kac representation $u(t,x) = E[\cdots \mid X_t = x]$ converts a PDE problem into a statistical estimation problem. Given a PDE that is expensive to solve numerically (high-dimensional, complex geometry), one can instead:

1. Simulate $N$ independent paths $X^{(1)}, \ldots, X^{(N)}$ of the SDE starting from $X_t = x$.

2. Compute the sample mean $\hat{u}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^N \left[e^{-\int_t^T c\,ds}g(X_T^{(k)}) + \int_t^T e^{-\int_t^s c}f\,ds\right]$.

3. By the law of large numbers, $\hat{u}_N \to u(t,x)$ almost surely as $N \to \infty$, with statistical error $O(N^{-1/2})$ by the central limit theorem.

This Monte Carlo method scales well with dimension - unlike finite difference or finite element methods - making it the method of choice for high-dimensional financial PDEs.
