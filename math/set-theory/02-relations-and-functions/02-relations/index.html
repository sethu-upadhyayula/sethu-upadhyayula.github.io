2.2 Relations

A binary relation formalises the idea of a connection between elements of two sets. Since ordered pairs are now sets, a relation is simply a set of ordered pairs.

Definition 2.2.1 — Binary Relation

A binary relation from $A$ to $B$ is a subset $R \subseteq A \times B$. We write $a \mathrel{R} b$ to mean $(a,b) \in R$. A relation on $A$ means a relation from $A$ to $A$, i.e. $R \subseteq A \times A$.

Definition 2.2.2 — Domain, Range, and Inverse

For a relation $R \subseteq A \times B$:

  • Domain: $\mathrm{dom}(R) = \{a \in A : \exists b \in B,\, (a,b) \in R\}$.
  • Range: $\mathrm{ran}(R) = \{b \in B : \exists a \in A,\, (a,b) \in R\}$.
  • Inverse: $R^{-1} = \{(b,a) : (a,b) \in R\} \subseteq B \times A$.

Definition 2.2.3 — Composition

If $R \subseteq A \times B$ and $S \subseteq B \times C$, the composition is $$S \circ R = \{(a,c) \in A \times C : \exists b \in B,\, (a,b) \in R \text{ and } (b,c) \in S\}.$$

Properties of Relations on a Set

Definition 2.2.4 — Relational Properties

Let $R \subseteq A \times A$. We say $R$ is:

  • Reflexive: $\forall a \in A,\; a \mathrel{R} a$.
  • Symmetric: $a \mathrel{R} b \Rightarrow b \mathrel{R} a$.
  • Antisymmetric: $a \mathrel{R} b$ and $b \mathrel{R} a$ imply $a = b$.
  • Transitive: $a \mathrel{R} b$ and $b \mathrel{R} c$ imply $a \mathrel{R} c$.
  • Total: for all $a, b$, either $a \mathrel{R} b$ or $b \mathrel{R} a$.

Reflexive + antisymmetric + transitive gives a partial order; adding total gives a total (linear) order.

Example 2.2.5

On $\mathbb{Z}$:

  • $\leq$ is reflexive, antisymmetric, transitive, and total — a total order.
  • $|$ (divisibility) is reflexive, antisymmetric, transitive — a partial order (e.g. $2 \nmid 3$ and $3 \nmid 2$).
  • Congruence mod $n$ is reflexive, symmetric, transitive — an equivalence relation.
