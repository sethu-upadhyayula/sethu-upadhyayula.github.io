Chapter 6: Axiom of Choice

The Axiom of Choice (AC) is the most controversial and most useful of the ZFC axioms. It asserts that for any family of nonempty sets, we can simultaneously choose one element from each — even if the family is infinite and no explicit rule for choosing is available. Its logical independence from the other ZFC axioms was proved by Gödel (1938) and Cohen (1963).

The Axiom

Axiom of Choice

For any family $\mathcal{F}$ of nonempty sets, there exists a choice function $f: \mathcal{F} \to \bigcup \mathcal{F}$ such that $f(A) \in A$ for every $A \in \mathcal{F}$.

Remark — Intuition

For finite families, a choice function is easily constructed by explicit selection. For countable families, one can often construct a choice function using properties of the specific sets (e.g., "choose the smallest rational in each set"). AC is non-trivial only when the family is uncountable and no uniform selection rule is available — e.g., choosing one element from each coset of $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Q}$.

Equivalent Formulations

Theorem 6.1 — Equivalents of AC (assuming ZF)

The following are equivalent to AC over ZF:

  • Well-Ordering Theorem: Every set can be well-ordered.
  • Zorn's Lemma: If every chain in a partially ordered set has an upper bound, then the set has a maximal element.
  • Tychonoff's Theorem: An arbitrary product of compact topological spaces is compact.
  • Every vector space has a basis.
  • Every surjection has a right inverse.
  • Cardinals are comparable: For any two sets $A, B$, either $|A| \leq |B|$ or $|B| \leq |A|$.

Consequences

Hahn-Banach Theorem

Every bounded linear functional on a subspace of a normed vector space extends to the whole space. The proof uses Zorn's lemma (equivalent to AC). Without AC, this theorem can fail.

Banach-Tarski Paradox

Using AC, a solid ball in $\mathbb{R}^3$ can be decomposed into finitely many pieces (5 suffice) and reassembled — by rigid motions only — into two solid balls of the same radius as the original. The pieces are non-measurable and are constructed using AC. This is not a physical paradox but a mathematical theorem about the subtlety of uncountable sets and non-measurable sets.

Vitali Non-Measurable Sets

Using AC, one constructs a subset of $[0,1]$ that is not Lebesgue measurable: the Vitali set, obtained by choosing one representative from each coset of $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Q}$. Under the Axiom of Determinacy (AD, inconsistent with AC), all sets of reals are measurable.

Independence

Theorem 6.2 — Independence of AC

AC is independent of ZF (the axioms of ZFC without choice). Gödel (1938) showed AC holds in the constructible universe $L$, so ZF cannot disprove AC. Cohen (1963) used forcing to construct a model of ZF in which AC fails, so ZF cannot prove AC. The Axiom of Determinacy provides a well-studied alternative to AC in descriptive set theory.
