5.3 Continuum Hypothesis

Cantor proved $\aleph_0 < 2^{\aleph_0}$, but he could not determine how much larger $2^{\aleph_0}$ is. He conjectured in 1878 that there is no infinite cardinality strictly between $\aleph_0$ and $2^{\aleph_0}$ — this is the Continuum Hypothesis, the first of Hilbert's famous 23 problems (1900). Its resolution, through Gödel and Cohen, was one of the deepest results of twentieth-century mathematics.

Definition 5.3.1 — Continuum Hypothesis (CH)

$$2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1.$$ Equivalently: there is no set $A$ with $\aleph_0 < |A| < 2^{\aleph_0}$. Every uncountable subset of $\mathbb{R}$ has the cardinality of $\mathbb{R}$.

Definition 5.3.2 — Generalised Continuum Hypothesis (GCH)

$2^{\aleph_\alpha} = \aleph_{\alpha+1}$ for every ordinal $\alpha$. GCH implies CH but is much stronger.

Independence from ZFC

Theorem 5.3.3 — Gödel (1938): CH is Consistent with ZFC

Gödel constructed the constructible universe $L$ — a minimal inner model of ZFC — in which GCH (and hence CH) holds. Therefore ZFC cannot disprove CH: if ZFC is consistent, so is ZFC + CH.

Theorem 5.3.4 — Cohen (1963): $\neg$CH is Consistent with ZFC

Cohen invented the method of forcing to construct a model of ZFC in which $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$ (so CH fails). Therefore ZFC cannot prove CH: if ZFC is consistent, so is ZFC + $\neg$CH.

Corollary 5.3.5 — Independence

CH is independent of ZFC: it can neither be proved nor disproved from the ZFC axioms (assuming ZFC is consistent). The same holds for many natural combinatorial statements about the reals.

Remark — Large Cardinals

The independence of CH has spurred the study of large cardinal axioms — strong additional axioms (measurable cardinals, supercompact cardinals, etc.) that are not provable from ZFC but are widely believed to be consistent. Large cardinal axioms resolve some statements left open by ZFC, though CH itself remains undecided even by most large cardinals.
