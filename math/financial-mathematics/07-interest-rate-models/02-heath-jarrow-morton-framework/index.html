7.2 Heath–Jarrow–Morton Framework

The HJM framework (1992) models the dynamics of the entire forward rate curve $f(t,T)$ directly, instead of just the short rate. The key insight is that no-arbitrage places a constraint — the HJM drift condition — on the forward rate dynamics.

Forward Rates and Bond Prices

Definition — Forward Rate

The instantaneous forward rate $f(t,T)$ is defined via: $$P(t,T) = \exp\!\left(-\int_t^T f(t,u)\,du\right).$$ The short rate is recovered as $r_t = f(t,t)$. HJM specifies dynamics for $f(t,T)$ as a function of the maturity $T$: $$df(t,T) = \alpha(t,T)\,dt + \sigma(t,T)\,dW_t.$$

HJM Drift Condition

Theorem — HJM No-Arbitrage Drift Condition

Under the risk-neutral measure $Q$ (where $W_t$ is a $Q$-Brownian motion), the drift $\alpha(t,T)$ is not free: no-arbitrage requires $$\alpha(t,T) = \sigma(t,T)\int_t^T \sigma(t,u)\,du.$$

Proof sketch: Bond price $P(t,T) = \exp(-\int_t^T f(t,u)\,du)$. Applying Itô's lemma, the condition for $e^{-\int_0^t r_s\,ds}P(t,T)$ to be a $Q$-martingale forces the drift of $f(t,T)$ to be exactly $\sigma(t,T)\int_t^T\sigma(t,u)\,du$. Thus specifying the volatility surface $\sigma(t,T)$ completely determines the forward rate drift — only volatility is a free input.

Short Rate Models as HJM Special Cases

Example — Vasicek as HJM

For $\sigma(t,T) = \sigma e^{-\kappa(T-t)}$ (exponentially decaying vol), the HJM drift condition gives $$\alpha(t,T) = \sigma e^{-\kappa(T-t)}\int_t^T \sigma e^{-\kappa(u-t)}\,du = \frac{\sigma^2}{\kappa}e^{-\kappa(T-t)}(1-e^{-\kappa(T-t)}).$$ This is precisely the Vasicek forward rate dynamics. Thus the Vasicek model is an HJM model with exponential volatility.

LIBOR Market Model

Definition — LIBOR Market Model (BGM)

The HJM framework is theoretically elegant but leads to non-Markovian dynamics unless $\sigma(t,T)$ has special form. The LIBOR Market Model (Brace–Gatarek–Musiela 1997) avoids this by modeling discrete LIBOR rates $L_n(t)$ for period $[T_{n}, T_{n+1}]$ directly.

Under the $T_{n+1}$-forward measure: $$dL_n(t) = \sigma_n(t)\,L_n(t)\,d\widetilde{W}_t^{T_{n+1}}.$$ Each $L_n$ follows Black dynamics, so caplets are priced exactly by Black's formula with caplet volatility $\sigma_n$. Swaptions can be priced approximately using a frozen-drift approximation. The LMM is the market standard for calibrating and pricing interest rate volatility products.
