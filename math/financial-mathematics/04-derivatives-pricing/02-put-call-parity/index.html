4.2 Put-Call Parity

Put-call parity is one of the most useful model-free relationships in options theory. It links European call and put prices with the same strike, maturity, and underlying.

The Parity Relation

Theorem — Put-Call Parity

For European options on an asset with continuous dividend yield $q$, risk-free rate $r$, maturity $T$, strike $K$: $$C - P = S\,e^{-qT} - K\,e^{-rT}.$$

Proof: Consider two portfolios at time 0:

  • Portfolio A: long call + $Ke^{-rT}$ cash (invested at $r$).
  • Portfolio B: long put + $Se^{-qT}$ units of stock (dividends reinvested, giving exactly 1 share at $T$).
At $T$: A pays $\max(S_T-K,0) + K = \max(S_T,K)$. B pays $\max(K-S_T,0) + S_T = \max(S_T,K)$. Identical payoffs $\Rightarrow C + Ke^{-rT} = P + Se^{-qT}$.

For futures/forwards with forward price $F$: $C - P = (F-K)e^{-rT}$.

Applications

Remark — Uses of Put-Call Parity
  • Model-free pricing: Given call prices, recover put prices (or vice versa) without a model.
  • Implied forward: $F = K + (C - P)e^{rT}$; the forward price can be extracted from option prices.
  • Box spread arbitrage: If market prices violate put-call parity, a riskless profit is available via a box spread (long call spread + short put spread at same strikes).
  • Stress testing vol models: A consistent vol model must produce call and put prices satisfying parity for all strikes and maturities.
