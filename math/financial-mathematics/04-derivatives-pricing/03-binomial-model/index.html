4.3 Binomial Model

The binomial model is a discrete-time, complete market model for option pricing. Its simplicity makes it ideal for building intuition about risk-neutral pricing, delta hedging, and American option valuation.

One-Step Binomial Model

Definition - One-Step Model

In one step of length $\Delta t$, stock price moves from $S$ to $uS$ (up) or $dS$ (down) with real-world probabilities $p$ and $1-p$. The risk-neutral probability is: $$p^* = \frac{e^{r\Delta t} - d}{u - d}.$$ No-arbitrage requires $d < e^{r\Delta t} < u$, which ensures $p^* \in (0,1)$. The option price is: $$C = e^{-r\Delta t}\bigl[p^* C_u + (1-p^*)C_d\bigr].$$

The replicating delta (number of shares to hold) is: $$\Delta = \frac{C_u - C_d}{uS - dS}.$$

Multi-Step and CRR Parametrisation

Definition - CRR Binomial Tree

Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (1979): with $n$ steps of length $\Delta t = T/n$, set $$u = e^{\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}}, \quad d = 1/u = e^{-\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}}, \quad p^* \to \tfrac{1}{2} \text{ as } n\to\infty.$$ The $n$-step tree is computed by backward induction: at maturity, $C_T = $ payoff; at each prior node, $C = e^{-r\Delta t}(p^* C_u + (1-p^*)C_d)$.

For an American option, compare continuation value with immediate exercise at each node: $C_{\text{Am}} = \max(\text{exercise value},\;\text{continuation value})$.

Convergence to Black-Scholes

Theorem - Convergence

As $n\to\infty$ with CRR parametrisation, the binomial European call price converges to the Black-Scholes formula. The convergence of price is $O(1/n)$ (approximately); variance convergence is slower due to the discrete grid misaligning with the strike. The smoothing trick (placing the strike exactly on a node) speeds convergence to $O(1/n^2)$.
