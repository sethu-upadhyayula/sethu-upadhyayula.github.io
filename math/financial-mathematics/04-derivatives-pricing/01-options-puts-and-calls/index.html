4.1 Options: Puts and Calls

An option gives its holder the right, but not the obligation, to transact at a fixed price. The flexibility this right confers makes options valuable and their pricing non-trivial.

Definitions

Definition - European and American Options

A European call option with strike $K$ and maturity $T$ on underlying $S$ has payoff $$C_T = \max(S_T - K, 0) = (S_T - K)^+.$$ A European put option has payoff $P_T = (K - S_T)^+$.

An American option may be exercised at any time $\tau \le T$ chosen by the holder (a stopping time). Its value is at least as large as its European counterpart.

Definition - Moneyness

A call is in-the-money (ITM) if $S > K$, at-the-money (ATM) if $S \approx K$, out-of-the-money (OTM) if $S < K$. The intrinsic value is $(S-K)^+$ (call) or $(K-S)^+$ (put); the time value is the option price minus intrinsic value.

No-Arbitrage Bounds

Theorem - Lower Bounds for European Options

With continuous dividend yield $q$ and risk-free rate $r$: $$C \ge \max\!\left(S\,e^{-qT} - K\,e^{-rT},\; 0\right), \qquad P \ge \max\!\left(K\,e^{-rT} - S\,e^{-qT},\; 0\right).$$ The upper bounds are $C \le S\,e^{-qT}$ (call can never be worth more than the (dividend-adjusted) stock) and $P \le K\,e^{-rT}$ (put can never be worth more than PV of strike).

Proposition - American Call on Non-Dividend-Paying Stock

For a non-dividend-paying stock ($q=0$), it is never optimal to exercise an American call early: $C_{\text{Am}} = C_{\text{Eu}}$. Proof: the European call's lower bound $\ge Se^{-qT} - Ke^{-rT} = S - Ke^{-rT} > S - K$ (for $r>0$, $T>0$) strictly exceeds the exercise value $S-K$. Hence it is always better to sell the call than exercise it. For American puts and dividend-paying stocks, early exercise can be optimal.

P&L Diagrams and Spreads

Definition - Standard Strategies

At expiry, the P&L (per unit, net of premium) for the four basic positions:

  • Long call: $\max(S_T-K,0) - C$
  • Short call: $C - \max(S_T-K,0)$
  • Long put: $\max(K-S_T,0) - P$
  • Short put: $P - \max(K-S_T,0)$
A straddle (long call + long put, same $K,T$) profits from large moves in either direction. A strangle uses OTM call and put (lower net premium, wider break-evens). A bull call spread (long call at $K_1$, short call at $K_2 > K_1$) caps upside but reduces premium.
