1.1 Interest and Discounting

Interest is the price of money over time. The three conventions - simple, compound, and continuous - differ in how interest accrues, and each has its domain of application in financial markets.

Accumulation Functions

Definition - Simple Interest

Simple interest accrues linearly: a principal $P$ invested at annual rate $r$ for time $t$ (in years) grows to $$A = P(1 + rt).$$ Used for short-term instruments (money market, Treasury bills with maturity $\le 1$ year).

Definition - Compound Interest

With compound interest at nominal annual rate $r$ compounded $m$ times per year, the accumulation over $t$ years is: $$A = P\!\left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{mt}.$$ Common conventions: $m=1$ (annual), $m=2$ (semi-annual, used for US Treasury bonds), $m=12$ (monthly, mortgages), $m=365$ (daily).

Definition - Continuous Compounding

Taking $m\to\infty$: $\lim_{m\to\infty}(1+r/m)^{mt} = e^{rt}$. Under continuous compounding at rate $r$: $$A = Pe^{rt}.$$ This is the natural convention in derivatives theory because it makes discounting multiplicative and additive over time.

Effective Annual Rate

Definition - Effective Annual Rate

The effective annual rate (EAR) is the annually compounded rate that produces the same accumulation as the nominal rate $r$ compounded $m$ times per year: $$i_{\text{eff}} = \left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^m - 1.$$ For continuous compounding: $i_{\text{eff}} = e^r - 1$. The EAR allows comparison across different compounding conventions.

Present Value and Discounting

Definition - Present Value

The present value (PV) of a future cashflow $FV$ received at time $T$, discounted at continuously compounded rate $r$, is $$PV = FV \cdot e^{-rT}.$$ The factor $e^{-rT}$ is the discount factor. For a stream of cashflows $C_{t_k}$ at times $t_k$: $$NPV = \sum_k C_{t_k} e^{-r t_k}.$$

Net Present Value and IRR

Definition - Net Present Value and Internal Rate of Return

The net present value (NPV) of a project with initial outlay $C_0 < 0$ and subsequent cashflows $C_1,\ldots,C_n$ is $$NPV(r) = C_0 + \sum_{k=1}^n C_k e^{-rt_k}.$$ A project adds value iff $NPV > 0$.

The internal rate of return (IRR) is the rate $r^*$ solving $NPV(r^*) = 0$. For a conventional project (one sign change in cashflows), $r^*$ is unique. Accept the project iff $r^* > r_{\text{hurdle}}$.

Real vs Nominal Rates

Theorem - Fisher Equation

Let $r_{\text{nom}}$ be the nominal interest rate, $r_{\text{real}}$ the real rate, and $\pi$ the inflation rate. The exact Fisher equation is: $$(1 + r_{\text{real}}) = \frac{1 + r_{\text{nom}}}{1 + \pi}.$$ For small rates, $r_{\text{real}} \approx r_{\text{nom}} - \pi$. Inflation-indexed cashflows should be discounted at the real rate; nominal cashflows at the nominal rate.

Example - Loan Present Value

A borrower receives £10,000 today and promises to repay £11,500 in 2 years. The continuously compounded yield on 2-year instruments is $r = 6\%$ p.a. Is this a positive-NPV borrowing?

$NPV = +10{,}000 - 11{,}500\,e^{-0.06\times 2} = 10{,}000 - 11{,}500 \times 0.8869 = 10{,}000 - 10{,}199 = -199 < 0$. The borrower pays above the market rate: negative NPV for the borrower (positive for the lender). The IRR solves $11{,}500\,e^{-r^*\times 2} = 10{,}000$, giving $r^* = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(1.15) \approx 7.0\%$.
