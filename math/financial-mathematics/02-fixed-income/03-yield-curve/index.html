2.3 Yield Curve

The yield curve plots interest rates (spot or par) against maturity. Its shape encodes the market's expectations of future rates, risk premia, and supply-demand dynamics across maturities.

Shapes and Theories

Definition — Yield Curve Shapes

Common shapes: normal (upward sloping: long rates $>$ short rates, the usual case), inverted (short rates $>$ long rates, often a recession signal), humped (intermediate rates highest), flat. Three main explanatory theories:

  • Expectations hypothesis: $f(S,T) = E[r_{S,T}]$; the forward rate equals the expected future spot rate. Implies no liquidity premium.
  • Liquidity premium theory: long-term bonds carry a term premium because they expose holders to more price risk; the forward rate exceeds the expected spot rate by this premium.
  • Market segmentation: different maturities serve different investors (banks vs pension funds); supply and demand in each segment independently determine rates.

Par Rate and Bootstrapping

Definition — Par Rate

The par rate $y_n$ for maturity $n$ is the coupon rate that makes the bond price equal to face value $F = 100$: $$100 = y_n \sum_{k=1}^n P(0,k) + 100\,P(0,n),$$ so $y_n = (1 - P(0,n))\big/\sum_{k=1}^n P(0,k)$ where $P(0,k) = e^{-z_k k}$ are zero-coupon bond prices. Given a table of par rates, one can bootstrap the zero-coupon curve sequentially.

Nelson–Siegel Model

Definition — Nelson–Siegel Parametrisation

The Nelson–Siegel model fits the instantaneous forward rate curve with 4 parameters $(\beta_0,\beta_1,\beta_2,\tau)$: $$f(t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 e^{-t/\tau} + \beta_2 \frac{t}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau}.$$ The spot rate is $z_t = \tfrac{1}{t}\int_0^t f(s)\,ds$. $\beta_0$ is the long-run rate, $\beta_0+\beta_1$ the instantaneous short rate, and $\beta_2$ controls hump shape. Central banks (ECB, Bank of England) use Svensson's extension (two decay terms) for official yield curve estimates.

Interpolation Methods

Remark — Curve Interpolation

Linear interpolation on yields is simple but produces discontinuities in forward rates. Log-linear interpolation on discount factors is equivalent to linear interpolation on continuously compounded rates. Cubic spline interpolation on zero rates gives smooth curves and smooth forward rate curves, and is the standard in most yield curve libraries. The natural cubic spline (zero second derivative at endpoints) is often used; monotone cubic splines prevent unphysical oscillations in the forward curve.
