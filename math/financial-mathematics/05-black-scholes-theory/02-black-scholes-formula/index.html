5.2 Black-Scholes Formula

The Black-Scholes formula gives the exact closed-form price for European calls and puts under GBM, derived either from solving the Black-Scholes PDE or as a risk-neutral expectation.

The Formula

Theorem - Black-Scholes Formula

For a European call with spot $S$, strike $K$, risk-free rate $r$, dividend yield $q$, volatility $\sigma$, and time to maturity $T$: $$C = S\,e^{-qT}N(d_1) - K\,e^{-rT}N(d_2),$$ $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r - q + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T},$$ where $N(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF. The European put: $$P = K\,e^{-rT}N(-d_2) - S\,e^{-qT}N(-d_1).$$

Note put-call parity is automatically satisfied: $C - P = Se^{-qT} - Ke^{-rT}$.

Risk-Neutral Derivation

Proposition - Derivation via Risk-Neutral Expectation

Under the risk-neutral measure $Q$, $S_T = S\,e^{(r-q-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T + \sigma\sqrt{T}Z}$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$. Then $$C = e^{-rT}E^Q[(S_T-K)^+] = e^{-rT}\int_{d_2}^{\infty}(Se^{(r-q-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)T+\sigma\sqrt{T}z}-K)\phi(z)\,dz$$ where $\phi$ is the standard normal PDF. Splitting the integral and completing the square in the exponent yields the formula above; $N(d_2) = Q(S_T > K)$ is the risk-neutral probability of the call finishing in the money, and $N(d_1)$ is the delta.

Numerical Example

Example - ATM Call

$S = K = 100$, $r = 5\%$, $q = 0$, $\sigma = 20\%$, $T = 1$ year. $$d_1 = \frac{0 + (0.05 + 0.02)\times 1}{0.20} = \frac{0.07}{0.20} = 0.35, \quad d_2 = 0.35 - 0.20 = 0.15.$$ $N(0.35) \approx 0.6368$, $N(0.15) \approx 0.5596$. $$C = 100\times 0.6368 - 100\,e^{-0.05}\times 0.5596 = 63.68 - 95.12\times 0.5596 \approx 63.68 - 53.23 = 10.45.$$

The Black-76 formula for a European option on a futures price $F$: set $S = Fe^{-rT}$, $q = r$, giving $C = e^{-rT}[FN(d_1) - KN(d_2)]$ with $d_1 = [\ln(F/K) + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 T]/(\sigma\sqrt{T})$.
