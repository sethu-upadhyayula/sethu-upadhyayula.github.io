5.3 Greeks

The Greeks are partial derivatives of the option price with respect to the model parameters. They quantify sensitivities and are the primary tools for hedging and risk management.

Delta and Gamma

Definition - Delta ($\Delta$) and Gamma ($\Gamma$)

$$\Delta_C = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} = e^{-qT}N(d_1), \qquad \Delta_P = \frac{\partial P}{\partial S} = -e^{-qT}N(-d_1) = \Delta_C - e^{-qT}.$$

$$\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2} = \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial S^2} = \frac{e^{-qT}n(d_1)}{S\sigma\sqrt{T}},$$ where $n(\cdot)$ is the standard normal PDF. Gamma is the same for calls and puts (follows from put-call parity). Gamma is largest for ATM options near expiry.

Theta, Vega, and Rho

Definition - Theta ($\Theta$), Vega ($\nu$), Rho ($\rho$)

$$\Theta_C = \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = -\frac{Se^{-qT}n(d_1)\sigma}{2\sqrt{T}} - rKe^{-rT}N(d_2) + qSe^{-qT}N(d_1).$$ Theta is typically negative for long options (time decay).

$$\nu = \frac{\partial C}{\partial \sigma} = Se^{-qT}n(d_1)\sqrt{T} > 0. \quad \text{(same for calls and puts)}$$

$$\rho_C = \frac{\partial C}{\partial r} = KTe^{-rT}N(d_2) > 0, \qquad \rho_P = -KTe^{-rT}N(-d_2) < 0.$$

Gamma-Theta Relationship

Theorem - BS PDE in Terms of Greeks

Rewriting the Black-Scholes PDE using Greeks: $$\Theta + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\,\Gamma + rS\,\Delta - rV = 0.$$ For a delta-neutral portfolio ($\Delta = 0$): $\Theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\Gamma = rV$. A long gamma position gains value from large moves (positive $\Gamma$) but pays time decay (negative $\Theta$) - the fundamental gamma-theta trade-off of options market-making.
