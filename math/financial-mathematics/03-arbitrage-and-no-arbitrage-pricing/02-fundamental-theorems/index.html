3.2 Fundamental Theorems of Asset Pricing

The fundamental theorems of asset pricing (FTAP) formalise the connection between the economic notion of no-arbitrage and the mathematical concept of an equivalent martingale measure. Together they characterise when a market is arbitrage-free and when it is complete.

First Fundamental Theorem

Theorem — First FTAP

A financial market (on a finite probability space) is arbitrage-free if and only if there exists a probability measure $Q$ equivalent to the real-world measure $P$ (i.e., $Q$ and $P$ have the same null sets) such that all discounted asset prices $\tilde{S}_t = S_t/B_t$ (where $B_t = e^{rt}$ is the bank account) are $Q$-martingales. Such $Q$ is called a risk-neutral measure or equivalent martingale measure (EMM).

Second Fundamental Theorem

Theorem — Second FTAP

An arbitrage-free market is complete (every contingent claim is replicable by a self-financing portfolio) if and only if the equivalent martingale measure $Q$ is unique. In an incomplete market, there are multiple EMMs and the no-arbitrage price of an unhedgeable claim is not unique — it lies in an interval $(V^-, V^+)$ corresponding to the range of $E^Q[e^{-rT}V_T]$ over all EMMs $Q$.

Pricing Formula

Corollary — Risk-Neutral Pricing

In a complete arbitrage-free market, the unique no-arbitrage price at time 0 of a European contingent claim paying $V_T$ at time $T$ is: $$V_0 = e^{-rT}\,E^Q[V_T],$$ where $Q$ is the unique EMM. Under $Q$, all assets earn the risk-free rate: $E^Q[S_T] = S_0 e^{rT}$ (assuming no dividends). This is not saying the investor is risk-neutral; rather, we price in a hypothetical risk-neutral world to obtain the correct no-arbitrage price.

Arrow–Debreu State Prices

Definition — State Prices

In a finite market with states $\omega_1,\ldots,\omega_m$, the state price $\phi_i$ is the no-arbitrage price of a security paying £1 if state $\omega_i$ occurs and £0 otherwise (an Arrow–Debreu security). The EMM probabilities are $q_i = \phi_i / \sum_j \phi_j = \phi_i \cdot e^{rT}$. No-arbitrage $\Leftrightarrow$ all $\phi_i > 0$; completeness $\Leftrightarrow$ a unique set of $\phi_i$ exists.
