3.1 Triangulations and Meshes

Before assembling a finite element space, we must partition the computational domain $\Omega$ into simple geometric pieces. The quality of this partition — its conformity, regularity, and refinement level — directly controls the approximation properties of the resulting finite element space and the conditioning of the stiffness matrix.

Triangulations

Definition — Triangulation

A triangulation (or simplicial mesh) $\mathcal{T}_h$ of a polygonal domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is a finite collection of closed triangles $\{K\}$ such that:

  1. $\bar{\Omega} = \bigcup_{K \in \mathcal{T}_h} K$.
  2. For any two distinct elements $K, K' \in \mathcal{T}_h$, the intersection $K \cap K'$ is either empty, a common vertex, or a complete common edge (no partial overlaps).
  3. Every element $K$ is non-degenerate (positive area).

The second condition is called conformity (or regularity of the mesh in the topological sense). A mesh violating it (e.g.\ a hanging node) is non-conforming and requires special treatment.

Mesh Size and Shape Regularity

Definition — Mesh Size

For each element $K$, define the diameter $h_K = \operatorname{diam}(K) = \max_{x,y \in K}|x-y|$. The global mesh size is $$h = \max_{K \in \mathcal{T}_h} h_K.$$ A sequence of meshes $\{\mathcal{T}_h\}_{h \to 0}$ is obtained by successive refinement; convergence of the FEM solution to the exact solution is studied as $h \to 0$.

Definition — Shape Regularity

Let $\rho_K$ denote the inradius of $K$ (radius of the largest inscribed ball). The triangulation $\mathcal{T}_h$ is shape regular if there exists a constant $\sigma > 0$, independent of $h$, such that $$\frac{\rho_K}{h_K} \geq \sigma \quad \forall\, K \in \mathcal{T}_h, \quad \forall\, h.$$ Shape regularity prevents elements from becoming arbitrarily flat (degenerate) as the mesh is refined. Equivalently, all interior angles of triangles are bounded away from $0$ and $\pi$.

Definition — Quasi-Uniform Mesh

A shape-regular family of meshes is quasi-uniform if there exists $c > 0$ such that $$h_K \geq c\, h \quad \forall\, K \in \mathcal{T}_h, \quad \forall\, h.$$ This means all elements have comparable size; no element is much smaller than the global mesh size. Quasi-uniformity is needed for certain inverse estimates but can be relaxed for adaptive methods.

The Finite Element Space

Definition — $P_k$ Lagrange Finite Element Space

Given a triangulation $\mathcal{T}_h$ and polynomial degree $k \geq 1$, the $P_k$ conforming finite element space with homogeneous Dirichlet conditions is $$V_h = \{v_h \in C^0(\bar\Omega) : v_h|_K \in P_k(K) \text{ for all } K \in \mathcal{T}_h,\; v_h|_{\partial\Omega} = 0\},$$ where $P_k(K)$ denotes the space of polynomials of total degree $\leq k$ on $K$.

The global continuity condition $v_h \in C^0(\bar\Omega)$ ensures $V_h \subset H^1(\Omega)$, which is required for the bilinear form $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$ to be well-defined on $V_h$. Discontinuous Galerkin methods relax this requirement.

Proposition — Dimension of $V_h$

For a $P_1$ triangulation of $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$, the nodes of $V_h$ are the interior vertices of $\mathcal{T}_h$, and $\dim V_h$ equals the number of interior vertices $N_{\text{int}}$. For $P_2$, the nodes include interior vertices and interior/boundary edge midpoints; $\dim V_h$ counts all such nodes not on $\partial\Omega$. In general, $\dim V_h = O(h^{-2})$ for $d=2$ as $h \to 0$.

Remark — Node Counts for $P_1$ and $P_2$

On a shape-regular, quasi-uniform mesh of $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$:

  • $P_1$: 3 nodes per triangle (vertices); globally $N_v$ vertices, so $\dim V_h \approx N_v - N_{\partial}$ where $N_\partial$ is the number of boundary vertices.
  • $P_2$: 6 nodes per triangle (3 vertices + 3 edge midpoints); $\dim V_h$ grows as $O(h^{-2})$ but roughly four times larger than $P_1$ for the same mesh.
Example — Uniform Mesh on the Unit Square

Let $\Omega = (0,1)^2$ and divide into $n \times n$ squares, each split into 2 triangles. Then $h = \sqrt{2}/n$, $|\mathcal{T}_h| = 2n^2$ elements, and the number of interior vertices is $(n-1)^2$. For $P_1$, $\dim V_h = (n-1)^2 \approx h^{-2}/2$. The shape-regularity constant $\sigma = \rho_K/h_K = (h/\sqrt{2})/(h) = 1/\sqrt{2}$ is uniform, confirming shape regularity.
