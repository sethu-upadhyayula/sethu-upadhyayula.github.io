7.1 Semi-Discrete Formulation

The method of lines (or semi-discrete approach) applies FEM discretisation in space only, leaving time as a continuous variable. The result is a system of ODEs for the time-dependent nodal values, which can then be integrated by any suitable ODE solver.

The Heat Equation

Model Problem - Heat Equation

Find $u: \Omega \times (0,T] \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} - \Delta u = f(x,t) \quad \text{in } \Omega \times (0,T],$$ $$u = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega \times (0,T], \qquad u(\cdot,0) = u_0 \quad \text{in } \Omega.$$ The weak form at each time $t$: find $u(t) \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ such that $$\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t},\, v\right)_{L^2} + a(u(t),v) = \ell(v;t) \quad \forall\, v \in H^1_0(\Omega),$$ where $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$ and $\ell(v;t) = \int_\Omega f(x,t)\,v\,dx$.

Galerkin Discretisation in Space

Let $V_h = \operatorname{span}\{\varphi_1, \ldots, \varphi_N\} \subset H^1_0(\Omega)$ be a finite element space of dimension $N$ (the number of interior nodes). Seek an approximate solution of the form $$u_h(x,t) = \sum_{j=1}^N u_j(t)\,\varphi_j(x),$$ where the coefficients $\mathbf{u}(t) = (u_1(t),\ldots,u_N(t))^\top$ are now functions of time. Semi-Discrete System

The Galerkin spatial discretisation of the heat equation yields the ODE system $$M\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt}(t) + K\mathbf{u}(t) = \mathbf{f}(t), \qquad \mathbf{u}(0) = \mathbf{u}_0,$$ where the mass matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$, stiffness matrix $K \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$, and load vector $\mathbf{f}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^N$ are given by $$M_{ij} = \int_\Omega \varphi_i\,\varphi_j\,dx, \qquad K_{ij} = \int_\Omega \nabla\varphi_i \cdot \nabla\varphi_j\,dx, \qquad f_i(t) = \int_\Omega f(x,t)\,\varphi_i\,dx,$$ and $(\mathbf{u}_0)_j = (u_0, \varphi_j)_{L^2} / (\varphi_j, \varphi_j)_{L^2}$ approximates the initial condition.

Properties of the Mass Matrix

The mass matrix $M$ is symmetric positive definite: symmetry is immediate; positive definiteness follows because $\mathbf{v}^\top M\mathbf{v} = \|v_h\|_{L^2}^2 > 0$ for any nonzero $\mathbf{v}$ (where $v_h = \sum v_j \varphi_j$). Since $M$ is SPD, the ODE system can be written as $\dot{\mathbf{u}} = -M^{-1}K\mathbf{u} + M^{-1}\mathbf{f}(t)$, which is a linear ODE with coefficient matrix $-M^{-1}K$. Since $K$ is also SPD, all eigenvalues of $M^{-1}K$ are positive, confirming that the semi-discrete system is stable (solutions decay for $f=0$).

Lumped Mass Matrix

Definition - Lumped Mass Matrix

The lumped mass matrix $M_L$ is the diagonal matrix obtained by replacing each row of $M$ by a single diagonal entry equal to the row sum: $$(M_L)_{ii} = \sum_j M_{ij} = \int_\Omega \varphi_i\,dx.$$ Since $\sum_j \varphi_j = 1$ on the mesh, this gives $(M_L)_{ii} = \int_\Omega \varphi_i \cdot 1\,dx$. The advantage of $M_L$ is that it is trivially invertible ($M_L^{-1}$ is just a scaling), making explicit time-stepping schemes cheap - no linear system needs to be solved at each time step.

Semi-Discrete Error Estimate

Theorem - Semi-Discrete Error Estimate

Let $u$ be the exact solution of the heat equation and $u_h(t)$ the semi-discrete Galerkin solution with $P_1$ elements. Assume $u \in L^2(0,T; H^2(\Omega))$ and $u_0 \in H^2(\Omega)$. Then $$\|u(\cdot,t) - u_h(\cdot,t)\|_{L^2(\Omega)} \leq C h^2 \bigl(\|u_0\|_{H^2(\Omega)} + \|u\|_{L^2(0,t; H^2(\Omega))}\bigr).$$ The spatial convergence rate is the same $O(h^2)$ as for the stationary problem, valid uniformly in $t \in [0,T]$. For $P_k$ elements, the rate improves to $O(h^{k+1})$ in $L^2$ under sufficient regularity.

Parabolic Regularity

Remark - Parabolic Smoothing

A remarkable property of parabolic equations is instantaneous regularisation: even if the initial data $u_0 \in L^2(\Omega)$ (not in $H^2$), the solution $u(\cdot,t) \in H^2(\Omega)$ for any $t > 0$, with the bound $\|u(t)\|_{H^2} \leq C t^{-1}\|u_0\|_{L^2}$ (parabolic regularity). This means initial singularities are smoothed out and the FEM error estimate remains valid for $t > 0$, though the constant may blow up as $t \to 0^+$.
