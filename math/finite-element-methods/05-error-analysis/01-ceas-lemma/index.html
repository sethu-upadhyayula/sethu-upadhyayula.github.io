5.1 Céa's Lemma

Céa's lemma is the cornerstone of finite element error analysis. It shows that the Galerkin solution $u_h \in V_h$ is a quasi-best approximation to the exact solution $u \in V$ in the energy norm: the FEM error is bounded, up to a constant, by the best approximation error achievable in the discrete space $V_h$.

Setting

Recall the abstract variational problem: given a Hilbert space $V$, a continuous coercive bilinear form $a: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$, and a bounded linear functional $\ell \in V'$, we seek $u \in V$ such that $$a(u, v) = \ell(v) \quad \forall\, v \in V.$$ The Galerkin problem on a finite-dimensional subspace $V_h \subset V$ is: find $u_h \in V_h$ such that $$a(u_h, v_h) = \ell(v_h) \quad \forall\, v_h \in V_h.$$ We denote by $\alpha > 0$ the coercivity constant and by $M > 0$ the continuity constant of $a$: $$a(v,v) \geq \alpha \|v\|_V^2, \qquad |a(u,v)| \leq M\|u\|_V \|v\|_V.$$

Galerkin Orthogonality

Lemma — Galerkin Orthogonality

Let $u \in V$ solve the continuous problem and $u_h \in V_h$ solve the Galerkin problem. Then $$a(u - u_h,\, v_h) = 0 \quad \forall\, v_h \in V_h.$$

Proof. Subtracting the two variational equalities: $$a(u, v_h) - a(u_h, v_h) = \ell(v_h) - \ell(v_h) = 0. \qquad \square$$

This says the error $e = u - u_h$ is $a$-orthogonal to the entire discrete space $V_h$. Geometrically, $u_h$ is the oblique projection of $u$ onto $V_h$ with respect to the inner product induced by $a$ (when $a$ is symmetric, it is the orthogonal projection).

Céa's Lemma

Theorem — Céa's Lemma

Let $u \in V$ and $u_h \in V_h$ be the solutions of the continuous and Galerkin problems, respectively, with $a$ coercive (constant $\alpha$) and continuous (constant $M$). Then $$\|u - u_h\|_V \leq \frac{M}{\alpha} \min_{v_h \in V_h} \|u - v_h\|_V.$$

Proof. Let $w_h \in V_h$ be arbitrary. Write $$u - u_h = (u - w_h) + (w_h - u_h).$$ Since $w_h - u_h \in V_h$, coercivity gives $$\alpha \|u - u_h\|_V^2 \leq a(u - u_h,\, u - u_h).$$ Expand using $u - u_h = (u - w_h) + (w_h - u_h)$: $$a(u - u_h,\, u - u_h) = a(u - u_h,\, u - w_h) + a(u - u_h,\, w_h - u_h).$$ By Galerkin orthogonality, $a(u - u_h,\, w_h - u_h) = 0$ (since $w_h - u_h \in V_h$). Therefore $$\alpha \|u - u_h\|_V^2 \leq a(u - u_h,\, u - w_h) \leq M \|u - u_h\|_V \|u - w_h\|_V.$$ Dividing both sides by $\alpha\|u - u_h\|_V$ (assuming the error is nonzero; otherwise the bound is trivial): $$\|u - u_h\|_V \leq \frac{M}{\alpha}\|u - w_h\|_V.$$ Since $w_h \in V_h$ was arbitrary, minimise over all $w_h \in V_h$. $\square$

The Energy Norm

When $a$ is symmetric and coercive, it defines an inner product on $V$. The associated energy norm is $\|v\|_a = \sqrt{a(v,v)}$, which is equivalent to $\|\cdot\|_V$ via $\sqrt{\alpha}\|v\|_V \leq \|v\|_a \leq \sqrt{M}\|v\|_V$. In the energy norm, the Galerkin solution is exactly the best approximation:

Corollary — Optimality in the Energy Norm (Symmetric Case)

If $a$ is symmetric, then $$\|u - u_h\|_a = \min_{v_h \in V_h} \|u - v_h\|_a,$$ i.e., $u_h$ is the best approximation to $u$ from $V_h$ in the energy norm. In the general (non-symmetric) case, Céa's lemma gives quasi-optimality with constant $M/\alpha \geq 1$.

Interpretation and Significance

Remark — Separation of Analysis

Céa's lemma decouples the error analysis into two independent problems:

  1. Approximation theory: How well can functions in $V$ be approximated by elements of $V_h$? This is answered by interpolation error estimates (Section 5.2).
  2. Stability of the Galerkin method: The ratio $M/\alpha$ measures the conditioning of the bilinear form. For the Poisson problem with $a(u,v) = \int \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$ in $H^1_0(\Omega)$, coercivity holds with $\alpha = 1$ (Poincaré inequality) and $M = 1$, so the constant is exactly $1$.

Example — Poisson Equation

For $-\Delta u = f$ with $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$ on $H^1_0(\Omega)$, we have $\alpha = M = 1$ (after applying Poincaré). Céa's lemma gives $$\|u - u_h\|_{H^1(\Omega)} \leq \min_{v_h \in V_h} \|u - v_h\|_{H^1(\Omega)}.$$ The FEM solution is the $H^1$-best approximation from $V_h$ to $u$.
