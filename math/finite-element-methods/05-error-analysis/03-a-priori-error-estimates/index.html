5.3 A Priori Error Estimates

Combining Céa's lemma with interpolation error estimates yields quantitative bounds on the FEM error in terms of the mesh size $h$ and the regularity of the exact solution. These are called a priori estimates because they are expressed in terms of the exact solution $u$ (not the computed solution $u_h$) and must be established before any computation.

Energy-Norm ($H^1$) Estimate

Theorem — $H^1$ A Priori Error Estimate

Let $u \in H^{k+1}(\Omega) \cap H^1_0(\Omega)$ be the exact solution of a second-order elliptic problem and $u_h \in V_h$ the Galerkin solution on a shape-regular mesh with $P_k$ elements. Then $$\|u - u_h\|_{H^1(\Omega)} \leq C\, h^k\, \|u\|_{H^{k+1}(\Omega)},$$ where $C$ is independent of $h$ and $u$. In particular, for $P_1$ elements ($k=1$): $$\|u - u_h\|_{H^1(\Omega)} \leq C\, h\, \|u\|_{H^2(\Omega)}.$$

Proof. By Céa's lemma (with constant $M/\alpha$, which equals 1 for the symmetric Poisson problem): $$\|u - u_h\|_{H^1} \leq \frac{M}{\alpha} \min_{v_h \in V_h} \|u - v_h\|_{H^1} \leq \frac{M}{\alpha} \|u - I_h u\|_{H^1} \leq \frac{M}{\alpha} C h^k \|u\|_{H^{k+1}}. \qquad \square$$

$L^2$ Estimate via Aubin–Nitsche Duality

The $H^1$ estimate gives convergence of rate $h^k$. The Aubin–Nitsche duality argument (also called the elliptic regularity trick) lifts this to an $L^2$ estimate of rate $h^{k+1}$, gaining one extra power of $h$ at the cost of requiring an elliptic regularity assumption.

Assumption — Elliptic Regularity

The domain $\Omega$ and the operator are such that for any $g \in L^2(\Omega)$, the dual problem $$-\Delta z = g \quad \text{in } \Omega, \qquad z = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega$$ has a unique solution $z \in H^2(\Omega)$ satisfying $\|z\|_{H^2(\Omega)} \leq C_{\mathrm{reg}}\|g\|_{L^2(\Omega)}$. This holds when $\Omega$ is convex or has a $C^{1,1}$ boundary.

Theorem — $L^2$ A Priori Error Estimate (Aubin–Nitsche)

Under the elliptic regularity assumption, and with $u \in H^{k+1}(\Omega)$: $$\|u - u_h\|_{L^2(\Omega)} \leq C\, h^{k+1}\, \|u\|_{H^{k+1}(\Omega)}.$$ For $P_1$ elements: $\|u - u_h\|_{L^2} \leq C h^2 \|u\|_{H^2}$.

Proof. Set $g = u - u_h \in L^2(\Omega)$ and let $z \in H^2(\Omega)$ solve the dual problem $-\Delta z = g$. Then: $$\|u - u_h\|_{L^2}^2 = (u - u_h,\, g)_{L^2} = (u - u_h,\, -\Delta z)_{L^2} = a(u - u_h,\, z).$$ By Galerkin orthogonality, for any $z_h \in V_h$: $$a(u - u_h,\, z) = a(u - u_h,\, z - z_h).$$ By continuity of $a$: $$|a(u - u_h,\, z - z_h)| \leq M \|u - u_h\|_{H^1} \|z - z_h\|_{H^1}.$$ Choose $z_h = I_h z$. The interpolation estimate gives $\|z - I_h z\|_{H^1} \leq Ch\|z\|_{H^2} \leq C h C_{\mathrm{reg}} \|g\|_{L^2} = Ch C_{\mathrm{reg}} \|u - u_h\|_{L^2}$. Substituting the $H^1$ estimate for $\|u - u_h\|_{H^1}$: $$\|u - u_h\|_{L^2}^2 \leq M \cdot Ch^k\|u\|_{H^{k+1}} \cdot Ch C_{\mathrm{reg}} \|u - u_h\|_{L^2}.$$ Dividing by $\|u - u_h\|_{L^2}$ yields the result. $\square$

Convergence Rates Summary

The following table collects the a priori error rates for smooth solutions $u \in H^{k+1}(\Omega)$:

Table — Convergence Rates
Element $k$ $\|u - u_h\|_{H^1}$ $\|u - u_h\|_{L^2}$ Regularity needed
$P_1$ (linear) 1 $O(h)$ $O(h^2)$ $u \in H^2$
$P_2$ (quadratic) 2 $O(h^2)$ $O(h^3)$ $u \in H^3$
$P_3$ (cubic) 3 $O(h^3)$ $O(h^4)$ $u \in H^4$
$P_k$ $k$ $O(h^k)$ $O(h^{k+1})$ $u \in H^{k+1}$

Limitations: Non-Smooth Solutions

Remark — Loss of Regularity

The a priori estimates require $u \in H^{k+1}(\Omega)$. If $\Omega$ has re-entrant corners (e.g., an L-shaped domain), $u$ may only lie in $H^{1+s}(\Omega)$ for some $s < 1$, and the convergence rate degrades to $O(h^s)$ in $H^1$. In such cases, adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) guided by a posteriori error estimators can recover optimal convergence.

Remark — Checking Convergence Rates Numerically

To verify the expected rate $O(h^p)$ numerically, compute the error on a sequence of meshes with $h, h/2, h/4, \ldots$ and compute the convergence rate as $$r \approx \log_2\!\left(\frac{\|u - u_{h}\|}{\|u - u_{h/2}\|}\right).$$ An observed rate of $r \approx k$ for the $H^1$ error (and $r \approx k+1$ for $L^2$) confirms the theoretical predictions.
