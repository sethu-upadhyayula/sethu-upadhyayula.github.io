2.2 Lax-Milgram Theorem

The Lax-Milgram theorem is the central existence and uniqueness result underpinning the variational approach to elliptic PDEs. It generalises the Riesz representation theorem to non-symmetric bilinear forms and provides the theoretical foundation justifying the well-posedness of both the continuous and discrete Galerkin problems.

Assumptions

Definition - Coercivity and Continuity

Let $V$ be a real Hilbert space with norm $\|\cdot\|_V$ and inner product $(\cdot,\cdot)_V$. A bilinear form $a: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ is:

  • Continuous (bounded): there exists $M > 0$ such that $|a(u,v)| \leq M\|u\|_V \|v\|_V$ for all $u,v \in V$.
  • Coercive (elliptic): there exists $\alpha > 0$ such that $a(v,v) \geq \alpha \|v\|_V^2$ for all $v \in V$.

A linear functional $\ell: V \to \mathbb{R}$ is bounded if $\|\ell\|_{V^*} = \sup_{\|v\|_V=1}|\ell(v)| < \infty$, i.e.\ $\ell \in V^*$ (the dual space).

The Theorem

Theorem - Lax-Milgram

Let $V$ be a real Hilbert space, $a: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ a continuous and coercive bilinear form with constants $M$ and $\alpha$, and $\ell \in V^*$ a bounded linear functional. Then there exists a unique $u \in V$ such that $$a(u, v) = \ell(v) \quad \forall\, v \in V.$$ Moreover, the solution satisfies the stability estimate $$\|u\|_V \leq \frac{\|\ell\|_{V^*}}{\alpha}.$$

Proof Sketch

The proof proceeds in two steps: first reducing to a fixed-point problem via the Riesz representation theorem, then applying the Banach contraction mapping principle.

Proposition - Reduction to Fixed Point

By the Riesz representation theorem, for each fixed $u \in V$ the map $v \mapsto a(u,v)$ defines an element $Au \in V^{**} \cong V$, and similarly $\ell$ corresponds to some $f \in V$ via $(f,v)_V = \ell(v)$. More precisely, define $A: V \to V$ by $(Au, v)_V = a(u, v)$ for all $v \in V$; this is well-defined by continuity. Then the variational problem becomes: find $u \in V$ such that $Au = f$ in $V$. One shows $A$ is bounded ($\|Au\|_V \leq M\|u\|_V$) and that the map $$T_\rho u = u - \rho(Au - f)$$ is a contraction on $V$ for $\rho \in (0, 2\alpha/M^2)$: $$\|T_\rho u - T_\rho w\|_V^2 \leq (1 - 2\rho\alpha + \rho^2 M^2)\|u - w\|_V^2 < \|u-w\|_V^2.$$ By the Banach fixed-point theorem, $T_\rho$ has a unique fixed point, which satisfies $Au = f$, i.e.\ the original variational problem. The stability estimate follows from coercivity: $\alpha\|u\|_V^2 \leq a(u,u) = \ell(u) \leq \|\ell\|_{V^*}\|u\|_V$. $\square$

Application: Poisson Equation on $H^1_0$

Example - Well-posedness of the Poisson Problem

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain, $V = H^1_0(\Omega)$, and $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$. For $f \in L^2(\Omega)$, set $\ell(v) = \int_\Omega fv\,dx$.

  • Continuity: $|a(u,v)| \leq \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}\|\nabla v\|_{L^2} \leq \|u\|_{H^1}\|v\|_{H^1}$, so $M = 1$.
  • Coercivity: by the Poincaré inequality $\|v\|_{H^1_0}^2 \leq C_P^{-2}\|\nabla v\|_{L^2}^2 + \|\nabla v\|_{L^2}^2$; more precisely $a(v,v) = \|\nabla v\|_{L^2}^2 \geq \alpha\|v\|_{H^1}^2$ for some $\alpha > 0$ depending on $\Omega$.
  • Boundedness of $\ell$: by Cauchy-Schwarz and Poincaré, $|\ell(v)| \leq \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{L^2} \leq C\|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{H^1}$.

Lax-Milgram then guarantees a unique $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ satisfying the weak form, with $\|u\|_{H^1_0} \leq C\|f\|_{L^2}$. This $u$ solves $-\Delta u = f$ in the weak sense.

Energy Minimisation Equivalence (Symmetric Case)

Theorem - Variational Principle

If additionally $a$ is symmetric, define the energy functional $$J(v) = \frac{1}{2}a(v,v) - \ell(v).$$ Then the unique solution $u$ of the variational problem is also the unique minimiser of $J$ over $V$: $$u = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{v \in V} J(v).$$ Proof. For any $v \in V$ and $t \in \mathbb{R}$, $J(u + t(v-u)) = J(u) + t\,[a(u,v-u) - \ell(v-u)] + \frac{t^2}{2}a(v-u,v-u)$. At $u$ satisfying the variational problem the linear term vanishes, and the quadratic term is non-negative by coercivity. $\square$

Corollary - Well-posedness of the Discrete Problem

Since $V_h \subset V$ is a finite-dimensional Hilbert space, and $a$ restricted to $V_h \times V_h$ is still continuous and coercive (with the same constants), the Lax-Milgram theorem applies to the Galerkin problem on $V_h$ without modification. This guarantees the stiffness matrix $K$ is invertible (indeed SPD when $a$ is symmetric).

Remark - Non-symmetric Problems

Lax-Milgram applies to non-symmetric $a$, e.g.\ advection-diffusion $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega (\nabla u \cdot \nabla v + \mathbf{b} \cdot \nabla u\, v)\,dx$ with $\mathbf{b}$ a velocity field. Coercivity still holds when diffusion dominates (or via a Gårding inequality), but the stiffness matrix is no longer symmetric. For convection-dominated problems, the standard Galerkin method may be unstable; stabilised methods (SUPG, DG) are then needed.
