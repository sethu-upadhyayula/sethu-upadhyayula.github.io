2.1 Abstract Galerkin Framework

Given the weak formulation — find $u \in V$ such that $a(u,v) = \ell(v)$ for all $v \in V$ — the Galerkin method replaces the infinite-dimensional space $V$ by a finite-dimensional subspace $V_h \subset V$. The resulting finite-dimensional problem is equivalent to a linear system of equations, which can be assembled and solved on a computer.

The Galerkin Problem

Definition — Galerkin Approximation

Let $V$ be a Hilbert space and $V_h \subset V$ a finite-dimensional subspace with $\dim V_h = N$. The Galerkin problem is: find $u_h \in V_h$ such that $$a(u_h, v_h) = \ell(v_h) \quad \forall\, v_h \in V_h.$$ Here $a: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ is a bilinear form and $\ell: V \to \mathbb{R}$ is a bounded linear functional. The subscript $h$ refers to a discretisation parameter (e.g.\ the mesh size) that governs how well $V_h$ approximates $V$.

Basis Representation and the Stiffness System

Let $\{\varphi_1, \ldots, \varphi_N\}$ be a basis for $V_h$. Every element $v_h \in V_h$ can be written uniquely as $$v_h = \sum_{j=1}^N v_j \varphi_j, \qquad v_j \in \mathbb{R}.$$ We write the unknown solution as $u_h = \sum_{j=1}^N u_j \varphi_j$ and test against each basis function $\varphi_i$, $i = 1, \ldots, N$:

$$a\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^N u_j \varphi_j,\, \varphi_i\right) = \ell(\varphi_i) \implies \sum_{j=1}^N u_j\, a(\varphi_j, \varphi_i) = \ell(\varphi_i).$$
Definition — Stiffness Matrix and Load Vector

The stiffness matrix $K \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ and load vector $\mathbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^N$ are defined by $$K_{ij} = a(\varphi_j, \varphi_i), \qquad f_i = \ell(\varphi_i), \quad i,j = 1,\ldots,N.$$ The Galerkin problem is then equivalent to the linear system $$K\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f},$$ where $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, \ldots, u_N)^\top$ are the coefficients of $u_h$ in the basis $\{\varphi_j\}$.

Proposition — Symmetry and Positive Definiteness

If the bilinear form $a$ is symmetric (i.e.\ $a(u,v) = a(v,u)$ for all $u,v$) and coercive (i.e.\ $a(v,v) \geq \alpha \|v\|_V^2$ for some $\alpha > 0$), then $K$ is symmetric positive definite (SPD). In particular, $K$ is invertible and the Galerkin problem has a unique solution.

Galerkin Orthogonality

One of the most fundamental properties of the Galerkin method is that the error $e_h = u - u_h$ is orthogonal to $V_h$ with respect to the bilinear form $a$.

Theorem — Galerkin Orthogonality

Let $u \in V$ satisfy the continuous problem $a(u, v) = \ell(v)$ for all $v \in V$, and let $u_h \in V_h$ satisfy the Galerkin problem $a(u_h, v_h) = \ell(v_h)$ for all $v_h \in V_h$. Then $$a(u - u_h,\, v_h) = 0 \quad \forall\, v_h \in V_h.$$

Proof. Subtracting the Galerkin equation from the continuous equation (using $V_h \subset V$): $$a(u, v_h) - a(u_h, v_h) = \ell(v_h) - \ell(v_h) = 0. \qquad \square$$

Remark — Geometric Interpretation

Galerkin orthogonality says that $u_h$ is the orthogonal projection of $u$ onto $V_h$ in the inner product induced by $a$ (when $a$ is symmetric positive definite). That is, $u_h = P_{V_h} u$ where $P_{V_h}$ is the $a$-orthogonal projector.

Best Approximation in the Energy Norm

When $a$ is symmetric and coercive, it defines the energy norm $\|v\|_a = \sqrt{a(v,v)}$, which is equivalent to $\|\cdot\|_V$. A powerful consequence of Galerkin orthogonality is that the Galerkin solution is the best possible approximation to $u$ in this norm.

Theorem — Best Approximation Property

If $a$ is symmetric and coercive, then $$\|u - u_h\|_a = \min_{v_h \in V_h} \|u - v_h\|_a.$$ That is, the Galerkin solution $u_h$ is the best approximation to $u$ from $V_h$ in the energy norm.

Proof. For any $v_h \in V_h$, write $w_h = v_h - u_h \in V_h$. Then by Galerkin orthogonality: $$\|u - v_h\|_a^2 = \|u - u_h - w_h\|_a^2 = \|u - u_h\|_a^2 - 2a(u - u_h, w_h) + \|w_h\|_a^2 = \|u - u_h\|_a^2 + \|w_h\|_a^2 \geq \|u - u_h\|_a^2.$$ Equality holds when $w_h = 0$, i.e.\ $v_h = u_h$. $\square$

Remark — Céa's Lemma Preview

When $a$ is not symmetric, we cannot directly apply the best approximation argument. Instead, Céa's Lemma (Chapter 5) uses Galerkin orthogonality together with coercivity and continuity to obtain a quasi-best-approximation estimate: $\|u - u_h\|_V \leq (M/\alpha)\min_{v_h \in V_h}\|u - v_h\|_V$.

Example — Poisson Equation

For the Poisson problem $-\Delta u = f$ on $H^1_0(\Omega)$, we have $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$ and $\ell(v) = \int_\Omega fv\,dx$. The energy norm is $\|v\|_a = \|\nabla v\|_{L^2(\Omega)}$ (equivalent to the $H^1_0$ norm by Poincaré). The Galerkin problem on the finite element space $V_h \subset H^1_0(\Omega)$ is: find $u_h \in V_h$ such that $$\int_\Omega \nabla u_h \cdot \nabla v_h\,dx = \int_\Omega f v_h\,dx \quad \forall\, v_h \in V_h.$$ The stiffness matrix is $K_{ij} = \int_\Omega \nabla\varphi_j \cdot \nabla\varphi_i\,dx$, which is SPD, and the Galerkin solution minimises $\|\nabla(u - u_h)\|_{L^2}$ over all $v_h \in V_h$.
