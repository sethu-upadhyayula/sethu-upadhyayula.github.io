1.1 Weak Form of Boundary Value Problems

The starting point of the finite element method is the strong form of a boundary value problem — a classical PDE that the solution must satisfy pointwise. We then derive a weaker formulation by multiplying by a test function and integrating by parts, which requires less regularity and opens the door to Galerkin approximation.

The Model Problem

Definition — Strong Form (Poisson Equation)

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ ($d = 2$ or $3$) be a bounded Lipschitz domain with boundary $\partial\Omega$. Given $f \in L^2(\Omega)$, the strong form of the Poisson problem with homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions is: $$-\Delta u = f \quad \text{in } \Omega, \qquad u = 0 \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega.$$ A classical solution is a function $u \in C^2(\Omega) \cap C^0(\bar\Omega)$ satisfying these equations pointwise.

Derivation of the Weak Form

To derive the weak form, we multiply the PDE by a test function $v$ and integrate over $\Omega$. The key tool is Green's first identity.

Theorem — Green's First Identity

Let $u \in C^2(\bar\Omega)$ and $v \in C^1(\bar\Omega)$. Then: $$\int_\Omega (-\Delta u)\, v\,dx = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx - \int_{\partial\Omega} \frac{\partial u}{\partial n}\, v\,dS,$$ where $\mathbf{n}$ is the outward unit normal to $\partial\Omega$.

Applying this with test functions $v \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ (which vanish on $\partial\Omega$, so the boundary integral drops), we multiply $-\Delta u = f$ by $v$ and integrate:

$$\int_\Omega (-\Delta u)\,v\,dx = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx = \int_\Omega f\,v\,dx.$$
Definition — Weak Form

Define the bilinear form $a: H^1_0(\Omega) \times H^1_0(\Omega) \to \mathbb{R}$ and linear functional $\ell: H^1_0(\Omega) \to \mathbb{R}$ by: $$a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx, \qquad \ell(v) = \int_\Omega f\,v\,dx.$$ The weak form of the Poisson problem is: find $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ such that $$a(u,v) = \ell(v) \quad \forall\, v \in H^1_0(\Omega).$$

Equivalence with the Strong Form

Theorem — Equivalence

If $u \in C^2(\Omega) \cap C^0(\bar\Omega)$ is a classical solution to the strong form, then $u$ satisfies the weak form. Conversely, if $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ satisfies the weak form and additionally $u \in C^2(\Omega)$, then $u$ is a classical solution.

Remark — Why Weaker is Better

The weak form requires only $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ — one weak derivative in $L^2$ — rather than two classical derivatives. This broader function space admits solutions to problems with discontinuous data $f$ or non-smooth domains where classical solutions may not exist. The Lax-Milgram theorem then guarantees existence and uniqueness in $H^1_0$.

Neumann Boundary Conditions

When we prescribe the flux $\partial u / \partial n = g$ on (part of) the boundary, the boundary integral in Green's identity does not vanish. This is called a natural boundary condition because it arises naturally in the weak form without special treatment.

Definition — Pure Neumann Problem

The strong form $-\Delta u = f$ in $\Omega$, $\partial u/\partial n = g$ on $\partial\Omega$ leads to the weak form: find $u \in H^1(\Omega)$ such that $$\int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx = \int_\Omega f\,v\,dx + \int_{\partial\Omega} g\,v\,dS \quad \forall\, v \in H^1(\Omega).$$ Note that $u$ is now sought in $H^1(\Omega)$ (not $H^1_0$), since no Dirichlet condition is imposed. A compatibility condition $\int_\Omega f\,dx + \int_{\partial\Omega} g\,dS = 0$ is required for existence.

Mixed Boundary Conditions

Many physical problems involve different boundary conditions on different parts of the boundary. Let $\partial\Omega = \Gamma_D \cup \Gamma_N$ (disjoint, $|\Gamma_D| > 0$).

Definition — Mixed Boundary Problem

Strong form: $-\Delta u = f$ in $\Omega$, $u = 0$ on $\Gamma_D$, $\partial u/\partial n = g$ on $\Gamma_N$. Define $V = \{v \in H^1(\Omega) : v = 0 \text{ on } \Gamma_D\}$. The weak form is: find $u \in V$ such that $$\int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx = \int_\Omega f\,v\,dx + \int_{\Gamma_N} g\,v\,dS \quad \forall\, v \in V.$$ The Dirichlet condition is enforced as an essential condition on the space $V$; the Neumann condition enters the right-hand side naturally.

Example — 1D BVP

Consider $-u'' = f$ on $(0,1)$, $u(0) = 0$, $u'(1) = g$. Multiplying by $v \in H^1_0(0,1)$ (wait — here we only have $u(0)=0$, so $V = \{v \in H^1(0,1): v(0)=0\}$) and integrating by parts: $$\int_0^1 u'v'\,dx = \int_0^1 fv\,dx + g\,v(1) \quad \forall\,v \in V.$$ The Neumann data $g$ enters as a point load at $x=1$.
