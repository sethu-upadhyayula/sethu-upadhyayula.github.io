1.2 Function Spaces for BVPs

The rigorous analysis of the finite element method rests on the theory of Sobolev spaces. These spaces capture precisely the regularity needed for the weak formulation: square-integrable functions whose weak derivatives are also square-integrable.

The Space $L^2(\Omega)$

Definition — $L^2(\Omega)$

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a bounded domain. The space $$L^2(\Omega) = \left\{ u : \Omega \to \mathbb{R} \;\Big|\; \int_\Omega u^2\,dx < \infty \right\}$$ (where functions equal almost everywhere are identified) is a Hilbert space with inner product and norm $$(u,v)_{L^2} = \int_\Omega u\,v\,dx, \qquad \|u\|_{L^2} = \left(\int_\Omega u^2\,dx\right)^{1/2}.$$

The Sobolev Space $H^1(\Omega)$

Definition — Weak Derivative

A function $g \in L^2(\Omega)$ is the weak partial derivative $\partial u / \partial x_i$ of $u \in L^2(\Omega)$ if $$\int_\Omega g\,\varphi\,dx = -\int_\Omega u\,\frac{\partial\varphi}{\partial x_i}\,dx \quad \forall\,\varphi \in C^\infty_0(\Omega).$$ This generalises the classical derivative via integration by parts without requiring pointwise differentiability.

Definition — $H^1(\Omega)$

The first-order Sobolev space is $$H^1(\Omega) = \{ u \in L^2(\Omega) : \nabla u \in L^2(\Omega)^d \},$$ equipped with the inner product and norm $$(u,v)_{H^1} = \int_\Omega uv\,dx + \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx, \qquad \|u\|_{H^1}^2 = \|u\|_{L^2}^2 + \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2.$$ The seminorm $|u|_{H^1} = \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}$ measures only the gradient.

The Space $H^1_0(\Omega)$ and the Trace

Definition — $H^1_0(\Omega)$

The space $H^1_0(\Omega)$ is the closure of $C^\infty_0(\Omega)$ (smooth functions compactly supported in $\Omega$) in the $H^1$ norm: $$H^1_0(\Omega) = \overline{C^\infty_0(\Omega)}^{\|\cdot\|_{H^1}}.$$ Equivalently, by the trace theorem, $H^1_0(\Omega) = \{ u \in H^1(\Omega) : \gamma_0 u = 0 \text{ on } \partial\Omega \}$, where $\gamma_0: H^1(\Omega) \to L^2(\partial\Omega)$ is the trace (boundary restriction) operator.

Remark — Why $H^1_0$?

Functions in $H^1_0(\Omega)$ satisfy homogeneous Dirichlet conditions in the sense of traces. This is the correct space for the variational formulation of $-\Delta u = f$ with $u|_{\partial\Omega} = 0$: both the solution $u$ and the test functions $v$ live in $H^1_0(\Omega)$.

The Poincaré Inequality

Theorem — Poincaré Inequality

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a bounded domain. There exists a constant $C_P = C_P(\Omega) > 0$ such that $$\|u\|_{L^2(\Omega)} \leq C_P \|\nabla u\|_{L^2(\Omega)} \quad \forall\, u \in H^1_0(\Omega).$$ The constant $C_P$ depends only on $\Omega$ (specifically, on its diameter in one direction). For a domain of diameter $D$, one can take $C_P = D/\pi$.

Remark — Consequence: Equivalent Norm

The Poincaré inequality implies that on $H^1_0(\Omega)$, the seminorm $|\cdot|_{H^1} = \|\nabla\cdot\|_{L^2}$ is in fact a norm equivalent to $\|\cdot\|_{H^1}$: $$\|\nabla u\|_{L^2} \leq \|u\|_{H^1} \leq \sqrt{1 + C_P^2}\,\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}.$$ This allows us to use $\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}$ as the energy norm on $H^1_0(\Omega)$.

Coercivity and Continuity of $a(\cdot,\cdot)$

The bilinear form $a(u,v) = \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx$ has two key properties that ensure the Lax-Milgram theorem applies.

Proposition — Coercivity

For all $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$: $$a(u,u) = \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2 \geq \frac{1}{1+C_P^2}\|u\|_{H^1}^2.$$ Setting $\alpha = (1+C_P^2)^{-1} > 0$, we have $a(u,u) \geq \alpha \|u\|_{H^1}^2$.

Proposition — Continuity (Boundedness)

For all $u, v \in H^1_0(\Omega)$, by the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality: $$|a(u,v)| = \left|\int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx\right| \leq \|\nabla u\|_{L^2}\|\nabla v\|_{L^2} \leq \|u\|_{H^1}\|v\|_{H^1}.$$ Thus $a$ is continuous with continuity constant $M = 1$.

Theorem — Well-Posedness via Lax-Milgram

Since $a$ is coercive and continuous on $H^1_0(\Omega)$, and $\ell(v) = \int_\Omega fv\,dx$ is a bounded linear functional ($|\ell(v)| \leq \|f\|_{L^2}\|v\|_{L^2} \leq \|f\|_{L^2}C_P\|v\|_{H^1}$ by Cauchy-Schwarz and Poincaré), the Lax-Milgram theorem guarantees existence and uniqueness of $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ satisfying $a(u,v) = \ell(v)$ for all $v \in H^1_0(\Omega)$, with stability estimate $\|u\|_{H^1} \leq \|f\|_{L^2} C_P / \alpha$.

Higher-Order Sobolev Spaces

Definition — $H^k(\Omega)$

For integer $k \geq 0$, the Sobolev space $H^k(\Omega)$ consists of functions $u \in L^2(\Omega)$ whose weak partial derivatives of all orders $|\alpha| \leq k$ belong to $L^2(\Omega)$: $$H^k(\Omega) = \{ u \in L^2(\Omega) : D^\alpha u \in L^2(\Omega) \;\forall\, |\alpha| \leq k \},$$ with norm $\|u\|_{H^k}^2 = \sum_{|\alpha|\leq k}\|D^\alpha u\|_{L^2}^2$ and seminorm $|u|_{H^k}^2 = \sum_{|\alpha|=k}\|D^\alpha u\|_{L^2}^2$. The spaces satisfy $H^k \hookrightarrow H^{k-1} \hookrightarrow \cdots \hookrightarrow L^2$, with all inclusions continuous and dense.
