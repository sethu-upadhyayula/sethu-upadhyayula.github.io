6.2 Elasticity

Linear elasticity models the small deformation of a solid body under applied loads. The governing PDE is a vector-valued elliptic system, and the finite element formulation extends the scalar Poisson framework to a vector unknown with a coupled bilinear form involving the symmetric strain tensor.

Kinematics: Strain and Displacement

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain (the reference configuration). The unknown is the displacement field $\mathbf{u}: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^2$. The linearised strain tensor is the symmetric part of the displacement gradient: $$\varepsilon_{ij}(\mathbf{u}) = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}\right), \qquad i,j \in \{1,2\}.$$ In matrix form: $$\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}) = \begin{pmatrix} \partial_1 u_1 & \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_2 u_1 + \partial_1 u_2) \\ \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_1 u_2 + \partial_2 u_1) & \partial_2 u_2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

Constitutive Law: Hooke's Law

An isotropic linearly elastic material obeys Hooke's law: the Cauchy stress tensor $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is a linear function of the strain $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$: $$\sigma_{ij} = 2\mu\,\varepsilon_{ij} + \lambda\,(\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})\,\delta_{ij} = 2\mu\,\varepsilon_{ij} + \lambda\,(\varepsilon_{11}+\varepsilon_{22})\,\delta_{ij},$$ where $\mu > 0$ and $\lambda \geq 0$ are the Lamé constants. They are related to the more physical Young's modulus $E > 0$ and Poisson's ratio $\nu \in [0, 1/2)$ by $$\mu = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}, \qquad \lambda = \frac{E\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}.$$ Note that $\lambda \to \infty$ as $\nu \to 1/2$ (incompressible limit).

Equilibrium Equation and Weak Form

Definition — Strong Form of Linear Elasticity

Given body force $\mathbf{f} \in L^2(\Omega;\mathbb{R}^2)$, find $\mathbf{u}: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^2$ satisfying $$-\operatorname{div}\boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{f} \quad \text{in } \Omega, \qquad \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{on } \partial\Omega.$$ Componentwise: $-\sum_j \partial_j \sigma_{ij}(\mathbf{u}) = f_i$ for $i = 1, 2$.

Multiply by a test function $\mathbf{v} \in H^1_0(\Omega;\mathbb{R}^2)$ (each component in $H^1_0(\Omega)$), integrate, and apply integration by parts. Using the symmetry of $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ and the identity $\boldsymbol{\sigma}:\nabla\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\sigma}:\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})$ (since $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ is symmetric):

Definition — Weak Form of Linear Elasticity

Let $V = H^1_0(\Omega;\mathbb{R}^2)$. Find $\mathbf{u} \in V$ such that $$a(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \ell(\mathbf{v}) \quad \forall\,\mathbf{v} \in V,$$ where $$a(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = \int_\Omega \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u}) : \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})\,dx = \int_\Omega \bigl(2\mu\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v}) + \lambda\,(\operatorname{div}\mathbf{u})(\operatorname{div}\mathbf{v})\bigr)\,dx,$$ $$\ell(\mathbf{v}) = \int_\Omega \mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{v}\,dx.$$ Here $\boldsymbol{A}:\boldsymbol{B} = \sum_{i,j} A_{ij}B_{ij}$ denotes the Frobenius inner product.

Coercivity of $a$ on $V$ follows from Korn's inequality: there exists $c_K > 0$ such that $\|\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v})\|_{L^2}^2 \geq c_K \|\mathbf{v}\|_{H^1}^2$ for all $\mathbf{v} \in V$, so $a(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}) \geq 2\mu c_K \|\mathbf{v}\|_{H^1}^2$.

FEM Discretisation: Vector $P_1$ Elements

For the scalar Poisson problem, a single degree of freedom (DOF) is placed at each node. For elasticity, we place 2 DOFs per node — one for each displacement component. If the mesh has $N_{\mathrm{int}}$ interior nodes, the global system has $2N_{\mathrm{int}}$ unknowns and a $2N_{\mathrm{int}} \times 2N_{\mathrm{int}}$ stiffness matrix. The structure of the local stiffness matrix on element $K$ is a $6 \times 6$ block (three nodes, two DOFs each) with entries $$K^K_{(i,\alpha),(j,\beta)} = \int_K \sigma_{\alpha\gamma}(\varphi_i \mathbf{e}_\alpha) \varepsilon_{\gamma\beta}(\varphi_j \mathbf{e}_\beta)\,dx,$$ which can be computed exactly since $P_1$ basis functions have constant gradients.

Volumetric Locking

Remark — Locking for Nearly Incompressible Materials

As $\nu \to 1/2$ (equivalently $\lambda \to \infty$), the constraint $\operatorname{div}\mathbf{u} \approx 0$ becomes increasingly dominant in the energy. For $P_1$ elements, the discrete space $V_h$ cannot adequately represent divergence-free fields — the constraint is over-enforced, and the FEM solution $\mathbf{u}_h \to \mathbf{0}$ as $\lambda \to \infty$ regardless of the forcing $\mathbf{f}$. This spurious stiffening is called volumetric locking.

Quantitatively, Céa's lemma gives $\|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_h\|_{H^1} \leq (M/\alpha)\min_{v_h}\|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}_h\|_{H^1}$ where now $M/\alpha \sim \lambda/\mu \to \infty$ for $P_1$ elements applied to nearly-incompressible problems. Standard error estimates deteriorate or fail entirely.

Remedies for Locking

Remedies for Volumetric Locking
  1. Mixed finite elements. Introduce the pressure $p = -\lambda\,\operatorname{div}\mathbf{u}$ as an independent variable. The mixed formulation: find $(\mathbf{u}_h, p_h) \in V_h \times Q_h$ satisfying $$2\mu\int_\Omega \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}_h):\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{v}_h)\,dx - \int_\Omega p_h\,\operatorname{div}\mathbf{v}_h\,dx = \ell(\mathbf{v}_h),$$ $$\int_\Omega q_h\,\operatorname{div}\mathbf{u}_h\,dx + \frac{1}{\lambda}\int_\Omega p_h q_h\,dx = 0.$$ Stability requires the pair $(V_h, Q_h)$ to satisfy the Ladyzhenskaya–Babuška–Brezzi (LBB) inf-sup condition.
  2. Selective/reduced integration. Integrate the volumetric part $\lambda(\operatorname{div}\mathbf{u})(\operatorname{div}\mathbf{v})$ with a lower-order quadrature rule (e.g., one-point quadrature on quadrilateral elements), which effectively under-constrains the incompressibility condition and alleviates locking.
  3. Higher-order elements. Using $P_2$ or higher-degree elements can reduce locking, though for severe incompressibility ($\nu$ very close to $1/2$) mixed methods remain preferable.
