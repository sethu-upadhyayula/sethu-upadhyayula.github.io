4.3 Solvers for Linear Systems

After assembly and boundary condition enforcement, we must solve $K\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f}$ where $K \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ is sparse and symmetric positive definite. The choice of solver — direct or iterative — depends critically on the problem size $N$, the spatial dimension $d$, and the available computational resources.

Direct Methods: Cholesky Factorisation

Definition — Cholesky Factorisation

Since $K$ is SPD, it admits a unique factorisation $K = LL^\top$ where $L$ is lower triangular with positive diagonal entries. The system is then solved in two triangular sweeps: $$L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{f} \;\text{(forward substitution)}, \qquad L^\top\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{y} \;\text{(back substitution)}.$$ Each sweep costs $O(N \cdot \text{bw})$ where $\text{bw}$ is the bandwidth of $K$.

Proposition — Complexity of Direct Methods

For a quasi-uniform mesh in $\mathbb{R}^d$ with $N$ DOFs, a bandwidth-optimal ordering (e.g.\ Cuthill–McKee) gives:

  • $d = 1$: bandwidth $O(1)$; Cholesky costs $O(N)$ flops and $O(N)$ storage.
  • $d = 2$: bandwidth $O(N^{1/2})$; Cholesky costs $O(N^{3/2})$ flops and $O(N^{3/2})$ fill-in storage.
  • $d = 3$: bandwidth $O(N^{2/3})$; Cholesky costs $O(N^2)$ flops and $O(N^{4/3})$ fill-in storage.
Direct methods are highly reliable but become expensive for large 3D problems.

Iterative Methods: Conjugate Gradient

Definition — Conjugate Gradient Method

The Conjugate Gradient (CG) method is the optimal Krylov subspace method for SPD systems. Starting from initial guess $\mathbf{u}^0$, it generates iterates $\mathbf{u}^k$ minimising the energy error $\|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}^k\|_K^2 = (\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{u}^k)^\top K(\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{u}^k)$ over the Krylov subspace $\mathcal{K}_k(K, \mathbf{r}^0) = \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf{r}^0, K\mathbf{r}^0, \ldots, K^{k-1}\mathbf{r}^0\}$, where $\mathbf{r}^0 = \mathbf{f} - K\mathbf{u}^0$. Each iteration requires one matrix-vector product $K\mathbf{p}$ (cost $O(N)$ for sparse $K$) and a few vector operations.

Theorem — CG Convergence

Let $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}(K)/\lambda_{\min}(K)$ be the spectral condition number of $K$. After $k$ CG iterations: $$\frac{\|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}^k\|_K}{\|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}^0\|_K} \leq 2\left(\frac{\sqrt\kappa - 1}{\sqrt\kappa + 1}\right)^k.$$ To reduce the error by a factor $\varepsilon$, one needs $k = O(\sqrt\kappa \log(1/\varepsilon))$ iterations. For the Poisson equation on a quasi-uniform mesh, $\kappa(K) = O(h^{-2})$, so CG without preconditioning requires $O(h^{-1})$ iterations — giving $O(N^{3/2})$ total operations in 2D, comparable to direct methods.

Preconditioning

Definition — Preconditioned CG (PCG)

A preconditioner $P \approx K$ is an SPD matrix such that $P^{-1}K$ has a much smaller condition number than $K$. Preconditioned CG solves the equivalent system $P^{-1}K\mathbf{u} = P^{-1}\mathbf{f}$ without forming $P^{-1}$ explicitly; each iteration applies $P^{-1}$ once (the preconditioning step) and multiplies by $K$ once. Common choices:

  • Diagonal (Jacobi): $P = \operatorname{diag}(K)$; cheap ($O(N)$) but reduces $\kappa$ by at most a constant for FEM matrices.
  • Incomplete Cholesky (IC/ICCG): $K \approx \tilde L\tilde L^\top$ with fill-in restricted to the sparsity pattern of $K$; reduces $\kappa$ to $O(h^{-1})$, giving $O(N^{5/4})$ total operations in 2D.
  • Algebraic Multigrid (AMG): constructs a hierarchy of coarser problems automatically from $K$; achieves $\kappa(P^{-1}K) = O(1)$ and $O(N)$ total operations — optimal complexity.
Definition — Geometric Multigrid

Geometric multigrid exploits the mesh hierarchy explicitly: a sequence of nested meshes $\mathcal{T}_{h_0} \supset \mathcal{T}_{h_1} \supset \cdots \supset \mathcal{T}_{h_L}$ yields coarser spaces $V_{h_0} \subset V_{h_1} \subset \cdots$. The multigrid V-cycle alternates between smoothing (e.g.\ Gauss–Seidel) on fine grids and exact (or recursive) coarse-grid correction. Each V-cycle costs $O(N)$, and convergence is $\kappa(P^{-1}K) = O(1)$, independent of $h$.
