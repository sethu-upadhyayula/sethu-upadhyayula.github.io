Chapter 3: Expectation

Expectation is the central summary of a random variable's distribution: it gives the long-run average value under repeated sampling. Beyond the mean, we study variance and covariance as measures of spread and co-variation, and moment generating functions as analytical tools for computing moments and identifying distributions.

Chapter Overview

3.1 — Expected Value

The expected value $E[X]$ is defined as a sum (discrete) or integral (continuous) and satisfies linearity. The Law of the Unconscious Statistician allows us to compute $E[g(X)]$ directly from the distribution of $X$. We also cover the tail formula and Jensen's inequality.

3.2 — Variance and Covariance

Variance measures the spread of a distribution around its mean; covariance and correlation measure the linear relationship between two random variables. Chebyshev's inequality provides a universal bound on tail probabilities in terms of variance.

3.3 — Moment Generating Functions

The moment generating function (MGF) $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ encodes all moments of $X$ via differentiation. When it exists in a neighbourhood of zero, it uniquely determines the distribution. The MGF of a sum of independent random variables is the product of individual MGFs.
