3.2 Variance and Covariance

Variance measures the typical squared deviation of a random variable from its mean. Covariance and correlation measure the linear dependence between two random variables.

Definition 3.2.1 — Variance

The variance of $X$ with mean $\mu = E[X]$ is $$\text{Var}(X) = E\!\left[(X - \mu)^2\right] = E[X^2] - \mu^2.$$ The standard deviation is $\sigma_X = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$.

The computational formula $E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$ follows by expanding $(X-\mu)^2$ and using linearity. Variance is always non-negative; $\text{Var}(X) = 0$ iff $X$ is constant a.s.

Theorem 3.2.2 — Variance of a Linear Function

For constants $a, b$: $$\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\, \text{Var}(X).$$ Adding a constant shifts the distribution but does not change its spread; scaling by $a$ scales the standard deviation by $|a|$.

Definition 3.2.3 — Covariance

The covariance of $X$ and $Y$ is $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E\!\left[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])\right] = E[XY] - E[X]\,E[Y].$$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$ (the converse need not hold). Covariance is bilinear and symmetric.

Theorem 3.2.4 — Variance of a Sum

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\,\text{Cov}(X, Y).$$ For independent $X, Y$: $\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$. More generally, $\text{Var}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_i \text{Var}(X_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$.

Definition 3.2.5 — Correlation

The Pearson correlation coefficient is $$\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}.$$

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, $\rho \in [-1, 1]$. $\rho = 1$ iff $Y = aX + b$ a.s. for some $a > 0$; $\rho = -1$ iff $a < 0$. Correlation is dimensionless and invariant under positive linear transformations.

Theorem 3.2.6 — Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

For random variables $X, Y$ with finite second moments: $$|E[XY]|^2 \le E[X^2]\, E[Y^2], \quad \text{i.e.,}\quad |\text{Cov}(X,Y)| \le \sigma_X \sigma_Y.$$

Theorem 3.2.7 — Chebyshev's Inequality

For a random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$, and for any $k > 0$: $$P(|X - \mu| \ge k\sigma) \le \frac{1}{k^2}.$$

Chebyshev requires no assumption on the distribution; it is used to prove the Weak Law of Large Numbers. At least $1 - 1/k^2$ of the probability mass lies within $k$ standard deviations of the mean.

Proof of Chebyshev's Inequality

By Markov's inequality applied to $(X - \mu)^2$: $$P(|X-\mu| \ge k\sigma) = P\!\left((X-\mu)^2 \ge k^2\sigma^2\right) \le \frac{E[(X-\mu)^2]}{k^2\sigma^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2\sigma^2} = \frac{1}{k^2}. \quad\square$$
