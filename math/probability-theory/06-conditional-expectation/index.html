Chapter 6: Conditional Expectation

Conditional expectation is the central concept linking probability theory to statistics, Bayesian inference, and stochastic processes. The measure-theoretic definition — as an orthogonal projection in $L^2$ — unifies the discrete and continuous cases and provides a clean foundation for martingale theory.

Chapter Overview

6.1 — Conditional Expectation

$E[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is defined as the unique $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variable $Y$ satisfying $\int_A Y\,dP = \int_A X\,dP$ for all $A \in \mathcal{G}$. Existence follows from the Radon-Nikodym theorem. Key properties include the tower property, linearity, monotonicity, and Jensen's inequality for conditional expectations. Geometrically, $E[X\mid\mathcal{G}]$ is the orthogonal projection of $X$ onto the closed subspace $L^2(\mathcal{G})$.

6.2 — Conditional Distributions

A regular conditional distribution (RCD) is a version of conditional expectation that is simultaneously a probability measure. For Polish spaces, RCDs always exist. Conditional densities $f_{Y|X}(y|x) = f_{X,Y}(x,y)/f_X(x)$ provide a concrete calculus for computing conditional expectations, and Bayes' theorem emerges as a special case of disintegration of measures.
