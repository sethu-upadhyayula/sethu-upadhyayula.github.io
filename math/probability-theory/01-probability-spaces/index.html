Chapter 1: Probability Spaces

The modern mathematical foundation of probability rests on measure theory, developed by Kolmogorov in 1933. Rather than relying on vague intuitions about "equally likely" outcomes, we define probability rigorously as a measure on a structured collection of sets. This chapter builds the three pillars of the framework: sample spaces, sigma-algebras, and probability measures.

The measure-theoretic approach unifies discrete and continuous probability under one roof and provides the tools needed to handle limit theorems, conditional expectations, and stochastic processes with full mathematical rigour.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Sample Spaces and Events

The sample space $\Omega$ is the set of all possible outcomes. Events are measurable subsets of $\Omega$, organised into a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{F}$. We discuss why not every subset can be assigned a probability (the Vitali set), motivating the need for the $\sigma$-algebra framework.

1.2 — Probability Measures

A probability measure $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ satisfies $P(\Omega) = 1$ and countable additivity. The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is called a probability space. We derive key consequences: complementation, monotonicity, continuity, and inclusion-exclusion.

1.3 — Conditional Probability and Independence

Conditioning updates probabilities given partial information. We prove Bayes' formula and the law of total probability. Independence — both pairwise and mutual — is defined precisely, and we see that pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence.
