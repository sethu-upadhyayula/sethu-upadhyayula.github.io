1.3 Conditional Probability and Independence

Conditional probability formalises the notion of updating probabilities when we learn that some event has occurred. Independence captures the idea that knowing one event gives no information about another.

Definition 1.3.1 - Conditional Probability

If $P(B) > 0$, the conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ is: $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

Intuitively, we restrict the sample space to $B$ and rescale so the total probability is 1.

Theorem 1.3.2 - Chain Rule

For events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ with $P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1}) > 0$: $$P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_1) \cdot P(A_2|A_1) \cdot P(A_3|A_1 \cap A_2) \cdots P(A_n | A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1})$$

Theorem 1.3.3 - Law of Total Probability

If $\{B_i\}_{i=1}^n$ is a partition of $\Omega$ with $P(B_i) > 0$ for all $i$, then: $$P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A \mid B_i) P(B_i)$$

This allows us to compute $P(A)$ by conditioning on a partition.

Theorem 1.3.4 - Bayes' Formula

If $\{B_i\}$ partitions $\Omega$ and $P(A) > 0$: $$P(B_i \mid A) = \frac{P(A \mid B_i) P(B_i)}{\sum_j P(A \mid B_j) P(B_j)}$$

$P(B_i)$ is the prior probability; $P(B_i|A)$ is the posterior probability after observing $A$.

Example 1.3.5 - Medical Test

A disease has prevalence 1%. A test has sensitivity $P(\text{pos}|\text{disease}) = 0.99$ and specificity $P(\text{neg}|\text{no disease}) = 0.95$. The false positive rate is $5\%$. What is the probability of disease given a positive test?

$$P(\text{disease}|\text{pos}) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.01}{0.99 \times 0.01 + 0.05 \times 0.99} \approx \frac{0.0099}{0.0099 + 0.0495} \approx 0.167$$

Despite the accurate test, only about 17% of positive testers actually have the disease - illustrating the base rate fallacy.

Independence

Definition 1.3.6 - Independence of Events

Events $A$ and $B$ are independent if: $$P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)$$

Equivalently (when $P(B)>0$): $P(A|B) = P(A)$, so knowing $B$ does not change the probability of $A$.

Definition 1.3.7 - Mutual Independence

Events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ are mutually independent if for every subset $S \subseteq \{1,\ldots,n\}$: $$P\!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right) = \prod_{i \in S} P(A_i)$$

Pairwise independence (only requiring $P(A_i \cap A_j) = P(A_i)P(A_j)$ for $i \ne j$) does not imply mutual independence.

Example 1.3.8 - Pairwise but not Mutually Independent

Toss two fair coins. Let $A = \{\text{1st is H}\}$, $B = \{\text{2nd is H}\}$, $C = \{\text{both same}\}$. Then $P(A) = P(B) = P(C) = 1/2$ and $P(A\cap B) = P(A\cap C) = P(B\cap C) = 1/4$, so pairwise independent. But $P(A\cap B\cap C) = 1/4 \ne 1/8 = P(A)P(B)P(C)$.

Mutual independence requires checking all subsets - pairwise independence is strictly weaker.

Definition 1.3.9 - Conditional Independence

$A$ and $B$ are conditionally independent given $C$ (with $P(C) > 0$) if: $$P(A \cap B \mid C) = P(A \mid C) P(B \mid C)$$
