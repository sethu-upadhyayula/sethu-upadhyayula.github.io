4.1 Discrete Distributions

This section provides a systematic reference for the key discrete probability distributions, including their PMFs, means, variances, MGFs, and relationships to one another.

Bernoulli$(p)$

PMF: $p(1) = p$, $p(0) = 1-p$, $p \in [0,1]$.

Mean: $p$. Variance: $p(1-p)$. MGF: $1-p+pe^t$.

Binomial$(n, p)$

PMF: $p(k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$, $k = 0,\ldots,n$.

Mean: $np$. Variance: $np(1-p)$. MGF: $(1-p+pe^t)^n$.

Relation: Sum of $n$ independent Bernoulli$(p)$. If $X \sim \text{Bin}(m,p)$ and $Y \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$ are independent, then $X+Y \sim \text{Bin}(m+n,p)$.

Geometric$(p)$

PMF: $p(k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p$, $k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

Mean: $1/p$. Variance: $(1-p)/p^2$. MGF: $pe^t/(1-(1-p)e^t)$ for $t < -\log(1-p)$.

Memoryless: $P(X > m+n \mid X > m) = P(X > n)$. The Geometric distribution is the unique discrete memoryless distribution.

Negative Binomial$(r, p)$

PMF: $p(k) = \binom{k-1}{r-1}p^r(1-p)^{k-r}$, $k = r, r+1, \ldots$ (number of trials to get $r$ successes).

Mean: $r/p$. Variance: $r(1-p)/p^2$. MGF: $(pe^t/(1-(1-p)e^t))^r$.

Relation: Sum of $r$ independent Geometric$(p)$ random variables. Negative Binomial$(1,p)$ = Geometric$(p)$.

Poisson$(\lambda)$

PMF: $p(k) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k / k!$, $k = 0,1,2,\ldots$

Mean: $\lambda$. Variance: $\lambda$. MGF: $e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$.

Relations: Sum of independent Poisson$(\lambda_i)$ is Poisson$(\sum \lambda_i)$. Poisson approximation to Binomial: if $X \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$ with $n \to \infty$, $p \to 0$, $np \to \lambda$, then $X \xrightarrow{d} \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$. Rule of thumb: use approximation when $n \ge 20$ and $p \le 0.05$.

Relationships Between Discrete Distributions

Remark — Family Tree

The key relationships:

  • Bernoulli$(p)$ $\xrightarrow{\text{sum of }n}$ Binomial$(n,p)$ $\xrightarrow{n\to\infty,\,np\to\lambda}$ Poisson$(\lambda)$
  • Geometric$(p)$ $\xrightarrow{\text{sum of }r}$ Negative Binomial$(r,p)$
  • Binomial$(n,p)|X+Y=n$ $\sim$ Hypergeometric (sampling without replacement)

The Poisson distribution also arises as the distribution of counts in a Poisson process: the number of events in a fixed time interval when events occur at rate $\lambda$ independently.
