4.3 Multivariate Distributions

When studying multiple random variables simultaneously, we need the joint distribution. The key concepts are joint PDF/PMF, marginalisation, independence, and conditional distributions.

Definition 4.3.1 - Joint PDF and PMF

For a pair $(X, Y)$:

  • Discrete: joint PMF $p(x,y) = P(X=x, Y=y)$; satisfies $\sum_x \sum_y p(x,y) = 1$.
  • Continuous: joint PDF $f(x,y) \ge 0$ with $\int\!\int f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = 1$ and $P((X,Y)\in A) = \int\!\int_A f(x,y)\,dx\,dy$.

The joint distribution extends naturally to $n$ random variables.

Definition 4.3.2 - Marginal Distributions

The marginal distributions of $X$ and $Y$ are obtained by integrating (or summing) out the other variable: $$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x,y)\,dy, \qquad f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x,y)\,dx.$$ The marginals give the individual distributions of $X$ and $Y$, but do not fully describe their joint behaviour.

Definition 4.3.3 - Independence

$X$ and $Y$ are independent if and only if their joint density (or PMF) factorises: $$f(x,y) = f_X(x)\, f_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y.$$ Equivalently, $P(X \in A, Y \in B) = P(X \in A)\, P(Y \in B)$ for all Borel sets $A, B$.

Definition 4.3.4 - Conditional Distribution

The conditional PDF of $Y$ given $X = x$ is $$f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) = \frac{f(x,y)}{f_X(x)}, \quad f_X(x) > 0.$$ If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, $f_{Y|X}(y|x) = f_Y(y)$: knowing $X$ gives no information about $Y$.

Multivariate Normal Distribution

Definition 4.3.5 - Multivariate Normal

A random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_d)^T$ follows the multivariate normal distribution $N(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ with mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and positive definite covariance matrix $\Sigma$ if its density is $$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T \Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right).$$

$\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$. The bivariate normal with correlation $\rho$ has $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}\sigma_1^2 & \rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 \\ \rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 & \sigma_2^2\end{pmatrix}$.

Theorem 4.3.6 - Properties of the Multivariate Normal
  • Marginals: Any sub-vector of a multivariate normal is multivariate normal. In particular, each $X_i \sim N(\mu_i, \Sigma_{ii})$.
  • Linear combinations: $\mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{X} \sim N(\mathbf{a}^T\boldsymbol{\mu},\, \mathbf{a}^T\Sigma\mathbf{a})$ for any $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^d$.
  • Conditionals: $X_2 | X_1 = x_1$ is normal (see §6.2 for the bivariate formula).
  • Independence $\Leftrightarrow$ uncorrelated: For jointly normal variables, $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = 0$ iff $X_i \perp X_j$.

The multivariate normal is entirely determined by its mean vector and covariance matrix.
