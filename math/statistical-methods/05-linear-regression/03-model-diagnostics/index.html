5.3 Model Diagnostics

The validity of regression inference rests on model assumptions — linearity, constant variance, independence, and normality of errors. Model diagnostics are the tools by which we probe whether these assumptions hold and identify observations that exert undue influence on results.

Residual Plots

Definition 5.3.1 — Residuals

The ordinary residuals are $\hat{\varepsilon}_i = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i$. The standardised residuals are $r_i = \hat{\varepsilon}_i / (\hat{\sigma}\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}})$ where $h_{ii} = [\mathbf{H}]_{ii}$ is the $i$-th diagonal of the hat matrix (the leverage).

A residuals vs.\ fitted plot should show no pattern (random scatter around zero) if the model is correctly specified. Curvature suggests a nonlinear relationship; a funnel shape suggests heteroskedasticity.

Definition 5.3.2 — Q-Q Plot and Normality Tests

A normal Q-Q plot of residuals plots the empirical quantiles of $\hat{\varepsilon}_i$ against the theoretical quantiles of $N(0,1)$. Deviations from the diagonal indicate departures from normality. The Shapiro-Wilk test provides a formal hypothesis test: $H_0:\varepsilon \sim N$, with high power for small to moderate $n$. The test statistic $W$ is the squared correlation between the ordered residuals and normal order statistics; small $W$ rejects normality.

Heteroskedasticity

Definition 5.3.3 — Breusch-Pagan Test

The Breusch-Pagan test detects heteroskedasticity by regressing squared residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_i^2$ on the predictors. Under $H_0$ (homoskedasticity), the test statistic $nR^2_{\hat{\varepsilon}^2} \stackrel{d}{\to} \chi^2_p$. If rejected, weighted least squares (WLS) with weights $w_i = 1/\hat{\sigma}_i^2$ (estimated from the auxiliary regression) restores efficiency.

Autocorrelation

Definition 5.3.4 — Durbin-Watson Test

For time-series data, the Durbin-Watson statistic $$DW = \frac{\sum_{i=2}^n(\hat{\varepsilon}_i - \hat{\varepsilon}_{i-1})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{\varepsilon}_i^2} \approx 2(1 - \hat{\rho}),$$ where $\hat{\rho}$ is the lag-1 autocorrelation of residuals. Values near 2 indicate no autocorrelation; values near 0 indicate positive autocorrelation (common in time series). Tabulated critical values $d_L, d_U$ determine significance.

Influential Observations

Definition 5.3.5 — Leverage

The leverage of observation $i$ is $h_{ii} = [\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top]_{ii} \in [1/n, 1]$. High leverage observations have predictor values far from the centroid $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$ and can strongly influence $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$. A common threshold is $h_{ii} > 2(p+1)/n$.

Definition 5.3.6 — Cook's Distance

Cook's distance measures the aggregate influence of observation $i$ on all fitted values: $$D_i = \frac{(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} - \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{(i)})^\top \mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} - \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{(i)})}{(p+1)\hat{\sigma}^2} = \frac{r_i^2}{p+1}\cdot\frac{h_{ii}}{1-h_{ii}},$$ where $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}_{(i)}$ is the OLS estimator with observation $i$ deleted. $D_i > 1$ is often taken as an indicator of strong influence. Observations with both large residuals and high leverage have the greatest potential to distort results.

Box-Cox Transformation

Definition 5.3.7 — Box-Cox Transformation

The Box-Cox power transformation $$Y^{(\lambda)} = \begin{cases} (Y^\lambda - 1)/\lambda & \lambda \ne 0 \\ \log Y & \lambda = 0 \end{cases}$$ can simultaneously stabilise variance and achieve approximate normality of residuals. The optimal $\lambda$ is estimated by maximum likelihood. Special cases include $\lambda = 1$ (no transformation), $\lambda = 0$ (log transform), and $\lambda = -1$ (reciprocal transform). After transformation, inference is conducted on $Y^{(\lambda)}$ and results must be back-transformed with care.
