4.3 Common Tests

This section catalogues the most frequently applied parametric hypothesis tests, deriving their test statistics from first principles and stating the exact or asymptotic null distributions needed for inference.

$t$-Tests for Means

Theorem 4.3.1 — One-Sample $t$-Test

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu,\sigma^2)$, $\sigma$ unknown. Test $H_0:\mu=\mu_0$. Test statistic: $$T = \frac{\bar{X}-\mu_0}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1} \text{ under } H_0.$$

Reject $H_0:\mu\ne\mu_0$ at level $\alpha$ if $|T| > t_{n-1,\alpha/2}$; for $H_1:\mu>\mu_0$ if $T > t_{n-1,\alpha}$.

Theorem 4.3.2 — Two-Sample $t$-Test (Equal Variances)

Let $X_i \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu_X,\sigma^2)$, $i=1,\ldots,m$, and $Y_j \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu_Y,\sigma^2)$, $j=1,\ldots,n$, independent. Test $H_0:\mu_X=\mu_Y$. With pooled variance $S_p^2 = \frac{(m-1)S_X^2+(n-1)S_Y^2}{m+n-2}$: $$T = \frac{\bar{X}-\bar{Y}}{S_p\sqrt{1/m+1/n}} \sim t_{m+n-2} \text{ under } H_0.$$

Use Welch's $t$ (see Section 3.2) when variances may differ.

Theorem 4.3.3 — Paired $t$-Test

For paired observations $(X_i, Y_i)$ with differences $D_i = X_i - Y_i$, test $H_0:\mu_D = 0$ by applying the one-sample $t$-test to $D_1,\ldots,D_n$. This controls for within-subject variability and is more powerful than the two-sample test when the pairs are positively correlated.

Chi-Squared Tests

Theorem 4.3.4 — $\chi^2$ Goodness-of-Fit Test

Let $O_1,\ldots,O_k$ be observed frequencies in $k$ categories from $n$ observations, and $E_i = np_i^0$ the expected frequencies under $H_0:\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{p}^0$. The Pearson statistic $$X^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i} \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{k-1} \quad \text{under } H_0.$$

Reject $H_0$ if $X^2 > \chi^2_{k-1,\alpha}$. The approximation is adequate when all $E_i \ge 5$.

Theorem 4.3.5 — $\chi^2$ Test of Independence

For an $r \times c$ contingency table with observed cell frequencies $O_{ij}$ and row/column totals $R_i, C_j$, test $H_0:$ row and column variables are independent. Expected frequencies $E_{ij} = R_i C_j / n$. The statistic $$X^2 = \sum_{i=1}^r\sum_{j=1}^c \frac{(O_{ij}-E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}} \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(r-1)(c-1)} \quad \text{under } H_0.$$

The degrees of freedom $(r-1)(c-1)$ reflect the number of free cell probabilities after imposing marginal constraints.

$F$-Test and ANOVA

Theorem 4.3.6 — $F$-Test for Equality of Variances

Let $S_X^2$ and $S_Y^2$ be sample variances from independent normal samples of sizes $m$ and $n$. Test $H_0:\sigma_X^2 = \sigma_Y^2$. Under $H_0$: $$F = \frac{S_X^2}{S_Y^2} \sim F_{m-1,\,n-1}.$$

Reject for $F > F_{m-1,n-1,\alpha/2}$ or $F < F_{m-1,n-1,1-\alpha/2}$. This test is sensitive to non-normality; Levene's or Brown-Forsythe test is more robust in practice.

Theorem 4.3.7 — One-Way ANOVA $F$-Test

Suppose $k$ independent groups have observations $X_{ij} \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu_i, \sigma^2)$, $j=1,\ldots,n_i$. Test $H_0:\mu_1=\cdots=\mu_k$. Define $$\mathrm{SSB} = \sum_{i=1}^k n_i(\bar{X}_i - \bar{X})^2, \quad \mathrm{SSW} = \sum_{i=1}^k\sum_{j=1}^{n_i}(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_i)^2,$$ with $N = \sum n_i$. Then under $H_0$: $$F = \frac{\mathrm{SSB}/(k-1)}{\mathrm{SSW}/(N-k)} \sim F_{k-1,\,N-k}.$$

$\mathrm{SSB}/(k-1)$ (mean square between) estimates $\sigma^2$ plus a non-centrality term proportional to group mean differences; $\mathrm{SSW}/(N-k)$ (mean square within) is an unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$ regardless of $H_0$. Large $F$ values provide evidence against $H_0$.
