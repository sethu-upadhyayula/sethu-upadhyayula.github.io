4.1 Null and Alternative Hypotheses

The Neyman-Pearson framework casts every statistical test as a decision problem: on the basis of observed data, we choose between a null hypothesis $H_0$ and an alternative hypothesis $H_1$. The framework specifies how to build optimal decision rules that control error probabilities.

Definition 4.1.1 - Hypotheses

A statistical hypothesis is a claim about the distribution of the data, typically stated as a restriction on the parameter $\theta \in \Theta$. The null hypothesis $H_0: \theta \in \Theta_0$ is the default or baseline claim. The alternative hypothesis $H_1: \theta \in \Theta_1$ is the claim we seek evidence for, where $\Theta_0 \cap \Theta_1 = \emptyset$.

A hypothesis is simple if it specifies the distribution completely (e.g.\ $H_0:\theta = \theta_0$) and composite otherwise (e.g.\ $H_1:\theta > \theta_0$). Alternatives may be one-sided ($\theta > \theta_0$ or $\theta < \theta_0$) or two-sided ($\theta \ne \theta_0$).

Definition 4.1.2 - Test Statistic and Rejection Region

A test statistic $T = T(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ is a function of the data chosen to be sensitive to departures from $H_0$. The rejection region (critical region) $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of values of $T$ for which $H_0$ is rejected: $$\text{Reject } H_0 \iff T \in \mathcal{R}.$$

The critical value} $c$ is determined by the level $\alpha$ constraint: $P_{H_0}(T \in \mathcal{R}) \le \alpha$. For a two-sided $Z$-test, $\mathcal{R} = \{|Z| > z_{\alpha/2}\}$.

$p$-values

Definition 4.1.3 - $p$-value

The $p$-value is the probability, computed under $H_0$, of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one obtained: $$p = P_{H_0}(T \ge t_\mathrm{obs}) \quad \text{(one-sided, right)}.$$

We reject $H_0$ at level $\alpha$ if and only if $p \le \alpha$. The $p$-value is not the probability that $H_0$ is true; it is the probability of data at least as extreme as observed, assuming $H_0$ is true.

Level and Power

Definition 4.1.4 - Level $\alpha$ Test

A test has level $\alpha$ (or size at most $\alpha$) if $$\sup_{\theta \in \Theta_0} P_\theta(T \in \mathcal{R}) \le \alpha.$$ The power function is $\beta(\theta) = P_\theta(T \in \mathcal{R})$ for all $\theta \in \Theta$. A good test has $\beta(\theta) \approx \alpha$ for $\theta \in \Theta_0$ and $\beta(\theta) \to 1$ for $\theta$ far from $\Theta_0$.

The Neyman-Pearson Lemma

For testing a simple $H_0:\theta = \theta_0$ vs.\ simple $H_1:\theta = \theta_1$, the Neyman-Pearson lemma identifies the uniformly most powerful (UMP) test.

Theorem 4.1.5 - Neyman-Pearson Lemma

Let $f_0, f_1$ be the densities (or pmfs) of the data under $H_0$ and $H_1$ respectively. The likelihood ratio test that rejects $H_0$ when $$\Lambda(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{f_1(\mathbf{x})}{f_0(\mathbf{x})} > k$$ for some $k \ge 0$ chosen so that $P_{H_0}(\Lambda > k) = \alpha$ is the most powerful level-$\alpha$ test of $H_0$ vs.\ $H_1$.

That is, among all level-$\alpha$ tests, the likelihood ratio test maximises $\beta(\theta_1) = P_{\theta_1}(\text{reject } H_0)$. For composite one-sided alternatives in exponential families, the LRT is also UMP (uniformly most powerful).

Example 4.1.6 - One-Sample $Z$-Test

Suppose $X_1,\ldots,X_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known. Test $H_0:\mu = \mu_0$ vs.\ $H_1:\mu \ne \mu_0$ at level $\alpha$.

Test statistic: $Z = (\bar{X} - \mu_0)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n}) \sim N(0,1)$ under $H_0$.

Rejection region: $|Z| > z_{\alpha/2}$.

$p$-value: $p = 2P(Z > |z_\mathrm{obs}|)$ where $z_\mathrm{obs}$ is the observed value. This is equivalent to checking whether $\mu_0$ lies outside the $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval $\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.
