1.1 Measures of Location

Measures of location (or central tendency) indicate where the bulk of a dataset lies. Different measures are appropriate for different distributional shapes and data quality.

Definition 1.1.1 - Sample Mean

For observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, the sample mean is $$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i.$$

Theorem 1.1.2 - Mean Minimises Squared Deviations

$\bar{x} = \arg\min_{c \in \mathbb{R}} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - c)^2.$ Setting the derivative $-2\sum(x_i - c) = 0$ gives $c = \bar{x}$.

Definition 1.1.3 - Sample Median

Order the data as $x_{(1)} \le x_{(2)} \le \cdots \le x_{(n)}$. The sample median is $$\tilde{x} = \begin{cases} x_{((n+1)/2)} & n \text{ odd} \\ \frac{1}{2}\bigl(x_{(n/2)} + x_{(n/2+1)}\bigr) & n \text{ even.} \end{cases}$$

The median is robust to outliers; it minimises $\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i - c|$.

Definition 1.1.4 - Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value (for discrete data) or the peak of the density (for continuous data). A distribution may be unimodal, bimodal, or multimodal.

Definition 1.1.5 - Trimmed Mean

The $\alpha$-trimmed mean removes the $\lfloor\alpha n\rfloor$ smallest and $\lfloor\alpha n\rfloor$ largest observations and averages the remaining $n - 2\lfloor\alpha n\rfloor$ values. It balances efficiency (near mean for small $\alpha$) and robustness (near median for large $\alpha$).

Definition 1.1.6 - Weighted Mean

With non-negative weights $w_1, \ldots, w_n$ satisfying $\sum w_i = 1$: $$\bar{x}_w = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i x_i.$$ Used when observations have differing reliabilities or represent groups of different sizes.

Example 1.1.7 - Comparison

For data $\{2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9\}$: $\bar{x} = 5$, $\tilde{x} = 4.5$, mode $= 4$.

Replacing $9$ by $90$: $\bar{x}$ jumps to $15.625$ while $\tilde{x}$ remains $4.5$, illustrating the mean's sensitivity to outliers.
