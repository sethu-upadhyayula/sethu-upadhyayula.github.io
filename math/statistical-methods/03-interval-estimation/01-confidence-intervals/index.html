3.1 Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval augments a point estimate with a measure of uncertainty, yielding a random interval that contains the true parameter with a prescribed probability. Unlike a point estimator, a CI communicates the precision of estimation directly to the practitioner.

Definition 3.1.1 - Confidence Interval

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ be a random sample from a distribution depending on parameter $\theta$. Statistics $L = L(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ and $U = U(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ form a $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for $\theta$ if $$P_\theta(L \le \theta \le U) = 1 - \alpha \quad \text{for all } \theta,$$ where $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is the significance level and $1-\alpha$ is the confidence level. Common choices are $\alpha = 0.05$ (95% CI) and $\alpha = 0.01$ (99% CI).

Remark 3.1.2 - Frequentist Interpretation

The parameter $\theta$ is a fixed (unknown) constant, not a random variable. It is therefore incorrect to say "there is a 95% probability that $\theta$ lies in the observed interval $[l, u]$." Instead, the correct frequentist statement is: if the experiment were repeated indefinitely and a 95% CI constructed each time, then 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true $\theta$.

The randomness resides entirely in $L$ and $U$; once data are observed and $l, u$ are computed, the interval either does or does not contain $\theta$.

One-Sample CI for the Mean - Known Variance

Suppose $X_1,\ldots,X_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known. The standardised mean $$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1).$$ Let $z_{\alpha/2}$ denote the upper $\alpha/2$ quantile of $N(0,1)$, so $P(Z > z_{\alpha/2}) = \alpha/2$.

Theorem 3.1.3 - Z-Interval (Known $\sigma$)

A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI for $\mu$ is $$\left(\bar{X} - z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},\; \bar{X} + z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right).$$ The margin of error is $E = z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}$. The interval has length $2E$ which decreases as $\sqrt{n}$.

One-Sample CI for the Mean - Unknown Variance

When $\sigma^2$ is unknown it is replaced by the sample variance $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar{X})^2$. The standardised statistic now follows a $t$-distribution:

Theorem 3.1.4 - $t$-Interval (Unknown $\sigma$)

If $X_i \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma$ unknown, then $$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}.$$ A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI for $\mu$ is $$\left(\bar{X} - t_{n-1,\,\alpha/2}\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}},\; \bar{X} + t_{n-1,\,\alpha/2}\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}\right),$$

where $t_{n-1,\alpha/2}$ is the upper $\alpha/2$ quantile of the $t_{n-1}$ distribution. Because the $t$-distribution has heavier tails than $N(0,1)$, this interval is wider than the $Z$-interval, reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$.

CI for a Proportion

Let $X \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n,p)$ and $\hat{p} = X/n$. By the CLT, $\hat{p} \approx N(p, p(1-p)/n)$ for large $n$.

Proposition 3.1.5 - Wald Interval for $p$

An approximate $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI for $p$ is $$\hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}.$$ This is called the Wald interval. It performs poorly when $p$ is near 0 or 1 or $n$ is small. The Wilson (score) interval $$\frac{\hat{p} + \frac{z^2}{2n} \pm z\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n} + \frac{z^2}{4n^2}}}{1 + z^2/n},\quad z = z_{\alpha/2},$$ has superior coverage and is preferred in practice.

Worked Example

Example 3.1.6 - Blood Pressure Study

A clinician measures systolic blood pressure (mmHg) for $n = 25$ patients, obtaining $\bar{x} = 132.4$ and $s = 14.8$. Construct a 95% CI for the population mean $\mu$.

Solution. Since $\sigma$ is unknown and the data are approximately normal, use the $t$-interval with $n-1 = 24$ degrees of freedom. From $t$-tables, $t_{24, 0.025} = 2.064$.

Margin of error: $E = 2.064 \times 14.8/\sqrt{25} = 2.064 \times 2.96 = 6.11$.

The 95% CI is $(132.4 - 6.11,\; 132.4 + 6.11) = (126.3, 138.5)$ mmHg. We interpret this as: were the study repeated many times, 95% of such intervals would contain the true mean systolic pressure.

Remark 3.1.7 - Sample Size Determination

To achieve margin of error at most $E$ with confidence $1-\alpha$ (using the $Z$-interval), solve $z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma/\sqrt{n} \le E$ for $n$: $$n \ge \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2.$$ For proportions with $\sigma = \sqrt{p(1-p)} \le 1/2$, a conservative bound gives $n \ge z_{\alpha/2}^2/(4E^2)$.
