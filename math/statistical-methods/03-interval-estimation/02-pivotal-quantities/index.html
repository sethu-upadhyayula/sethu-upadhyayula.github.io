3.2 Pivotal Quantities

The pivotal quantity method provides a systematic, exact approach to constructing confidence intervals. By identifying a function of the data and the parameter whose distribution is completely known and free of all unknown parameters, we can directly invert probability statements into interval bounds.

Definition 3.2.1 — Pivotal Quantity

A function $Q = Q(X_1,\ldots,X_n;\theta)$ is a pivotal quantity (or pivot) if its distribution is completely determined — that is, it does not depend on $\theta$ or any other unknown parameter. Finding bounds $a, b$ such that $P(a \le Q \le b) = 1-\alpha$ and then solving $a \le Q \le b$ for $\theta$ yields an exact $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI.

The $t$-Pivot for the Mean

Theorem 3.2.2 — Student's $t$ Pivot

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ unknown. Then $$Q = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1},$$

where $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar{X})^2$. This holds exactly for normal data. The $t_{n-1}$ distribution has density proportional to $(1 + t^2/(n-1))^{-n/2}$ and converges to $N(0,1)$ as $n\to\infty$.

The $\chi^2$ Pivot for the Variance

Theorem 3.2.3 — Chi-Squared Pivot

Under the same normal model, $$Q = \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1},$$

and this pivot is independent of $\bar{X}$. Let $\chi^2_{n-1,\alpha/2}$ and $\chi^2_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}$ be the lower and upper $\alpha/2$ quantiles. Inverting $P\!\left(\chi^2_{n-1,\alpha/2} \le \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \le \chi^2_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}\right)=1-\alpha$ gives the exact CI for $\sigma^2$: $$\left(\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\chi^2_{n-1,1-\alpha/2}},\; \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\chi^2_{n-1,\alpha/2}}\right).$$

Two-Sample Confidence Intervals

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_m \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu_X,\sigma^2)$ and $Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu_Y,\sigma^2)$ be independent with a common variance $\sigma^2$.

Theorem 3.2.4 — Pooled Two-Sample $t$ CI

The pooled sample variance is $$S_p^2 = \frac{(m-1)S_X^2 + (n-1)S_Y^2}{m+n-2}.$$ Then $$T = \frac{(\bar{X}-\bar{Y})-(\mu_X-\mu_Y)}{S_p\sqrt{1/m+1/n}} \sim t_{m+n-2}.$$

A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI for $\mu_X - \mu_Y$ is $$(\bar{X}-\bar{Y}) \pm t_{m+n-2,\,\alpha/2}\,S_p\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{m}+\tfrac{1}{n}}.$$

Welch's $t$-Interval (Unequal Variances)

When $\sigma_X^2 \ne \sigma_Y^2$ the pooled $t$ is inappropriate.

Theorem 3.2.5 — Welch's Approximate $t$ CI

The statistic $$T_W = \frac{(\bar{X}-\bar{Y})-(\mu_X-\mu_Y)}{\sqrt{S_X^2/m + S_Y^2/n}}$$ is approximately $t_\nu$ where the Welch-Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are $$\nu = \frac{(S_X^2/m + S_Y^2/n)^2}{\dfrac{(S_X^2/m)^2}{m-1} + \dfrac{(S_Y^2/n)^2}{n-1}}.$$

The resulting CI is $(\bar{X}-\bar{Y}) \pm t_{\nu,\alpha/2}\sqrt{S_X^2/m + S_Y^2/n}$. This is an approximation; the pooled CI is exact when the equal-variance assumption holds.

Remark 3.2.6 — Exact vs Approximate Intervals

Pivotal-quantity intervals derived from exact distributional results (e.g.\ $t_{n-1}$, $\chi^2_{n-1}$, $F$) achieve the nominal coverage exactly under the stated model assumptions. Approximate intervals (Wald intervals for proportions, CLT-based intervals for non-normal data, Welch's $t$) achieve nominal coverage only asymptotically, and coverage can be poor for small $n$. One should always verify model assumptions — in particular normality — before claiming exact coverage.
