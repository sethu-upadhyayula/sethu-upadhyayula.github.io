7.3 Kernel Density Estimation

Kernel density estimation (KDE) produces a smooth, continuous estimate of an unknown density from data, without assuming any parametric form. It generalises the histogram by replacing the discrete bin indicator with a smooth kernel function, yielding a differentiable density estimate.

Definition 7.3.1 - Kernel Density Estimator

Given observations $X_1,\ldots,X_n$, the kernel density estimator with kernel $K$ and bandwidth $h > 0$ is $$\hat{f}_h(x) = \frac{1}{nh}\sum_{i=1}^n K\!\left(\frac{x - X_i}{h}\right),$$

where $K$ is a symmetric density function (i.e.\ $\int K(u)\,du = 1$, $K(u) \ge 0$, $\int u\,K(u)\,du = 0$). The Gaussian kernel is $K(u) = (2\pi)^{-1/2}e^{-u^2/2}$, the most widely used choice. The bandwidth $h$ controls the smoothness: small $h$ gives a spiky estimate, large $h$ gives an oversmoothed one.

Bias-Variance Trade-off

Theorem 7.3.2 - Bias and Variance of KDE

Under smoothness conditions on $f$ (specifically $\int u^2 K(u)\,du = \kappa_2 < \infty$ and $f''$ exists and is square-integrable): $$\mathrm{Bias}[\hat{f}_h(x)] \approx \frac{h^2 \kappa_2}{2} f''(x), \qquad \mathrm{Var}[\hat{f}_h(x)] \approx \frac{f(x)\int K^2}{nh}.$$

The mean integrated squared error (MISE) is $\mathrm{MISE}(h) = \int E[(\hat{f}_h(x) - f(x))^2]\,dx \approx \frac{h^4\kappa_2^2}{4}\int(f'')^2 + \frac{\int K^2}{nh}$. This is minimised at the optimal bandwidth $$h^* = \left(\frac{\int K^2}{n\kappa_2^2 \int(f'')^2}\right)^{1/5} \propto n^{-1/5}.$$ The optimal MISE decays as $O(n^{-4/5})$, slower than the parametric rate $O(n^{-1})$, reflecting the cost of estimating an infinite-dimensional object.

Bandwidth Selection

Definition 7.3.3 - Silverman's Rule of Thumb

Silverman's rule estimates $h^*$ by assuming $f$ is Gaussian to approximate $\int(f'')^2$: $$h_{\mathrm{Silverman}} = 1.06\,\hat\sigma\, n^{-1/5},$$ where $\hat\sigma = \min(s, \mathrm{IQR}/1.34)$ is a robust spread estimate (the IQR-based term guards against inflation by heavy tails). Despite being a rough estimate, it performs well in practice for unimodal distributions. For multimodal distributions, more sophisticated methods (cross-validation, plug-in bandwidth selectors) are needed.

Multivariate KDE

Definition 7.3.4 - Multivariate KDE

For $d$-dimensional data $\mathbf{X}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{X}_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the KDE with bandwidth matrix $\mathbf{H}$ (positive definite, $d\times d$) is $$\hat{f}_{\mathbf{H}}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{n|\mathbf{H}|^{1/2}}\sum_{i=1}^n K\!\left(\mathbf{H}^{-1/2}(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{X}_i)\right).$$ The optimal bandwidth matrix scales as $\mathbf{H}^* \propto n^{-2/(d+4)}\mathbf{I}_d$, exhibiting the curse of dimensionality: the convergence rate $O(n^{-4/(d+4)})$ deteriorates as $d$ increases, making KDE impractical for $d \gg 5$.

Applications

Remark 7.3.5 - Applications of KDE

KDE is used for: (i) exploratory data analysis - visualising distributional shape, identifying modes and skewness without committing to a parametric family; (ii) density-based classification - estimating class-conditional densities $\hat{f}(x\mid Y=k)$ and classifying via estimated posterior probabilities (non-parametric Bayes classifier); (iii) anomaly detection - observations in low-density regions $\{\hat{f}_h(x) < c\}$ are flagged as anomalies; (iv) bandwidth cross-validation - leave-one-out likelihood $\sum_i \log \hat{f}_{h,-i}(X_i)$ is maximised to select $h$ data-adaptively.
