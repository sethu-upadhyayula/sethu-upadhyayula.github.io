6.2 Conjugate Priors

A conjugate prior is one for which the posterior belongs to the same parametric family as the prior. Conjugacy yields closed-form posteriors and often admits intuitive interpretations of the hyperparameters as "pseudo-observations."

Definition 6.2.1 - Conjugate Family

A family of priors $\mathcal{P}$ is conjugate to a likelihood family $\{p(x\mid\theta)\}$ if for every prior $p(\theta) \in \mathcal{P}$ and every observed $x$, the posterior $p(\theta \mid x) \in \mathcal{P}$.

Beta-Binomial Model

Theorem 6.2.2 - Beta-Binomial Conjugacy

Let $X \mid \theta \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n,\theta)$ and $\theta \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)$. Then $$\theta \mid X = x \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha + x,\; \beta + n - x).$$

The prior hyperparameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ act as pseudo-successes and pseudo-failures. The posterior mean is $(\alpha+x)/(\alpha+\beta+n)$, a weighted average of the prior mean $\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$ and the MLE $x/n$. As $n\to\infty$, the posterior mean converges to the MLE.

Normal-Normal Model (Known $\sigma^2$)

Theorem 6.2.3 - Normal-Normal Conjugacy

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \mid \mu \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known, and $\mu \sim N(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$. Then $$\mu \mid \mathbf{x} \sim N(\mu_n, \tau_n^2), \quad \text{where}$$ $$\frac{1}{\tau_n^2} = \frac{1}{\tau_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}, \qquad \mu_n = \tau_n^2\!\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\tau_0^2} + \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2}\right).$$

The posterior mean $\mu_n$ is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the sample mean. As $n\to\infty$, $\mu_n \to \bar{x}$ and $\tau_n^2 \to 0$: the data overwhelm the prior.

Gamma-Poisson Model

Theorem 6.2.4 - Gamma-Poisson Conjugacy

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \mid \lambda \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and $\lambda \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(a, b)$ (shape $a$, rate $b$). Then $$\lambda \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathrm{Gamma}\!\left(a + \sum_{i=1}^n x_i,\; b + n\right).$$ The posterior mean is $(a + \sum x_i)/(b+n)$. The prior acts as $b$ pseudo-observations with total count $a$.

Dirichlet-Multinomial Model

Theorem 6.2.5 - Dirichlet-Multinomial Conjugacy

Let $\mathbf{X} \mid \boldsymbol{\pi} \sim \mathrm{Multinomial}(n, \boldsymbol{\pi})$ over $k$ categories and $\boldsymbol{\pi} \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$. Then $$\boldsymbol{\pi} \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\alpha_1+x_1, \ldots, \alpha_k+x_k).$$ The Dirichlet is the multivariate generalisation of the Beta; setting all $\alpha_j = 1$ gives a uniform prior over the simplex.

Exponential Family and Conjugacy

Proposition 6.2.6 - Conjugate Priors for the Exponential Family

Every member of the exponential family $p(x\mid\boldsymbol{\eta}) = h(x)\exp(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top T(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta}))$ has a natural conjugate prior $$p(\boldsymbol{\eta} \mid \boldsymbol{\chi}, \nu) \propto \exp(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top\boldsymbol{\chi} - \nu A(\boldsymbol{\eta})),$$

where $\nu > 0$ and $\boldsymbol{\chi}$ are hyperparameters. After observing $n$ i.i.d.\ observations with sufficient statistic $\sum T(x_i)$, the posterior hyperparameters update to $\boldsymbol{\chi}' = \boldsymbol{\chi} + \sum T(x_i)$ and $\nu' = \nu + n$ - a simple additive update regardless of the specific family.
