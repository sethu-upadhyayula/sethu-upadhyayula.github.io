6.1 Prior and Posterior

The Bayesian paradigm treats $\theta$ as a random variable. Before observing data, beliefs about $\theta$ are encoded in the prior distribution $p(\theta)$. After observing data $x$, beliefs are updated to the posterior $p(\theta \mid x)$ via Bayes' theorem.

Theorem 6.1.1 - Bayes' Theorem

$$p(\theta \mid x) = \frac{p(x \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)}{p(x)} \propto p(x \mid \theta)\,p(\theta),$$

where $p(x \mid \theta)$ is the likelihood, $p(\theta)$ is the prior, $p(x) = \int p(x\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta$ is the marginal likelihood (normalising constant), and $p(\theta\mid x)$ is the posterior. The posterior is proportional to likelihood times prior.

Definition 6.1.2 - Posterior Mean and MAP

The posterior mean (Bayes estimator under squared error loss) is $$\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{Bayes}} = E[\theta \mid x] = \int \theta\, p(\theta \mid x)\,d\theta.$$ The maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator is $$\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta p(\theta \mid x) = \arg\max_\theta \left[\log p(x\mid\theta) + \log p(\theta)\right].$$

MAP reduces to the MLE when the prior is uniform. The posterior mean minimises posterior expected squared error; MAP minimises posterior expected 0-1 loss.

Credible Intervals

Definition 6.1.3 - Credible Interval

A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ credible interval (or posterior interval) $[a, b]$ satisfies $$P(a \le \theta \le b \mid x) = 1 - \alpha.$$ Unlike a frequentist CI, this has a direct probability interpretation: given the observed data and prior, the probability that $\theta$ lies in $[a,b]$ is $1-\alpha$. The highest posterior density (HPD) interval is the shortest such interval.

Posterior Predictive Distribution

Definition 6.1.4 - Posterior Predictive

The posterior predictive distribution for a new observation $\tilde{x}$ integrates out parameter uncertainty: $$p(\tilde{x} \mid x) = \int p(\tilde{x} \mid \theta)\,p(\theta \mid x)\,d\theta.$$ This automatically propagates all uncertainty about $\theta$ into predictions, in contrast to the plug-in approach of substituting $\hat{\theta}$ directly.

Bayesian vs.\ Frequentist Inference

Remark 6.1.5 - Philosophical Contrast

The key differences:

  • Parameter status: Frequentist - $\theta$ is a fixed unknown constant; Bayesian - $\theta$ is a random variable with a distribution.
  • Probability: Frequentist - long-run frequency; Bayesian - degree of belief.
  • Interval: Frequentist CI - 95% of such intervals contain $\theta$; Bayesian credible interval - $P(\theta \in [a,b] \mid x) = 0.95$.
  • Prior: Bayesian inference requires a prior; the choice can be subjective. Frequentist methods are prior-free.
  • Asymptotics: Under regularity conditions, the Bayesian posterior concentrates around the MLE as $n \to \infty$ (Bernstein-von Mises theorem), so both approaches agree in large samples.
