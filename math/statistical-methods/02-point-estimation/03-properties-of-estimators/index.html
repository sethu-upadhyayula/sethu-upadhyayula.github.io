2.3 Properties of Estimators

We evaluate estimators by their accuracy (bias), variability (variance), and large-sample behaviour (consistency, efficiency). These properties guide the choice among competing estimators.

Definition 2.3.1 — Bias

The bias of $\hat{\theta}$ is $\mathrm{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta$. An estimator is unbiased if $E[\hat{\theta}] = \theta$ for all $\theta$. Example: $s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$.

Definition 2.3.2 — Mean Squared Error

$\mathrm{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = E[(\hat{\theta}-\theta)^2] = \mathrm{Var}(\hat{\theta}) + [\mathrm{Bias}(\hat{\theta})]^2.$ MSE balances variance against bias; a biased estimator with lower variance may have smaller MSE.

Definition 2.3.3 — Consistency

$\hat{\theta}_n$ is consistent if $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$ as $n \to \infty$. Sufficient condition: $\mathrm{Bias} \to 0$ and $\mathrm{Var} \to 0$.

Theorem 2.3.4 — Cramér–Rao Lower Bound

Under regularity conditions, for any unbiased estimator $\hat{\theta}$: $$\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \ge \frac{1}{n\,I(\theta)},$$ where $I(\theta)$ is the Fisher information for one observation.

An unbiased estimator achieving this bound is called efficient. The MLE is asymptotically efficient.

Definition 2.3.5 — Sufficiency

A statistic $T(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ if the conditional distribution of the sample given $T$ does not depend on $\theta$.

Factorisation theorem: $T$ is sufficient iff $f(\mathbf{x};\theta) = g(T(\mathbf{x}),\theta)\,h(\mathbf{x})$ for non-negative functions $g, h$. A sufficient statistic captures all information about $\theta$ in the data.

Example 2.3.6

For $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ known $\sigma^2$: $T = \sum X_i$ (or $\bar{X}$) is sufficient for $\mu$. The MLE $\hat{\mu} = \bar{X}$ is unbiased, consistent, and achieves the Cramér–Rao bound.
