2.2 Method of Moments

The method of moments (MOM) produces estimators by matching population moments to their sample counterparts. It predates MLE and is often analytically simpler, though generally less efficient.

Definition 2.2.1 - Population and Sample Moments

The $k$-th population moment is $\mu_k' = E[X^k]$, which depends on the parameter $\theta$.

The $k$-th sample moment is $m_k = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^k$. By the LLN, $m_k \xrightarrow{p} \mu_k'$.

Definition 2.2.2 - Method of Moments Estimator

If the model has $p$ parameters $\theta = (\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_p)$, set $\mu_k'(\theta) = m_k$ for $k = 1, \ldots, p$ and solve for $\theta$. The solution $\tilde{\theta}$ is the MOM estimator.

Example 2.2.3 - Normal Distribution

For $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$: $\mu_1' = \mu$ and $\mu_2' - (\mu_1')^2 = \sigma^2$. Matching: $\tilde{\mu} = \bar{x}$ and $\tilde{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$. These coincide with the MLEs.

Example 2.2.4 - Gamma Distribution

For $\Gamma(\alpha, \beta)$ with mean $\alpha/\beta$ and variance $\alpha/\beta^2$: $$\tilde{\alpha} = \frac{\bar{x}^2}{m_2 - \bar{x}^2}, \qquad \tilde{\beta} = \frac{\bar{x}}{m_2 - \bar{x}^2},$$ where $m_2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum x_i^2$.

MOM estimators are consistent (by continuity and the LLN) but may be less efficient than MLEs.

Remark - MOM vs. MLE

MOM is computationally simpler (no optimisation required) and always produces estimates even when the likelihood is hard to maximise. However, MOM estimators can be inefficient and may lie outside the natural parameter space. MLE is generally preferred when computationally feasible.
