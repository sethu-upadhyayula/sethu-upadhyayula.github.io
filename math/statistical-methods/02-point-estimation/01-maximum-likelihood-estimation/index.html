2.1 Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) selects the parameter value that makes the observed data most probable. It is the most widely used estimation method due to its optimality properties in large samples.

Definition 2.1.1 — Likelihood Function

For i.i.d. observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ from a model $f(\cdot\,;\theta)$, the likelihood function is $$L(\theta) = L(\theta; x_1,\ldots,x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i;\theta).$$ The log-likelihood is $\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log f(x_i;\theta)$.

Definition 2.1.2 — Maximum Likelihood Estimator

The MLE is $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta \ell(\theta)$. When $\ell$ is differentiable, $\hat{\theta}$ satisfies the score equation $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta} = 0$.

Example 2.1.3 — Normal Distribution

For $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ i.i.d.: $$\ell(\mu,\sigma^2) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\mu)^2.$$ Score equations give $\hat{\mu} = \bar{x}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2$ (biased).

Note: $\hat{\sigma}^2_\mathrm{MLE}$ is biased; the corrected estimator $s^2$ (dividing by $n-1$) is unbiased.

Example 2.1.4 — Exponential Distribution

For $X_i \sim \mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$, $\ell(\lambda) = n\log\lambda - \lambda\sum x_i$. Score: $n/\lambda - \sum x_i = 0$, so $\hat{\lambda} = 1/\bar{x}$.

Definition 2.1.5 — Fisher Information

The Fisher information for a single observation is $$I(\theta) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log f(X;\theta)\right)^2\right] = -E\!\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log f(X;\theta)\right].$$

Under regularity conditions, the MLE satisfies $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1/I(\theta))$, making the MLE asymptotically efficient.

Theorem 2.1.6 — Invariance of MLE

If $\hat{\theta}$ is the MLE of $\theta$ and $g$ is a function, then $g(\hat{\theta})$ is the MLE of $g(\theta)$.
