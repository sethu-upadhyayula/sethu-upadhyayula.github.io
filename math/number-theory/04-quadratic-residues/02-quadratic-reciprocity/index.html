4.2 Quadratic Reciprocity

The Law of Quadratic Reciprocity is one of the most celebrated results in number theory. Euler observed it empirically and Legendre stated it, but it was Gauss (age 19, in 1796) who gave the first rigorous proof. Gauss called it his theorema aureum — the golden theorem — and went on to give eight distinct proofs of it over his lifetime. Over 200 proofs are now known.

The Law of Quadratic Reciprocity

Theorem 4.2.1 — Law of Quadratic Reciprocity (Gauss, 1796)

For distinct odd primes $p$ and $q$: $$\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}\cdot\frac{q-1}{2}}.$$

Equivalently, $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$ unless both $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ and $q \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, in which case $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = -\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$.

Plain-Language Interpretation

The exponent $\frac{p-1}{2} \cdot \frac{q-1}{2}$ is odd if and only if both $\frac{p-1}{2}$ and $\frac{q-1}{2}$ are odd, i.e., both $p$ and $q$ are congruent to $3 \pmod{4}$. In all other cases the product $(-1)^{(\ldots)} = 1$, so reciprocity "swaps" freely:

  • If $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ or $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$: $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$.
  • If $p \equiv q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$: $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = -\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$ (exactly one is a QR).

Algorithm for Evaluating Legendre Symbols

Quadratic reciprocity, together with the two supplements and multiplicativity, gives a Euclidean-style algorithm for computing any Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$:

Algorithm 4.2.2 — Evaluation of Legendre Symbols
  1. Reduce $a \bmod p$ to lie in $\{0, 1, \ldots, p-1\}$.
  2. Factor out $-1$ and $2$ using the supplements (Theorems 4.1.6, 4.1.7) and multiplicativity.
  3. For the remaining odd part $q$ (which may be composite), factor it into primes and apply multiplicativity.
  4. For each prime factor $q$, use reciprocity to swap $\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) \to \left(\frac{p \bmod q}{q}\right)$, tracking the sign change.
  5. Reduce the argument modulo the new denominator and repeat (like the Euclidean algorithm). Terminate when the numerator is $1$ (symbol $= +1$) or a known case.

Worked Example

Example — Is 7 a Quadratic Residue mod 101?

We compute $\left(\frac{7}{101}\right)$.

Step 1. Apply reciprocity. Both 7 and 101 are odd primes. We have $\frac{7-1}{2} = 3$ and $\frac{101-1}{2} = 50$. The exponent $3 \cdot 50 = 150$ is even, so: $$\left(\frac{7}{101}\right)\left(\frac{101}{7}\right) = (-1)^{150} = 1.$$ Therefore $\left(\frac{7}{101}\right) = \left(\frac{101}{7}\right)$.

Step 2. Reduce $101 \bmod 7$: $101 = 14 \cdot 7 + 3$, so $101 \equiv 3 \pmod{7}$. $$\left(\frac{101}{7}\right) = \left(\frac{3}{7}\right).$$

Step 3. Compute $\left(\frac{3}{7}\right)$ by reciprocity. Both 3 and 7 are $\equiv 3 \pmod 4$, so $\frac{3-1}{2} \cdot \frac{7-1}{2} = 1 \cdot 3 = 3$ (odd): $$\left(\frac{3}{7}\right)\left(\frac{7}{3}\right) = (-1)^3 = -1.$$

Step 4. Reduce $7 \bmod 3$: $7 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$, so $\left(\frac{7}{3}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{3}\right) = 1$.

Conclusion. From Step 3: $\left(\frac{3}{7}\right) \cdot 1 = -1$, so $\left(\frac{3}{7}\right) = -1$. Therefore: $$\left(\frac{7}{101}\right) = \left(\frac{3}{7}\right) = -1.$$ 7 is a quadratic non-residue mod 101.

Example — Is 5 a QR mod 13?

$\left(\frac{5}{13}\right)$: $\frac{5-1}{2} = 2$, $\frac{13-1}{2} = 6$. Exponent $2 \cdot 6 = 12$ is even, so $\left(\frac{5}{13}\right) = \left(\frac{13}{5}\right)$. Reduce: $13 \equiv 3 \pmod 5$, so $\left(\frac{13}{5}\right) = \left(\frac{3}{5}\right)$. Now $\frac{3-1}{2} \cdot \frac{5-1}{2} = 1 \cdot 2 = 2$ (even), so $\left(\frac{3}{5}\right) = \left(\frac{5}{3}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)$. Since $3 \equiv 3 \pmod 8$, $\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) = -1$. Hence $\left(\frac{5}{13}\right) = -1$: 5 is a QNR mod 13.

Verify directly: squares mod 13 are $1^2=1$, $2^2=4$, $3^2=9$, $4^2=3$, $5^2=12$, $6^2=10$. The QRs are $\{1,3,4,9,10,12\}$ and 5 is not among them. ✓

Historical Note

Gauss's first proof (1796) used induction on a complicated sequence of cases. His later proofs used Gauss sums $\tau = \sum_{a=0}^{p-1} \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) e^{2\pi i a/p}$, showing that $\tau^2 = (-1)^{(p-1)/2} p$. A beautiful modern proof uses the group of lattice points in a rectangle to count lattice points below a diagonal, cleverly relating the counts for $(p,q)$ and $(q,p)$. Quadratic reciprocity extends to the Jacobi symbol and further to higher reciprocity laws in algebraic number theory (Artin reciprocity).
