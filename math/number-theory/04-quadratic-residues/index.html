Chapter 4: Quadratic Residues

Quadratic residues study which integers are perfect squares modulo a prime, leading to one of the most beautiful and surprising results in all of mathematics: the Law of Quadratic Reciprocity. Discovered by Euler and Legendre and first completely proved by Gauss at age 19, this law connects the solvability of $x^2 \equiv p \pmod{q}$ to $x^2 \equiv q \pmod{p}$ in a profound and unexpected way.

The main tool is the Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$, a compact notation encoding whether $a$ is a square modulo the odd prime $p$. Euler's criterion expresses this symbol as a power of $a$, making it computable and multiplicative.

Chapter Overview

4.1 — Legendre Symbol

We define quadratic residues and non-residues modulo a prime, count that exactly $(p-1)/2$ residues exist among $\{1, \ldots, p-1\}$, introduce the Legendre symbol, prove Euler's criterion $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right) \equiv a^{(p-1)/2} \pmod{p}$, establish multiplicativity, and derive the two supplements: $\left(\frac{-1}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p-1)/2}$ and $\left(\frac{2}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p^2-1)/8}$.

4.2 — Quadratic Reciprocity

The crown jewel of elementary number theory: for distinct odd primes $p$ and $q$, the product $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p-1}{2}\cdot\frac{q-1}{2}}$. We state Gauss's theorem (which he proved eight different ways), interpret it in plain language, and use it together with the supplements to evaluate Legendre symbols efficiently via a Euclidean-style algorithm.
