5.2 Convergents

The convergents of a continued fraction $\alpha = [a_0; a_1, a_2, \ldots]$ are the rational numbers obtained by truncating the expansion. They form the sequence of best rational approximations to $\alpha$ and satisfy elegant recurrence relations.

Definition and Recurrences

Definition 5.2.1 - Convergents

The $n$-th convergent of $\alpha = [a_0; a_1, a_2, \ldots]$ is the rational number: $$\frac{p_n}{q_n} = [a_0; a_1, \ldots, a_n].$$

The numerators $p_n$ and denominators $q_n$ are defined with initial values $p_{-1} = 1$, $p_0 = a_0$, $q_{-1} = 0$, $q_0 = 1$, and satisfy the recurrences: $$p_n = a_n p_{n-1} + p_{n-2}, \qquad q_n = a_n q_{n-1} + q_{n-2} \quad (n \ge 1).$$

Example - Convergents of $\sqrt{2} = [1; 2, 2, 2, \ldots]$

With $a_0 = 1$, $a_n = 2$ for $n \ge 1$:

Key Identity

Theorem 5.2.2 - Stern-Brocot Identity

For all $n \ge 0$: $$p_n q_{n-1} - p_{n-1} q_n = (-1)^{n-1}.$$ In particular, $\gcd(p_n, q_n) = 1$: every convergent is already in lowest terms.

Proof

Induction. Base case $n=0$: $p_0 q_{-1} - p_{-1} q_0 = a_0 \cdot 0 - 1 \cdot 1 = -1 = (-1)^{-1}$. ✓

Inductive step: $p_n q_{n-1} - p_{n-1} q_n = (a_n p_{n-1} + p_{n-2})q_{n-1} - p_{n-1}(a_n q_{n-1} + q_{n-2}) = p_{n-2} q_{n-1} - p_{n-1} q_{n-2} = -(-1)^{n-2} = (-1)^{n-1}$ (using induction). $\square$

Alternating Convergence

Theorem 5.2.3 - Convergents Alternate About $\alpha$

For an irrational $\alpha = [a_0; a_1, a_2, \ldots]$, the even-indexed convergents increase to $\alpha$ from below and the odd-indexed convergents decrease to $\alpha$ from above: $$\frac{p_0}{q_0} < \frac{p_2}{q_2} < \frac{p_4}{q_4} < \cdots < \alpha < \cdots < \frac{p_5}{q_5} < \frac{p_3}{q_3} < \frac{p_1}{q_1}.$$ Moreover, $\alpha - p_n/q_n = (-1)^n / (q_n(\alpha_{n+1} q_n + q_{n-1}))$, showing the sign alternates.

Best Approximation Property

Theorem 5.2.4 - Best Approximation

The convergents give the best rational approximations in two senses:

  1. Quality: $\left|\alpha - \dfrac{p_n}{q_n}\right| < \dfrac{1}{q_n q_{n+1}} \le \dfrac{1}{q_n^2}.$
  2. Optimality: If $p/q$ is a rational with $0 < q \le q_n$, then $|q_n \alpha - p_n| \le |q\alpha - p|$. That is, no rational with denominator $\le q_n$ approximates $\alpha$ better than $p_n/q_n$ does (in the sense of $|q\alpha - p|$).
Theorem 5.2.5 - Hurwitz's Theorem

For any irrational $\alpha$, there are infinitely many rationals $p/q$ such that: $$\left|\alpha - \frac{p}{q}\right| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}\, q^2}.$$ The constant $\sqrt{5}$ is best possible, achieved in the limit by the golden ratio $\phi = [1;\overline{1}]$. All infinitely many such $p/q$ are convergents of $\alpha$.

Remark - Mediants and the Stern-Brocot Tree

The identity $p_n q_{n-1} - p_{n-1} q_n = \pm 1$ means adjacent convergents are Farey neighbors: their difference is $\pm 1/(q_n q_{n-1})$. The Stern-Brocot tree organises all positive rationals by the mediant construction and has continued fractions encoded in its path structure. Every positive rational appears exactly once in the tree.
