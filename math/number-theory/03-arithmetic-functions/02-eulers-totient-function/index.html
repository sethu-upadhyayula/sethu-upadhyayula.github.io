3.2 Euler's Totient Function

Euler's totient function $\phi(n)$ counts the positive integers up to $n$ that are coprime to $n$. It appears in Euler's theorem - the generalisation of Fermat's little theorem - and is central to RSA cryptography.

Definition 3.2.1 - Euler's Totient

$$\phi(n) = \#\{k : 1 \leq k \leq n,\; \gcd(k,n) = 1\} = |(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times|.$$ Equivalently, $\phi(n)$ is the order of the multiplicative group of units modulo $n$.

Theorem 3.2.2 - Formula for $\phi$

$\phi$ is multiplicative and for $n = p_1^{a_1} \cdots p_k^{a_k}$: $$\phi(n) = n \prod_{p \mid n} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right) = \prod_{i=1}^k p_i^{a_i - 1}(p_i - 1).$$

Proof sketch: By inclusion-exclusion (sieve) and multiplicativity. For prime power: $\phi(p^a) = p^a - p^{a-1}$ since exactly $p^{a-1}$ multiples of $p$ lie in $\{1,\ldots,p^a\}$.

Example 3.2.3

$\phi(12) = \phi(4)\phi(3) = 2 \cdot 2 = 4$. The units mod 12 are $\{1,5,7,11\}$. Also $\phi(p) = p-1$ for any prime $p$, and $\phi(p^2) = p^2 - p = p(p-1)$.

Theorem 3.2.4 - Summatory Formula

$$\sum_{d \mid n} \phi(d) = n.$$ Proof: Partition $\{1/n, 2/n, \ldots, n/n\}$ by reduced form denominators; those with denominator $d \mid n$ contribute $\phi(d)$ fractions.

Theorem 3.2.5 - Euler's Theorem

If $\gcd(a,n)=1$ then $a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$. This follows because $a \mapsto a \cdot (-)$ is a bijection on $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$, so multiplying all elements gives $a^{\phi(n)} \prod u \equiv \prod u$, whence $a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1$.

Remark - RSA

RSA cryptography is based on: given $n = pq$ (product of two large primes), encryption $e$ and decryption $d = e^{-1} \bmod \phi(n)$ satisfy $(m^e)^d = m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod n$ for $\gcd(m,n) = 1$, since $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$ implies $ed = 1 + k\phi(n)$ and $m^{ed} = m \cdot (m^{\phi(n)})^k \equiv m$ by Euler's theorem.
