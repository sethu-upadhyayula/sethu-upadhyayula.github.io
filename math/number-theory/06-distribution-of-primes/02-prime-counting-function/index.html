6.2 Prime Counting Function

The prime counting function $\pi(x)$ records the number of primes up to $x$. Understanding its growth is one of the central problems of analytic number theory.

Definition — Prime Counting Function

For $x \ge 0$, define

$$\pi(x) = \#\{p \le x : p \text{ prime}\}$$

the number of primes not exceeding $x$.

Theorem — Chebyshev's Bounds (1852)

There exist constants $0 < c_1 < c_2$ such that for all sufficiently large $x$:

$$c_1 \frac{x}{\ln x} \le \pi(x) \le c_2 \frac{x}{\ln x}$$

Chebyshev showed one can take $c_1 = \ln 2$ and $c_2 = 2\ln 2$ (approximately $0.693$ and $1.386$).

Proof Sketch — Upper Bound

Consider $\binom{2n}{n}$. On one hand $4^n / (2n+1) \le \binom{2n}{n} \le 4^n$. On the other, every prime $p$ with $n < p \le 2n$ divides $\binom{2n}{n}$ (since $p \mid (2n)!$ but $p \nmid (n!)^2$). Hence $\prod_{n < p \le 2n} p \le \binom{2n}{n} \le 4^n$. Taking logs: $\sum_{n < p \le 2n} \ln p \le n \ln 4$. Iterating gives $\sum_{p \le x} \ln p \le x \ln 4$, from which $\pi(x) \le (2\ln 2) x/\ln x$.

Theorem — Bertrand's Postulate (Chebyshev, 1852)

For every integer $n \ge 1$, there exists a prime $p$ with $n < p \le 2n$.

Proof Sketch

Let $N = \binom{2n}{n}$. Any prime power $p^a$ dividing $N$ satisfies $p^a \le 2n$. Primes $p > \sqrt{2n}$ contribute at most a single factor to $N$. The contribution of primes $p \le \sqrt{2n}$ is at most $(2n)^{\sqrt{2n}}$. If no prime $p$ satisfies $n < p \le 2n$, then all primes contributing to $N$ are at most $n$, and the product of primes up to $n$ is at most $4^{2n/3}$ by the Chebyshev bound. A careful calculation using $N \ge 4^n/(2n)$ yields a contradiction for large $n$. Small cases are verified directly.

Corollary

For $n \ge 25$, there exists a prime $p$ with $n < p < \frac{3n}{2}$. More generally, Bertrand's postulate implies $p_{n+1} < 2p_n$ for all $n$, where $p_n$ is the $n$-th prime.

Theorem — Mertens' Theorems

Let $p$ range over primes. As $x \to \infty$:

  1. $\displaystyle\sum_{p \le x} \frac{\ln p}{p} = \ln x + O(1)$
  2. $\displaystyle\sum_{p \le x} \frac{1}{p} = \ln \ln x + M + o(1)$, where $M \approx 0.2615$ is the Meissel–Mertens constant.
  3. $\displaystyle\prod_{p \le x} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p}\right) \sim \frac{e^{-\gamma}}{\ln x}$, where $\gamma \approx 0.5772$ is the Euler–Mascheroni constant.
Remark — Growth Rate

Mertens' second theorem shows that the reciprocals of primes form a divergent series (much more slowly than the harmonic series), providing another proof of the infinitude of primes. The sum $\sum_{p \le x} 1/p \approx \ln \ln x$ grows extremely slowly: it only reaches 3 around $x \approx e^{e^3} \approx 10^8$.
