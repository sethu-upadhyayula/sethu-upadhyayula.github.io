Chapter 1: Divisibility

Divisibility is the cornerstone of elementary number theory. The simple question "does $a$ divide $b$?" leads, through careful analysis, to the division algorithm, the greatest common divisor, and ultimately to the fundamental theorem of arithmetic — the unique factorisation of integers into primes.

This chapter develops these ideas rigorously, establishing the algebraic and number-theoretic tools that will be used throughout the course.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Divisibility and the Division Algorithm

We define $a \mid b$ and study its basic properties: transitivity, closure under linear combinations, and the bound $a \le b$ when $b > 0$. The Division Algorithm asserts that for any $a \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $b \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ there exist unique integers $q$ (quotient) and $r$ (remainder) with $a = bq + r$ and $0 \le r < b$.

1.2 — Greatest Common Divisor

The greatest common divisor $\gcd(a,b)$ is characterised via Bézout's identity: $\gcd(a,b) = sa + tb$ for integers $s, t$. We prove the key lemma $\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b)$ and Euclid's lemma: if $p$ is prime and $p \mid ab$ then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$.

1.3 — The Euclidean Algorithm

The Euclidean Algorithm iteratively applies the division algorithm to compute $\gcd(a,b)$ in $O(\log \min(a,b))$ steps (Lamé's theorem). The extended version back-substitutes to find explicit Bézout coefficients, enabling computation of modular inverses.

1.4 — Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Every integer $n > 1$ factors uniquely as a product of primes $n = p_1^{a_1} \cdots p_k^{a_k}$. Existence follows by strong induction; uniqueness uses Euclid's lemma. Consequences include formulas for $\gcd$, $\text{lcm}$, and the divisor functions $\tau$ and $\sigma$.
