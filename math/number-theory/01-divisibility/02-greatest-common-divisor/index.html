1.2 Greatest Common Divisor

The greatest common divisor captures the "shared divisibility" of two integers. Its deep characterisation via Bézout's identity — as a linear combination of the two integers — is one of the most useful results in elementary number theory.

Definition 1.2.1 — Greatest Common Divisor

Let $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, not both zero. The greatest common divisor $d = \gcd(a,b)$ is the largest positive integer dividing both $a$ and $b$. Equivalently, $d$ is the unique positive integer such that (i) $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$, and (ii) if $c \mid a$ and $c \mid b$ then $c \mid d$. If $\gcd(a,b)=1$, we say $a$ and $b$ are coprime (or relatively prime).

Bézout's Identity

Theorem 1.2.2 — Bézout's Identity

For any integers $a, b$ (not both zero), there exist integers $s, t$ such that $$\gcd(a,b) = sa + tb.$$ The set $\{ma + nb : m, n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ equals the set of all multiples of $\gcd(a,b)$.

Proof

Let $S = \{ma + nb : m, n \in \mathbb{Z},\; ma + nb > 0\}$. Since $a^2 + b^2 > 0$, $S$ is nonempty. By the Well-Ordering Principle, $S$ has a smallest element $d = s_0 a + t_0 b$.

We claim $d \mid a$. By the Division Algorithm, $a = dq + r$ with $0 \le r < d$. Then $r = a - dq = a - (s_0 a + t_0 b)q = (1 - s_0 q)a + (-t_0 q)b$. If $r > 0$ then $r \in S$, contradicting minimality of $d$. So $r = 0$ and $d \mid a$. Similarly $d \mid b$.

Any common divisor $c$ of $a$ and $b$ satisfies $c \mid sa + tb = d$, so $c \le d$. Thus $d = \gcd(a,b)$. $\square$

Key Properties

Theorem 1.2.3 — GCD and Remainder

If $a = bq + r$, then $\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,r)$. In other words, the common divisors of $a$ and $b$ are the same as the common divisors of $b$ and $r = a \bmod b$.

Proof

If $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$, then $d \mid a - bq = r$. Conversely, if $d \mid b$ and $d \mid r$, then $d \mid bq + r = a$. So the sets of common divisors are identical, and in particular their largest elements are equal. $\square$

Theorem 1.2.4 — GCD–LCM Relation

For positive integers $a$ and $b$, $$\gcd(a,b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a,b) = ab.$$

Euclid's Lemma

Theorem 1.2.5 — Euclid's Lemma

If $p$ is prime and $p \mid ab$, then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$.

Proof

Suppose $p \nmid a$. Since $p$ is prime, its only positive divisors are $1$ and $p$. Since $p \nmid a$, we have $\gcd(p, a) = 1$. By Bézout's identity there exist $s, t$ with $sp + ta = 1$. Multiplying by $b$: $spb + tab = b$. Now $p \mid spb$ and $p \mid tab$ (since $p \mid ab$), so $p \mid b$. $\square$

Example 1.2.6

Find $\gcd(48, 36)$ and express it as a linear combination.

$\gcd(48,36) = \gcd(36,12) = \gcd(12,0) = 12$.

Back-substituting: $12 = 48 - 1 \cdot 36$. So $s = 1$, $t = -1$, and $12 = 1 \cdot 48 + (-1) \cdot 36$.

Remark

Bézout coefficients $s, t$ are not unique: if $\gcd(a,b) = sa + tb$ then also $\gcd(a,b) = (s + b/d)a + (t - a/d)b$ where $d = \gcd(a,b)$. The extended Euclidean algorithm (Section 1.3) gives a systematic way to find one pair $(s,t)$.
