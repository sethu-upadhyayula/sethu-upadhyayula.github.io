2.2 Chinese Remainder Theorem

The Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) — known in China as early as the 3rd century CE — solves systems of simultaneous congruences with pairwise coprime moduli. It is a cornerstone of computational number theory and modern cryptography.

Theorem 2.2.1 — Chinese Remainder Theorem

Let $m_1, \ldots, m_k$ be pairwise coprime positive integers (i.e., $\gcd(m_i, m_j) = 1$ for $i \neq j$), and let $a_1, \ldots, a_k$ be any integers. Then the system $$x \equiv a_1 \pmod{m_1}, \quad x \equiv a_2 \pmod{m_2}, \quad \ldots, \quad x \equiv a_k \pmod{m_k}$$ has a unique solution modulo $M = m_1 m_2 \cdots m_k$.

Proof — Construction

Set $M_i = M / m_i$ for each $i$. Since $\gcd(M_i, m_i) = 1$ (as $m_i$ divides none of the other $m_j$), there exists $y_i$ with $M_i y_i \equiv 1 \pmod{m_i}$ (found by extended Euclidean algorithm). Set $$x = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i M_i y_i.$$ For each $j$: $M_i \equiv 0 \pmod{m_j}$ for $i \neq j$, so $x \equiv a_j M_j y_j \equiv a_j \cdot 1 = a_j \pmod{m_j}$.

Uniqueness: If $x$ and $x'$ are both solutions, then $m_i \mid x - x'$ for all $i$. Since the $m_i$ are pairwise coprime, $M \mid x - x'$, so $x \equiv x' \pmod{M}$. $\square$

Theorem 2.2.2 — Ring Isomorphism

If $m_1, \ldots, m_k$ are pairwise coprime, then the map $x \mapsto (x \bmod m_1, \ldots, x \bmod m_k)$ is a ring isomorphism: $$\mathbb{Z}/M\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/m_1\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/m_k\mathbb{Z}.$$

Example 2.2.3 — Solve $x \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$, $x \equiv 3 \pmod{5}$, $x \equiv 2 \pmod{7}$

$M = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 = 105$. $M_1 = 35$, $M_2 = 21$, $M_3 = 15$.

  • $35 y_1 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$: $35 \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, so $2y_1 \equiv 1$, $y_1 = 2$.
  • $21 y_2 \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$: $21 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$, so $y_2 = 1$.
  • $15 y_3 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$: $15 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$, so $y_3 = 1$.

$x = 2 \cdot 35 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 21 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 15 \cdot 1 = 140 + 63 + 30 = 233 \equiv 233 - 2 \cdot 105 = 23 \pmod{105}$.

Check: $23 = 7 \cdot 3 + 2 \equiv 2$; $23 = 4 \cdot 5 + 3 \equiv 3$; $23 = 3 \cdot 7 + 2 \equiv 2$. $\checkmark$

Corollary 2.2.4 — Multiplicativity of $\phi$

If $\gcd(m,n)=1$ then $\phi(mn) = \phi(m)\phi(n)$. This follows from the CRT isomorphism applied to the unit groups: $(\mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z})^* \cong (\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z})^* \times (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^*$.
