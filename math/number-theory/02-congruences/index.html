Chapter 2: Congruences

Modular arithmetic organises integers into residue classes, replacing equality with a notion of "sameness modulo $m$." This framework enables elegant proofs about divisibility and primality, and underlies modern cryptography.

The central insight is that $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ is a ring under the natural operations, and is even a field when $m$ is prime. The Chinese Remainder Theorem breaks computations modulo a composite into independent computations modulo coprime factors.

Chapter Overview

2.1 — Congruence Relations

We define $a \equiv b \pmod{m}$ and show it is an equivalence relation partitioning $\mathbb{Z}$ into $m$ residue classes. Addition and multiplication are well-defined on classes, making $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ a ring. Linear congruences $ax \equiv b \pmod{m}$ have solutions precisely when $\gcd(a,m) \mid b$.

2.2 — Chinese Remainder Theorem

When $m_1, \ldots, m_k$ are pairwise coprime, any system of simultaneous congruences $x \equiv a_i \pmod{m_i}$ has a unique solution modulo $M = m_1 \cdots m_k$. This yields a ring isomorphism $\mathbb{Z}/M\mathbb{Z} \cong \prod \mathbb{Z}/m_i\mathbb{Z}$.

2.3 — Euler's Theorem and Fermat's Little Theorem

Fermat's Little Theorem: $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ for prime $p \nmid a$. Euler's generalisation: $a^{\phi(m)} \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$ when $\gcd(a,m)=1$. These are the theoretical foundation of the RSA cryptosystem.
