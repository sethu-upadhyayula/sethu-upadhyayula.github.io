2.3 Euler's Theorem and Fermat's Little Theorem

Fermat's Little Theorem (1640) and Euler's generalisation (1763) give powerful constraints on the order of elements in the multiplicative group modulo $m$. They are indispensable in primality testing, cryptography, and the theory of cyclic groups.

Theorem 2.3.1 — Fermat's Little Theorem

Let $p$ be prime and $a$ an integer with $p \nmid a$. Then $$a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}.$$ Equivalently, $a^p \equiv a \pmod{p}$ for all integers $a$.

Proof

Consider the set $\{a, 2a, 3a, \ldots, (p-1)a\}$ modulo $p$. Since $p \nmid a$ and $p$ is prime, multiplication by $a$ permutes the nonzero residues $\{1, 2, \ldots, p-1\}$. Therefore $$a \cdot 2a \cdot 3a \cdots (p-1)a \equiv 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdots (p-1) \pmod{p},$$ i.e., $a^{p-1}(p-1)! \equiv (p-1)! \pmod{p}$. Since $\gcd((p-1)!, p) = 1$, we may cancel to get $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. $\square$

Example 2.3.2

$7^{12} \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$ by Fermat (since $p = 13$, $p-1 = 12$). So $7^{100} = 7^{12 \cdot 8 + 4} = (7^{12})^8 \cdot 7^4 \equiv 7^4 = 2401 \equiv 2401 - 184 \cdot 13 = 2401 - 2392 = 9 \pmod{13}$.

Euler's Theorem

Theorem 2.3.3 — Euler's Theorem

If $\gcd(a, m) = 1$, then $$a^{\phi(m)} \equiv 1 \pmod{m},$$ where $\phi(m)$ is Euler's totient function (the number of integers in $\{1,\ldots,m\}$ coprime to $m$).

Proof

Let $\{r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_{\phi(m)}\}$ be the units modulo $m$ (i.e., the elements of $(\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z})^*$). Since $\gcd(a,m)=1$, multiplication by $a$ permutes this set: $\{ar_1, ar_2, \ldots, ar_{\phi(m)}\}$ is the same set modulo $m$. Multiplying all elements: $$(ar_1)(ar_2)\cdots(ar_{\phi(m)}) \equiv r_1 r_2 \cdots r_{\phi(m)} \pmod{m}.$$ Factoring: $a^{\phi(m)} \prod r_i \equiv \prod r_i \pmod{m}$. Since each $r_i$ is a unit, $\prod r_i$ is invertible; cancel to get $a^{\phi(m)} \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$. $\square$

Remark

Fermat's Little Theorem is the special case $m = p$ prime, since $\phi(p) = p-1$. Euler's theorem generalises it to any modulus, using the group-theoretic fact that $a^{|G|} = e$ for any element $a$ of a finite group $G$.

Application: RSA Cryptosystem

RSA Sketch

Choose large distinct primes $p$ and $q$; set $n = pq$. Choose $e$ with $\gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$ and find $d$ with $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$ (via extended Euclidean algorithm).

  • Public key: $(n, e)$. Private key: $d$.
  • Encryption: $c \equiv m^e \pmod{n}$.
  • Decryption: $c^d \equiv m^{ed} \equiv m^{1 + k\phi(n)} \equiv m \cdot (m^{\phi(n)})^k \equiv m \pmod{n}$ by Euler's theorem (when $\gcd(m,n)=1$).

Security relies on the difficulty of factoring $n = pq$ without knowing $p$ and $q$.
