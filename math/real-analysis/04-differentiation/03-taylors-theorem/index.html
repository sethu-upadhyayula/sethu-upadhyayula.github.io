4.3 Taylor's Theorem

Taylor's theorem approximates a smooth function near a point by a polynomial, with an explicit bound on the error. This is one of the most used results in all of analysis and applied mathematics.

Definition 4.3.1 - Taylor Polynomial

If $f$ is $n$-times differentiable at $a$, its $n$-th order Taylor polynomial centred at $a$ is $$P_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k.$$ By construction, $P_n^{(k)}(a) = f^{(k)}(a)$ for $0 \le k \le n$.

Theorem 4.3.2 - Taylor's Theorem with Lagrange Remainder

If $f$ is $(n+1)$-times differentiable on an interval containing $a$ and $x$, then $$f(x) = P_n(x) + R_n(x),$$ where $$R_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}$$ for some $c$ strictly between $a$ and $x$.

Proof: apply the Cauchy MVT $n+1$ times to appropriate auxiliary functions. The MVT ($n=0$) is the special case $f(x) = f(a) + f'(c)(x-a)$.

Theorem 4.3.3 - Peano Remainder

If $f$ is $n$-times differentiable at $a$ (not necessarily in a neighbourhood), then $f(x) = P_n(x) + o\!\left((x-a)^n\right)$ as $x \to a$. This is weaker than Lagrange but requires fewer hypotheses - $n$ derivatives at a single point suffice.

Theorem 4.3.4 - Integral Form of the Remainder

If $f^{(n+1)}$ is continuous, then $$R_n(x) = \frac{1}{n!}\int_a^x (x-t)^n f^{(n+1)}(t)\,dt.$$ This form is often the most useful for bounding remainders: $|R_n(x)| \le \dfrac{\sup|f^{(n+1)}|}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1}$.

Example 4.3.5 - $C^\infty$ Function with Trivial Taylor Series

Define $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ by $f(x) = e^{-1/x^2}$ for $x \ne 0$ and $f(0) = 0$. One shows by induction that $f^{(n)}(0) = 0$ for every $n \ge 0$. Thus the Taylor series of $f$ at 0 is identically 0.

But $f(x) \ne 0$ for $x \ne 0$. So the Taylor series converges everywhere but equals $f$ only at 0. This shows that $C^\infty \ne$ analytic - the Taylor series of a smooth function can fail to represent the function.
