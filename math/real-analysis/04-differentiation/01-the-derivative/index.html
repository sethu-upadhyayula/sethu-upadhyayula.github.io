4.1 The Derivative

The derivative formalises the instantaneous rate of change as a limit of difference quotients. Differentiability is a stronger condition than continuity, and Carathéodory's characterisation replaces the awkward "0/0" form with a clean multiplicative factorisation.

Definition 4.1.1 — The Derivative

$f$ is differentiable at $x_0$ if the limit $f'(x_0) = \lim_{h\to 0} \dfrac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h}$ exists and is finite. Equivalently, $f'(x_0) = \lim_{x\to x_0} \dfrac{f(x) - f(x_0)}{x - x_0}$.

Theorem 4.1.2 — Differentiability Implies Continuity

If $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$. Proof: $f(x) - f(x_0) = \dfrac{f(x) - f(x_0)}{x - x_0} \cdot (x - x_0) \to f'(x_0) \cdot 0 = 0$. The converse fails: $f(x) = |x|$ is continuous at 0 but not differentiable there (left derivative $= -1$, right derivative $= +1$).

Theorem 4.1.3 — Carathéodory's Characterisation

$f$ is differentiable at $a$ if and only if there exists a function $\phi$ that is continuous at $a$ such that $f(x) - f(a) = \phi(x)(x - a)$ for all $x$. When this holds, $\phi(a) = f'(a)$.

Chain rule via Carathéodory: If $f$ is differentiable at $a$ and $g$ is differentiable at $f(a)$, write $f(x) - f(a) = \phi(x)(x-a)$ and $g(y) - g(f(a)) = \psi(y)(y - f(a))$. Then $g(f(x)) - g(f(a)) = \psi(f(x))\phi(x)(x-a)$, and continuity gives $(g \circ f)'(a) = \psi(f(a))\phi(a) = g'(f(a))f'(a)$.

Differentiation Rules

Theorem 4.1.4 — Algebra of Derivatives

If $f$ and $g$ are differentiable at $x$:

  • $(f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'$
  • $(fg)' = f'g + fg'$ (product rule)
  • $(f/g)' = (f'g - fg')/g^2$ where $g \ne 0$ (quotient rule)
  • $(g \circ f)'(x) = g'(f(x)) \cdot f'(x)$ (chain rule)
Theorem 4.1.5 — Leibniz Rule

For $n$-times differentiable $f$ and $g$: $$(fg)^{(n)} = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} f^{(k)} g^{(n-k)}.$$ Proof by induction on $n$ using the product rule. The binomial structure mirrors the binomial theorem.
