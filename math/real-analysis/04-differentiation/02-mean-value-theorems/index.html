4.2 Mean Value Theorems

The Mean Value Theorem is the workhorse of differential calculus, linking local information (the derivative) to global behaviour (changes in function values). It follows from the simpler Rolle's theorem by a geometric tilt.

Theorem 4.2.1 — Fermat's Theorem

If $f$ has a local extremum at $c$ and is differentiable at $c$, then $f'(c) = 0$. Proof: if $f$ has a local maximum at $c$, then $(f(c+h) - f(c))/h \le 0$ for $h > 0$ small and $\ge 0$ for $h < 0$ small; taking $h \to 0$ from each side gives $f'(c) \le 0$ and $f'(c) \ge 0$, so $f'(c) = 0$.

Theorem 4.2.2 — Rolle's Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, differentiable on $(a, b)$, and $f(a) = f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ with $f'(c) = 0$. Proof: by the Extreme Value Theorem, $f$ attains its maximum and minimum on $[a,b]$. If both extrema are at the endpoints, $f$ is constant and $f' \equiv 0$. Otherwise some extremum is in the interior, and Fermat's theorem applies.

Theorem 4.2.3 — Mean Value Theorem (Lagrange)

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then $\exists c \in (a, b)$ with $$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}.$$

Proof: Apply Rolle's theorem to $h(x) = f(x) - \dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$. Note $h(a) = f(a) = h(b)$ (after the linear term cancels), so $h'(c) = 0$ for some $c$, giving $f'(c) = (f(b)-f(a))/(b-a)$.

Theorem 4.2.4 — Cauchy Mean Value Theorem

If $f, g$ are continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then $\exists c \in (a,b)$ with $(f(b)-f(a))g'(c) = (g(b)-g(a))f'(c)$. If $g' \ne 0$ on $(a,b)$ and $g(a) \ne g(b)$, this reads $f'(c)/g'(c) = (f(b)-f(a))/(g(b)-g(a))$. Taking $g(x) = x$ recovers the standard MVT.

Corollaries and Applications

Theorem 4.2.5 — Corollaries of the MVT
  • $f' = 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ is constant on $[a,b]$.
  • $f' \ge 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ is non-decreasing; $f' > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ strictly increasing.
  • $|f'| \le M$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow$ $|f(x)-f(y)| \le M|x-y|$ (Lipschitz with constant $M$).
Theorem 4.2.6 — Darboux's Theorem

If $f$ is differentiable on $[a,b]$ and $\lambda$ is any value between $f'(a)$ and $f'(b)$, then $\exists c \in (a,b)$ with $f'(c) = \lambda$. In other words, $f'$ satisfies the Intermediate Value Property, even if $f'$ is not continuous. This shows derivatives cannot have simple jump discontinuities.
