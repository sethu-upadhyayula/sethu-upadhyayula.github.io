Chapter 2: Sequences and Series

Sequences are the fundamental vehicles for passing to limits in analysis. This chapter develops the rigorous theory of convergence, introducing the powerful tools of $\limsup$ and $\liminf$, Cauchy sequences, and series. Everything here rests on the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.

Chapter Overview

2.1 - Sequences of Real Numbers

We define $\limsup$ and $\liminf$ as limits of suprema and infima of tails: $\limsup_{n\to\infty}a_n = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sup_{k\geq n}a_k$. A sequence converges iff $\liminf=\limsup$. Bolzano-Weierstrass follows: every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence.

2.2 - Limits and Convergence

The Cauchy criterion characterises convergent sequences without knowing the limit: $(a_n)$ converges iff it is Cauchy. The equivalence of Cauchy completeness and the least upper bound property is established. We discuss absolute vs conditional convergence and the Riemann rearrangement theorem.

2.3 - Series and Convergence Tests

Rigorous proofs of comparison, ratio, root, alternating series (Leibniz), Dirichlet, Abel, and Cauchy condensation tests. The condensation test gives a clean proof that $\sum n^{-p}$ converges iff $p>1$.
