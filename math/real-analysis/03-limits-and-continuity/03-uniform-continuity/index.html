3.3 Uniform Continuity

Pointwise continuity allows the tolerance $\delta$ to depend on both $\varepsilon$ and the base point $x$. Uniform continuity demands a single $\delta$ that works everywhere simultaneously — a much stronger condition, automatically satisfied on compact domains.

Definition 3.3.1 — Uniform Continuity

$f: E \to \mathbb{R}$ is uniformly continuous on $E$ if: $\forall\varepsilon > 0,\,\exists\delta > 0$ such that $\forall x, y \in E$: $|x - y| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - f(y)| < \varepsilon$. The key distinction from pointwise continuity is that $\delta$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on the particular points $x, y$.

Example 3.3.2 — Uniform vs Non-Uniform

$f(x) = x^2$ on $(0, \infty)$: not uniformly continuous. For any $\delta > 0$, take $x = 1/\delta$, $y = 1/\delta + \delta/2$; then $|x-y| = \delta/2 < \delta$ but $|f(x)-f(y)| \approx 1$.

$f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, M]$ for any $M < \infty$: uniformly continuous (compact domain).

Theorem 3.3.3 — Heine-Cantor Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on a compact set $K$, then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$.

Proof sketch: Suppose not. Then $\exists\varepsilon_0 > 0$ and sequences $(x_n), (y_n)$ in $K$ with $|x_n - y_n| < 1/n$ but $|f(x_n) - f(y_n)| \ge \varepsilon_0$. By compactness, pass to a subsequence $x_{n_k} \to c \in K$. Then $y_{n_k} \to c$ also. By continuity, $f(x_{n_k}) \to f(c)$ and $f(y_{n_k}) \to f(c)$, so $|f(x_{n_k}) - f(y_{n_k})| \to 0$, contradicting $\ge \varepsilon_0$.

Theorem 3.3.4 — Extension Theorem

If $f: E \to \mathbb{R}$ is uniformly continuous on $E$ and $(x_n)$ is a Cauchy sequence in $E$, then $(f(x_n))$ is also Cauchy. Consequently, $f$ extends uniquely to a uniformly continuous function on $\bar{E}$. This is used to extend functions defined on dense subsets.

Remark 3.3.5 — Lipschitz Implies Uniform Continuity

If $|f(x) - f(y)| \le L|x - y|$ for all $x, y$ (Lipschitz condition with constant $L$), then $f$ is uniformly continuous: given $\varepsilon$, take $\delta = \varepsilon/L$.

The converse fails: $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ on $[0,1]$ is uniformly continuous (compact domain) but not Lipschitz near 0, since $f'(x) = 1/(2\sqrt{x}) \to \infty$.
