3.2 Continuity

A function is continuous at a point when the limit at that point equals the function value - there is no jump, hole, or oscillation. Three equivalent characterisations (analytic, sequential, topological) give complementary tools for proofs.

Definition 3.2.1 - Continuity at a Point

$f$ is continuous at $c$ if $\lim_{x\to c} f(x) = f(c)$, i.e.\ $\forall\varepsilon > 0,\,\exists\delta > 0,\,|x-c| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x)-f(c)| < \varepsilon$.

Equivalent sequential form: $x_n \to c \Rightarrow f(x_n) \to f(c)$ for every sequence $(x_n)$.

Theorem 3.2.2 - Topological Characterisation

$f: X \to Y$ is continuous (on all of $X$) if and only if for every open set $V \subseteq Y$, the preimage $f^{-1}(V)$ is open in $X$. Equivalently, preimages of closed sets are closed. This formulation is basis-free and extends directly to metric spaces and topological spaces.

Algebraic and Compositional Stability

Theorem 3.2.3 - Algebra of Continuous Functions

If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $c$, so are $f \pm g$, $fg$, and $f/g$ (provided $g(c) \ne 0$). Compositions: if $f$ is continuous at $c$ and $g$ is continuous at $f(c)$, then $g \circ f$ is continuous at $c$.

Consequence: all polynomials and rational functions are continuous on their domains.

Theorem 3.2.4 - Pasting Lemma

Let $X = A \cup B$ where $A$ and $B$ are both closed (or both open). If $f|_A$ and $f|_B$ are continuous and agree on $A \cap B$, then $f$ is continuous on $X$. This allows functions to be defined piecewise.

Key Theorems

Theorem 3.2.5 - Intermediate Value Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $y$ is any value between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then $\exists c \in (a,b)$ with $f(c) = y$. Proof: the continuous image of a connected set (here $[a,b]$) is connected. In $\mathbb{R}$, connected subsets are intervals, so $f([a,b])$ is an interval containing $f(a)$ and $f(b)$.

Theorem 3.2.6 - Extreme Value Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on the compact set $K$, then $f$ attains its maximum and minimum on $K$: $\exists x^*, x_* \in K$ with $f(x^*) = \sup_K f$ and $f(x_*) = \inf_K f$. Proof: the continuous image of a compact set is compact; compact subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are closed and bounded, hence contain their supremum and infimum.

Definition 3.2.7 - Types of Discontinuity

At a discontinuity $c$ of $f$:

  • Removable: $\lim_{x\to c} f(x)$ exists but $\ne f(c)$ (or $f$ undefined at $c$).
  • Jump: one-sided limits exist but differ: $f(c^-) \ne f(c^+)$.
  • Essential: at least one one-sided limit does not exist (e.g.\ $\sin(1/x)$ at 0).
