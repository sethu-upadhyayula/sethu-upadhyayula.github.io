6.2 Power Series

A power series $\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n$ defines a function on its interval of convergence, and enjoys all the good properties of polynomials — differentiability and integrability term-by-term — inside that interval.

Theorem 6.2.1 — Cauchy-Hadamard Formula

The radius of convergence of $\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n$ is $$R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n\to\infty} |a_n|^{1/n}}$$ (with $R = \infty$ if $\limsup = 0$ and $R = 0$ if $\limsup = \infty$).

The series converges absolutely for $|x-c| < R$ and diverges for $|x-c| > R$. At $|x-c| = R$, convergence must be checked case-by-case.

Theorem 6.2.2 — Uniform Convergence on Compact Sub-Intervals

For any $0 < r < R$, the power series converges uniformly on $[c-r, c+r]$. Proof: for $|x-c| \le r$, $|a_n(x-c)^n| \le |a_n|r^n$, and $\sum |a_n|r^n < \infty$ since $r < R$ (Weierstrass M-test).

Theorem 6.2.3 — Term-by-Term Differentiation and Integration

If $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x-c)^n$ has radius of convergence $R > 0$, then:

  • $f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n (x-c)^{n-1}$, also with radius of convergence $R$.
  • $\int_c^x f(t)\,dt = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \dfrac{a_n}{n+1}(x-c)^{n+1}$, also with radius of convergence $R$.
Remark 6.2.4 — Analytic Functions

A function is called analytic at $c$ if it equals its Taylor series in some neighbourhood of $c$. By the term-by-term differentiation theorem, every power series sum is $C^\infty$ and analytic on its open interval of convergence. (Not every $C^\infty$ function is analytic — see Section 4.3.)

Theorem 6.2.5 — Abel's Theorem

If $\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n R^n$ converges to $L$, then $\lim_{x \to R^-} \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n = L$. That is, the power series is left-continuous at the boundary of convergence if the series converges there. Application: $\ln 2 = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^{n+1}/n$, obtained by Abel's theorem applied to $\ln(1+x) = \sum (-1)^{n+1}x^n/n$ at $x = 1$.
