7.3 Compactness

Compactness is one of the most powerful notions in analysis: it is the abstract property that makes finite-dimensional intuition ("closed and bounded") work, and it is precisely the hypothesis needed for existence theorems such as the extreme value theorem.

Open Covers and Compactness

Definition 7.3.1 - Open Cover and Compactness

An open cover of a subset $K$ of a metric space $X$ is a collection $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}$ of open sets with $K \subseteq \bigcup_{\alpha \in A} U_\alpha$.

$K$ is compact if every open cover of $K$ has a finite subcover: there exist $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_n \in A$ with $K \subseteq U_{\alpha_1}\cup\cdots\cup U_{\alpha_n}$.

Definition 7.3.2 - Sequential Compactness

$K \subseteq X$ is sequentially compact if every sequence in $K$ has a subsequence converging to a point of $K$.

Theorem 7.3.3 - Equivalence of Compactness Notions in Metric Spaces

For a subset $K$ of a metric space $(X,d)$, the following are equivalent:

  1. $K$ is compact (every open cover has a finite subcover);
  2. $K$ is sequentially compact;
  3. $K$ is complete and totally bounded.
Definition 7.3.4 - Total Boundedness

$K \subseteq X$ is totally bounded if for every $\varepsilon>0$, $K$ can be covered by finitely many balls of radius $\varepsilon$: there exist $x_1,\dots,x_n \in K$ with $K \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^n B(x_i,\varepsilon)$. Total boundedness is strictly stronger than boundedness in general (though equivalent in $\mathbb{R}^n$).

Proof Sketch of (2) $\implies$ (3) $\implies$ (1)

(2)$\implies$(3): If $K$ is sequentially compact, it is complete (any Cauchy sequence has a convergent subsequence by sequential compactness, and a Cauchy sequence with a convergent subsequence converges). If $K$ were not totally bounded, there would be $\varepsilon>0$ and a sequence $(x_n)$ with $d(x_n,x_m)\ge\varepsilon$ for all $n\ne m$ - such a sequence has no Cauchy (hence no convergent) subsequence, contradicting sequential compactness.

(3)$\implies$(1): Given an open cover with no finite subcover, total boundedness lets one repeatedly bisect $K$ into smaller totally bounded pieces, none of which admit a finite subcover, producing a nested sequence of pieces with diameters $\to 0$. Completeness gives a common point $x$ in all pieces; $x$ lies in some cover element $U$, and for small enough pieces the whole piece lies in $U$ - a finite (in fact single-set) subcover, a contradiction.

Heine-Borel Theorem

Theorem 7.3.5 - Heine-Borel Theorem

A subset $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded.

Proof Sketch

($\Rightarrow$) Compact sets in any metric space are closed and bounded: boundedness follows from total boundedness, and closedness follows because a limit point of $K$ not in $K$ would yield a sequence in $K$ converging outside $K$, with no subsequence converging in $K$.

($\Leftarrow$) If $K$ is closed and bounded, $K$ is contained in some closed cube $[-M,M]^n$. By repeated bisection (the Bolzano-Weierstrass argument in $\mathbb{R}^n$), every sequence in $[-M,M]^n$ has a convergent subsequence; since $K$ is closed, the limit lies in $K$. Hence $K$ is sequentially compact, so compact by Theorem 7.3.3. $\square$

Example 7.3.6 - Failure in Infinite Dimensions

The closed unit ball of $(C[a,b],\|\cdot\|_\infty)$ is closed and bounded but not compact: the sequence $f_n(x)=x^n$ on $[0,1]$ lies in the unit ball but has no uniformly convergent subsequence (its pointwise limit, the indicator of $\{1\}$, is discontinuous). So Heine-Borel is special to finite-dimensional spaces.

Compactness and Continuity

Theorem 7.3.7 - Continuous Image of a Compact Set is Compact

If $f: X \to Y$ is continuous and $K \subseteq X$ is compact, then $f(K)$ is compact in $Y$.

Proof

Let $\{V_\alpha\}$ be an open cover of $f(K)$. Since $f$ is continuous, each $f^{-1}(V_\alpha)$ is open (Theorem 7.1.10), and these sets cover $K$. By compactness of $K$, finitely many $f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_1}),\dots,f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_n})$ cover $K$, so $V_{\alpha_1},\dots,V_{\alpha_n}$ cover $f(K)$. $\square$

Theorem 7.3.8 - Extreme Value Theorem

If $K$ is a nonempty compact metric space and $f: K \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, then $f$ is bounded and attains a maximum and a minimum value on $K$.

Proof

By Theorem 7.3.7, $f(K) \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is compact, hence closed and bounded by Heine-Borel (applied in $\mathbb{R}=\mathbb{R}^1$). Let $M=\sup f(K)$, which is finite since $f(K)$ is bounded. Since $f(K)$ is closed, $M \in f(K)$, i.e.\ $M=f(x^*)$ for some $x^* \in K$ - so $f$ attains its maximum. The argument for the minimum is analogous. $\square$

The Arzelà-Ascoli Theorem

Definition 7.3.9 - Equicontinuity

A family $\mathcal{F} \subseteq C[a,b]$ is equicontinuous if for every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that for all $f \in \mathcal{F}$ and $x,y \in [a,b]$, $$|x-y|<\delta \implies |f(x)-f(y)|<\varepsilon.$$ The family is uniformly bounded if there exists $M$ with $|f(x)| \le M$ for all $f \in \mathcal{F}$ and $x \in [a,b]$.

Theorem 7.3.10 - Arzelà-Ascoli Theorem

A subset $\mathcal{F} \subseteq C[a,b]$ has compact closure in $(C[a,b],\|\cdot\|_\infty)$ if and only if $\mathcal{F}$ is uniformly bounded and equicontinuous. Equivalently, every sequence $(f_n)$ in $\mathcal{F}$ has a uniformly convergent subsequence.

Remark 7.3.11 - Significance

Arzelà-Ascoli is the infinite-dimensional analogue of Heine-Borel: it identifies which subsets of $C[a,b]$ are "small enough" (precompact) despite the failure of Heine-Borel in this space (Example 7.3.6). It is the key compactness tool behind existence theorems for ODEs and PDEs (e.g.\ the Peano existence theorem), and underlies the Ascoli-type arguments used throughout functional analysis.
