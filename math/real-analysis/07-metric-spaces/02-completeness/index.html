7.2 Completeness

Completeness of a metric space says that the space has "no holes" — every sequence whose terms get arbitrarily close together actually converges to a point of the space. This single property underlies the most important existence theorems in analysis.

Cauchy Sequences and Completeness

Definition 7.2.1 — Cauchy Sequence

A sequence $(x_n)$ in a metric space $(X,d)$ is Cauchy if for every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $$m,n \ge N \implies d(x_m,x_n) < \varepsilon.$$

Theorem 7.2.2 — Convergent $\implies$ Cauchy

Every convergent sequence in a metric space is Cauchy.

Proof

Suppose $x_n \to x$. Given $\varepsilon>0$, choose $N$ such that $d(x_n,x)<\varepsilon/2$ for all $n \ge N$. Then for $m,n \ge N$, $$d(x_m,x_n) \le d(x_m,x) + d(x,x_n) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon.$$ $\square$

Definition 7.2.3 — Complete Metric Space

A metric space $(X,d)$ is complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to a point of $X$. The converse of Theorem 7.2.2 need not hold — Cauchy sequences may fail to converge if the "limit" is missing from the space.

Example 7.2.4 — $\mathbb{R}$ is Complete, $\mathbb{Q}$ is Not

$(\mathbb{R}, |\cdot|)$ is complete: this is essentially the completeness axiom (every Cauchy sequence is bounded, so by Bolzano–Weierstrass it has a convergent subsequence; a Cauchy sequence with a convergent subsequence converges to the same limit).

$(\mathbb{Q}, |\cdot|)$ is not complete: the sequence of rational truncations of $\sqrt{2}$ (e.g.\ $1, 1.4, 1.41, 1.414, \dots$) is Cauchy in $\mathbb{Q}$ but its limit $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$.

Example 7.2.5 — $(C[a,b],\|\cdot\|_\infty)$ is Complete

Let $(f_n)$ be Cauchy in $(C[a,b],\|\cdot\|_\infty)$. For each fixed $x$, $|f_n(x)-f_m(x)| \le \|f_n-f_m\|_\infty \to 0$, so $(f_n(x))$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$ and converges to some $f(x)$ — this defines $f$ pointwise.

Given $\varepsilon>0$, choose $N$ so that $\|f_n-f_m\|_\infty<\varepsilon$ for $n,m\ge N$. Letting $m\to\infty$ gives $|f_n(x)-f(x)|\le\varepsilon$ for all $x$ and $n \ge N$, i.e.\ $f_n \to f$ uniformly.

Since each $f_n$ is continuous and $f_n \to f$ uniformly, $f$ is continuous (uniform limit theorem), so $f \in C[a,b]$. Hence $(C[a,b],\|\cdot\|_\infty)$ is complete — it is a Banach space.

The Banach Fixed Point Theorem

Definition 7.2.6 — Contraction Mapping

A map $T: X \to X$ on a metric space $(X,d)$ is a contraction if there exists $k \in [0,1)$ such that $$d(T(x),T(y)) \le k\, d(x,y) \quad \text{for all } x,y \in X.$$

Theorem 7.2.7 — Banach Fixed Point Theorem (Contraction Mapping Principle)

Let $(X,d)$ be a nonempty complete metric space and $T: X \to X$ a contraction with constant $k \in [0,1)$. Then $T$ has a unique fixed point $x^* \in X$, i.e.\ $T(x^*)=x^*$. Moreover, for any $x_0 \in X$, the iterates $x_{n+1}=T(x_n)$ converge to $x^*$, with the error bound $$d(x_n,x^*) \le \frac{k^n}{1-k}\,d(x_1,x_0).$$

Proof

Step 1: $(x_n)$ is Cauchy. By induction, $d(x_{n+1},x_n) \le k^n d(x_1,x_0)$. For $m>n$, by the triangle inequality and the geometric series, $$d(x_m,x_n) \le \sum_{j=n}^{m-1} d(x_{j+1},x_j) \le \sum_{j=n}^{m-1} k^j d(x_1,x_0) \le \frac{k^n}{1-k}d(x_1,x_0),$$ which $\to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. So $(x_n)$ is Cauchy.

Step 2: Existence. Since $X$ is complete, $x_n \to x^*$ for some $x^* \in X$. Since $T$ is a contraction, it is continuous, so $$T(x^*) = T\left(\lim_n x_n\right) = \lim_n T(x_n) = \lim_n x_{n+1} = x^*.$$ Thus $x^*$ is a fixed point.

Step 3: Uniqueness. If $T(y)=y$ as well, then $$d(x^*,y) = d(T(x^*),T(y)) \le k\,d(x^*,y),$$ and since $k<1$ this forces $d(x^*,y)=0$, i.e.\ $x^*=y$.

Error bound: letting $m \to \infty$ in Step 1's inequality gives $d(x^*,x_n) \le \frac{k^n}{1-k}d(x_1,x_0)$. $\square$

Completion of a Metric Space

Theorem 7.2.8 — Existence of a Completion

Every metric space $(X,d)$ has a completion: a complete metric space $(\hat{X},\hat{d})$ together with an isometry $\iota: X \to \hat{X}$ such that $\iota(X)$ is dense in $\hat{X}$. The completion is unique up to isometry. (Sketch: $\hat{X}$ is constructed as the set of equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences in $X$, two sequences being equivalent if their "interleaving" is Cauchy, with $\hat{d}([(x_n)],[(y_n)]) = \lim_n d(x_n,y_n)$.) The completion of $\mathbb{Q}$ under $|\cdot|$ is $\mathbb{R}$.

Remark 7.2.9 — Picard Iteration and ODEs

The Banach fixed point theorem underlies the Picard–Lindelöf theorem on existence and uniqueness of solutions to the IVP $y'=f(t,y)$, $y(t_0)=y_0$. If $f$ is Lipschitz in $y$, the integral operator $$(Ty)(t) = y_0 + \int_{t_0}^t f(s,y(s))\,ds$$ is a contraction on a suitable closed subset of $C[t_0-h,t_0+h]$ (with the sup metric, for small $h$), and its unique fixed point is the solution. The iterates $y_{n+1}=Ty_n$ are the classical Picard iterates, and the error bound from Theorem 7.2.7 quantifies their rate of convergence.
