Chapter 2: Predicate Logic

Predicate logic — also called first-order logic (FOL) — extends propositional logic by introducing predicates that can express properties of individual objects, and quantifiers that range over a domain. This single extension dramatically increases expressive power: arithmetic, set theory, geometry, and virtually all of mathematics can be formalised within first-order logic.

The price of this power is that first-order logic is not decidable in general (though it is complete, as Gödel showed). Understanding its syntax, semantics, and proof theory is the core of mathematical logic.

Chapter Overview

2.1 — Predicates and Quantifiers

Predicates are property-expressing symbols like $\text{Prime}(x)$ or $x < y$. The universal quantifier $\forall x$ ("for all $x$") and existential quantifier $\exists x$ ("there exists $x$") bind variables and allow statements about all or some elements of a domain. We study negation of quantifiers, free/bound variables, and nested quantifiers.

2.2 — Formal Language

A first-order language is determined by its signature: a collection of constant, function, and predicate symbols. We define terms (denoting objects) and well-formed formulas (wffs) by induction. A sentence is a wff with no free variables.

2.3 — Interpretations and Models

A structure $\mathcal{M}$ interprets the language over a nonempty domain $M$. We define the satisfaction relation $\mathcal{M} \models \phi[s]$ (with variable assignment $s$) inductively. A model of a theory $T$ is a structure satisfying all sentences of $T$.

2.4 — Proof Systems

We examine three equivalent proof systems: Hilbert-style axiom schemas, natural deduction (with introduction and elimination rules for each connective), and Gentzen's sequent calculus. Each defines a notion of provability $\Gamma \vdash \phi$.
