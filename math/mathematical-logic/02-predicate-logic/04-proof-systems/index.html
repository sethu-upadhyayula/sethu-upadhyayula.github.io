2.4 Proof Systems

A proof system gives a purely syntactic (symbol-manipulation) account of provability. Three major systems are in common use, all provably equivalent for first-order logic.

Hilbert-Style System

A Hilbert system consists of axiom schemas (infinitely many axioms obtained by substituting formulas into templates) together with a single inference rule:

Modus Ponens (MP): from $\phi$ and $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$, derive $\psi$.

Representative axiom schemas include tautologies like $\phi \Rightarrow (\psi \Rightarrow \phi)$, and quantifier axioms such as $\forall x\,\phi(x) \Rightarrow \phi(t)$ (universal instantiation with term $t$ free for $x$).

A proof from hypotheses $\Gamma$ is a finite sequence $\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_n$ where each $\phi_i$ is an axiom, a member of $\Gamma$, or follows from earlier formulas by MP. We write $\Gamma \vdash \phi$ if such a proof exists with last line $\phi$.

Natural Deduction

Introduced by Gentzen, natural deduction has introduction ($I$) and elimination ($E$) rules for each connective:

  • $\wedge$-I: from $\phi$ and $\psi$, derive $\phi \wedge \psi$.
  • $\wedge$-E: from $\phi \wedge \psi$, derive $\phi$ (or $\psi$).
  • $\Rightarrow$-I: if $\psi$ is derivable from assumption $\phi$, derive $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$ (discharging $\phi$).
  • $\Rightarrow$-E (= MP): from $\phi$ and $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$, derive $\psi$.
  • $\forall$-I: if $\phi(x)$ is provable for arbitrary $x$ (not free in hypotheses), derive $\forall x\,\phi(x)$.
  • $\forall$-E: from $\forall x\,\phi(x)$, derive $\phi(t)$ for any term $t$.

Natural deduction proofs closely mirror the structure of informal mathematical arguments.

Sequent Calculus (Gentzen LK)

A sequent has the form $\Gamma \vdash \Delta$ where $\Gamma$ (antecedent) and $\Delta$ (succedent) are finite multisets of formulas. Intuitively it means: if all formulas in $\Gamma$ hold, then at least one in $\Delta$ holds.

Rules are given for introducing each connective on the left and right of $\vdash$. Cut-elimination (Gentzen's Hauptsatz) states that the cut rule is admissible, giving a subformula property: every provable sequent has a proof using only subformulas of the conclusion.

Definition — Consistency

A set of formulas $\Gamma$ is consistent if $\Gamma \not\vdash \bot$ (the falsum / contradiction). It is inconsistent if $\Gamma \vdash \bot$, equivalently if $\Gamma \vdash \phi$ for every formula $\phi$.
