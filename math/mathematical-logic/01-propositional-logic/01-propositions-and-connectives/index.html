1.1 Propositions and Connectives

Definition — Proposition

A proposition (or statement) is a declarative sentence that has a definite truth value: either true (T) or false (F). Examples: "2 is prime" (T), "7 is even" (F). Non-examples: "Is it raining?" (question), "Close the door" (command), "$x > 3$" (depends on $x$).

Propositions are divided into atomic propositions (indivisible, denoted $P, Q, R, \ldots$) and compound propositions formed from atoms by connectives.

The Five Standard Connectives

Connective — Negation

$\neg P$ (read "not $P$") is true when $P$ is false, and false when $P$ is true. It flips the truth value.

Example: If $P$ = "It is raining", then $\neg P$ = "It is not raining".

Connective — Conjunction

$P \wedge Q$ (read "$P$ and $Q$") is true precisely when both $P$ and $Q$ are true.

Example: "It is raining $\wedge$ it is cold" is true only when both conditions hold.

Connective — Disjunction

$P \vee Q$ (read "$P$ or $Q$") is true when at least one of $P$, $Q$ is true. This is the inclusive or.

Example: "I will take the bus $\vee$ I will walk" is false only when both are false.

Connective — Implication

$P \Rightarrow Q$ (read "if $P$ then $Q$", or "$P$ implies $Q$") is false only when $P$ is true and $Q$ is false; it is true in all other cases.

The proposition $P$ is called the hypothesis (antecedent) and $Q$ the conclusion (consequent).

Key equivalence: $P \Rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \vee Q$.

Connective — Biconditional

$P \Leftrightarrow Q$ (read "$P$ if and only if $Q$") is true when $P$ and $Q$ have the same truth value.

Equivalently, $P \Leftrightarrow Q \equiv (P \Rightarrow Q) \wedge (Q \Rightarrow P)$.

Precedence

Operator Precedence (highest to lowest)

$$\neg \;>\; \wedge \;>\; \vee \;>\; \Rightarrow \;>\; \Leftrightarrow$$ So $\neg P \wedge Q \vee R \Rightarrow S$ parses as $((\neg P) \wedge Q) \vee R) \Rightarrow S$. When in doubt, use parentheses.

Converse and Contrapositive

Theorem — Contrapositive Equivalence

For any propositions $P$ and $Q$:

  • Converse of $P \Rightarrow Q$: the statement $Q \Rightarrow P$. This is not logically equivalent to $P \Rightarrow Q$ in general.
  • Contrapositive of $P \Rightarrow Q$: the statement $\neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P$. This is logically equivalent to $P \Rightarrow Q$.

$$P \Rightarrow Q \;\equiv\; \neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P$$

Translating English

Example — English to Logic

Let $P$ = "It rains", $Q$ = "I carry an umbrella".

  • "If it rains, I carry an umbrella": $P \Rightarrow Q$.
  • "I carry an umbrella only if it rains": $Q \Rightarrow P$.
  • "I carry an umbrella if and only if it rains": $P \Leftrightarrow Q$.
  • "It rains but I do not carry an umbrella": $P \wedge \neg Q$.
  • "Either it does not rain or I carry an umbrella": $\neg P \vee Q \equiv P \Rightarrow Q$.

Notice the last two lines demonstrate $P \Rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \vee Q$ in English.
