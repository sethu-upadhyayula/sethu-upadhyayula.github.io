Chapter 1: Propositional Logic

Propositional logic — also called sentential logic or the propositional calculus — is the study of compound statements built from atomic propositions using logical connectives. Each statement has a definite truth value: true or false. This binary discipline provides the simplest setting in which to develop the notions of logical equivalence, tautology, and formal proof.

Although propositional logic cannot express properties of individual objects or quantify over domains, it is the essential foundation for predicate logic, proof theory, and every formal system we will study later.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Propositions and Connectives

We define atomic propositions and the five standard connectives — negation $\neg$, conjunction $\wedge$, disjunction $\vee$, implication $\Rightarrow$, and biconditional $\Leftrightarrow$ — together with their precedence rules and the contrapositive equivalence.

1.2 — Truth Tables

With $n$ propositional variables there are $2^n$ rows. We build complete truth tables for all connectives, verify De Morgan's laws, and define logical equivalence $A \equiv B$ as identical truth-value columns.

1.3 — Tautologies and Contradictions

A tautology is true in every interpretation; a contradiction is false in every interpretation. We catalogue the key equivalences — double negation, distribution, absorption, idempotency — and prove them via truth tables or algebraic argument.

1.4 — Normal Forms

Every propositional formula can be converted to Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) or Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF). We explain the minterm/maxterm construction and introduce the resolution rule, which underlies automated theorem proving.
