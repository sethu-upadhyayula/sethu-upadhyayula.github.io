Chapter 5: Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems

In 1931, Kurt Gödel proved two theorems that shattered Hilbert's programme of axiomatising all of mathematics. They show that any sufficiently expressive formal system is either incomplete (has true statements that cannot be proved) or inconsistent (proves a contradiction).

Prerequisite: Gödel Numbering

Definition 5.1 - Gödel Numbering

A Gödel numbering is an effective assignment of a unique natural number $\ulcorner \phi \urcorner$ (the Gödel number) to every formula $\phi$ of the formal language. This encoding allows the formal system to talk about its own formulas as numbers.

Given such an encoding, a predicate $\text{Prov}_T(n)$ - meaning "the formula with Gödel number $n$ is provable in $T$" - is expressible as a formula of arithmetic within $T$ itself.

First Incompleteness Theorem

Theorem 5.1 - Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem (1931)

Let $T$ be a consistent formal system that is sufficiently expressive (i.e., interprets Peano Arithmetic). Then there exists a sentence $G_T$ such that: $$T \nvdash G_T \quad \text{and} \quad T \nvdash \neg G_T.$$

That is, $T$ is incomplete: there are true statements of arithmetic that $T$ cannot prove.

Construction - The Gödel Sentence

The Gödel sentence $G_T$ is constructed to assert its own unprovability: $$G_T \equiv \neg\,\text{Prov}_T(\ulcorner G_T \urcorner).$$ In plain language: "$G_T$ says: I am not provable in $T$."

This is an arithmetical statement (not merely self-referential rhetoric), made possible by Gödel numbering.

Proof Sketch - Why $G_T$ is Independent of $T$

Case 1: Suppose $T \vdash G_T$. Then $G_T$ is provable in $T$, so $\text{Prov}_T(\ulcorner G_T \urcorner)$ is true. But $G_T$ asserts $\neg\,\text{Prov}_T(\ulcorner G_T \urcorner)$. Since $T$ proves $G_T$ and $G_T$ is false under this assumption, $T$ proves a false statement - contradicting the consistency of $T$.

Case 2: Suppose $T \vdash \neg G_T$. Then $G_T$ is not provable in $T$, making $G_T$ true (it correctly says it is unprovable). So $T$ proves a false sentence $\neg G_T$ - contradicting the soundness (or $\omega$-consistency) of $T$.

In both cases we reach a contradiction, so neither $G_T$ nor $\neg G_T$ is provable in $T$. $\square$

Second Incompleteness Theorem

Theorem 5.2 - Gödel's Second Incompleteness Theorem

Under the same hypotheses as Theorem 5.1, let $\text{Con}(T)$ be the arithmetical statement asserting the consistency of $T$ (i.e., $\neg\,\text{Prov}_T(\ulcorner \bot \urcorner)$). Then: $$T \nvdash \text{Con}(T).$$

A consistent theory that is strong enough to interpret arithmetic cannot prove its own consistency.

Proof Idea

The proof of the First Theorem can be formalised within $T$. Inside $T$, one can derive: $$\text{Con}(T) \Rightarrow \neg\,\text{Prov}_T(\ulcorner G_T \urcorner),$$ i.e., $T$ proves: "if $T$ is consistent then $G_T$ is not provable." But $G_T \equiv \neg\,\text{Prov}_T(\ulcorner G_T \urcorner)$, so: $$T \vdash \text{Con}(T) \Rightarrow G_T.$$

If $T \vdash \text{Con}(T)$, then $T \vdash G_T$, contradicting the First Theorem. Therefore $T \nvdash \text{Con}(T)$. $\square$

Significance and Consequences

Hilbert's Programme

David Hilbert's programme (early 20th century) sought to axiomatise all of mathematics in a complete, consistent, decidable formal system, and to prove its consistency by finitary means. Gödel's theorems refuted this: no such system can be both complete and consistent, and no sufficiently strong consistent system can prove its own consistency.

Truth vs. Provability

The Gödel sentence $G_T$ is true (in the standard model of arithmetic) but unprovable in $T$. This shows that mathematical truth is strictly larger than what any fixed formal system can prove. Every consistent extension of Peano Arithmetic is likewise incomplete.

Relationship to Computability

Turing's proof that the Halting Problem is undecidable (1936) is deeply connected: the undecidability of $HP$ implies the incompleteness of any complete consistent extension of arithmetic, and vice versa. Both results arise from the same diagonal argument applied to self-reference. Church's undecidability of first-order logic (Entscheidungsproblem) is another facet of the same phenomenon.

Examples of Independent Statements

The following statements are independent of standard axiom systems:

  • The Continuum Hypothesis ($2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1$): independent of ZFC (Cohen, Gödel).
  • The Axiom of Choice: independent of ZF (Cohen, Gödel).
  • Goodstein's theorem: true (provable in ZFC) but unprovable in Peano Arithmetic.
  • Paris-Harrington theorem: a combinatorial statement unprovable in Peano Arithmetic.

These are natural mathematical statements, showing incompleteness is not an artificial curiosity.
