2.4 Change of Basis

Definition - Change-of-Basis Matrix

Let $\mathcal{B}=(b_1,\ldots,b_n)$ and $\mathcal{B}'=(b_1',\ldots,b_n')$ be two bases for $V$. The change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{B}'$ to $\mathcal{B}$ is $$P = [I]_{\mathcal{B}'}^{\mathcal{B}},$$ whose $j$-th column is $[b_j']_{\mathcal{B}}$ (the coordinates of $b_j'$ in the old basis $\mathcal{B}$). Then: $$[v]_{\mathcal{B}} = P\,[v]_{\mathcal{B}'} \qquad\text{for all }v\in V.$$

Theorem - Similarity Relation

Let $T:V\to V$ be a linear operator and $\mathcal{B},\mathcal{B}'$ two bases for $V$, with $P=[I]_{\mathcal{B}'}^{\mathcal{B}}$ the change-of-basis matrix. Then: $$[T]_{\mathcal{B}'} = P^{-1}[T]_{\mathcal{B}}\,P.$$ Two matrices $A$ and $B$ are called similar (written $A\sim B$) if there exists an invertible $P$ with $B=P^{-1}AP$. Similar matrices represent the same linear map in different bases.

Example - Finding $P$

In $\mathbb{R}^2$, let $\mathcal{B}=\{e_1,e_2\}$ (standard) and $\mathcal{B}'=\{(1,1),(1,-1)\}$. Express the new basis in terms of old: $(1,1)=1\cdot e_1+1\cdot e_2$ and $(1,-1)=1\cdot e_1+(-1)\cdot e_2$. So: $$P = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad P^{-1}=\frac{1}{-2}\begin{pmatrix}-1&-1\\-1&1\end{pmatrix}=\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}.$$ If $T$ has matrix $A$ in the standard basis, then $[T]_{\mathcal{B}'}=P^{-1}AP$.

Remark - Why Change of Basis Matters

The goal of diagonalization (§4.2) is precisely to find a basis $\mathcal{B}'$ in which the matrix of $T$ is diagonal - i.e., a basis of eigenvectors. The change-of-basis matrix $P$ in that setting has eigenvectors of $T$ as its columns, and $P^{-1}AP=D$ (diagonal).

Remark - Invariants of Similar Matrices

Similar matrices share the same: determinant, trace, characteristic polynomial, eigenvalues, rank, and nullity. These are all intrinsic properties of the linear operator $T$, independent of the choice of basis.
