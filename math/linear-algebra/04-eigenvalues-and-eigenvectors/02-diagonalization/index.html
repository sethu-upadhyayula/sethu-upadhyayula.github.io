4.2 Diagonalisation

Definition — Diagonalisable Matrix

A matrix $A\in M_n(\mathbb{C})$ is diagonalisable if it is similar to a diagonal matrix: there exists an invertible $P$ and diagonal $D=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)$ such that:

$$A = PDP^{-1},\qquad\text{equivalently}\quad AP=PD.$$

The columns of $P$ are linearly independent eigenvectors of $A$, and the diagonal entries of $D$ are the corresponding eigenvalues. Equivalently, $A$ is diagonalisable if and only if $\mathbb{C}^n$ (resp. $\mathbb{R}^n$) has a basis consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.

Theorem — Sufficient Condition: Distinct Eigenvalues

If $A\in M_n(\mathbb{C})$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues, then $A$ is diagonalisable. (The converse is false: repeated eigenvalues do not necessarily prevent diagonalisability.)

Theorem — Necessary and Sufficient Condition

$A$ is diagonalisable if and only if for every eigenvalue $\lambda$, the geometric multiplicity equals the algebraic multiplicity: $\dim\ker(A-\lambda I)=$ (multiplicity of $\lambda$ as root of $p$).

Example — Diagonalisable vs Non-Diagonalisable (Defective)

Diagonalisable: $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&2\end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_1=1$, $\lambda_2=2$ (distinct), so it is diagonalisable. Eigenvectors: $v_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$, $v_2=\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$. Then $P=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$, $D=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}$.

Defective (non-diagonalisable): $B=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ has $p(\lambda)=(\lambda-1)^2$, so $\lambda=1$ with algebraic multiplicity 2. But $\ker(B-I)=\ker\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}=\operatorname{span}\{e_1\}$ has dimension 1. Geometric multiplicity $1\neq2$, so $B$ is not diagonalisable.

Theorem — Computing Matrix Powers

If $A=PDP^{-1}$, then for any $k\ge0$:

$$A^k = PD^kP^{-1},\qquad\text{where }D^k=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1^k,\ldots,\lambda_n^k).$$

This reduces computing $A^k$ to a diagonal power, which is trivial — a major computational advantage over repeated matrix multiplication.

Example — Fibonacci Sequence via Diagonalisation

The Fibonacci recurrence $F_{n+1}=F_n+F_{n-1}$ can be written as: $$\begin{pmatrix}F_{n+1}\\F_n\end{pmatrix}=\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}}_{A}\begin{pmatrix}F_n\\F_{n-1}\end{pmatrix}.$$ The characteristic polynomial of $A$ is $\lambda^2-\lambda-1=0$, with roots (the golden ratio and its conjugate): $$\lambda_\pm=\frac{1\pm\sqrt5}{2}.$$ Diagonalising $A=PDP^{-1}$ and applying the power formula yields the closed-form Binet's formula: $$F_n=\frac{1}{\sqrt5}\left[\left(\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}\right)^n-\left(\frac{1-\sqrt5}{2}\right)^n\right].$$
