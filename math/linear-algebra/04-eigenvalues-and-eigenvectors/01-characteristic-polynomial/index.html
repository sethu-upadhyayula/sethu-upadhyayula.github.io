4.1 Characteristic Polynomial

Definition — Eigenvalue and Eigenvector

Let $A\in M_n(\mathbb{R})$. A scalar $\lambda\in\mathbb{C}$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ if there exists a nonzero vector $v\in\mathbb{C}^n$ such that:

$$Av = \lambda v.$$

Such a vector $v\neq\mathbf{0}$ is called an eigenvector of $A$ corresponding to $\lambda$. Rewriting: $(A-\lambda I)v=\mathbf{0}$, so a non-trivial solution exists if and only if $A-\lambda I$ is singular, i.e., $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$.

Definition — Characteristic Polynomial

The characteristic polynomial of $A\in M_n(\mathbb{R})$ is:

$$p(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A).$$

This is a monic polynomial of degree $n$ in $\lambda$. The eigenvalues of $A$ are precisely the roots of $p(\lambda)$ in $\mathbb{C}$.

Key relations (Vieta's formulas for eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n$ counted with multiplicity): $$\operatorname{tr}(A)=\sum_{i=1}^n\lambda_i,\qquad \det(A)=\prod_{i=1}^n\lambda_i.$$

Definition — Eigenspace, Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

The eigenspace of $\lambda$ is: $$E_\lambda = \ker(A-\lambda I) = \{v\in\mathbb{C}^n : Av=\lambda v\}.$$ This is a subspace (containing at least the zero vector; for a genuine eigenvalue it has dimension $\ge 1$).

  • The algebraic multiplicity of $\lambda$ is its multiplicity as a root of $p(\lambda)$.
  • The geometric multiplicity of $\lambda$ is $\dim E_\lambda$.

We always have $1\le\text{geom. mult.}\le\text{alg. mult.}$ for each eigenvalue.

Example — $2\times2$ Upper Triangular Matrix

Let $A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\0&2\end{pmatrix}$. Then: $$p(\lambda)=\det\begin{pmatrix}\lambda-3&-1\\0&\lambda-2\end{pmatrix}=(\lambda-3)(\lambda-2).$$ Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=3$ and $\lambda_2=2$.

Eigenvector for $\lambda_1=3$: $(A-3I)v=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}v=0$, so $v_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}=e_1$.

Eigenvector for $\lambda_2=2$: $(A-2I)v=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}v=0$, so $v_2=\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Check: $\operatorname{tr}(A)=5=3+2$ and $\det(A)=6=3\cdot2$. $\checkmark$

Remark — Eigenvalues of Special Matrices
  • The eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are its diagonal entries.
  • The eigenvalues of $A^k$ are $\lambda_i^k$ (with the same eigenvectors).
  • The eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$ (when it exists) are $1/\lambda_i$.
  • The eigenvalues of $A+cI$ are $\lambda_i+c$.
  • Similar matrices $A\sim B=PAP^{-1}$ have the same characteristic polynomial, hence the same eigenvalues.
