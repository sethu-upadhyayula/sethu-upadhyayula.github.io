1.2 Subspaces

Definition — Subspace

A non-empty subset $W\subseteq V$ of a vector space $V$ is a subspace if $W$ is itself a vector space under the operations inherited from $V$. Rather than verifying all ten axioms, we use the subspace criterion: $W$ is a subspace if and only if

  1. $\mathbf{0}\in W$ (equivalently $W\ne\emptyset$, since $0\cdot w=\mathbf{0}$ for any $w\in W$),
  2. Closed under addition: $u,v\in W \Rightarrow u+v\in W$,
  3. Closed under scalar multiplication: $c\in\mathbb{R},\, v\in W \Rightarrow cv\in W$.

The remaining seven axioms are satisfied automatically since elements of $W$ already belong to $V$.

Example — Trivial Subspaces

For any vector space $V$, both $\{\mathbf{0}\}$ and $V$ itself are subspaces, called the trivial subspaces of $V$.

Example — Lines and Planes Through the Origin

In $\mathbb{R}^2$, any line through the origin $L=\{t(a,b):t\in\mathbb{R}\}$ is a subspace: it contains $\mathbf{0}$ (take $t=0$), is closed under addition ($ta+sa=(t+s)a$), and closed under scalar multiplication. A line not through the origin fails condition 1 and hence is not a subspace.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, lines and planes through the origin are subspaces; the $xy$-plane $\{(x,y,0)\}$ is a classic example.

Example — Kernel and Image of a Linear Map

If $T:V\to W$ is a linear transformation, then: $$\ker(T) = \{v\in V : T(v)=\mathbf{0}_W\} \subseteq V \quad\text{and}\quad \operatorname{im}(T) = T(V) = \{T(v):v\in V\} \subseteq W$$ are both subspaces. These are studied in depth in §2.2.

Definition — Span

Given vectors $v_1,\ldots,v_k\in V$, their span is the set of all linear combinations: $$\operatorname{span}\{v_1,\ldots,v_k\} = \left\{\sum_{i=1}^k c_i v_i : c_i\in\mathbb{R}\right\}.$$ This is always a subspace of $V$ (verify the three conditions), and in fact it is the smallest subspace of $V$ containing all the $v_i$. We say $\{v_1,\ldots,v_k\}$ spans $V$ if $\operatorname{span}\{v_1,\ldots,v_k\}=V$.

Example — Span in $\mathbb{R}^3$

$\operatorname{span}\{(1,0,0),(0,1,0)\} = \{(x,y,0):x,y\in\mathbb{R}\}$, which is the $xy$-plane — a $2$-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Definition — Sum and Direct Sum

Let $U$ and $W$ be subspaces of $V$. Their sum is $$U+W = \{u+w : u\in U,\, w\in W\},$$ which is the smallest subspace of $V$ containing both $U$ and $W$. Their intersection $U\cap W$ is also a subspace of $V$.

If every vector $v\in U+W$ decomposes uniquely as $v=u+w$ with $u\in U$, $w\in W$ — equivalently, if $U\cap W=\{\mathbf{0}\}$ — the sum is called a direct sum, written $U\oplus W$.

Theorem — Dimension of a Sum

For finite-dimensional subspaces $U,W\subseteq V$: $$\dim(U+W) = \dim U + \dim W - \dim(U\cap W).$$ In particular, $\dim(U\oplus W)=\dim U+\dim W$ when $U\cap W=\{\mathbf{0}\}$.
