5.2 Orthogonality and Gram–Schmidt

Definition — Orthogonality and Orthonormal Bases

Two vectors $u,v\in V$ are orthogonal, written $u\perp v$, if $\langle u,v\rangle=0$.

A set $\{v_1,\ldots,v_k\}$ is:

  • Orthogonal if $\langle v_i,v_j\rangle=0$ for all $i\neq j$.
  • Orthonormal if in addition $\|v_i\|=1$ for all $i$, i.e., $\langle v_i,v_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$.

An orthonormal basis $\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}$ of $V$ satisfies $\langle e_i,e_j\rangle=\delta_{ij}$ and spans $V$. In such a basis, $v=\sum_i\langle v,e_i\rangle e_i$ (the coordinates are just inner products).

Theorem — Pythagoras

If $u\perp v$, then $\|u+v\|^2=\|u\|^2+\|v\|^2$. More generally, for mutually orthogonal vectors $v_1,\ldots,v_k$: $$\left\|\sum_{i=1}^k v_i\right\|^2=\sum_{i=1}^k\|v_i\|^2.$$

Theorem — Gram–Schmidt Orthonormalisation

Given linearly independent vectors $\{v_1,\ldots,v_k\}$ in an inner product space, the following algorithm produces an orthonormal set $\{e_1,\ldots,e_k\}$ with the same span:

  1. Set $u_1=v_1$; $e_1=u_1/\|u_1\|$.
  2. For $j=2,\ldots,k$: $$u_j = v_j - \sum_{i=1}^{j-1}\langle v_j,e_i\rangle e_i,\qquad e_j=\frac{u_j}{\|u_j\|}.$$

At each step, $u_j$ is the component of $v_j$ orthogonal to the span of $\{v_1,\ldots,v_{j-1}\}$. By induction, $\{e_1,\ldots,e_j\}$ is orthonormal and $\operatorname{span}\{e_1,\ldots,e_j\}=\operatorname{span}\{v_1,\ldots,v_j\}$ for each $j$.

Example — Gram–Schmidt in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $v_1=(1,1,0)^T$, $v_2=(1,0,1)^T$, $v_3=(0,1,1)^T$.

Step 1: $u_1=v_1=(1,1,0)^T$, $\|u_1\|=\sqrt{2}$, $e_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}(1,1,0)^T$.

Step 2: $\langle v_2,e_1\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}(1+0+0)=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}$. Then: $$u_2=v_2-\frac{1}{\sqrt2}e_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}-\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1/2\\-1/2\\1\end{pmatrix},\quad \|u_2\|=\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}},\quad e_2=\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\begin{pmatrix}1/2\\-1/2\\1\end{pmatrix}.$$

Step 3: Subtract projections onto $e_1$ and $e_2$ from $v_3$, then normalise to get $e_3$.

Theorem — QR Decomposition

If $A\in M_{m\times n}(\mathbb{R})$ has linearly independent columns ($m\ge n$), then $A=QR$ where:

  • $Q\in M_{m\times n}(\mathbb{R})$ has orthonormal columns (obtained by applying Gram–Schmidt to the columns of $A$).
  • $R\in M_{n\times n}(\mathbb{R})$ is upper triangular with positive diagonal entries.

This decomposition is unique (under the positivity constraint on $R$). QR decomposition is numerically stable and is the foundation for the QR algorithm for computing eigenvalues.
