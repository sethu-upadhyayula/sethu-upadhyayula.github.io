6.2 Spectral Theorem

Theorem — Real Spectral Theorem

Every real symmetric matrix $A=A^T\in M_n(\mathbb{R})$ is orthogonally diagonalisable: there exists an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (i.e., $Q^TQ=QQ^T=I$) and a diagonal matrix $\Lambda=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)$ with real entries such that:

$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T.$$

The columns of $Q$ are orthonormal eigenvectors of $A$, and $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n$ are the corresponding real eigenvalues. This is the most powerful structure theorem for symmetric matrices.

Proof Sketch — Induction on $n$

By the fundamental theorem of algebra (and the fact that eigenvalues are real for symmetric matrices), $A$ has at least one real eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ with a unit eigenvector $q_1$. Extend $q_1$ to an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$; in this basis $A$ becomes: $$Q_1^TAQ_1=\begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1&\mathbf{0}^T\\\mathbf{0}&A'\end{pmatrix}$$ where $A'$ is $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ and symmetric (this follows from $A=A^T$ and orthogonality). Apply the induction hypothesis to $A'$.

$\square$

Theorem — Spectral Decomposition

From $A=Q\Lambda Q^T$ with columns $q_i$ and eigenvalues $\lambda_i$, we get the spectral decomposition:

$$A = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\, q_i q_i^T.$$

Each term $q_iq_i^T$ is a rank-1 orthogonal projection onto $\operatorname{span}\{q_i\}$. This writes $A$ as a weighted sum of orthogonal rank-1 projections. For a matrix function $f(A)$ (e.g., $e^A$, $\sqrt{A}$): $f(A)=\sum_i f(\lambda_i)q_iq_i^T$.

Theorem — Quadratic Forms and the Spectral Theorem

A quadratic form associated with $A=A^T$ is $Q(x)=x^TAx=\sum_{i,j}a_{ij}x_ix_j$. Under the change of variables $x=Qy$ (where $Q$ diagonalises $A$):

$$x^TAx = y^T\Lambda y = \sum_{i=1}^n\lambda_i y_i^2.$$

This eliminates all cross-terms. Consequently:

  • $A$ is positive definite $\Leftrightarrow$ all $\lambda_i>0$.
  • $A$ is positive semi-definite $\Leftrightarrow$ all $\lambda_i\ge0$.
  • The maximum of $x^TAx$ over the unit sphere $\|x\|=1$ is $\lambda_{\max}$ (attained at the corresponding eigenvector).
Example — Diagonalising a $2\times2$ Symmetric Matrix

Let $A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$. Characteristic polynomial: $p(\lambda)=(\lambda-3)^2-1=(\lambda-4)(\lambda-2)$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1=4$, $\lambda_2=2$.

Eigenvectors: $\lambda_1=4$: $(A-4I)v=\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}v=0$, so $q_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

$\lambda_2=2$: $q_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}-1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

Then $Q=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1&-1\\1&1\end{pmatrix}$ and $A=Q\begin{pmatrix}4&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}Q^T=4q_1q_1^T+2q_2q_2^T$.
