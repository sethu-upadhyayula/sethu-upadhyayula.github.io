5.1 Harmonic Functions

A function $u \in C^2(\Omega)$ is harmonic on an open set $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ if it satisfies the Laplace equation $\Delta u = 0$ identically on $\Omega$. Harmonic functions arise as steady-state temperature distributions, gravitational and electrostatic potentials, and as the real and imaginary parts of holomorphic functions in the plane. Their most striking feature is the mean value property, from which almost all other properties follow.

Definition 5.1.1 - Harmonic Function

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be open. A function $u \in C^2(\Omega)$ is harmonic on $\Omega$ if

$$\Delta u(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x_i^2}(x) = 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in \Omega.$$

More generally, $u$ is a solution of the Poisson equation if $-\Delta u = f$ for some given $f \in C(\Omega)$.

Mean Value Property

Theorem 5.1.2 - Mean Value Property

Let $u$ be harmonic in $\Omega$. For any ball $B(x_0,r) \subset\subset \Omega$:

(Spherical mean value):

$$u(x_0) = \frac{1}{|\partial B(x_0,r)|} \int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} u \, dS = \frac{1}{n\omega_n r^{n-1}} \int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} u \, dS.$$

(Ball mean value):

$$u(x_0) = \frac{1}{|B(x_0,r)|} \int_{B(x_0,r)} u \, dy = \frac{n}{\omega_n r^n} \int_{B(x_0,r)} u \, dy.$$

Here $\omega_n$ denotes the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^n$, so $|\partial B(x_0,r)| = n\omega_n r^{n-1}$ and $|B(x_0,r)| = \omega_n r^n$.

Proof

Define $\phi(r) = \frac{1}{|\partial B(x_0,r)|}\int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} u \, dS$. By the change of variables $y = x_0 + rz$ with $z \in \partial B(0,1)$:

$$\phi(r) = \frac{1}{n\omega_n} \int_{\partial B(0,1)} u(x_0 + rz)\, dS(z).$$

Differentiating under the integral sign and applying the divergence theorem:

$$\phi'(r) = \frac{1}{n\omega_n} \int_{\partial B(0,1)} \nabla u(x_0+rz)\cdot z \, dS(z) = \frac{1}{n\omega_n r^{n-1}} \int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} \frac{\partial u}{\partial \nu} \, dS = \frac{r}{n} \fint_{B(x_0,r)} \Delta u \, dy = 0,$$

since $\Delta u = 0$. Thus $\phi$ is constant. As $r \to 0^+$, $\phi(r) \to u(x_0)$ by continuity, so $\phi(r) = u(x_0)$ for all $r$. The ball mean value follows by integrating the spherical formula in $r$. $\square$

Maximum Principle

Theorem 5.1.3 - Weak Maximum Principle

Let $\Omega$ be bounded and $u \in C^2(\Omega) \cap C(\overline{\Omega})$ harmonic. Then

$$\max_{\overline{\Omega}} u = \max_{\partial \Omega} u \quad \text{and} \quad \min_{\overline{\Omega}} u = \min_{\partial \Omega} u.$$

Proof sketch: If $u$ attains its maximum at an interior point $x_0$, then $u(x_0) = \fint_{B(x_0,r)} u \, dy \leq u(x_0)$ by the mean value property, forcing $u \equiv u(x_0)$ on $B(x_0,r)$. By connectedness, $u$ is constant on $\Omega$, and the boundary maximum equals the interior value. $\square$

Theorem 5.1.4 - Strong Maximum Principle

Let $\Omega$ be connected and bounded, and $u \in C^2(\Omega)\cap C(\overline\Omega)$ harmonic. If $u$ attains its maximum (or minimum) at an interior point, then $u$ is constant throughout $\Omega$. In particular, a non-constant harmonic function can only attain its extrema on $\partial\Omega$.

Corollary 5.1.5 - Uniqueness of Dirichlet Problem

The Dirichlet problem $\Delta u = 0$ in $\Omega$, $u = g$ on $\partial\Omega$, has at most one solution $u \in C^2(\Omega)\cap C(\overline\Omega)$.

Proof: If $u$ and $v$ both solve the problem, $w = u - v$ is harmonic with zero boundary data. By the maximum principle, $\max_{\overline\Omega} w = 0$ and $\min_{\overline\Omega} w = 0$, so $w \equiv 0$. $\square$

Liouville's Theorem

Theorem 5.1.6 - Liouville's Theorem for Harmonic Functions

If $u$ is harmonic and bounded on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$, then $u$ is constant.

Proof

For any two points $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and any $r > |x - y|$, the mean value property gives

$$u(x) - u(y) = \frac{n}{\omega_n r^n}\left(\int_{B(x,r)} u\, dz - \int_{B(y,r)} u\, dz\right).$$

The symmetric difference $B(x,r) \triangle B(y,r)$ has volume $O(r^{n-1}|x-y|)$, so

$$|u(x) - u(y)| \leq \frac{n \|u\|_\infty}{\omega_n r^n} \cdot C r^{n-1} |x-y| = \frac{C\|u\|_\infty |x-y|}{r}.$$

Sending $r \to \infty$ gives $u(x) = u(y)$. $\square$

Harmonic Functions in 2D

Proposition 5.1.7 - Real and Imaginary Parts of Holomorphic Functions

If $f = u + iv$ is holomorphic on an open set $\Omega \subset \mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2$, then both $u$ and $v$ are harmonic on $\Omega$.

Proof: The Cauchy-Riemann equations give $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$. Differentiating: $\Delta u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} = (v_y)_x + (-v_x)_y = v_{yx} - v_{xy} = 0$ by equality of mixed partials. Similarly $\Delta v = 0$. $\square$

Example 5.1.8 - Basic Harmonic Functions

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: $u = x$, $u = x^2 - y^2$, $u = e^x \cos y$, $u = \log|x|$ (away from origin).

In $\mathbb{R}^n$ ($n \geq 3$): $u = |x|^{2-n}$ is harmonic on $\mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{0\}$. To verify: using $|x|^2 = x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2$,

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}|x|^{2-n} = (2-n)|x|^{-n} x_i, \quad \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i^2}|x|^{2-n} = (2-n)|x|^{-n} + (2-n)(-n)x_i^2|x|^{-n-2}.$$

Summing over $i$: $\Delta(|x|^{2-n}) = (2-n)n|x|^{-n} + (2-n)(-n)|x|^{-n} = 0$.
