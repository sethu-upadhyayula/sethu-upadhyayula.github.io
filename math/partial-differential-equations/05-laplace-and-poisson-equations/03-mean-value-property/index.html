5.3 Mean Value Property

We have seen that harmonic functions satisfy the mean value property. The converse is also true: continuity plus the mean value property forces $\Delta u = 0$. This section explores the full landscape of mean value characterizations, sub- and superharmonic functions, and several fundamental consequences including the Harnack inequality and the Hopf lemma.

Theorem 5.3.1 - Converse Mean Value Property

Let $u \in C(\Omega)$ satisfy $u(x) = \fint_{\partial B(x,r)} u\, dS$ for every ball $B(x,r) \subset\subset \Omega$. Then $u \in C^\infty(\Omega)$ and $\Delta u = 0$ in $\Omega$.

Proof Sketch

Let $\eta_\varepsilon$ be a standard mollifier. Since $u$ satisfies the MVP, $u * \eta_\varepsilon = u$ in $\{x: d(x,\partial\Omega) > \varepsilon\}$ - this follows because $\eta_\varepsilon$ is radially symmetric and the MVP holds for all $r < \varepsilon$. Hence $u$ equals its own mollification, so $u \in C^\infty$. Now apply the MVP to $\Delta u$: if $\Delta u(x_0) \neq 0$ at some $x_0$, then $\Delta u > 0$ (say) near $x_0$. Choose $r$ small and use Green's theorem: $0 = \int_{B(x_0,r)} \Delta u\, dy = \int_{\partial B} \frac{\partial u}{\partial\nu}\, dS = \frac{d}{dr}\!\left(r^{n-1} \fint_{\partial B} u\, dS\right) \cdot \frac{1}{n-1}$, giving $\int_{B(x_0,r)} \Delta u\, dy = 0$, a contradiction. $\square$

Sub- and Superharmonic Functions

Definition 5.3.2 - Sub- and Superharmonic Functions

A function $u \in C^2(\Omega)$ is subharmonic if $\Delta u \geq 0$ in $\Omega$, and superharmonic if $\Delta u \leq 0$ in $\Omega$.

Equivalently, $u$ is subharmonic iff $u(x) \leq \fint_{B(x,r)} u\, dy$ for all admissible balls, i.e., $u$ lies below its spherical averages. Convex functions of harmonic functions are subharmonic: if $u$ is harmonic and $\varphi$ is convex, then $\varphi \circ u$ is subharmonic.

Theorem 5.3.3 - Harnack Inequality

Let $u \geq 0$ be harmonic in $\Omega$ and $V \subset\subset \Omega$ be connected and open. There exists a constant $C = C(n, V, \Omega) > 0$ such that

$$\sup_V u \leq C \inf_V u.$$

In particular, a non-negative harmonic function cannot be zero at an interior point without vanishing identically. The constant $C$ depends on the geometry of $V$ relative to $\Omega$ but not on $u$.

Proof Sketch

For any two points $x, y \in B(x_0, r) \subset B(x_0, 2r) \subset\subset \Omega$, the Poisson integral formula gives

$$u(x) = \frac{r^2 - |x - x_0|^2}{n\omega_n r} \int_{\partial B(x_0,r)} \frac{u(z)}{|x-z|^n}\, dS(z).$$

Since $r - |x - x_0| \leq |x - z| \leq r + |x - x_0| \leq 3r$ for $x \in B(x_0, r/2)$ and $z \in \partial B(x_0,r)$, we obtain $u(x_0) \cdot 3^{-n} \leq u(x) \leq 3^n u(x_0)$. Covering $V$ with a finite chain of such balls connects any two points and multiplies these estimates. $\square$

Removable Singularities

Theorem 5.3.4 - Removable Singularity

Let $x_0 \in \Omega$ and suppose $u$ is harmonic in $\Omega \setminus \{x_0\}$ with $u(x) = o(\Phi(x - x_0))$ as $x \to x_0$ (in particular, if $u$ is bounded near $x_0$). Then $u$ extends to a harmonic function on all of $\Omega$.

Hopf Boundary Point Lemma

Lemma 5.3.5 - Hopf Boundary Point Lemma

Let $u$ be harmonic in $\Omega$, continuous on $\overline\Omega$, and suppose $u(x_0) = \max_{\overline\Omega} u$ at a boundary point $x_0 \in \partial\Omega$ where $\partial\Omega$ satisfies an interior sphere condition. If $u$ is not constant, then

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial \nu}(x_0) > 0,$$

where $\nu$ is the outward unit normal at $x_0$. This shows that the maximum on the boundary is attained with a positive outward normal derivative - a refinement of the strong maximum principle.

Remark 5.3.6 - Applications to Maximum Principles

The Hopf lemma is essential for proving uniqueness in Neumann boundary value problems and for deriving a priori estimates for elliptic equations. It also implies that if $u$ solves $-\Delta u = f \geq 0$ in $\Omega$ and $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$, then $u \geq 0$ throughout $\Omega$ and $\frac{\partial u}{\partial\nu} < 0$ on $\partial\Omega$ - the solution points inward.
