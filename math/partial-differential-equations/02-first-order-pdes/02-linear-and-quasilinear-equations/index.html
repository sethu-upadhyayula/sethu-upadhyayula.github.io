2.2 Linear and Quasilinear Equations

In a quasilinear first-order PDE, the coefficients of the derivatives may depend on $u$ itself — not just on the independent variables. This causes the characteristic curves to depend on the solution, creating the possibility of shock formation.

Definition 2.2.1 — Quasilinear First-Order PDE

A PDE of the form

$$a(x, y, u)\, u_x + b(x, y, u)\, u_y = c(x, y, u)$$

is called quasilinear. The characteristic ODEs become:

$$\frac{dx}{ds} = a(x,y,u),\quad \frac{dy}{ds} = b(x,y,u),\quad \frac{du}{ds} = c(x,y,u)$$

Unlike the linear case, here all three equations must be solved simultaneously since $a$ and $b$ depend on $u$. The characteristics in the $(x,y)$-plane are not known in advance.

Inviscid Burgers' Equation

Example 2.2.1 — Burgers' Equation $u_t + u\,u_x = 0$

Here $a = u$, $b = 1$ (in $(x,t)$ coordinates), $c = 0$. Characteristic ODEs:

$$\frac{dx}{ds} = u, \quad \frac{dt}{ds} = 1, \quad \frac{du}{ds} = 0$$

Since $du/ds = 0$, $u$ is constant along each characteristic. Therefore $dx/ds = u = u_0 = \text{const}$, giving straight-line characteristics:

$$x = x_0 + u_0(x_0)\, t, \quad u(x,t) = u_0(x_0)$$

where $x_0$ satisfies $x = x_0 + u_0(x_0)t$ implicitly. Key consequence: characteristics have different slopes $1/u_0(x_0)$ in the $(x,t)$-plane.

Theorem 2.2.1 — Shock Formation

If $u_0'(x_0) < 0$ for some $x_0$ (the initial profile has a decreasing portion), characteristics emanating from nearby points will intersect at a finite time:

$$t^* = \frac{-1}{\min_{x_0} u_0'(x_0)}$$

At time $t^*$, the classical solution breaks down — the characteristics cross and $u$ becomes multi-valued. This is the formation of a shock (a discontinuity in $u$).

Example 2.2.2 — Compression and Rarefaction

Consider the step initial data: $u_0(x) = 1$ for $x < 0$ and $u_0(x) = 0$ for $x > 0$.

  • Left characteristics have slope $1/u = 1$; right characteristics have slope $1/u = \infty$ (vertical).
  • These immediately intersect at $t = 0^+$, creating an instantaneous shock at $x = 0$.
  • This is a compression wave (fast characteristics catching up with slow ones).

If instead $u_0(x) = 0$ for $x < 0$ and $u_0(x) = 1$ for $x > 0$, characteristics diverge and a rarefaction fan fills the gap — a continuous solution $u(x,t) = x/t$ for $0 < x < t$.

Remark — Need for Weak Solutions

Once characteristics cross, the classical solution ceases to exist. We must extend the notion of solution to allow discontinuities — these are weak solutions, which satisfy the PDE in an integral sense. The theory of conservation laws (Section 2.3) provides the framework for selecting physically meaningful weak solutions via entropy conditions.
