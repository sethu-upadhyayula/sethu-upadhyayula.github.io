2.3 Conservation Laws

A conservation law expresses that the total amount of some quantity $u$ (mass, momentum, charge) changes only due to flux through the boundary. In one spatial dimension, the scalar conservation law takes the form:

Definition 2.3.1 — Scalar Conservation Law

$$u_t + f(u)_x = 0$$

where $u = u(x,t)$ is the conserved density and $f(u)$ is the flux function. Integrating over $[a,b]$: $\frac{d}{dt}\int_a^b u\,dx = f(u(a,t)) - f(u(b,t))$, confirming conservation.

Examples of Conservation Laws
  • Transport: $f(u) = cu$ → $u_t + cu_x = 0$
  • Burgers: $f(u) = u^2/2$ → $u_t + uu_x = 0$
  • Traffic flow: $f(u) = u(1-u)$ (LWR model), $u$ = density, $f$ = flow rate

Rankine–Hugoniot Condition

When a shock forms, classical solutions break down. We seek weak solutions satisfying the integral form. Across a shock at position $x = s(t)$, the shock speed must satisfy:

Theorem 2.3.1 — Rankine–Hugoniot Jump Condition

If $u$ has a jump discontinuity across the shock curve $x = s(t)$, with left state $u_L = \lim_{x \to s^-} u$ and right state $u_R = \lim_{x \to s^+} u$, then the shock speed satisfies:

$$s'(t) = \frac{f(u_R) - f(u_L)}{u_R - u_L}$$

This is derived by integrating the conservation law across the shock and applying the divergence theorem. For Burgers: $s' = (u_L + u_R)/2$.

Entropy Condition

The Rankine–Hugoniot condition alone does not uniquely determine the solution — there may be multiple weak solutions. The physically correct one is selected by an entropy condition:

Definition 2.3.2 — Lax Entropy Condition

A shock at speed $s$ is admissible (entropy-satisfying) if:

$$f'(u_L) > s > f'(u_R)$$

Equivalently, characteristics must run into the shock from both sides (characteristics on the left have speed $f'(u_L)$ greater than $s$; on the right, $f'(u_R)$ less than $s$). This ensures the shock is compressive, not expansive.

Rarefaction Waves

Definition 2.3.3 — Rarefaction Wave

When $u_L < u_R$ and $f''(u) > 0$ (convex flux), characteristics diverge. The entropy solution in the expanding region is a continuous rarefaction wave:

$$u(x,t) = (f')^{-1}(x/t) \quad \text{for } f'(u_L) < x/t < f'(u_R)$$

For Burgers ($f' = u$): $u(x,t) = x/t$ in the fan $u_L < x/t < u_R$. Rarefaction waves are smooth and do not violate the entropy condition.

Riemann Problem

The Riemann problem — initial data that is piecewise constant with a single jump — has an explicit solution consisting of at most one shock or rarefaction fan. It is the building block for numerical methods (Godunov's method, Roe's method) and the theory of systems of conservation laws.
