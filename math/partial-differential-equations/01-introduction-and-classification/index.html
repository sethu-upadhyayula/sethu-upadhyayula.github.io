Chapter 1: Introduction and Classification

Partial differential equations (PDEs) are equations involving unknown functions of several variables and their partial derivatives. They appear throughout mathematics, physics, and engineering, modelling phenomena from heat conduction to quantum mechanics. This chapter establishes the fundamental vocabulary and taxonomy.

We classify PDEs by order, linearity, and - for second-order equations - by the sign of the discriminant into elliptic, parabolic, and hyperbolic types. Each type requires different boundary conditions for a well-posed problem.

Chapter Overview

1.1 - First and Second Order PDEs

Order, linearity, and the four canonical examples: transport $u_t+cu_x=0$, wave $u_{tt}=c^2u_{xx}$, heat $u_t=ku_{xx}$, and Laplace $\Delta u=0$ equations.

1.2 - Classification

The discriminant $B^2 - AC$ classifies second-order linear PDEs as elliptic ($B^2-AC<0$), parabolic ($B^2-AC=0$), or hyperbolic ($B^2-AC>0$). Each type has distinct physical behavior and requires different solution methods.

1.3 - Well-Posed Problems

Hadamard's criteria: existence, uniqueness, and continuous dependence on data. Appropriate boundary conditions for each type. The backward heat equation as a canonical ill-posed example.
