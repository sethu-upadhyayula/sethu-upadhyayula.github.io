1.1 First and Second Order PDEs

A partial differential equation (PDE) is an equation involving an unknown function of two or more variables and its partial derivatives. We begin by introducing order, linearity, and the classical examples that motivate the entire theory.

Definition 1.1.1 — Order of a PDE

The order of a PDE is the order of the highest partial derivative appearing in the equation. A PDE is first order if only first derivatives appear, second order if second derivatives appear (but not higher), and so on.

Definition 1.1.2 — Linear, Quasilinear, and Nonlinear PDEs

A PDE is linear if it is linear in the unknown $u$ and all its derivatives (with coefficients depending only on the independent variables). It is quasilinear if it is linear in the highest-order derivatives only. Otherwise it is fully nonlinear.

Example: $u_t + u_x = 0$ is linear; $u_t + u u_x = 0$ is quasilinear; $u_x^2 + u_y^2 = 1$ is fully nonlinear.

The Four Fundamental Examples

Four PDEs appear throughout mathematics and physics as canonical models of different types of behavior:

Transport Equation (1st order, linear)

$$u_t + c\, u_x = 0, \qquad c \in \mathbb{R}$$

Describes advection of a quantity at speed $c$. The solution is $u(x,t) = u_0(x - ct)$: the initial profile propagates unchanged to the right at speed $c$. All information travels along the characteristic lines $x - ct = \mathrm{const}$.

Wave Equation (2nd order, linear, hyperbolic)

$$u_{tt} = c^2\, u_{xx}$$

Models vibrating strings, acoustic waves, and electromagnetic fields. Admits traveling wave solutions $F(x+ct) + G(x-ct)$. Solutions preserve their initial energy and propagate at finite speed $c$ — information cannot travel faster than $c$.

Heat Equation (2nd order, linear, parabolic)

$$u_t = k\, u_{xx}, \qquad k > 0$$

Models diffusion of heat or a chemical concentration. Solutions are immediately infinitely smooth for $t > 0$ even if the initial data is discontinuous — the smoothing effect. Solutions decay exponentially as $t \to \infty$.

Laplace Equation (2nd order, linear, elliptic)

$$\Delta u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0$$

Solutions are called harmonic functions. They model steady-state temperature distributions, electrostatic potentials, and incompressible irrotational fluid flow. They are automatically real-analytic (equal to their Taylor series everywhere).

Poisson Equation

The Poisson equation $\Delta u = f$ is the inhomogeneous Laplace equation. It describes steady heat distribution with a source $f$, or the electric potential due to a charge density $f$. The inhomogeneous term $f$ is called the source or forcing term.

Initial and Boundary Conditions

A PDE alone has infinitely many solutions. To select a unique solution, we impose auxiliary conditions:

  • Initial conditions: specify $u$ (and possibly time derivatives) at $t = 0$.
  • Boundary conditions: specify $u$ or its normal derivative on the spatial boundary $\partial\Omega$.

Different PDE types require different combinations to yield a well-posed problem, as we discuss in Section 1.3.
