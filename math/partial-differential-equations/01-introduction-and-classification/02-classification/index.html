1.2 Classification of Second-Order PDEs

The most important classification applies to second-order linear PDEs in two independent variables. Three canonical types emerge — elliptic, parabolic, and hyperbolic — each with distinct qualitative behavior and appropriate solution methods.

Definition 1.2.1 — General Second-Order Linear PDE

The general second-order linear PDE in variables $x, y$ is:

$$A u_{xx} + 2B u_{xy} + C u_{yy} + D u_x + E u_y + F u = G$$

where $A, B, C, D, E, F, G$ are functions of $x$ and $y$ (not depending on $u$). The principal part is $Au_{xx} + 2Bu_{xy} + Cu_{yy}$, which determines the type.

Definition 1.2.2 — Discriminant and Classification

Define the discriminant $\Delta = B^2 - AC$. The PDE is classified at a point $(x_0, y_0)$ as:

  • Elliptic if $B^2 - AC < 0$
  • Parabolic if $B^2 - AC = 0$
  • Hyperbolic if $B^2 - AC > 0$

The type may vary from point to point for variable-coefficient equations.

Canonical Forms and Examples

Elliptic: Laplace Equation $\Delta u = 0$

Here $A = C = 1$, $B = 0$, so $B^2 - AC = -1 < 0$. Elliptic everywhere.

Canonical form: $u_{\xi\xi} + u_{\eta\eta} = 0$. Elliptic equations describe steady-state phenomena. Solutions are real-analytic and satisfy the maximum principle: the maximum occurs on the boundary.

Parabolic: Heat Equation $u_t = k u_{xx}$

Rewrite as $ku_{xx} - u_t = 0$: $A = k$, $B = 0$, $C = 0$, so $B^2 - AC = 0$. Parabolic.

Canonical form: $u_{\xi\xi} = u_\eta$. Parabolic equations describe diffusion/evolution. They have infinite propagation speed but strong smoothing: $t > 0$ solutions are instantly $C^\infty$.

Hyperbolic: Wave Equation $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$

Rewrite as $c^2 u_{xx} - u_{tt} = 0$: $A = c^2$, $B = 0$, $C = -1$, so $B^2 - AC = c^2 > 0$. Hyperbolic.

Canonical form: $u_{\xi\eta} = 0$ (with $\xi = x+ct$, $\eta = x-ct$). Hyperbolic equations describe wave propagation at finite speed. Information travels along characteristic curves.

Reduction to Canonical Form

By a change of variables $\xi = \xi(x,y)$, $\eta = \eta(x,y)$, any second-order linear PDE with constant coefficients can be reduced to one of three canonical forms. The characteristics $\xi = \text{const}$ satisfy the characteristic equation:

Characteristic Equation

The characteristics of $Au_{xx} + 2Bu_{xy} + Cu_{yy} + \ldots = 0$ satisfy:

$$A\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 - 2B\frac{dy}{dx} + C = 0$$

Hyperbolic equations have two real characteristic families; parabolic have one repeated family; elliptic have two complex conjugate families (no real characteristics). This explains why hyperbolic equations propagate information along specific curves.

Physical Significance

Elliptic: equilibrium (steady heat, electrostatics, elastostatics). Parabolic: irreversible diffusion (heat flow, Brownian motion). Hyperbolic: reversible wave propagation (acoustics, electromagnetism, fluid waves). The classification predicts what boundary/initial conditions are appropriate and how solutions behave.
