1.3 Well-Posed Problems

Not every PDE together with boundary and initial conditions yields a meaningful mathematical problem. Hadamard formalized the notion of a well-posed problem: one that has a unique solution which depends continuously on the given data.

Definition 1.3.1 - Hadamard's Criteria

A PDE problem is well-posed (in the sense of Hadamard) if it satisfies all three conditions:

  1. Existence: A solution exists.
  2. Uniqueness: The solution is unique.
  3. Continuous dependence: The solution depends continuously on the data (initial/boundary conditions and source terms). Small changes in data produce small changes in the solution.

A problem failing any one of these conditions is called ill-posed.

Appropriate Boundary Conditions by Type

Elliptic Problems (e.g., $\Delta u = f$ on $\Omega$)

Elliptic equations require boundary conditions on the entire boundary $\partial\Omega$:

  • Dirichlet: $u = g$ on $\partial\Omega$ (prescribed value)
  • Neumann: $\partial u/\partial n = g$ on $\partial\Omega$ (prescribed normal derivative)
  • Robin: $\partial u/\partial n + \alpha u = g$ on $\partial\Omega$

No initial conditions are needed - elliptic problems are time-independent (steady-state). Imposing conditions on only part of the boundary typically yields an ill-posed problem.

Parabolic Problems (e.g., $u_t = k u_{xx}$)

Parabolic equations require:

  • Initial condition: $u(x,0) = \phi(x)$ (prescribed at $t=0$)
  • Boundary conditions on the spatial boundary for all $t > 0$

The problem is well-posed forward in time ($t > 0$). Running it backward in time ($t < 0$) gives the backward heat equation, which is ill-posed.

Hyperbolic Problems (e.g., $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$)

Hyperbolic equations require:

  • Two initial conditions: $u(x,0) = \phi(x)$ and $u_t(x,0) = \psi(x)$
  • Boundary conditions on the spatial boundary (or none for the Cauchy problem on $\mathbb{R}$)

Information propagates at finite speed $c$, so boundary conditions only influence the solution within their domain of influence.

The Backward Heat Equation - An Ill-Posed Example

Example: Backward Heat Equation

Consider solving $u_t = k u_{xx}$ backward in time: given $u(x,T) = g(x)$, find $u(x,0)$. This is equivalent to the forward problem with $t \mapsto T - t$, i.e., solving $u_t = -k u_{xx}$.

Take $g_n(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sin(nx)$. The corresponding backward solution is $u_n(x,t) = \frac{1}{n}e^{kn^2(T-t)}\sin(nx)$.

As $n \to \infty$, $\|g_n\|_\infty \to 0$ but $\|u_n(\cdot, 0)\|_\infty = \frac{1}{n}e^{kn^2T} \to \infty$.

Continuous dependence fails catastrophically: tiny changes in final data produce enormous changes in the reconstructed initial data. The backward heat equation is ill-posed.

Remark on Regularization

Ill-posed problems are not meaningless - they arise in inverse problems and data assimilation. They require regularization (e.g., Tikhonov regularization) which introduces additional constraints to stabilize the solution. The backward heat equation is stabilized by restricting to solutions with bounded $H^k$ norms.
