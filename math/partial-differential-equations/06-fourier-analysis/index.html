Chapter 6: Fourier Analysis

Fourier analysis is one of the most powerful tools for solving linear PDEs with constant coefficients. By decomposing functions into sums or integrals of trigonometric modes, differential operators become multiplication operators, transforming PDEs into algebraic or ordinary differential equations. This chapter develops Fourier series for periodic functions, the Fourier transform for functions on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$, and their applications to the heat and wave equations.

Chapter Overview

6.1 — Fourier Series

For a $2L$-periodic function $f$, we expand $f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_n e^{in\pi x/L}$ and study pointwise and $L^2$ convergence. Parseval's theorem gives $\|f\|_{L^2}^2 = 2L\sum|c_n|^2$, providing an isometric identification of $L^2([-L,L])$ with $\ell^2(\mathbb{Z})$.

6.2 — Fourier Transform

The Fourier transform $\hat f(\xi) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x) e^{-2\pi i \xi \cdot x}\, dx$ extends Fourier series to functions on $\mathbb{R}^n$. Key properties: differentiation becomes multiplication by $2\pi i\xi$, and the Plancherel theorem states $\|f\|_{L^2} = \|\hat f\|_{L^2}$. The Schwartz space provides the natural home for the theory.

6.3 — Applications

We solve the heat equation on $\mathbb{R}^n$ via the Fourier transform, deriving the heat kernel explicitly. The wave equation on $\mathbb{R}$ reduces to d'Alembert's formula via Fourier methods. We also cover the Poisson summation formula and the Shannon sampling theorem.
