6.2 Fourier Transform

The Fourier transform extends Fourier series from periodic functions to functions on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$. It diagonalises constant-coefficient differential operators — replacing differentiation by multiplication — and provides the natural framework for analysing PDEs on unbounded domains.

Definition 6.2.1 — Fourier Transform on $L^1(\mathbb{R})$

For $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, the Fourier transform of $f$ is

$$\hat{f}(\xi) = \mathcal{F}\{f\}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, e^{-2\pi i \xi x}\,dx, \quad \xi \in \mathbb{R}.$$

The inverse Fourier transform is

$$f(x) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{\hat{f}\}(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi)\, e^{2\pi i \xi x}\,d\xi.$$

The Fourier inversion theorem holds whenever $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and $\hat{f} \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, giving $f(x) = \int \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i\xi x}\,d\xi$ at every continuity point of $f$.

Basic Properties

Proposition 6.2.2 — Differentiation and Translation

For $f \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})$ (Schwartz class) or whenever the expressions are defined:

(Derivative rule): $\widehat{f'}(\xi) = 2\pi i \xi \,\hat{f}(\xi)$, and more generally $\widehat{D^k f}(\xi) = (2\pi i\xi)^k \hat{f}(\xi)$.

(Frequency shift): $\widehat{e^{2\pi i x_0 \xi_0} f(x)}(\xi) = \hat{f}(\xi - \xi_0)$.

(Translation): $\widehat{f(x-x_0)}(\xi) = e^{-2\pi i x_0 \xi}\hat{f}(\xi)$.

(Scaling): $\widehat{f(ax)}(\xi) = \frac{1}{|a|}\hat{f}(\xi/a)$ for $a \neq 0$.

(Conjugation): $\widehat{\overline{f}}(\xi) = \overline{\hat{f}(-\xi)}$.

Schwartz Space and Tempered Distributions

Definition 6.2.3 — Schwartz Space $\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$

The Schwartz space consists of all rapidly decreasing smooth functions:

$$\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \left\{ f \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n) : \sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} |x^\alpha D^\beta f(x)| < \infty \text{ for all multi-indices } \alpha, \beta \right\}.$$

Equivalently, $f \in \mathcal{S}$ if $f$ and all its derivatives decay faster than any polynomial. The Fourier transform is a topological isomorphism $\mathcal{F}: \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n) \to \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$.

A tempered distribution is a continuous linear functional $T: \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n) \to \mathbb{C}$. The space of tempered distributions $\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^n)$ includes all $L^p$ functions and polynomially growing functions. The Fourier transform extends to $\mathcal{S}'$ by duality: $\hat{T}[\phi] = T[\hat\phi]$.

Plancherel's Theorem and Convolution

Theorem 6.2.4 — Plancherel's Theorem

The Fourier transform extends uniquely to a unitary isomorphism $\mathcal{F}: L^2(\mathbb{R}^n) \to L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)$, satisfying

$$\|\hat{f}\|_{L^2} = \|f\|_{L^2}, \quad \text{i.e.} \quad \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2\,d\xi = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} |f(x)|^2\,dx.$$

More generally (Parseval's formula): $\langle \hat{f}, \hat{g}\rangle_{L^2} = \langle f, g\rangle_{L^2}$.

Theorem 6.2.5 — Convolution Theorem

For $f, g \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$, define the convolution $(f * g)(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x-y)g(y)\,dy$. Then $f * g \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and

$$\widehat{f * g}(\xi) = \hat{f}(\xi)\cdot\hat{g}(\xi).$$

Dually, $\widehat{fg}(\xi) = (\hat{f} * \hat{g})(\xi)$. This transforms convolution equations into pointwise algebraic equations in frequency space.

Key Examples

Example 6.2.6 — Gaussian Self-Duality

The Gaussian $f(x) = e^{-\pi x^2}$ is its own Fourier transform: $\hat{f}(\xi) = e^{-\pi\xi^2}$.

Proof: Let $I(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi x^2} e^{-2\pi i \xi x}\,dx$. Differentiate under the integral: $I'(\xi) = -2\pi i \int x e^{-\pi x^2}e^{-2\pi i\xi x}\,dx$. Integration by parts gives $I'(\xi) = -2\pi\xi I(\xi)$. Thus $I(\xi) = I(0)e^{-\pi\xi^2}$. Since $I(0) = \int e^{-\pi x^2}\,dx = 1$, we have $\hat{f}(\xi) = e^{-\pi\xi^2}$. $\square$

More generally, $\mathcal{F}\{e^{-ax^2}\}(\xi) = \sqrt{\pi/a}\,e^{-\pi^2\xi^2/a}$ for $\text{Re}(a) > 0$.

Example 6.2.7 — Rectangular Pulse and Sinc Function

The rectangular pulse $\text{rect}(x) = \mathbf{1}_{[-1/2,1/2]}(x)$ has Fourier transform

$$\widehat{\text{rect}}(\xi) = \int_{-1/2}^{1/2} e^{-2\pi i\xi x}\,dx = \frac{\sin(\pi\xi)}{\pi\xi} = \text{sinc}(\xi).$$

By the inversion theorem, $\widehat{\text{sinc}}(\xi) = \text{rect}(\xi)$. Note that $\text{sinc} \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$ but not $L^1(\mathbb{R})$, illustrating that the Fourier inversion must be interpreted in the $L^2$ sense.
