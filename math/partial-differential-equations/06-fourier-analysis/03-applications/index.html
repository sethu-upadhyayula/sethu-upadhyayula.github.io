6.3 Applications to PDEs

The Fourier transform converts PDEs on $\mathbb{R}^n$ into ODEs or algebraic equations in frequency space. Solutions in physical space are then recovered by the convolution theorem, yielding explicit integral representations. We illustrate this for the heat and wave equations, and prove the Poisson summation formula.

Heat Equation on $\mathbb{R}^n$

Theorem 6.3.1 — Solution via Heat Kernel

Consider the initial value problem $u_t = \Delta u$ on $\mathbb{R}^n \times (0,\infty)$ with $u(\cdot, 0) = u_0 \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$. Taking the Fourier transform in $x$ gives

$$\hat{u}_t(\xi,t) = -4\pi^2|\xi|^2 \hat{u}(\xi,t), \quad \hat{u}(\xi,0) = \hat{u}_0(\xi).$$

This ODE in $t$ has the explicit solution $\hat{u}(\xi,t) = \hat{u}_0(\xi)\,e^{-4\pi^2|\xi|^2 t}$. Since $e^{-4\pi^2|\xi|^2 t}$ is the Fourier transform of the heat kernel

$$G_t(x) = \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}} e^{-|x|^2/(4t)},$$

the convolution theorem gives

$$u(x,t) = (G_t * u_0)(x) = \frac{1}{(4\pi t)^{n/2}}\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{-|x-y|^2/(4t)} u_0(y)\,dy.$$

For $t > 0$, $u(\cdot,t) \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ even if $u_0$ is merely bounded and measurable (instant smoothing). The heat kernel satisfies $\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} G_t(x)\,dx = 1$ and $G_t \to \delta_0$ as $t \to 0^+$ in the sense of distributions.

Wave Equation on $\mathbb{R}$

Theorem 6.3.2 — d'Alembert's Formula via Fourier Transform

For $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$ on $\mathbb{R}\times(0,\infty)$ with $u(x,0)=f(x)$, $u_t(x,0)=g(x)$, taking the Fourier transform in $x$ gives

$$\hat{u}_{tt} = -4\pi^2 c^2 \xi^2 \hat{u},$$

an ODE with solution $\hat{u}(\xi,t) = \hat{f}(\xi)\cos(2\pi c|\xi|t) + \hat{g}(\xi)\frac{\sin(2\pi c|\xi|t)}{2\pi c|\xi|}$. Inverting via the convolution theorem:

$$u(x,t) = \frac{f(x+ct)+f(x-ct)}{2} + \frac{1}{2c}\int_{x-ct}^{x+ct} g(y)\,dy,$$

which is d'Alembert's formula. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n \geq 2$, the analogous formula involves spherical means and exhibits Huygens' principle for odd $n \geq 3$.

Poisson Summation Formula

Theorem 6.3.3 — Poisson Summation Formula

Let $f \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})$. Then

$$\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} f(n) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(n).$$

More generally, for the lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}^d \subset \mathbb{R}^d$:

$$\sum_{\mathbf{n} \in \mathbb{Z}^d} f(\mathbf{n}) = \sum_{\mathbf{m} \in \mathbb{Z}^d} \hat{f}(\mathbf{m}).$$
Proof

Define $F(x) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} f(x+n)$. Since $f \in \mathcal{S}$, this sum converges absolutely and uniformly to a $1$-periodic smooth function. The Fourier coefficients of $F$ are

$$\hat{F}(k) = \int_0^1 F(x) e^{-2\pi ikx}\,dx = \int_0^1 \sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} f(x+n) e^{-2\pi ikx}\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)e^{-2\pi ikx}\,dx = \hat{f}(k).$$

By the Fourier series of $F$, $F(x) = \sum_{k\in\mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(k) e^{2\pi ikx}$. Evaluating at $x=0$:

$$\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} f(n) = F(0) = \sum_{k\in\mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(k). \quad \square$$
Example 6.3.4 — Jacobi Theta Function Identity

Apply Poisson summation to $f(x) = e^{-\pi t x^2}$ (for $t > 0$), whose Fourier transform is $\hat{f}(\xi) = t^{-1/2} e^{-\pi\xi^2/t}$. The formula gives the Jacobi theta function identity:

$$\sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi t n^2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{t}} \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-\pi n^2/t}, \quad t > 0.$$

This identity is fundamental in the theory of modular forms and the proof of the functional equation of the Riemann zeta function.

Shannon Sampling Theorem

Theorem 6.3.5 — Shannon–Nyquist Sampling Theorem (Statement)

Let $f \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$ be band-limited with bandwidth $B$: $\hat{f}(\xi) = 0$ for $|\xi| > B$. Then $f$ is completely determined by its sample values $\{f(n/(2B))\}_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}$, and

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty f\!\left(\frac{n}{2B}\right) \text{sinc}(2Bx - n),$$

where $\text{sinc}(y) = \sin(\pi y)/(\pi y)$. The sampling rate $2B$ (samples per unit time) is the Nyquist rate; sampling below this rate causes aliasing, an irreversible loss of information.
