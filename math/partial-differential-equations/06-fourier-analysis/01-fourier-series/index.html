6.1 Fourier Series

Fourier series decompose a periodic function into a sum of harmonics — sines and cosines — revealing the frequency content of the function. They are indispensable for solving PDEs on bounded domains via separation of variables, and they underpin the $L^2$ theory of orthogonal expansions.

Definition 6.1.1 — Fourier Coefficients

Let $f \in L^1(-L, L)$ be a $2L$-periodic function. Its Fourier coefficients are

$$a_0 = \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\,dx,$$ $$a_n = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx, \quad n \geq 1,$$ $$b_n = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx, \quad n \geq 1.$$

The associated Fourier series is

$$f(x) \sim a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(a_n \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) + b_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\right).$$

In complex form, with $c_n = \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x)e^{-in\pi x/L}\,dx$, the series reads $f(x) \sim \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{in\pi x/L}$.

Pointwise Convergence: Dirichlet's Theorem

Theorem 6.1.2 — Dirichlet's Theorem

Let $f$ be $2L$-periodic and piecewise smooth on $[-L, L]$, meaning $f$ and $f'$ have at most finitely many jump discontinuities. Then the Fourier series of $f$ converges at every point $x$, and

$$a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(a_n \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) + b_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\right) = \frac{f(x^+) + f(x^-)}{2},$$

where $f(x^\pm) = \lim_{h\to 0^\pm} f(x+h)$. At points of continuity, the series converges to $f(x)$.

Remark 6.1.3 — Gibbs Phenomenon

Near a jump discontinuity, the partial sums $S_N f(x)$ overshoot the function value by approximately $8.9\%$ of the jump magnitude, regardless of how large $N$ is. More precisely, if $f$ has a jump of size $J$ at $x_0$, then

$$\limsup_{N\to\infty} \max_{x \approx x_0} S_N f(x) = f(x_0^+) + \frac{J}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \frac{\sin t}{t}\,dt - \frac{J}{2} \approx f(x_0^+) + 0.0895\,J.$$

This overshoot does not decrease with $N$; it merely narrows. The Gibbs phenomenon cannot be eliminated by adding more terms but can be mitigated by the Cesàro (Fejér) summation method.

$L^2$ Theory and Parseval's Identity

Theorem 6.1.4 — $L^2$ Convergence and Parseval's Identity

The trigonometric system $\{e^{in\pi x/L}\}_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}$ is an orthonormal basis of $L^2(-L,L)$ (with inner product $\langle f,g\rangle = \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L} f\bar{g}\,dx$). For any $f \in L^2(-L,L)$:

(i) $L^2$ convergence: $\|f - S_N f\|_{L^2} \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$.

(ii) Parseval's identity:

$$\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L} |f(x)|^2\,dx = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} |c_n|^2 = |a_0|^2 + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(a_n^2 + b_n^2).$$

Parseval's identity states that the Fourier transform is an isometry from $L^2(-L,L)$ onto $\ell^2(\mathbb{Z})$.

Examples

Example 6.1.5 — Fourier Series of $f(x) = x$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$

With $L = \pi$, the function $f(x) = x$ is odd, so all $a_n = 0$. For the sine coefficients:

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} x \sin(nx)\,dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi x\sin(nx)\,dx.$$

Integrating by parts ($u = x$, $dv = \sin(nx)\,dx$):

$$b_n = \frac{2}{\pi}\left[-\frac{x\cos(nx)}{n}\Big|_0^\pi + \frac{1}{n}\int_0^\pi \cos(nx)\,dx\right] = \frac{2}{\pi}\left(\frac{-\pi(-1)^n}{n} + \frac{\sin(n\pi)}{n^2}\right) = \frac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}.$$

Thus the Fourier sine series is

$$x = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nx) = 2\left(\sin x - \frac{\sin 2x}{2} + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} - \cdots\right), \quad x \in (-\pi, \pi).$$

By Parseval: $\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi x^2\,dx = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{4}{n^2}$, giving $\frac{2\pi^2}{3} = 4\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$, hence $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$.

Example 6.1.6 — Square Wave

Define the $2\pi$-periodic square wave $f(x) = \text{sgn}(\sin x)$, equal to $+1$ on $(0,\pi)$ and $-1$ on $(-\pi, 0)$. Since $f$ is odd and piecewise constant:

$$b_n = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \sin(nx)\,dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\cdot\frac{1-(-1)^n}{n} = \begin{cases} \frac{4}{n\pi} & n \text{ odd} \\ 0 & n \text{ even.}\end{cases}$$

The Fourier series is

$$f(x) \sim \frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\sin((2k+1)x)}{2k+1} = \frac{4}{\pi}\left(\sin x + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} + \frac{\sin 5x}{5} + \cdots\right).$$

At the jump discontinuities $x = 0, \pm\pi$, the series converges to $0$, the average of the left and right limits. The Gibbs overshoot is clearly visible in the partial sums near $x = 0$.

Application: Heat Equation on $[0,L]$

Example 6.1.7 — Heat Equation with Dirichlet Boundary Conditions

Consider $u_t = \kappa u_{xx}$ on $(0,L)\times(0,\infty)$ with $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0$ and $u(x,0) = f(x)$. Separation of variables $u = X(x)T(t)$ yields $T'/(\kappa T) = X''/X = -\lambda$, giving eigenfunctions $X_n(x) = \sin(n\pi x/L)$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2$. The solution is

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-\kappa(n\pi/L)^2 t},$$

where the coefficients $B_n$ are determined by the initial condition via the Fourier sine series:

$$B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx.$$

The exponential decay $e^{-\kappa(n\pi/L)^2 t}$ shows that high-frequency modes are damped faster, and for $t > 0$ the solution is real-analytic even if $f$ is merely $L^2$.
