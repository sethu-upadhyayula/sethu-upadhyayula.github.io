7.2 Weak Derivatives

Weak derivatives extend classical differentiation to functions that are not differentiable in the pointwise sense, requiring only that the integration-by-parts identity hold. This notion is the basis for Sobolev spaces, which are the natural function spaces for the variational formulation of PDEs.

Definition 7.2.1 — Weak Partial Derivative

Let $u \in L^1_\mathrm{loc}(\Omega)$ and $\alpha$ be a multi-index. A function $g \in L^1_\mathrm{loc}(\Omega)$ is the $\alpha$-th weak partial derivative of $u$, written $D^\alpha u = g$, if

$$\int_\Omega g(x)\,\phi(x)\,dx = (-1)^{|\alpha|}\int_\Omega u(x)\,D^\alpha\phi(x)\,dx \quad \text{for all } \phi \in \mathcal{D}(\Omega).$$

If such a $g$ exists, it is unique (up to a set of measure zero). This definition coincides with the classical derivative whenever $u \in C^{|\alpha|}(\Omega)$.

Example 7.2.2 — Weak Derivative of $|x|$

Let $u(x) = |x|$ on $\Omega = (-1,1) \subset \mathbb{R}$. Then $u$ is not differentiable at $x = 0$ in the classical sense. We claim $u' = \mathrm{sgn}(x)$ weakly, i.e., $g(x) = \mathrm{sgn}(x) = \mathbf{1}_{x>0} - \mathbf{1}_{x<0}$. Indeed, for $\phi \in \mathcal{D}(-1,1)$:

$$-\int_{-1}^1 |x|\phi'(x)\,dx = -\int_{-1}^0 (-x)\phi'\,dx - \int_0^1 x\phi'\,dx.$$

Integrating by parts each piece: $= -[{-x\phi}]_{-1}^0 - \int_{-1}^0 (-\phi)\,dx - [x\phi]_0^1 + \int_0^1 \phi\,dx = \int_{-1}^0(-1)\phi\,dx + \int_0^1(+1)\phi\,dx = \int_{-1}^1 \mathrm{sgn}(x)\phi\,dx.$

However, $\mathrm{sgn}(x)$ is itself not weakly differentiable in $L^1_\mathrm{loc}$, since its distributional derivative is $2\delta_0$, which is not a function.

Sobolev Spaces

Definition 7.2.3 — Sobolev Space $W^{k,p}(\Omega)$

For $k \in \mathbb{N}_0$ and $1 \leq p \leq \infty$, the Sobolev space is

$$W^{k,p}(\Omega) = \left\{u \in L^p(\Omega) : D^\alpha u \in L^p(\Omega) \text{ for all } |\alpha| \leq k\right\},$$

equipped with the norm

$$\|u\|_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)} = \left(\sum_{|\alpha|\leq k} \|D^\alpha u\|_{L^p(\Omega)}^p\right)^{1/p} \quad (1 \leq p < \infty),$$ $$\|u\|_{W^{k,\infty}(\Omega)} = \sum_{|\alpha|\leq k} \|D^\alpha u\|_{L^\infty(\Omega)}.$$

The Hilbert–Sobolev spaces $H^k(\Omega) = W^{k,2}(\Omega)$ carry the inner product $\langle u, v\rangle_{H^k} = \sum_{|\alpha|\leq k} \langle D^\alpha u, D^\alpha v\rangle_{L^2}$.

Theorem 7.2.4 — Completeness of Sobolev Spaces

For $1 \leq p \leq \infty$ and $k \in \mathbb{N}_0$, $W^{k,p}(\Omega)$ is a Banach space. When $p = 2$, $H^k(\Omega)$ is a Hilbert space. The completeness follows from a standard argument: if $u_j$ is Cauchy in $W^{k,p}$, then $u_j \to u$ and $D^\alpha u_j \to v_\alpha$ in $L^p$ for each $|\alpha| \leq k$, and one verifies that $D^\alpha u = v_\alpha$ weakly.

Definition 7.2.5 — Space $H^1_0(\Omega)$

The space $H^1_0(\Omega)$ is defined as the closure of $\mathcal{D}(\Omega) = C_0^\infty(\Omega)$ in $H^1(\Omega)$:

$$H^1_0(\Omega) = \overline{C_0^\infty(\Omega)}^{\|\cdot\|_{H^1}}.$$

Informally, $H^1_0(\Omega)$ consists of $H^1(\Omega)$ functions that vanish on $\partial\Omega$ in the sense of the trace operator. It is the correct function space for the Dirichlet problem with homogeneous boundary data.

Remark 7.2.6 — Motivation from PDEs

Classical solutions of $-\Delta u = f$ require $f \in C(\Omega)$ and $u \in C^2(\Omega) \cap C(\overline\Omega)$. For $f \in L^2(\Omega)$ or for domains with non-smooth boundaries, no classical solution may exist. The weak formulation — find $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ such that $\int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v\,dx = \int_\Omega fv\,dx$ for all $v \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ — admits a unique solution by Lax–Milgram, as we shall see in the next section.
