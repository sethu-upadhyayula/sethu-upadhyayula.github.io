7.1 Distributions

Distributions — also called generalised functions — extend the notion of a function by shifting attention from pointwise values to the action of an object against smooth test functions. Introduced by Laurent Schwartz in the 1940s, they provide the natural language for the Dirac delta, for weak solutions of PDEs, and for the Fourier transform on singular objects.

Test Functions

Definition 7.1.1 — Space of Test Functions $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be open. The space of test functions is

$$\mathcal{D}(\Omega) = C_0^\infty(\Omega) = \{\phi \in C^\infty(\Omega) : \text{supp}(\phi) \subset\subset \Omega\},$$

i.e., smooth functions with compact support in $\Omega$. A sequence $\phi_j \to \phi$ in $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$ if there exists a compact set $K \subset\subset \Omega$ such that $\text{supp}(\phi_j) \subset K$ for all $j$, and $D^\alpha \phi_j \to D^\alpha \phi$ uniformly on $K$ for every multi-index $\alpha \in \mathbb{N}_0^n$.

The canonical example is the bump function $\rho(x) = e^{-1/(1-|x|^2)}\mathbf{1}_{|x|<1}$, which belongs to $C_0^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)$.

Distributions

Definition 7.1.2 — Distribution

A distribution on $\Omega$ is a continuous linear functional $T: \mathcal{D}(\Omega) \to \mathbb{C}$. Continuity means: $T[\phi_j] \to T[\phi]$ whenever $\phi_j \to \phi$ in $\mathcal{D}(\Omega)$. The space of all distributions is denoted $\mathcal{D}'(\Omega)$.

We write $T[\phi] = \langle T, \phi \rangle$ for the pairing. Equivalently, $T \in \mathcal{D}'(\Omega)$ is characterised by the local estimate: for each compact $K \subset\subset \Omega$ there exist $C_K > 0$ and $N_K \in \mathbb{N}_0$ such that $|\langle T, \phi\rangle| \leq C_K \sum_{|\alpha|\leq N_K} \sup_K |D^\alpha \phi|$ for all $\phi \in \mathcal{D}(\Omega)$ with $\text{supp}(\phi) \subset K$.

Example 7.1.3 — Regular Distributions

Every locally integrable function $f \in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$ defines a regular distribution $T_f \in \mathcal{D}'(\Omega)$ by

$$\langle T_f, \phi\rangle = \int_\Omega f(x)\,\phi(x)\,dx.$$

The map $f \mapsto T_f$ is injective: if $T_f = T_g$, then $f = g$ almost everywhere. We identify $f$ with $T_f$ and regard $L^1_\mathrm{loc}(\Omega) \hookrightarrow \mathcal{D}'(\Omega)$.

Not every distribution is regular: the Dirac delta (below) is not of the form $T_f$ for any locally integrable $f$.

Definition 7.1.4 — Dirac Delta Distribution

For $x_0 \in \Omega$, the Dirac delta at $x_0$ is the distribution

$$\langle \delta_{x_0}, \phi\rangle = \phi(x_0), \quad \phi \in \mathcal{D}(\Omega).$$

Linearity is immediate; continuity follows since $\phi_j \to \phi$ in $\mathcal{D}$ implies $\phi_j(x_0) \to \phi(x_0)$ uniformly. The Dirac delta $\delta_{x_0}$ is a measure of order $0$ and is singular (not regular).

Example 7.1.5 — Principal Value Distribution

The function $1/x$ is not locally integrable at $0$, so it does not define a regular distribution on $\mathbb{R}$. Its Cauchy principal value is the distribution

$$\left\langle \mathrm{PV}\!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right), \phi\right\rangle = \lim_{\varepsilon \to 0^+} \int_{|x|>\varepsilon} \frac{\phi(x)}{x}\,dx.$$

The limit exists for every $\phi \in \mathcal{D}(\mathbb{R})$ by the cancellation between $+\varepsilon$ and $-\varepsilon$. Note $\mathrm{PV}(1/x) \neq 1/x$ as a distribution since the latter is not defined.

Operations on Distributions

Definition 7.1.6 — Multiplication and Differentiation

Let $T \in \mathcal{D}'(\Omega)$, $\psi \in C^\infty(\Omega)$, and $\alpha$ a multi-index.

Multiplication by a smooth function: $\langle \psi T, \phi\rangle = \langle T, \psi\phi\rangle$.

Distributional derivative: $\langle \partial^\alpha T, \phi\rangle = (-1)^{|\alpha|}\langle T, \partial^\alpha \phi\rangle$.

These definitions are forced by integration by parts: if $T = T_f$ with $f \in C^1$, then $\langle \partial_i T_f, \phi\rangle = -\int f\,\partial_i\phi = \int (\partial_i f)\phi = \langle T_{\partial_i f},\phi\rangle$, consistent with the classical derivative. Every distribution has distributional derivatives of all orders.

Theorem 7.1.7 — Derivative of the Heaviside Function

Let $H(x) = \mathbf{1}_{x > 0}$ be the Heaviside function, viewed as a distribution on $\mathbb{R}$. Then

$$\frac{d}{dx} H = \delta_0,$$

that is, $\langle H', \phi\rangle = \phi(0)$ for all $\phi \in \mathcal{D}(\mathbb{R})$.

Proof

By the definition of distributional derivative:

$$\langle H', \phi\rangle = -\langle H, \phi'\rangle = -\int_0^\infty \phi'(x)\,dx = -\left[\phi(x)\right]_0^\infty = -\phi(\infty) + \phi(0) = \phi(0),$$

since $\phi$ has compact support and thus $\phi(\infty) = 0$. $\square$

Remark 7.1.8 — Fundamental Solution of the Laplacian

In the sense of distributions, $-\Delta \Phi = \delta_0$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$, where $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{n(n-2)\omega_n}|x|^{2-n}$ for $n \geq 3$ and $\Phi(x) = -\frac{1}{2\pi}\log|x|$ for $n=2$. This is the fundamental solution of the Laplacian and is the starting point for Green's function theory.
