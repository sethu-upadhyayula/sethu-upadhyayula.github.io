4.1 Maximum Principle

The maximum principle is one of the most powerful tools in PDE theory. For the heat equation, it states that temperature maxima occur either initially or on the boundary — not in the interior at a later time. This encodes the physical intuition that heat cannot spontaneously concentrate.

Definition 4.1.1 — Parabolic Cylinder and Boundary

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be bounded and $T > 0$. The parabolic cylinder is $Q_T = \Omega \times (0,T]$. The parabolic boundary is:

$$\Gamma_T = \overline{Q}_T \setminus Q_T = (\Omega \times \{0\}) \cup (\partial\Omega \times [0,T])$$

The parabolic boundary consists of the bottom ($t=0$) and the lateral sides ($\partial\Omega$ for all $t$), but not the top ($t=T$).

Theorem 4.1.1 — Weak Maximum Principle

Let $u \in C^{2,1}(Q_T) \cap C(\overline{Q}_T)$ satisfy $u_t \leq k u_{xx}$ (a sub-solution). Then:

$$\max_{\overline{Q}_T} u = \max_{\Gamma_T} u$$

Proof sketch: Suppose the maximum is attained at an interior point $(x_0, t_0)$ with $t_0 > 0$. Then $u_t(x_0, t_0) \geq 0$ (otherwise $u$ was larger just before) and $u_{xx}(x_0, t_0) \leq 0$ (concave in $x$ at maximum). So $u_t - ku_{xx} \geq 0$, but we assumed $u_t - ku_{xx} \leq 0$. A careful argument handles the maximum at $t = T$. $\square$

The same applies to the minimum: $\min_{\overline{Q}_T} u = \min_{\Gamma_T} u$ for solutions.

Theorem 4.1.2 — Uniqueness

The IBVP $u_t = ku_{xx}$ on $(0,L)\times(0,T)$ with $u(x,0) = \phi(x)$ and $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = g(t)$ has at most one solution.

Proof: If $u,v$ are two solutions, $w = u-v$ satisfies the heat equation with zero data. By the maximum principle, $\max w = \max_{\Gamma_T} w = 0$ and $\min w = 0$. So $w \equiv 0$. $\square$

Theorem 4.1.3 — Strong Maximum Principle

If $u$ satisfies $u_t = ku_{xx}$ and attains its maximum at any interior point $(x_0, t_0) \in Q_T$, then $u$ is constant in the entire region $\{(x,t) : t \leq t_0\}$. In other words, a non-constant solution can never attain an interior maximum. This is much stronger than the weak maximum principle.

Application — Comparison Principle

If $u_t = ku_{xx}$ and $v_t = kv_{xx}$ with $u \leq v$ on $\Gamma_T$, then $u \leq v$ throughout $\overline{Q}_T$.

Proof: Apply the maximum principle to $w = u - v$. Since $w \leq 0$ on $\Gamma_T$, we get $\max w \leq 0$, so $w \leq 0$ everywhere. This comparison principle is used to bound solutions and establish stability.
