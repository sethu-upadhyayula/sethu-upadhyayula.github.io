4.2 Fundamental Solution

The fundamental solution (or heat kernel) is the solution corresponding to an initial point mass $\delta(x)$. It encodes all the information about the heat equation on $\mathbb{R}$ and provides an explicit formula for the general Cauchy problem via convolution.

Definition 4.2.1 — The Heat Kernel

The fundamental solution of $u_t = ku_{xx}$ on $\mathbb{R} \times (0,\infty)$ is:

$$\Phi(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi k t}}\, e^{-x^2/(4kt)}, \quad t > 0$$

Properties: (i) $\Phi_t = k\Phi_{xx}$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$, $t > 0$. (ii) $\Phi(x,t) > 0$. (iii) $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\Phi(x,t)\,dx = 1$ for all $t > 0$.

(iv) As $t \to 0^+$: $\Phi(x,t) \to \delta(x)$ in the distributional sense — the kernel concentrates near the origin as a Gaussian with standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{2kt}$.

Theorem 4.2.1 — Solution via Convolution

Given initial data $\phi \in L^\infty(\mathbb{R})$, the function:

$$u(x,t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Phi(x-y, t)\,\phi(y)\,dy = (\Phi(\cdot, t) * \phi)(x)$$

is a $C^\infty$ solution of $u_t = ku_{xx}$ for $t > 0$, with $u(x,t) \to \phi(x_0)$ as $(x,t) \to (x_0, 0)$ at any continuity point $x_0$ of $\phi$.

Explicitly: $u(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi kt}}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x-y)^2/(4kt)}\phi(y)\,dy$. This shows the smoothing effect: $u(\cdot,t)$ is $C^\infty$ for any $t > 0$, even if $\phi$ is only bounded.

Example — Step Function Initial Data

Take $\phi(x) = \mathbf{1}_{x > 0}$ (Heaviside). Then:

$$u(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi kt}}\int_0^\infty e^{-(x-y)^2/(4kt)}\,dy = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 + \mathrm{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{4kt}}\right)\right]$$

where $\mathrm{erf}(z) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_0^z e^{-s^2}\,ds$. The initial step is instantly smoothed into an error function that spreads with width $\sim\sqrt{kt}$.

Physical Interpretation

The heat kernel represents a point source of heat at $(x,t) = (0,0)$. At time $t$, the heat has spread into a Gaussian bell curve of width $\sqrt{4kt}$. The integral $\int \Phi\,dx = 1$ says the total heat is conserved (no heat lost). This is the one-dimensional version of the diffusion process.
