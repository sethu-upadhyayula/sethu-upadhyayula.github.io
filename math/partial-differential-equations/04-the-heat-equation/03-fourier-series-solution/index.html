4.3 Fourier Series Solution

For the heat equation on a bounded interval with homogeneous Dirichlet conditions, separation of variables yields a Fourier sine series solution. This approach reveals the smoothing effect quantitatively: high-frequency modes decay exponentially fast.

Theorem 4.3.1 — Fourier Series Solution

The IBVP $u_t = ku_{xx}$ on $(0,L)\times(0,\infty)$ with $u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0$ and $u(x,0)=\phi(x)$ has the solution:

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

where the Fourier sine coefficients are $b_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L \phi(x)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx$.

Each mode decays at rate $k(n\pi/L)^2$ — quadratically in frequency. High-frequency components (large $n$) decay much faster than low-frequency components.

Smoothing Effect

Even if $\phi$ is only $L^2[0,L]$ (not smooth), for any $t > 0$ the solution is $C^\infty$. The series converges faster than any power of $1/n$ because the exponential factors $e^{-kn^2\pi^2 t/L^2}$ decay super-algebraically in $n$.

Quantitatively: the $n$-th mode is suppressed by a factor $e^{-kn^2\pi^2 t/L^2}$. The time for the $n$-th mode to decay to $1/e$ of its initial amplitude is $\tau_n = L^2/(kn^2\pi^2)$ — much shorter for large $n$.

Example — Initial Temperature $\phi(x) = T_0$

Expand $T_0$ in a Fourier sine series: $b_n = \frac{2T_0}{n\pi}(1 - (-1)^n) = \frac{4T_0}{n\pi}$ for odd $n$, $0$ for even $n$. So:

$$u(x,t) = \frac{4T_0}{\pi}\sum_{n=1,3,5,\ldots}\frac{1}{n}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

At $t=0$ this recovers the constant $T_0$ (Fourier series for a constant). As $t \to \infty$, all terms decay to zero: the rod cools to $u = 0$, consistent with the Dirichlet BCs $u(0)=u(L)=0$.
