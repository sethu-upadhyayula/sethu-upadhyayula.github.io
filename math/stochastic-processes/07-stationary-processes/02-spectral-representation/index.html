7.2 Spectral Representation

The spectral theory of stationary processes is the frequency-domain counterpart of the time-domain covariance analysis from Section 7.1. Bochner's theorem identifies exactly which functions can be autocovariance functions, and the Wiener-Khinchin theorem connects the ACF to the power spectral density. This duality is fundamental to signal processing and time series analysis.

Positive Semidefinite Functions and Bochner's Theorem

Definition 7.2.1 — Positive Semidefinite Function

A function $R: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is positive semidefinite (or positive definite in the sense of Bochner) if for all $n \ge 1$, all $t_1, \ldots, t_n \in \mathbb{R}$, and all $c_1, \ldots, c_n \in \mathbb{C}$: $$\sum_{j=1}^n \sum_{k=1}^n c_j \overline{c_k} R(t_j - t_k) \ge 0.$$

Equivalently, the matrix $(R(t_j - t_k))_{j,k=1}^n$ is positive semidefinite for every choice of time points. An autocovariance function is always positive semidefinite, since $\sum_{j,k} c_j \overline{c_k} R(t_j - t_k) = \operatorname{Var}\!\left(\sum_j c_j X_{t_j}\right) \ge 0$.

Theorem 7.2.2 — Bochner's Theorem

A continuous function $R: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ with $R(0) \ge 0$ is positive semidefinite if and only if it is the Fourier transform of a finite non-negative measure $\mu$ on $\mathbb{R}$: $$R(\tau) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i\omega \tau}\,d\mu(\omega).$$

The measure $\mu$ is called the spectral measure of the process. It is unique and satisfies $\mu(\mathbb{R}) = R(0) = \operatorname{Var}(X_t)$.

Bochner's theorem characterises exactly which functions are valid autocovariance functions of weakly stationary processes: they are precisely the continuous positive semidefinite functions, which are exactly the Fourier transforms of non-negative finite measures. This gives a complete characterisation of the class of valid ACFs.

Spectral Density and the Wiener-Khinchin Theorem

Definition 7.2.3 — Spectral Density

If the spectral measure $\mu$ is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure, it has a density $f(\omega) \ge 0$ called the spectral density (or power spectral density): $$d\mu(\omega) = f(\omega)\,d\omega, \quad R(\tau) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i\omega\tau} f(\omega)\,d\omega.$$

The spectral density $f(\omega)$ describes how the variance of the process is distributed across frequencies $\omega$. The total power is $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(\omega)\,d\omega = R(0) = \operatorname{Var}(X_t)$. Processes with most power at low frequencies have slowly-varying realisations; high-frequency power means rapid fluctuations.

Theorem 7.2.4 — Wiener-Khinchin Theorem

For a weakly stationary process $(X_t)$ with spectral density $f$, the autocovariance and spectral density are a Fourier pair: $$R(\tau) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i\omega\tau} f(\omega)\,d\omega, \qquad f(\omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-i\omega\tau} R(\tau)\,d\tau$$ (when $R \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$).

For discrete-time processes $(X_n)_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$, the spectral density is $2\pi$-periodic: $$R(k) = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{i\omega k} f(\omega)\,d\omega, \qquad f(\omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty e^{-i\omega k} R(k).$$

The Wiener-Khinchin theorem is the operational version of Bochner's theorem: it provides a computable relationship between the time-domain correlation structure and the frequency-domain power distribution. It is the basis for spectral estimation from observed data (periodogram, Welch's method).

Examples

Example — Spectral Density of AR(1)

For the discrete-time AR(1) process with $R(k) = \frac{\sigma^2}{1-\phi^2} \phi^{|k|}$ (Section 7.1), the spectral density is: $$f(\omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty e^{-i\omega k} \frac{\sigma^2 \phi^{|k|}}{1-\phi^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{1}{|1 - \phi e^{-i\omega}|^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\pi(1 + \phi^2 - 2\phi\cos\omega)},$$ for $\omega \in (-\pi, \pi]$.

For $\phi > 0$ (positive autocorrelation), $f$ is peaked near $\omega = 0$ (low frequencies dominate — long-memory-like behaviour). For $\phi < 0$, $f$ is peaked near $\omega = \pm\pi$ (high-frequency oscillation).

The spectral density of the AR(1) innovations is $\sigma^2/(2\pi)$ (flat/white noise). The AR filter $1/(1-\phi z)$ shapes this flat spectrum into the peaked AR(1) spectrum. More generally, an ARMA$(p,q)$ process has spectral density $f(\omega) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{|\Theta(e^{-i\omega})|^2}{|\Phi(e^{-i\omega})|^2}$ where $\Phi$ and $\Theta$ are the AR and MA polynomials.

Example — White Noise and Ornstein-Uhlenbeck

White noise: The process with $R(\tau) = \sigma^2 \delta(\tau)$ (Dirac delta) has flat spectral density $f(\omega) = \sigma^2/(2\pi)$ — equal power at all frequencies. This is the continuous-time analogue of i.i.d. noise, though it does not have a classical sample-path interpretation.

Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process: The stationary solution of $dX_t = -\theta X_t\,dt + \sigma\,dB_t$ (with $\theta > 0$) has $R(\tau) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\theta} e^{-\theta|\tau|}$ and spectral density $$f(\omega) = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\pi(\theta^2 + \omega^2)}.$$

The OU spectral density is the Lorentzian (Cauchy) function of $\omega$, with half-power bandwidth $\theta$. As $\theta \to 0$ (less damping), the power concentrates at $\omega = 0$ (the process approaches a random walk). As $\theta \to \infty$ (strong damping), $f \to \sigma^2/(2\pi\theta^2)$ (flat) — the process becomes rapidly decorrelated.

Theorem 7.2.5 — Spectral Representation Theorem

Every zero-mean weakly stationary process $(X_t)_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ with spectral measure $\mu$ has a spectral representation: $$X_t = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i\omega t}\,dZ(\omega),$$ where $Z$ is a stochastic process with orthogonal increments satisfying $E[|dZ(\omega)|^2] = d\mu(\omega)$.

This is the Cramér representation. It decomposes the process into uncorrelated oscillatory components at each frequency $\omega$. If $f(\omega)$ exists, then $dZ(\omega) = \hat{X}(\omega)\,d\omega$ where $\hat{X}$ is a stochastic Fourier transform.

The spectral representation is the analogue for stationary processes of the Fourier decomposition of a periodic function. It connects the abstract theory of stationary processes to Hilbert space methods and the prediction theory of Wiener and Kolmogorov.

Remark — Discrete-Time Spectral Representation

For a discrete-time weakly stationary process $(X_n)_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$, the spectral representation is: $$X_n = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{in\omega}\,dZ(\omega),$$ where $Z$ has orthogonal increments with $E[|dZ(\omega)|^2] = f(\omega)\,d\omega$. The spectral representation is unique given the spectral measure $\mu$.

The spectral density $f(\omega)$ can be estimated from data using the periodogram $I_n(\omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi n}\left|\sum_{t=1}^n X_t e^{-it\omega}\right|^2$, which is asymptotically proportional to $f(\omega)$ but is not consistent without smoothing. Consistent estimators use spectral windows (Bartlett, Parzen, etc.) to smooth the periodogram.
