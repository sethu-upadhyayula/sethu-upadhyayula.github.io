1.4 Ergodic Theorem

The ergodic theorem is the central convergence result for Markov chains. It states that under mild conditions, the chain forgets its initial state and converges to the unique stationary distribution, both in the distributional sense and as time averages.

Definition 1.4.1 — Ergodic Chain

A Markov chain is called ergodic if it is:
(i) Irreducible: all states communicate;
(ii) Positive recurrent: $E_i[T_i] < \infty$ for all $i$;
(iii) Aperiodic: $d(i) = 1$ for all $i$.
Every finite irreducible aperiodic chain is ergodic.

Theorem 1.4.2 — Convergence to Stationarity

For an ergodic chain with unique stationary distribution $\pi$, $$(P^n)_{ij} \to \pi_j \quad \text{as } n \to \infty, \text{ for all } i, j \in S.$$ In particular, $\mu P^n \to \pi$ for any initial distribution $\mu$.

Theorem 1.4.3 — Ergodic Theorem (Time Averages)

For an irreducible positive recurrent chain with stationary distribution $\pi$, and any bounded function $f: S \to \mathbb{R}$, $$\frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f(X_k) \xrightarrow{a.s.} \sum_{i \in S} \pi_i f(i) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty,$$ regardless of the initial state. This says time averages equal ensemble averages under $\pi$.

Sketch of Proof

The key idea is to break the path into i.i.d. regeneration cycles between successive returns to a fixed state $i^*$. By the strong law of large numbers applied to the cycle rewards, the time average converges a.s. to $E[f \text{ over one cycle}] / E[\text{cycle length}] = \sum_j \pi_j f(j)$. $\square$

Rate of Convergence

Theorem 1.4.4 — Geometric Convergence (Finite Chains)

For a finite ergodic chain with stationary distribution $\pi$ and second-largest eigenvalue magnitude $\lambda^* < 1$, $$\|(P^n)_{i\cdot} - \pi\|_{\text{TV}} \le C \cdot (\lambda^*)^n$$ for some constant $C > 0$. The spectral gap $1 - \lambda^*$ controls how quickly the chain mixes.

Example — Lazy Random Walk on $\{0, \ldots, N\}$

A lazy random walk (stays put with probability $1/2$, moves left or right with probability $1/4$ each) on $N+1$ states has stationary distribution $\pi_i = 1/(N+1)$ and mixing time $O(N^2)$. The spectral gap is $\Theta(1/N^2)$, reflecting the diffusive nature of the walk.

Remark — Coupling

A powerful technique to prove convergence is coupling: run two copies of the chain (one from any initial state, one from $\pi$) until they meet (the coupling time $\tau$). Then $\|(P^n)_{i\cdot} - \pi\|_{\text{TV}} \le P(\tau > n)$, reducing mixing time estimates to questions about when two random walks meet.
