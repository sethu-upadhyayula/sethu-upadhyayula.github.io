4.2 Renewal Reward Theorem

The renewal reward theorem is a powerful law of large numbers for systems that evolve in cycles: it identifies the long-run rate of any cumulative reward process in terms of the expected reward and expected duration per cycle. It unifies and extends the elementary renewal theorem.

Reward Processes

Definition 4.2.1 - Renewal Reward Process

Let $\{T_n\}_{n \ge 1}$ be i.i.d. inter-renewal times with distribution $F$, mean $\mu = E[T_1] \in (0, \infty)$, and renewal counting process $N(t)$. Associate to the $n$-th renewal cycle a reward $R_n$, where $(R_n, T_n)$ are i.i.d. across cycles with $E[|R_1|] < \infty$.

The cumulative reward up to time $t$ is $$W(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{N(t)} R_n.$$

The reward $R_n$ may depend on $T_n$ (e.g., $R_n = T_n$ for time-spent rewards) or may be independent of $T_n$. The pair $(R_n, T_n)$ is i.i.d. across cycles; within a cycle they may be dependent.

The Renewal Reward Theorem

Theorem 4.2.2 - Renewal Reward Theorem

If $E[|R_1|] < \infty$ and $\mu = E[T_1] \in (0, \infty)$, then $$\frac{W(t)}{t} \xrightarrow{t \to \infty} \frac{E[R_1]}{E[T_1]} = \frac{E[R_1]}{\mu} \quad \text{almost surely.}$$

Proof. Write $W(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{N(t)} R_n$. Since $N(t) \to \infty$ a.s., the strong law of large numbers (SLLN) gives $$\frac{W(t)}{N(t)} = \frac{1}{N(t)}\sum_{n=1}^{N(t)} R_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} E[R_1].$$ Dividing by $t$: $$\frac{W(t)}{t} = \frac{W(t)}{N(t)} \cdot \frac{N(t)}{t}.$$ By the elementary renewal theorem (Section 4.1), $N(t)/t \to 1/\mu$ a.s. (SLLN applied to $S_{N(t)}/N(t) \to \mu$). Combining: $$\frac{W(t)}{t} \to E[R_1] \cdot \frac{1}{\mu} = \frac{E[R_1]}{\mu}. \quad\square$$

An analogous result holds in expectation: $E[W(t)]/t \to E[R_1]/\mu$ as $t \to \infty$ (under integrability conditions).

Elementary Renewal Theorem Revisited

Corollary 4.2.3 - Elementary Renewal Theorem

Setting $R_n = 1$ for all $n$ (unit reward per renewal), $W(t) = N(t)$, and the renewal reward theorem gives $$\frac{N(t)}{t} \to \frac{1}{\mu} \quad \text{a.s.}$$

This is the elementary renewal theorem. The renewal function $m(t) = E[N(t)]$ satisfies $m(t)/t \to 1/\mu$ by the expectation version. The renewal equation $m(t) = F(t) + \int_0^t m(t-s)\,dF(s)$ (Definition 4.1.2) provides an exact formula solved via Laplace transforms.

Blackwell's Theorem and the Renewal Equation

Theorem 4.2.4 - Key Renewal Theorem

Let $z(t)$ satisfy the renewal equation $z(t) = g(t) + \int_0^t z(t-s)\,dF(s)$ where $g$ is directly Riemann integrable. If $F$ is non-arithmetic (non-lattice), then $$z(t) \xrightarrow{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{\mu}\int_0^\infty g(s)\,ds.$$

This is the Key Renewal Theorem (also called the renewal theorem or Smith's theorem). It gives the long-run limit of solutions to renewal equations. Blackwell's theorem ($m(t+h) - m(t) \to h/\mu$) is an equivalent formulation. Together they enable computation of long-run limits for quantities like the age $A(t) = t - S_{N(t)}$ and excess life $E(t) = S_{N(t)+1} - t$.

Applications

Example - Alternating Renewal Process: Availability

A machine alternates between working (up) and repair (down) periods. Cycle $n$ has up time $U_n$ with mean $\mu_U$ and down time $D_n$ with mean $\mu_D$; the cycle length is $T_n = U_n + D_n$ with mean $\mu = \mu_U + \mu_D$. Define $R_n = U_n$ (time machine is up in cycle $n$). The long-run availability is $$A = \lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{\text{time up in }[0,t]}{t} = \frac{E[R_1]}{E[T_1]} = \frac{\mu_U}{\mu_U + \mu_D}.$$

This result is completely distribution-free: only the means $\mu_U$ and $\mu_D$ matter, regardless of the shapes of the up- and down-time distributions.

Example - Age of a Component

Define the age (backward recurrence time) at time $t$ as $A(t) = t - S_{N(t)}$ - the time since the last renewal. Define $R_n = \int_0^{T_n} s\,ds = T_n^2/2$ (integrated age over cycle $n$). Then $\int_0^t A(s)\,ds = \sum_{n=1}^{N(t)} T_n^2/2 + \text{partial term}$, and by the renewal reward theorem with $R_n = T_n^2/2$: $$\frac{1}{t}\int_0^t A(s)\,ds \to \frac{E[T_1^2]/2}{\mu} = \frac{E[T_1^2]}{2\mu}.$$

By the Key Renewal Theorem, the limiting distribution of $A(t)$ as $t \to \infty$ has CDF $F_A(x) = \frac{1}{\mu}\int_0^x [1 - F(s)]\,ds$, the size-biased distribution. This is the inspection paradox: a randomly observed interval is stochastically larger than a typical interval.

Remark - Inspection Paradox

The inspection paradox states that the inter-renewal interval containing a fixed time $t$ (large) is stochastically larger than a typical inter-renewal interval $T_1 \sim F$. More precisely, the length $A(t) + E(t)$ of the current interval has the size-biased distribution with mean $E[T_1^2]/E[T_1] \ge E[T_1]$ (by Jensen's inequality, with equality iff $T_1$ is degenerate).

This phenomenon explains why, for example, the bus you are waiting for always seems to take longer than average: you are more likely to arrive during a longer inter-bus interval. The renewal reward theorem quantifies this precisely through the long-run time-average of $A(t) + E(t)$.
