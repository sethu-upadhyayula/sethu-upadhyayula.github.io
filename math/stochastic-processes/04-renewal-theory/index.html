Chapter 4: Renewal Theory

Renewal theory studies the long-run behaviour of a sequence of i.i.d. random variables (inter-renewal times). It generalises the Poisson process to arbitrary inter-event distributions and provides powerful tools for analysing systems that "renew" or restart after each cycle.

Applications are vast: maintenance scheduling (replace a machine after each failure), insurance (claims arriving at random intervals), and queuing theory (busy periods in M/G/1 queues) all rest on renewal-theoretic foundations.

Chapter Overview

4.1 - Renewal Processes

A renewal process is defined by i.i.d. inter-renewal times $T_1, T_2, \ldots \ge 0$ with mean $\mu = E[T]$. The renewal counting process is $N(t) = \max\{n : S_n \le t\}$ and the renewal function $m(t) = E[N(t)]$ satisfies the renewal equation. The Elementary Renewal Theorem states $m(t)/t \to 1/\mu$ as $t \to \infty$.

4.2 - Renewal Reward Theorem

When a reward $R_n$ is earned in the $n$th renewal cycle, the long-run average reward rate converges a.s. to $E[R]/E[T]$. This encompasses Little's law, the long-run availability of a machine, and the long-run average cost of a maintenance policy.
