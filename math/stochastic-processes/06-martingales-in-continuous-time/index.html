Chapter 6: Martingales in Continuous Time

The theory of martingales - processes whose expected future value equals their current value - extends naturally from the discrete-time setting studied in introductory probability to the richly structured continuous-time world. Continuous-time martingale theory is the backbone of modern stochastic analysis and mathematical finance.

Brownian motion is itself a martingale, and the stochastic integrals built from it inherit the martingale property. The optional stopping theorem, maximal inequalities, and convergence theorems from discrete time all have continuous-time counterparts, though the proofs require more care due to uncountably many time points and the subtleties of right-continuous paths.

Chapter Overview

6.1 - Continuous-Time Martingales

A process $(M_t)_{t \ge 0}$ adapted to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ is a martingale if $E[M_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = M_s$ for all $s \le t$. We examine the key examples - $B_t$, $B_t^2 - t$, and the exponential martingale $e^{\theta B_t - \theta^2 t/2}$ - along with local martingales, the càdlàg (right-continuous with left limits) regularity condition, and the martingale convergence theorem.

6.2 - Optional Stopping

The optional stopping theorem gives conditions under which $E[M_\tau] = E[M_0]$ for a stopping time $\tau$. Applied to the Brownian martingales, it yields Wald's identities: $E[B_\tau] = 0$ and $E[B_\tau^2] = E[\tau]$ whenever $E[\tau] < \infty$. These powerful identities let us compute expected exit times and probabilities for BM without solving differential equations.
