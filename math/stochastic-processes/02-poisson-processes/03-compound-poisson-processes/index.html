2.3 Compound Poisson Processes

A compound Poisson process generalises the Poisson process by associating a random "mark" or "jump size" with each arrival. The cumulative sum of marks up to time $t$ models total claims, aggregate losses, or the total workload arriving to a queue.

Definition 2.3.1 — Compound Poisson Process

Let $\{N(t)\}$ be a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, and let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. random variables (independent of $N$) with common distribution $F_X$. The process $$Y(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{N(t)} X_k, \quad t \ge 0$$ is called a compound Poisson process. (By convention $Y(t)=0$ when $N(t)=0$.)

The $X_k$ represent the sizes of jumps (claims, batch sizes, etc.) and $N(t)$ counts the number of jumps in $[0,t]$.

Theorem 2.3.2 — Mean and Variance

By Wald's identity and the law of total variance, $$E[Y(t)] = E[N(t)] \cdot E[X] = \lambda t \, E[X],$$ $$\text{Var}[Y(t)] = E[N(t)] \cdot E[X^2] = \lambda t \, E[X^2].$$ Note that the variance depends on the second moment $E[X^2]$, not just $\text{Var}[X]$.

Theorem 2.3.3 — Moment Generating Function

If $M_X(\theta) = E[e^{\theta X}]$ is the MGF of $X$, then the MGF of $Y(t)$ is $$E[e^{\theta Y(t)}] = \exp\!\left(\lambda t \left(M_X(\theta) - 1\right)\right).$$ This follows by conditioning on $N(t)$ and using the i.i.d. structure of the marks.

Derivation

Conditioning on $N(t) = n$: $$E[e^{\theta Y(t)}] = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} E\!\left[e^{\theta \sum_{k=1}^n X_k}\right] P(N(t)=n) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} M_X(\theta)^n \frac{(\lambda t)^n e^{-\lambda t}}{n!} = e^{-\lambda t} e^{\lambda t M_X(\theta)} = e^{\lambda t(M_X(\theta)-1)}. \quad \square$$

Example — Insurance Risk Model

In the Cramér–Lundberg model for insurance, claims arrive as a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, and each claim has size $X_k \sim \text{Exp}(\mu)$. The insurer's surplus at time $t$ is $$U(t) = u + ct - Y(t),$$ where $u$ is the initial capital and $c$ is the premium rate. Ruin occurs if $U(t) < 0$ for some $t$.

With $E[X] = 1/\mu$ and the net profit condition $c > \lambda/\mu$, the ruin probability starting from $u$ is $(\lambda/c\mu) e^{-(c\mu/\lambda - 1)\mu u}$.

Remark — Lévy Processes

A compound Poisson process is a special case of a Lévy process — a process with stationary independent increments and càdlàg paths. The Lévy–Khintchine representation generalises the MGF formula to all Lévy processes, with the compound Poisson part accounting for large jumps.
