2.1 Definition and Properties

The Poisson process is the simplest and most important continuous-time stochastic process. It counts the number of random events (arrivals, failures, phone calls) occurring in a time interval, subject to a memorylessness and independence assumption.

Definition 2.1.1 — Poisson Process

A counting process $\{N(t), t \ge 0\}$ is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda > 0$ if:
(i) $N(0) = 0$;
(ii) The process has independent increments: for $0 \le s_1 < t_1 \le s_2 < t_2$, the increments $N(t_1)-N(s_1)$ and $N(t_2)-N(s_2)$ are independent;
(iii) The process has stationary increments: $N(t+s) - N(s) \overset{d}{=} N(t)$ for all $s, t \ge 0$;

(iv) $N(t) - N(s) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda(t-s))$ for $0 \le s < t$, so $$P(N(t)-N(s)=k) = \frac{(\lambda(t-s))^k e^{-\lambda(t-s)}}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

Theorem 2.1.2 — Mean and Variance

For a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, $$E[N(t)] = \text{Var}[N(t)] = \lambda t.$$ This equal mean and variance is the hallmark of the Poisson distribution. The standard deviation $\sqrt{\lambda t}$ grows much slower than the mean, so relative fluctuations vanish as $t \to \infty$.

Superposition and Thinning

Theorem 2.1.3 — Superposition

If $N_1(t), N_2(t), \ldots, N_k(t)$ are independent Poisson processes with rates $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$, then their sum $N(t) = N_1(t) + \cdots + N_k(t)$ is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_k$.

Theorem 2.1.4 — Thinning

If each arrival of a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$ is independently classified as type 1 with probability $p$ and type 2 with probability $1-p$, then the type-1 arrivals form a Poisson process with rate $\lambda p$ and the type-2 arrivals form an independent Poisson process with rate $\lambda(1-p)$.

Example — Infinitesimal Characterisation

An equivalent definition uses infinitesimals: as $h \to 0$, $$P(N(t+h)-N(t)=1) = \lambda h + o(h), \quad P(N(t+h)-N(t) \ge 2) = o(h).$$ This says arrivals happen at rate $\lambda$ and simultaneous arrivals have probability $o(h)$. Combined with independent increments, these properties characterise the Poisson process.

The infinitesimal view makes it natural to view the Poisson process as a CTMC on $\{0,1,2,\ldots\}$ with generator $q_{n,n+1} = \lambda$ and $q_{nn} = -\lambda$.

Remark — Poisson Approximation

By the law of rare events, the sum of many independent Bernoulli random variables with small success probabilities is approximately Poisson. More precisely, if $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $P(X_i = 1) = p_i$ and $\lambda = \sum p_i$, then $\sum X_i$ is approximately $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ when all $p_i$ are small.
