2.2 Interarrival Times

The Poisson process can equivalently be described through its interarrival times — the waiting times between consecutive events. This perspective connects the Poisson process to the exponential distribution via the memoryless property.

Definition 2.2.1 — Interarrival and Arrival Times

Let $S_n$ denote the time of the $n$th arrival, with $S_0 = 0$. The interarrival times are $$T_n = S_n - S_{n-1}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

So $S_n = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots + T_n$ is the time of the $n$th event.

Theorem 2.2.2 — Exponential Interarrivals

For a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, the interarrival times $T_1, T_2, \ldots$ are independent and identically distributed with $T_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, i.e., $$P(T_i > t) = e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \ge 0.$$ This is a consequence of the independent stationary increments and the memoryless property of the exponential distribution.

Theorem 2.2.3 — Gamma Arrival Times

The $n$th arrival time $S_n = T_1 + \cdots + T_n$ follows a Gamma distribution: $$S_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda), \quad f_{S_n}(t) = \frac{\lambda^n t^{n-1} e^{-\lambda t}}{(n-1)!}, \quad t \ge 0.$$ The mean is $E[S_n] = n/\lambda$ and the variance is $\text{Var}[S_n] = n/\lambda^2$.

Order Statistics Representation

Theorem 2.2.4 — Conditional Uniform Distribution

Given $N(t) = n$, the $n$ arrival times $S_1 < S_2 < \cdots < S_n$ have the same joint distribution as the order statistics $U_{(1)} \le U_{(2)} \le \cdots \le U_{(n)}$ of $n$ independent $\text{Uniform}(0, t)$ random variables. That is, conditional on $n$ arrivals in $[0,t]$, their locations are uniformly spread.

Sketch of Proof

The joint density of $(S_1, \ldots, S_n)$ given $N(t)=n$ factors as $n!/t^n$ on $\{0 < s_1 < \cdots < s_n < t\}$, which is exactly the density of $n$ ordered uniform points on $[0,t]$. $\square$

Example — Expected Arrival Time

Given $N(1) = n$, the expected position of the $k$th arrival is $E[S_k \mid N(1)=n] = k/(n+1)$, matching the expected value of the $k$th order statistic of $n$ uniforms on $[0,1]$. In particular, the arrivals are approximately evenly spaced conditional on their count.

Remark — Memoryless Property

The exponential distribution is the only continuous memoryless distribution: $P(T > s+t \mid T > s) = P(T > t)$. This memorylessness is what gives the Poisson process its Markov property and its characterisation via interarrival times.
