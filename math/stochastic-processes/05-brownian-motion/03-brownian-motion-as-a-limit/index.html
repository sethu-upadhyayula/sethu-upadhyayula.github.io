5.3 Brownian Motion as a Limit

The classical central limit theorem tells us that the rescaled sum of i.i.d. random variables converges in distribution to a Gaussian. Donsker's invariance principle is a far-reaching extension: the entire rescaled random walk path converges in distribution to Brownian motion as a random function. This is why BM is the universal scaling limit of a huge class of random walk models.

Rescaled Random Walk

Definition 5.3.1 — Rescaled Random Walk

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = 0$ and $E[X_i^2] = \sigma^2 \in (0,\infty)$. Define the partial sums $S_0 = 0$, $S_k = X_1 + \cdots + X_k$. The rescaled random walk (or Donsker interpolation) is the process $W^{(n)} \in C[0,1]$ defined by linear interpolation: $$W^{(n)}_t = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} S_{\lfloor nt \rfloor} + \frac{nt - \lfloor nt \rfloor}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} X_{\lfloor nt \rfloor + 1}, \quad t \in [0,1].$$

For integer times $t = k/n$, this gives $W^{(n)}_{k/n} = S_k / (\sigma\sqrt{n})$. Between integer multiples of $1/n$, $W^{(n)}$ is linearly interpolated, making it an element of the space $C[0,1]$ of continuous functions on $[0,1]$.

Weak Convergence in $C[0,1]$

Definition 5.3.2 — Weak Convergence in $C[0,1]$

The space $C[0,1]$ of continuous functions $f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ is a separable Banach space under the supremum norm $\|f\|_\infty = \sup_{t\in[0,1]}|f(t)|$. A sequence of probability measures $P_n$ on $C[0,1]$ converges weakly to $P$ (written $P_n \Rightarrow P$) if $$\int_{C[0,1]} g\,dP_n \to \int_{C[0,1]} g\,dP$$ for every bounded continuous function $g: C[0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$.

This is convergence in distribution of random elements taking values in the function space $C[0,1]$. The Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $C[0,1]$ (generated by the open sets in the $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ topology) makes this precise.

Theorem 5.3.3 — Donsker's Invariance Principle (Functional CLT)

Under the conditions of Definition 5.3.1, as $n \to \infty$: $$W^{(n)} \xRightarrow{d} B \quad \text{in } C[0,1],$$ where $B = (B_t)_{0 \le t \le 1}$ is standard Brownian motion. More precisely, the distributions of $W^{(n)}$ on $(C[0,1], \mathcal{B}(C[0,1]))$ converge weakly to Wiener measure.

Proof outline (three steps):

  1. Finite-dimensional convergence: For fixed $0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_k \le 1$, the vector $(W^{(n)}_{t_1}, \ldots, W^{(n)}_{t_k})$ converges in distribution to $(B_{t_1}, \ldots, B_{t_k})$ by the multivariate CLT, since the increments are sums of i.i.d. terms.
  2. Tightness: The sequence $\{P \circ (W^{(n)})^{-1}\}$ is tight in $C[0,1]$: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a compact set $K \subset C[0,1]$ with $P(W^{(n)} \in K) \ge 1-\varepsilon$ for all $n$. Tightness follows from the Kolmogorov–Chentsov criterion via $E[|W^{(n)}_t - W^{(n)}_s|^4] \le C|t-s|^2$.
  3. Uniqueness of limit points: By Prokhorov's theorem, tightness implies relative compactness. All subsequential limits must have the same finite-dimensional distributions as BM, so the unique limit is Wiener measure.

The result holds under the weaker condition $E[X_i^2] < \infty$ (no higher moments needed). The "invariance" in the name refers to the fact that the limit is the same for all distributions with mean 0 and finite variance — it depends only on $\sigma^2$, not on the specific distribution of the $X_i$.

Applications: Limits of Functionals

Theorem 5.3.4 — Continuous Mapping Theorem

If $W^{(n)} \Rightarrow B$ in $C[0,1]$ and $\phi: C[0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ is a continuous functional (with respect to $\|\cdot\|_\infty$), then $$\phi(W^{(n)}) \xrightarrow{d} \phi(B).$$

This is the key tool for computing distributional limits of functionals of random walks. Any functional that is continuous in the uniform topology can be handled this way. Examples include: $\phi(f) = \max_{t \in [0,1]} f(t)$ (maximum), $\phi(f) = \int_0^1 f(t)\,dt$ (integral), and $\phi(f) = f(T)$ for a fixed time $T$ (evaluation map).

Example — Maximum of Random Walk

Let $M_n = \max_{0 \le k \le n} S_k$ be the running maximum of a simple symmetric random walk. The map $f \mapsto \max_{t \in [0,1]} f(t)$ is continuous on $C[0,1]$, so by Donsker and the CMT: $$\frac{M_n}{\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \max_{0 \le t \le 1} B_t = M_1,$$ where $M_1$ has density $f_{M_1}(x) = \sqrt{2/\pi} e^{-x^2/2}$ for $x \ge 0$ (Section 5.2).

For simple symmetric random walk ($\sigma = 1$, $P(X_i = \pm 1) = 1/2$), this says $M_n / \sqrt{n}$ converges to the half-normal distribution. Since $E[M_1] = \sqrt{2/\pi}$, the expected maximum is approximately $\sqrt{2n/\pi}$.

Example — Kolmogorov–Smirnov Statistic

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $\sim F$ and let $F_n$ be the empirical CDF. The Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic is $D_n = \sup_{x} |F_n(x) - F(x)|$. Under $H_0 : F = F_0$ (continuous), a change of variables via the probability integral transform shows that $\sqrt{n} D_n$ converges in distribution to $\sup_{0 \le t \le 1} |B^0_t|$, where $B^0$ is the Brownian bridge. This follows from Donsker's theorem applied to the uniform empirical process.

The distribution of $\sup_{0 \le t \le 1} |B^0_t|$ is the Kolmogorov distribution, whose CDF is $K(x) = 1 - 2\sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^{k-1} e^{-2k^2 x^2}$. This distribution-free limit underpins the KS test for goodness of fit.

Remark — Extensions and Limitations

Donsker's theorem extends to: (1) triangular arrays (Lindeberg condition replacing identical distribution), (2) weakly dependent sequences (under mixing conditions), and (3) higher dimensions where the limit is $d$-dimensional BM.

The CMT does not apply to all functionals — notably those that are not continuous in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$. For example, passage times require more delicate arguments. For heavy-tailed $X_i$ with $E[X_i^2] = \infty$, the limit is an $\alpha$-stable process rather than BM.
