5.2 Reflection Principle

The reflection principle exploits the spatial symmetry and Markov property of Brownian motion to compute the distribution of the running maximum and of first-passage times. It is one of the most elegant tools in the study of BM.

The Markov Property

Theorem 5.2.1 - Markov Property

Let $(B_t)_{t \ge 0}$ be a standard BM and let $\mathcal{F}_s = \sigma(B_u : u \le s)$ be the natural filtration. For $s \ge 0$, the process $\tilde{B}_t = B_{s+t} - B_s$ ($t \ge 0$) is a standard BM that is independent of $\mathcal{F}_s$.

Equivalently, the conditional distribution of $(B_{s+t})_{t \ge 0}$ given $\mathcal{F}_s$ is that of $B_s + \tilde{B}_t$ where $\tilde{B}$ is an independent BM starting at 0. In other words, BM "restarts" from its current position at any fixed time $s$.

Theorem 5.2.2 - Strong Markov Property

Let $\tau$ be a stopping time with respect to $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ (i.e., $\{\tau \le t\} \in \mathcal{F}_t$ for all $t$) with $P(\tau < \infty) > 0$. On the event $\{\tau < \infty\}$, the process $\tilde{B}_t = B_{\tau+t} - B_\tau$ ($t \ge 0$) is a standard BM independent of $\mathcal{F}_\tau$.

This strengthens the ordinary Markov property by allowing the restart time to be a random stopping time rather than a fixed time. The strong Markov property is essential for the reflection principle because first-passage times are stopping times, not deterministic times.

The Reflection Principle

Theorem 5.2.3 - Reflection Principle

For $a > 0$ and $t > 0$, define the running maximum $M_t = \max_{0 \le s \le t} B_s$ and the first-passage time $T_a = \inf\{s \ge 0 : B_s = a\}$. Then: $$P(M_t \ge a) = P(T_a \le t) = 2P(B_t \ge a) = 2\left(1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{t}}\right)\right),$$ where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

Proof sketch. The events $\{M_t \ge a\} = \{T_a \le t\}$ are identical since BM has continuous paths. On the event $\{T_a \le t\}$, at the first time $T_a$ the process hits $a$, and by the strong Markov property the future increment $B_t - B_{T_a}$ is an independent BM increment. By symmetry ($B_{T_a} = a$ and the future increment is symmetric about 0): $$P(B_t \ge a \mid T_a \le t) = \frac{1}{2}.$$ Therefore $P(B_t \ge a) = P(B_t \ge a, T_a \le t) = \frac{1}{2} P(T_a \le t)$, giving $P(T_a \le t) = 2P(B_t \ge a)$. $\square$

Corollary 5.2.4 - Distribution of Running Maximum

The running maximum $M_t = \max_{0 \le s \le t} B_s$ has the distribution: $$P(M_t \le x) = 2\Phi\!\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\right) - 1 = \operatorname{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{2t}}\right), \quad x \ge 0,$$ with density $f_{M_t}(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi t}} e^{-x^2/(2t)}$ for $x \ge 0$.

In particular, $E[M_t] = \sqrt{2t/\pi}$ and $\operatorname{Var}(M_t) = t(1 - 2/\pi)$. More generally, $(M_t, B_t)$ has joint density $f(m,b) = \frac{2(2m-b)}{t\sqrt{2\pi t}} e^{-(2m-b)^2/(2t)}$ for $m \ge \max(b, 0)$.

First Passage Times

Definition 5.2.5 - First Passage Time

For $a \in \mathbb{R}$, the first passage time to level $a$ is $$T_a = \inf\{t \ge 0 : B_t = a\}.$$

By convention $\inf \emptyset = +\infty$. For BM starting at 0 and $a \ne 0$, $T_a$ is a stopping time with respect to the natural filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$.

Theorem 5.2.6 - Distribution of First Passage Time

For $a > 0$, the first passage time $T_a$ has the inverse Gaussian (Lévy) distribution: $$P(T_a \le t) = 2\left(1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{t}}\right)\right), \quad t > 0,$$ with density $$f_{T_a}(t) = \frac{a}{\sqrt{2\pi t^3}} e^{-a^2/(2t)}, \quad t > 0.$$

The mean and variance of $T_a$ are: $$E[T_a] = +\infty, \qquad P(T_a < \infty) = 1.$$

The infinite mean reflects the heavy tail: the density decays as $t^{-3/2}$ for large $t$, which is not integrable after multiplying by $t$. Yet $P(T_a < \infty) = 1$ since $\lim_{t\to\infty} 2(1-\Phi(a/\sqrt{t})) = 1$, i.e., standard BM is recurrent in 1D.

Proposition 5.2.7 - Recurrence of Brownian Motion

One-dimensional standard Brownian motion is recurrent: for every $a \in \mathbb{R}$, $$P(T_a < \infty) = 1.$$ Moreover, $B_t$ visits every level infinitely often a.s.; in particular, $\limsup_{t\to\infty} B_t = +\infty$ and $\liminf_{t\to\infty} B_t = -\infty$ a.s.

In contrast, $d$-dimensional BM ($d \ge 3$) is transient: $|B_t| \to \infty$ a.s. as $t \to \infty$. Two-dimensional BM is recurrent (it visits every open set infinitely often) but does not return to any fixed point a.s.

Example - Gambler's Ruin for Brownian Motion

A Brownian motion starting at $x \in (0, b)$ exits the interval $(0, b)$ at time $\tau = T_0 \wedge T_b$. By the optional stopping theorem for the martingale $B_t$ (Section 6.2): $$P(B_\tau = b) = \frac{x}{b}, \qquad P(B_\tau = 0) = \frac{b-x}{b}.$$

This is the continuous analogue of the gambler's ruin formula. The expected exit time $E[\tau] = x(b-x)$, obtained by the optional stopping theorem applied to $B_t^2 - t$.
