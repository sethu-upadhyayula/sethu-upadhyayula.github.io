5.1 Construction and Properties

Standard Brownian motion (also called the Wiener process) is characterised by a small set of axioms that uniquely pin down a Gaussian process with continuous paths. We begin with its definition, then discuss existence, and finally explore its remarkable analytical properties.

Definition

Definition 5.1.1 — Standard Brownian Motion

A stochastic process $(B_t)_{t \ge 0}$ defined on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a standard Brownian motion (or Wiener process) if:

  1. $B_0 = 0$ almost surely.
  2. Independent increments: For $0 \le t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n$, the increments $B_{t_1}-B_{t_0}, B_{t_2}-B_{t_1}, \ldots, B_{t_n}-B_{t_{n-1}}$ are mutually independent.
  3. Stationary Gaussian increments: For $s, t \ge 0$, $B_{t+s} - B_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, s)$.
  4. Continuous paths: The function $t \mapsto B_t(\omega)$ is continuous for almost every $\omega \in \Omega$.
Remark — Uniqueness of Distribution

Conditions (1)–(3) determine all finite-dimensional distributions of $(B_t)$. By the Kolmogorov extension theorem, there exists a probability measure on $\mathbb{R}^{[0,\infty)}$ with these finite-dimensional marginals. The non-trivial content is that this measure can be supported on the space of continuous functions — this is Wiener's 1923 result.

Existence: Lévy–Ciesielski Construction

Theorem 5.1.2 — Existence of Brownian Motion

Standard Brownian motion exists. One explicit construction on $[0,1]$ proceeds via the Lévy–Ciesielski (Haar/Schauder) series:

$$B_t = Z_0 t + \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{2^n - 1} Z_{n,k}\, H_{n,k}(t),$$

where $Z_0, Z_{n,k}$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $H_{n,k}$ are the Schauder functions (integrated Haar wavelets). The series converges uniformly a.s., yielding a continuous process with the correct distribution.

Alternatively, the Kolmogorov–Chentsov theorem provides path continuity from the moment criterion: since $E[|B_t - B_s|^{2k}] = (2k-1)!! \cdot |t-s|^k$ (moments of a Gaussian), choosing $k \ge 2$ gives a Hölder-continuous modification with exponent $\alpha < 1/2$.

Covariance Structure

Proposition 5.1.3 — Covariance of Brownian Motion

For $s, t \ge 0$: $$E[B_t] = 0, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(B_t) = t, \qquad \operatorname{Cov}(B_s, B_t) = E[B_s B_t] = \min(s,t).$$

Proof. WLOG $s \le t$. Write $B_t = B_s + (B_t - B_s)$. By independence of increments, $E[B_s(B_t - B_s)] = E[B_s] \cdot E[B_t-B_s] = 0$. Hence $$E[B_s B_t] = E[B_s^2] + E[B_s(B_t-B_s)] = s + 0 = s = \min(s,t). \quad\square$$

Thus $(B_t)$ is a Gaussian process with mean function $m(t)=0$ and covariance kernel $K(s,t)=\min(s,t)$. Conversely, any centred Gaussian process with this covariance kernel and continuous paths is a standard BM.

Path Properties

Theorem 5.1.4 — Hölder Continuity

Brownian motion paths are locally Hölder continuous of any exponent $\alpha < 1/2$: for each $T > 0$ and $\alpha < 1/2$, there exists a random $C(\omega) < \infty$ such that $$|B_t(\omega) - B_s(\omega)| \le C(\omega)\, |t-s|^\alpha \quad \text{for all } s,t \in [0,T].$$

However, Brownian paths are not Hölder-$1/2$: a.s., $\limsup_{h \to 0} |B_{t+h} - B_t|/\sqrt{h} = \infty$ for every $t$ (Lévy's modulus of continuity).

Theorem 5.1.5 — Non-Differentiability

With probability 1, Brownian motion is nowhere differentiable: for almost every $\omega$, the function $t \mapsto B_t(\omega)$ is not differentiable at any point $t \ge 0$.

Sketch. If $B$ were differentiable at $t_0$, then $|B_{t_0+h}-B_{t_0}| = O(h)$. But $B_{t_0+h} - B_{t_0} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,h)$, so $(B_{t_0+h}-B_{t_0})/h \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1/h) \to \infty$ in distribution, a contradiction. A rigorous argument uses the Borel–Cantelli lemma applied to dyadic intervals.

Definition 5.1.6 — Quadratic Variation

For a partition $\Pi_n = \{0 = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = t\}$ with mesh $|\Pi_n| \to 0$, the quadratic variation of $B$ on $[0,t]$ is $$[B]_t = \lim_{|\Pi_n| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^n (B_{t_i} - B_{t_{i-1}})^2.$$

For Brownian motion, $[B]_t = t$ almost surely (and in $L^2$). This is written $d[B]_t = dt$, or informally $(dB_t)^2 = dt$. This identity — that BM accumulates $t$ units of quadratic variation by time $t$ — is fundamental to Itô's formula.

Proof — Quadratic Variation equals $t$

Let $Q_n = \sum_{i=1}^n (B_{t_i}-B_{t_{i-1}})^2$ for a partition with $t_i = it/n$. Each term has mean $t/n$ and variance $2(t/n)^2$ (since $(B_{t_i}-B_{t_{i-1}})^2 - t/n$ are i.i.d. with mean 0). Thus $$E[Q_n] = t, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(Q_n) = n \cdot 2(t/n)^2 = \frac{2t^2}{n} \to 0.$$

Hence $Q_n \to t$ in $L^2$, and a subsequence argument gives a.s. convergence along dyadic partitions. $\square$

Scaling and Symmetry Properties

Proposition 5.1.7 — Scaling Properties

The following processes are also standard Brownian motions:

  • Scaling: For $c > 0$, $\tilde{B}_t = c^{-1/2} B_{ct}$ is a standard BM.
  • Time reversal: $\tilde{B}_t = B_{T-t} - B_T$ is a standard BM on $[0,T]$ (for fixed $T$).
  • Reflection: $\tilde{B}_t = -B_t$ is a standard BM.
  • Time inversion: $\tilde{B}_t = t B_{1/t}$ (with $\tilde{B}_0 = 0$) is a standard BM.

Each property follows by verifying the four defining conditions. Scaling expresses the self-similarity of BM: under the map $t \mapsto ct$, the process scales as $c^{1/2}$, making BM a self-similar process of index $H = 1/2$.

Example — Brownian Bridge

The Brownian bridge from 0 to 0 on $[0,1]$ is the process $X_t = B_t - t B_1$, which is a centred Gaussian process with covariance $\operatorname{Cov}(X_s, X_t) = \min(s,t) - st$ and $X_0 = X_1 = 0$ a.s.

It arises as the conditional distribution of $(B_t)_{0 \le t \le 1}$ given $B_1 = 0$. The Brownian bridge is the object that appears in the Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic for testing goodness of fit.
