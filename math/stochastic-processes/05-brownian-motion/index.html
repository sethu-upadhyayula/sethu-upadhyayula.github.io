Chapter 5: Brownian Motion

Brownian motion - also called the Wiener process - is the most fundamental continuous-time stochastic process. Originally introduced to model the erratic motion of pollen particles suspended in water (observed by botanist Robert Brown in 1827), it was placed on rigorous mathematical footing by Norbert Wiener in the 1920s.

Brownian motion occupies a central role in modern probability and its applications. It is the building block of stochastic calculus (Itô's theory), the canonical example of a continuous martingale, and the driving noise in the Black-Scholes model of option pricing. Its properties - Gaussian increments, continuous but nowhere-differentiable paths, and self-similarity - make it both tractable and rich.

Chapter Overview

5.1 - Construction and Properties

Standard Brownian motion $(B_t)_{t \ge 0}$ is characterised by $B_0 = 0$, independent and stationary Gaussian increments $B_{t+s} - B_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, s)$, and almost-surely continuous paths. We examine its existence (via the Kolmogorov extension theorem and the Lévy-Ciesielski construction), covariance structure $E[B_s B_t] = \min(s,t)$, and remarkable path properties including quadratic variation $[B]_t = t$ and a.s. non-differentiability.

5.2 - Reflection Principle

The Markov and strong Markov properties of Brownian motion underpin the reflection principle: for $a > 0$, the probability that the running maximum exceeds $a$ by time $t$ equals $2P(B_t \ge a)$. This yields the distribution of the running maximum and the law of the first passage time $T_a = \inf\{t : B_t = a\}$.

5.3 - Brownian Motion as a Limit

Donsker's invariance principle (the functional central limit theorem) states that suitably rescaled simple random walks converge in distribution to Brownian motion in the space $C[0,1]$. This makes BM the universal scaling limit of random walk models and allows one to compute limits of functionals of random walks.
