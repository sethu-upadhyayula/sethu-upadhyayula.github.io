2.1 Trees and Forests

Trees are the simplest connected graphs and appear in virtually every branch of mathematics and computer science. Their defining property — connected and acyclic — leads to many elegant equivalent characterisations.

Definition

Definition 2.1.1 — Tree and Forest

A tree is a connected acyclic graph. A forest is an acyclic graph (not necessarily connected); equivalently, a forest is a disjoint union of trees.

A leaf (or pendant vertex) is a vertex of degree 1.

Equivalent Characterisations

Theorem 2.1.2 — Six Equivalent Characterisations of Trees

Let $G$ be a graph on $n$ vertices. The following are equivalent:

  1. $G$ is connected and has $n - 1$ edges.
  2. $G$ is acyclic and has $n - 1$ edges.
  3. $G$ is connected and acyclic (i.e., $G$ is a tree by definition).
  4. There is a unique path between any two distinct vertices of $G$.
  5. $G$ is acyclic, but adding any edge between non-adjacent vertices creates exactly one cycle.
  6. $G$ is connected, but removing any edge disconnects it.
Proof Sketch

The key facts are: a connected graph on $n$ vertices has at least $n-1$ edges; an acyclic graph on $n$ vertices has at most $n-1$ edges. Together with the definitions, these give the equivalences. For (4): uniqueness of path fails iff two internally disjoint paths exist between some pair, creating a cycle. For (5): adding edge $\{u,v\}$ to an acyclic $G$ creates a cycle iff $u$ and $v$ are already connected — but then there is a unique path from $u$ to $v$ in $G$, and together with the new edge it forms the unique new cycle. $\square$

Leaves in Trees

Theorem 2.1.3 — Every Non-Trivial Tree Has at Least Two Leaves

Every tree $T$ with $n \ge 2$ vertices has at least two leaves.

Proof

Consider a longest path $P = v_0 v_1 \cdots v_k$ in $T$ (it has $k \ge 1$ since $n \ge 2$). We claim $v_0$ and $v_k$ are leaves. If $v_0$ had a neighbour $u \notin \{v_1\}$, then $u$ is not on $P$ (otherwise $T$ would contain a cycle), so $u v_0 v_1 \cdots v_k$ is a longer path — contradiction. By the same argument $v_k$ is a leaf. $\square$

Forests

Theorem 2.1.4 — Forests

A forest on $n$ vertices with $k$ connected components has exactly $n - k$ edges. In particular, a tree ($k = 1$) has $n - 1$ edges.

Rooted Trees

Definition 2.1.5 — Rooted Tree

A rooted tree is a tree with a designated vertex $r$ called the root. The unique path from $r$ to any vertex $v$ defines a natural parent-child relationship: the parent of $v \ne r$ is the vertex preceding $v$ on the path from $r$ to $v$.

The depth of $v$ is $d(r, v)$; the height of the tree is $\max_v d(r, v)$. The subtree} rooted at $v$ is the induced subgraph on all descendants of $v$ (including $v$ itself).

Rooted trees model hierarchical data (file systems, XML, decision trees) and are fundamental in algorithm design.
