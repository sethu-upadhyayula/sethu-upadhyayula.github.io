2.2 Spanning Trees

A spanning tree of a connected graph $G$ is a subgraph that is a tree and contains every vertex of $G$. Spanning trees are the backbone of many graph algorithms and admit remarkable combinatorial counting formulas.

Existence

Theorem 2.2.1 — Existence of Spanning Trees

A graph $G$ has a spanning tree if and only if $G$ is connected.

Proof

If $G$ has a spanning tree $T$, then any two vertices are connected by a path in $T \subseteq G$. Conversely, if $G$ is connected but not a tree, it contains a cycle. Removing any edge of the cycle preserves connectivity. Repeat until no cycles remain — the result is a spanning tree. $\square$

Counting: Cayley's Formula

Theorem 2.2.2 — Cayley's Formula (1889)

The number of labelled spanning trees of the complete graph $K_n$ is $$\tau(K_n) = n^{n-2}.$$

Proof via Prüfer Sequences

We construct a bijection between labelled spanning trees on $[n] = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ and sequences $(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_{n-2}) \in [n]^{n-2}$.

Encoding: Given a tree $T$, repeatedly remove the leaf with the smallest label and record its neighbour. After $n-2$ steps, two vertices remain. This gives a sequence (the Prüfer sequence) of length $n-2$.

Decoding: Given a sequence $(a_1, \ldots, a_{n-2})$, let $L$ be the set of labels not appearing in the remaining sequence. At each step, connect the smallest element of $L$ to $a_i$, remove it from $L$, update $L$. After $n-2$ steps, connect the two remaining vertices.

This process is a bijection. Since there are $n^{n-2}$ possible sequences, there are $n^{n-2}$ labelled spanning trees of $K_n$. $\square$

Example 2.2.3

$K_1$ has $1^{-1} = 1$ spanning tree (the single vertex). $K_2$ has $2^0 = 1$ spanning tree (the single edge). $K_3$ has $3^1 = 3$ spanning trees (the three paths). $K_4$ has $4^2 = 16$ spanning trees.

Matrix-Tree Theorem

Theorem 2.2.4 — Kirchhoff's Matrix-Tree Theorem (1847)

Let $G$ be a graph on $n$ vertices with Laplacian matrix $L = D - A$, where $D = \text{diag}(\deg(v_1), \ldots, \deg(v_n))$ and $A$ is the adjacency matrix. Then the number of spanning trees of $G$ is $$\tau(G) = \frac{1}{n}\lambda_1\lambda_2\cdots\lambda_{n-1},$$ where $0 = \lambda_0 \le \lambda_1 \le \cdots \le \lambda_{n-1}$ are the eigenvalues of $L$.

Equivalently, $\tau(G)$ equals any cofactor of $L$ (i.e., the determinant of any $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ submatrix obtained by deleting row $i$ and column $i$).

Fundamental Cycles and Cuts

Definition 2.2.5 — Fundamental Cycle and Cut

Let $T$ be a spanning tree of connected $G$. For each non-tree edge $e \in E(G) \setminus E(T)$, adding $e$ to $T$ creates exactly one cycle $C(T, e)$, the fundamental cycle of $e$ with respect to $T$.

For each tree edge $f \in E(T)$, removing $f$ splits $T$ into two components with vertex sets $S$ and $V \setminus S$. The fundamental cut $D(T, f)$ is the set of all edges of $G$ with one endpoint in $S$ and the other in $V \setminus S$.

Remark

The $n-1$ fundamental cycles and $n-1$ fundamental cuts form bases for the cycle space and cut space of $G$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$, respectively. These spaces are central in the study of graph connectivity and network reliability.
