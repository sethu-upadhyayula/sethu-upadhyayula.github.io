8.2 The Laplacian

The Laplacian matrix is the natural discrete analogue of the Laplace operator $-\nabla^2$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$. It encodes connectivity information and underpins random walks, electrical networks, and the matrix-tree theorem.

Definition 8.2.1 — Laplacian Matrix

The Laplacian of $G$ is $L = D - A$, where $D = \mathrm{diag}(d_1, \ldots, d_n)$ is the degree matrix and $A$ is the adjacency matrix. Explicitly, $L_{ii} = d_i$ and $L_{ij} = -1$ if $\{i,j\} \in E$, else $0$. We have $\mathbf{x}^T L \mathbf{x} = \sum_{\{i,j\}\in E}(x_i - x_j)^2 \ge 0$, so $L$ is positive semidefinite. Also, $L\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{0}$, so $0$ is always an eigenvalue with eigenvector $\mathbf{1}$.

Theorem 8.2.2 — Connectivity and the Fiedler Value

Let $0 = \mu_1 \le \mu_2 \le \cdots \le \mu_n$ be the eigenvalues of $L$. Then $G$ is connected if and only if $\mu_2 > 0$. The second smallest eigenvalue $\mu_2$ is called the algebraic connectivity or Fiedler value, and the corresponding eigenvector is the Fiedler vector, used in spectral partitioning.

Matrix-Tree Theorem

Theorem 8.2.3 — Kirchhoff's Matrix-Tree Theorem

The number of spanning trees of $G$ is $$\tau(G) = \frac{1}{n} \prod_{i=2}^{n} \mu_i,$$ where $\mu_2, \ldots, \mu_n$ are the nonzero eigenvalues of $L$. Equivalently, $\tau(G)$ equals any cofactor of $L$. For $K_n$: eigenvalues of $L(K_n)$ are $0$ (once) and $n$ ($n-1$ times), so $\tau(K_n) = n^{n-2}$ (Cayley's formula).

Random Walks and the Normalised Laplacian

Definition 8.2.4 — Random Walk and Mixing Time

The random walk matrix is $W = D^{-1}A$; a lazy walk uses $(I + W)/2$. Starting at any vertex, the walk converges to the stationary distribution $\pi_v = d_v / (2|E|)$. The mixing time (time to get close to $\pi$) is $O(\log n / \mu_2)$ for the lazy walk, so larger $\mu_2$ means faster mixing.

Definition 8.2.5 — Normalised Laplacian

The normalised Laplacian is $\mathcal{L} = D^{-1/2} L D^{-1/2} = I - D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}$. Its eigenvalues $0 = \nu_1 \le \nu_2 \le \cdots \le \nu_n$ all lie in $[0, 2]$. For $d$-regular graphs, $\mathcal{L} = (1/d)L$, so the spectra are proportional.
