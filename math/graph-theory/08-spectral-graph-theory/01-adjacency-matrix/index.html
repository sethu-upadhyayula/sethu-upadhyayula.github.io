8.1 Adjacency Matrix

The adjacency matrix is the most natural matrix representation of a graph. Its eigenvalues capture global structural properties that are difficult to read off directly from the graph.

Definition 8.1.1 — Adjacency Matrix

For a graph $G$ on $n$ vertices labelled $1, \ldots, n$, the adjacency matrix $A \in \{0,1\}^{n \times n}$ has $A_{ij} = 1$ if $\{i,j\} \in E(G)$ and $A_{ij} = 0$ otherwise. Since $G$ is undirected, $A$ is symmetric, so all eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_n$ are real.

Theorem 8.1.2 — Walks and Powers of $A$

$(A^k)_{ij}$ counts the number of walks of length $k$ from vertex $i$ to vertex $j$ in $G$. In particular, $\mathrm{tr}(A^2) = 2|E|$ and $\mathrm{tr}(A^3)/6$ equals the number of triangles.

Spectral Radius Bounds

Theorem 8.1.3 — Bounds on the Largest Eigenvalue

Let $\bar{d}$ be the average degree and $\Delta$ the maximum degree. Then: $$\bar{d} \le \lambda_1 \le \Delta.$$ The lower bound follows since $\mathbf{1}^T A \mathbf{1} = 2|E| = n\bar{d}$, giving $\lambda_1 \ge \bar{d}$ by the Rayleigh quotient. The upper bound follows since the spectral norm of $A$ is bounded by its row sums.

Also, $\lambda_1 \ge \sqrt{\Delta}$: take a vertex $v$ of degree $\Delta$ and use the subgraph induced by $v$ and its neighbours.

Theorem 8.1.4 — Bipartite Graphs and Symmetric Spectra

$G$ is bipartite if and only if its spectrum is symmetric about $0$: $\lambda_i = -\lambda_{n+1-i}$ for all $i$. Equivalently, $G$ is bipartite iff $-\lambda_1$ is also an eigenvalue.

Interlacing and Quotient Matrices

Theorem 8.1.5 — Interlacing Theorem

If $H$ is an induced subgraph of $G$ on $m$ vertices with eigenvalues $\mu_1 \ge \cdots \ge \mu_m$, then $\lambda_k \ge \mu_k \ge \lambda_{n-m+k}$ for $1 \le k \le m$ (eigenvalue interlacing). In particular, $\lambda_1(H) \le \lambda_1(G)$ and $\lambda_n(G) \le \lambda_m(H)$.

Definition 8.1.6 — Equitable Partition and Quotient Matrix

A partition $V = V_1 \cup \cdots \cup V_r$ is equitable if the number of neighbours of any $v \in V_i$ in $V_j$ depends only on $i$ and $j$ (not on $v$). The quotient matrix $B_{ij}$ records this count. Eigenvalues of $B$ are also eigenvalues of $A$ (the quotient interlacing theorem).
