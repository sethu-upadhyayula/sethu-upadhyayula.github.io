Chapter 6: Graph Coloring

Graph colouring assigns labels (colours) to vertices or edges subject to adjacency constraints. It is one of the most studied topics in combinatorics, with applications in scheduling, register allocation, frequency assignment, and map colouring.

The central question is: what is the minimum number of colours needed to colour a graph? This chapter develops the theory of chromatic number, examines the celebrated Four Colour Theorem, and studies edge colourings.

Chapter Overview

6.1 — Chromatic Number

The chromatic number $\chi(G)$ is the minimum number of colours in a proper vertex colouring. We prove greedy and Brooks' bounds, define the chromatic polynomial, and discuss perfect graphs.

6.2 — Four Color Theorem

Every planar graph is 4-colourable. We describe Appel and Haken's landmark 1976 proof via reducible configurations and discuss the history from Guthrie's 1852 conjecture to the Robertson–Sanders–Seymour–Thomas simplification of 1997.

6.3 — Edge Colorings

The chromatic index $\chi'(G)$ is the minimum number of colours for a proper edge colouring. Vizing's theorem asserts $\Delta \le \chi' \le \Delta + 1$; König's theorem gives $\chi' = \Delta$ for bipartite graphs.
