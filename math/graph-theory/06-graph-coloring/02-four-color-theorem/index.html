6.2 Four Color Theorem

The Four Colour Theorem is one of the most famous results in all of mathematics — both for its long history and for being the first major theorem whose proof relied essentially on computer computation.

Theorem 6.2.1 — Four Colour Theorem (Appel-Haken, 1976)

Every planar graph is properly 4-colourable, i.e., $\chi(G) \le 4$ for every planar graph $G$.

History

Historical Note

Francis Guthrie conjectured in 1852 that four colours suffice to colour any map so that adjacent regions have different colours. The problem resisted proof for over a century.

Appel and Haken proved the theorem in 1976 by reducing the problem to a finite case analysis of 1,936 reducible configurations, each checked by computer — the first major theorem proved with essential computer assistance.

Robertson, Sanders, Seymour, and Thomas gave a simplified proof in 1997, reducing to 633 configurations, though still computer-assisted.

Five Colour Theorem (Elementary Proof)

Theorem 6.2.2 — Five Colour Theorem

Every planar graph is properly 5-colourable.

Proof (by induction on $|V|$)

Every planar graph has a vertex $v$ with $\deg(v) \le 5$ (by $e \le 3v - 6$). Remove $v$; by induction the remaining graph is 5-coloured. If $\deg(v) \le 4$, at most 4 colours appear among $v$'s neighbours, so a fifth colour is free for $v$.

If $\deg(v) = 5$ with all 5 colours used by its 5 neighbours (in cyclic order $v_1, \ldots, v_5$ with colours $1, \ldots, 5$): consider the $1$-$3$ Kempe chain starting at $v_1$. If it does not reach $v_3$, swap colours $1$ and $3$ in its component — $v_1$ now has colour $3$, freeing colour $1$ for $v$. If it does reach $v_3$, the $2$-$4$ Kempe chain from $v_2$ cannot reach $v_4$ (blocked by the $1$-$3$ chain), so swap $2$ and $4$ in the component of $v_2$ — colour $2$ is freed for $v$. $\square$

Related Results

Theorem 6.2.3 — Grötzsch's Theorem (1959)

Every triangle-free planar graph is properly 3-colourable.

Theorem 6.2.4 — Heawood Conjecture (surfaces of genus $g > 0$)

For a surface of genus $g > 0$, any graph embeddable on that surface can be properly coloured with at most $\left\lfloor \frac{7 + \sqrt{1 + 48g}}{2} \right\rfloor$ colours. This bound is achieved by the complete graph $K_n$ for appropriate $n$ (proved by Ringel and Youngs for all $g > 0$; the torus case $g=1$ gives the bound 7).
