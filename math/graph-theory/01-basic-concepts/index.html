Chapter 1: Basic Concepts

Graphs model pairwise relationships between objects — from road networks and social connections to molecules and circuits. This chapter lays the essential groundwork: we define graphs and digraphs, study degree sequences, and introduce walks, paths, and cycles.

These foundational concepts underpin every subsequent chapter of the theory and appear throughout combinatorics, algorithms, and applied mathematics.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Graphs and Digraphs

We define graphs $G = (V, E)$ with vertices and edges, along with directed graphs (digraphs), complete graphs $K_n$, bipartite graphs $K_{m,n}$, complements, subgraphs, induced subgraphs, and graph isomorphism.

1.2 — Degree Sequences

The degree of a vertex counts its neighbours. We prove the Handshaking Lemma ($\sum \deg v = 2|E|$), discuss the Erdős–Gallai characterisation of graphical sequences, and introduce regular graphs including the Petersen graph.

1.3 — Paths and Cycles

Walks, trails, paths, and cycles capture the notion of traversal. We define distance, diameter, girth, and connectivity, and prove that a graph is bipartite if and only if it contains no odd cycle.
