1.2 Degree Sequences

The degree of a vertex measures how many edges are incident to it. Degree information encodes important structural data about a graph and leads to elegant global identities and characterisation theorems.

Degrees

Definition 1.2.1 - Degree

The degree of vertex $v$ in graph $G$ is $\deg_G(v) = |\{u : \{u,v\} \in E(G)\}|$, the number of vertices adjacent to $v$. The minimum degree is $\delta(G) = \min_{v \in V} \deg(v)$ and the maximum degree is $\Delta(G) = \max_{v \in V} \deg(v)$. The average degree is $\bar{d}(G) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v)$.

The Handshaking Lemma

Theorem 1.2.2 - Handshaking Lemma

For any graph $G = (V, E)$, $$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|.$$

Proof

Each edge $\{u, v\}$ contributes 1 to $\deg(u)$ and 1 to $\deg(v)$, so it contributes exactly 2 to the sum $\sum_{v} \deg(v)$. Summing over all edges gives $\sum_{v} \deg(v) = 2|E|$. $\square$

Corollary 1.2.3 - Even Number of Odd-Degree Vertices

In any graph, the number of vertices of odd degree is even.

Proof

Write $\sum_{v} \deg(v) = 2|E|$, which is even. The sum of degrees of even-degree vertices is even. So the sum of degrees of odd-degree vertices must also be even, which requires that there be an even number of them. $\square$

Example 1.2.4

$K_n$ is $(n-1)$-regular: every vertex has degree $n-1$. The Handshaking Lemma gives $\sum \deg = n(n-1) = 2\binom{n}{2}$, confirming $|E(K_n)| = \binom{n}{2}$.

Degree Sequences and Graphical Sequences

Definition 1.2.5 - Degree Sequence

The degree sequence of a graph $G$ with $n$ vertices is the sequence $(d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n)$ of vertex degrees listed in non-increasing order: $d_1 \ge d_2 \ge \cdots \ge d_n \ge 0$. A sequence of non-negative integers is called graphical if it is the degree sequence of some simple graph.

Theorem 1.2.6 - Erdős-Gallai Theorem (1960)

A sequence of non-negative integers $d_1 \ge d_2 \ge \cdots \ge d_n$ is graphical if and only if:

  1. $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n d_i$ is even, and
  2. for each $k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$: $$\sum_{i=1}^k d_i \;\le\; k(k-1) + \sum_{i=k+1}^n \min(d_i, k).$$

Condition (2) is the Erdős-Gallai condition; the right side counts the maximum possible contribution to the first $k$ vertices' degrees from within the group and from outside.

Regular Graphs

Definition 1.2.7 - $k$-Regular Graph

A graph $G$ is $k$-regular if $\deg(v) = k$ for every $v \in V(G)$. A 3-regular graph is also called a cubic graph. By the Handshaking Lemma, a $k$-regular graph on $n$ vertices has $kn/2$ edges, so $kn$ must be even.

Example 1.2.8 - The Petersen Graph

The Petersen graph is a famous 3-regular graph on 10 vertices and 15 edges. Its girth (length of shortest cycle) is 5, and it is vertex-transitive. It serves as a counterexample to many plausible graph-theoretic conjectures (e.g., it has no Hamiltonian cycle and is not 3-edge-colourable).

Construction: take an outer 5-cycle $v_0v_1v_2v_3v_4$, an inner pentagram $u_0u_2u_4u_1u_3$, and connect $v_i$ to $u_i$ for each $i$.
