Chapter 5: Planar Graphs

A graph is planar if it can be drawn in the plane without any edge crossings. Planarity is a fundamental structural property with deep consequences: Euler's formula relates vertices, edges, and faces, while Kuratowski's theorem gives an exact forbidden-subgraph characterisation.

Planar graphs play a central role in applications ranging from circuit design and geographic maps to graph drawing algorithms.

Chapter Overview

5.1 - Euler's Formula

We prove Euler's formula $v - e + f = 2$ for connected planar graphs and derive edge-count corollaries. These inequalities immediately show that $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$ are non-planar.

5.2 - Kuratowski's Theorem

Kuratowski's theorem (1930) characterises planar graphs completely: $G$ is planar if and only if it contains no subdivision of $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$. We also discuss Wagner's equivalent minor formulation and the Hopcroft-Tarjan linear-time planarity test.
