5.1 Euler's Formula

A graph is planar if it admits a drawing in the plane (or equivalently, on the sphere) with no edge crossings. Such a drawing divides the plane into regions called faces, including the unbounded outer face. Euler's formula is the fundamental identity relating these quantities.

Definition 5.1.1 — Planar Graph

A graph $G$ is planar if it can be drawn in the plane so that edges intersect only at their endpoints. Such a drawing is called a planar embedding or plane graph.

Definition 5.1.2 — Faces

Given a plane graph, the connected regions of $\mathbb{R}^2$ minus the drawing are called faces. Exactly one face is unbounded; it is called the outer face or infinite face.

Theorem 5.1.3 — Euler's Formula

Let $G$ be a connected plane graph with $v$ vertices, $e$ edges, and $f$ faces. Then $$v - e + f = 2.$$

Proof

By induction on $e$. Let $T$ be a spanning tree of $G$; $T$ has $v-1$ edges and, since it is a tree (acyclic and connected), it has exactly $f=1$ face (the entire plane). Thus $v - (v-1) + 1 = 2$. Base case holds.

Now suppose the formula holds for all connected plane graphs with fewer edges. If $e > v-1$ (i.e., $G$ is not a tree), then $G$ contains a cycle; pick an edge $e_0$ on a cycle. The plane graph $G - e_0$ is still connected, has $v$ vertices, $e-1$ edges, and $f-1$ faces (removing $e_0$ merges the two faces it separates). By the induction hypothesis, $v - (e-1) + (f-1) = 2$, giving $v - e + f = 2$. $\square$

Edge-Count Corollaries

Corollary 5.1.4 — Edge Bound for Planar Graphs

If $G$ is a simple planar graph with $v \ge 3$, then $e \le 3v - 6$.

Proof

Each face is bounded by at least 3 edges, and each edge borders at most 2 faces, so $3f \le 2e$. From Euler's formula $f = 2 - v + e$, substituting gives $3(2 - v + e) \le 2e$, which simplifies to $e \le 3v - 6$. $\square$

Corollary 5.1.5 — Edge Bound for Bipartite Planar Graphs

If $G$ is a simple bipartite planar graph with $v \ge 3$, then $e \le 2v - 4$. (Bipartite graphs have no triangles, so each face has at least 4 edges on its boundary.)

Non-Planarity of $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$

Example 5.1.6 — $K_5$ is Non-Planar

$K_5$ has $v = 5$ and $e = 10$. But $3v - 6 = 9 < 10$, violating Corollary 5.1.4. Hence $K_5$ is non-planar.

Similarly, $K_{3,3}$ has $v = 6$ and $e = 9$. Since $K_{3,3}$ is bipartite, Corollary 5.1.5 gives $2v - 4 = 8 < 9$, so $K_{3,3}$ is non-planar.

Remark

Every planar graph has a vertex of degree at most 5. (If all degrees were $\ge 6$ then $2e \ge 6v$, giving $e \ge 3v > 3v - 6$, a contradiction.) This fact drives the proof of the five-colour theorem.
