4.2 Continuity

Continuity is the natural condition that a function's limit at a point agrees with its value there. It formalises the intuition of a function with "no jumps, no holes, and no blowups."

Definition 1 - Continuity at a Point

$f$ is continuous at $c$ if all three conditions hold:

  1. $f(c)$ is defined.
  2. $\lim_{x\to c}f(x)$ exists.
  3. $\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=f(c)$.

Equivalently (combining all three): $\forall\,\varepsilon>0,\,\exists\,\delta>0: |x-c|<\delta\Rightarrow|f(x)-f(c)|<\varepsilon$. Note that unlike the limit definition, here we allow $x=c$.

Definition 2 - Continuity on an Interval

$f$ is continuous on an open interval $(a,b)$ if it is continuous at every $c\in(a,b)$. For a closed interval $[a,b]$: continuous on $(a,b)$, right-continuous at $a$ (i.e., $\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=f(a)$), and left-continuous at $b$.

Algebraic Stability

Theorem 1 - Algebra of Continuous Functions

If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $c$, so are:

  • $f+g$ and $f-g$,
  • $fg$,
  • $f/g$ (provided $g(c)\ne 0$),
  • $|f|$.

Moreover, if $g$ is continuous at $c$ and $f$ is continuous at $g(c)$, then $f\circ g$ is continuous at $c$.

Standard Continuous Functions

Example - Continuous Functions
  • Every polynomial $p(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_0$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ (constant and identity functions are continuous; apply algebra repeatedly).
  • Every rational function $p(x)/q(x)$ is continuous wherever $q(x)\ne 0$.
  • $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ are continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ (follows from $|\sin x|\le|x|$ and the addition formula).
  • $e^x$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$; $\ln x$ is continuous on $(0,\infty)$.
  • $\sqrt{x}$ is continuous on $[0,\infty)$; more generally $x^{1/n}$ for integer $n\ge 1$.

By the composition rule, $\sin(x^2)$, $e^{\cos x}$, $\ln(1+x^2)$ are all continuous on their natural domains.

Types of Discontinuity

Definition 3 - Classification of Discontinuities

Suppose $f$ is not continuous at $c$. We classify:

  • Removable discontinuity: $\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=L$ exists but either $f(c)$ is undefined or $f(c)\ne L$. Example: $f(x)=\frac{\sin x}{x}$ at $x=0$ (limit is 1, but $f(0)$ is undefined).
  • Jump discontinuity: both one-sided limits exist but are unequal. Example: $f(x)=\mathrm{sgn}(x)$ at $x=0$ (right limit $=1$, left limit $=-1$).
  • Essential (oscillatory/infinite) discontinuity: at least one one-sided limit does not exist (as a finite number). Examples: $\sin(1/x)$ at $0$ (oscillatory); $1/x$ at $0$ (infinite).

Removable discontinuities can be "repaired" by redefining $f(c)=L$. Jump and essential discontinuities cannot.
