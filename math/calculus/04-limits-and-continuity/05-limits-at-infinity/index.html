4.5 Limits at Infinity

We extend the limit concept to allow the input variable to grow without bound, and also to allow the output to grow without bound. These ideas give precise meaning to horizontal and vertical asymptotes.

Definition 1 - Limit at Positive Infinity

$\lim_{x\to+\infty}f(x)=L$ means: $$\forall\,\varepsilon>0,\;\exists\,M>0:\;x>M\;\Rightarrow\;|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon.$$

Similarly, $\lim_{x\to-\infty}f(x)=L$ uses $x<-M$. If both equal $L$, then $y=L$ is a horizontal asymptote.

Definition 2 - Infinite Limit

$\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=+\infty$ means: $\forall\,K>0,\;\exists\,\delta>0: 0<|x-c|<\delta\Rightarrow f(x)>K$.

If $\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)=\pm\infty$ or $\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)=\pm\infty$, the line $x=a$ is a vertical asymptote.

Example 2 - $\lim_{x\to+\infty}\dfrac{3x^2+1}{x^2-2}=3$

Divide numerator and denominator by $x^2$ (valid for large $x$): $$\frac{3x^2+1}{x^2-2}=\frac{3+1/x^2}{1-2/x^2}\to\frac{3+0}{1-0}=3.$$

Horizontal asymptote: $y=3$. Vertical asymptotes at $x=\pm\sqrt{2}$ (where the denominator is zero).

Theorem 1 - The Number $e$

$$\lim_{x\to+\infty}\left(1+\frac{1}{x}\right)^x = e \approx 2.71828\ldots$$ This is sometimes taken as the definition of $e$. The proof (without L'Hôpital) uses the squeeze theorem applied to the sequence $(1+1/n)^n$, which is monotone increasing and bounded above, hence convergent to a limit we call $e$. The extension from integer $n$ to real $x$ uses monotonicity arguments. We accept this limit for now; a complete proof appears in Chapter 8.

Oblique Asymptotes

Definition 3 - Oblique Asymptote

The line $y=mx+b$ is an oblique (slant) asymptote of $f$ as $x\to+\infty$ if: $$\lim_{x\to+\infty}[f(x)-(mx+b)]=0.$$

To find it: $m=\lim_{x\to\infty}f(x)/x$ and $b=\lim_{x\to\infty}[f(x)-mx]$. Oblique asymptotes occur when $f$ grows linearly but not faster.

Example 3 - $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2+1}{x}$

$f(x)=x+1/x$. As $x\to+\infty$, $1/x\to 0$, so $f(x)-(x+0)\to 0$. The oblique asymptote is $y=x$.

Note also the vertical asymptote $x=0$ (since $1/x\to\pm\infty$ as $x\to 0^\pm$).
