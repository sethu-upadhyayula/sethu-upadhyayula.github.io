4.3 Sequential Characterization

The Heine sequential characterization links the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition of continuity to the sequence convergence theory developed in earlier chapters. It is a powerful bridge: it lets us use all we know about sequences to study functions, and provides one of the most convenient ways to prove discontinuity.

Theorem 1 — Sequential Characterization of Continuity (Heine)

$f$ is continuous at $c$ if and only if: for every sequence $(x_n)$ with $x_n\to c$, we have $f(x_n)\to f(c)$.

In symbols: $f$ is continuous at $c$ $\iff$ $\bigl(x_n\to c \Rightarrow f(x_n)\to f(c)\bigr)$.

Proof

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $f$ is continuous at $c$, and let $x_n\to c$. Given $\varepsilon>0$, by continuity there exists $\delta>0$ such that $|x-c|<\delta\Rightarrow|f(x)-f(c)|<\varepsilon$. Since $x_n\to c$, there exists $N$ such that $n>N\Rightarrow|x_n-c|<\delta$. Then for $n>N$, $|f(x_n)-f(c)|<\varepsilon$. Hence $f(x_n)\to f(c)$.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $f$ is NOT continuous at $c$. Then there exists $\varepsilon_0>0$ such that for every $\delta>0$, there exists $x$ with $|x-c|<\delta$ but $|f(x)-f(c)|\ge\varepsilon_0$. Take $\delta=1/n$ for each $n\ge 1$: get $x_n$ with $|x_n-c|<1/n$ and $|f(x_n)-f(c)|\ge\varepsilon_0$. Then $x_n\to c$ but $f(x_n)\not\to f(c)$, giving a counterexample to the sequential condition.

$\square$

Using the Sequential Method to Prove Discontinuity

Example — $f(x)=\sin(1/x)$ is discontinuous at $0$

Define $f(0)=0$ (or any value; it does not matter). Consider: $$x_n = \frac{1}{n\pi}\to 0,\quad f(x_n)=\sin(n\pi)=0\to 0.$$ $$x_n' = \frac{1}{\pi/2+2n\pi}\to 0,\quad f(x_n')=\sin\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+2n\pi\right)=1\to 1.$$

Both sequences tend to $0$, but $f(x_n)\to 0$ and $f(x_n')\to 1$. By Heine's theorem (contrapositive), $f$ is discontinuous at $0$ regardless of the value assigned to $f(0)$.

This rigorously confirms what Section 4.1 observed about non-existence of the limit.

Example — Dirichlet Function

Define $D(x)=1$ if $x\in\mathbb{Q}$ and $D(x)=0$ if $x\notin\mathbb{Q}$. For any $c\in\mathbb{R}$:

  • By density of rationals, there is a sequence of rationals $q_n\to c$ with $D(q_n)=1$.
  • By density of irrationals, there is a sequence of irrationals $i_n\to c$ with $D(i_n)=0$.

These give contradictory sequential limits, so $D$ is discontinuous at every point of $\mathbb{R}$.

Corollary — Continuity of Composition

If $g$ is continuous at $c$ and $f$ is continuous at $g(c)$, then $f\circ g$ is continuous at $c$. Proof: if $x_n\to c$, then $g(x_n)\to g(c)$ (by continuity of $g$), so $f(g(x_n))\to f(g(c))$ (by continuity of $f$ at $g(c)$). Apply Heine's theorem. $\square$
