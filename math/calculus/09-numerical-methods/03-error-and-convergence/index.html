9.3 Error and Convergence

Understanding how quickly a root-finding method converges - and when it might fail - is essential for reliable numerical computation. This section formalises the concepts of error, order of convergence, and stopping criteria, and compares the main methods studied in this chapter.

Error and Order of Convergence

Definition - Absolute Error and Order of Convergence

Let $(x_n)$ be a sequence converging to $x^*$. The absolute error at step $n$ is: $$e_n = |x_n - x^*|.$$ The sequence converges with order $p$ if there exists a constant $C > 0$ such that: $$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{|e_{n+1}|}{|e_n|^p} = C.$$ The constant $C$ is called the asymptotic error constant.

Remark - Interpreting Orders
  • $p = 1$ (linear): Each step reduces the error by a constant factor $C < 1$. Slow but robust. Example: bisection ($C = 1/2$), basic fixed point iteration.
  • $p \approx 1.618$ (superlinear): Faster than linear. Example: the secant method, whose order is the golden ratio $\phi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$.
  • $p = 2$ (quadratic): The error is squared each step - the number of correct digits doubles. Example: Newton's method at a simple root.

Stopping Criteria

In practice we cannot compute $e_n = |x_n - x^*|$ directly since $x^*$ is unknown. Instead, we use one or more of the following stopping criteria:

Definition - Stopping Criteria
  1. Absolute step size: Stop when $|x_{n+1} - x_n| < \text{tol}$. Measures how much the iterate changed.
  2. Relative step size: Stop when $\dfrac{|x_{n+1} - x_n|}{|x_{n+1}|} < \text{tol}$ (avoids issues with scale).
  3. Residual (function value): Stop when $|f(x_n)| < \text{tol}$. Directly checks how close to zero $f$ is at the current estimate.
Remark - Which Criterion to Use

No single criterion is universally best. If $f'(x^*)$ is very large, the residual can be small even though $x_n$ is far from $x^*$. If $f'(x^*)$ is very small (near a multiple root), $x_n$ can be close to $x^*$ while $|f(x_n)|$ is still appreciable. In practice, combining the absolute step size criterion with the residual criterion gives a robust stopping rule.

Bisection Method

Theorem - Bisection Convergence

Suppose $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ with $f(a)f(b) < 0$ (so a root $x^*$ exists by IVT). The bisection method bisects $[a,b]$ at each step, retaining the half containing a sign change. After $n$ steps: $$e_n \le \frac{b-a}{2^n}.$$ This is linear convergence with $C = 1/2$. To achieve $n_0$ correct decimal digits one needs approximately $n_0 \log_2(10) \approx 3.32\,n_0$ steps.

Bisection always converges (given a sign change) but is slow compared to Newton's method.

Example - Bisection Rate

Starting on an interval of length $1$, after 10 bisection steps the error is at most $1/2^{10} = 1/1024 \approx 9.77 \times 10^{-4}$, giving roughly 3 correct decimal digits. After 20 steps: $\approx 10^{-6}$ (6 digits). Newton's method starting from the same point might reach 12 digits in 4 steps.

Failure Modes of Newton's Method

Newton's Method Failure Cases
  1. Multiple root ($f'(x^*) = 0$): Quadratic convergence degrades to linear. The formula $x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ still works, but the asymptotic error constant $C = 1 - 1/m$ where $m$ is the multiplicity of the root.
  2. Starting far from the root: The tangent at $x_0$ may intersect the $x$-axis at a point far from $x^*$, or outside the domain. The method may diverge or converge to a different root.
  3. Oscillation / cycles: For some functions (e.g., $f(x) = x^3 - 2x$, starting at $x_0 = 1$) the iterates can cycle between two or more values without converging.
  4. $f'(x_n) = 0$: The method is undefined; the tangent is horizontal and has no $x$-intercept.

Comparison of Root-Finding Methods

Summary Table
Method Order $p$ Pros Cons
Bisection $1$ ($C = 1/2$) Always converges; robust Slow; requires sign change bracket
Fixed Point Iteration $1$ (when $k < 1$) Simple; flexible rearrangement Requires contraction; convergence can be slow
Secant Method $\approx 1.618$ No derivative needed Needs two initial points; may diverge
Newton's Method $2$ (simple root) Very fast near root Needs $f'$; local convergence only; fails at multiple roots

In practice, Newton's method or a hybrid (e.g., bisection to localise, then Newton to refine) is preferred for smooth functions.
