Chapter 11: Applications of Integration

The Riemann integral and FTC unlock a wide range of geometric and physical computations: areas, lengths, volumes, surface areas, work, and average values. The unifying idea is to express a quantity as a limit of Riemann sums — an integral.

Area Between Curves

Area Between Two Curves

The area of the region between $y = f(x)$ and $y = g(x)$ over $[a,b]$ is: $$A = \int_a^b |f(x) - g(x)|\,dx.$$ If $f(x) \ge g(x)$ throughout, this simplifies to $\int_a^b [f(x)-g(x)]\,dx$.

Example — Area Between Parabola and Line

Find the area enclosed by $y = x^2$ and $y = x$. They intersect where $x^2 = x$, i.e., $x = 0$ or $x = 1$. On $[0,1]$, $x \ge x^2$, so: $$A = \int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}.$$

Arc Length

Arc Length Formula

The length of the curve $y = f(x)$ from $x = a$ to $x = b$ (with $f'$ continuous) is: $$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}\,dx.$$

Derivation: A small arc element has $\Delta s \approx \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2} = \sqrt{1 + (f')^2}\,\Delta x$. Summing and taking the limit gives the integral.

Example — Arc Length of $y = x^{3/2}$

$f'(x) = \tfrac{3}{2}x^{1/2}$, so $1 + (f')^2 = 1 + \tfrac{9}{4}x$. Thus $L = \int_0^1\sqrt{1+\tfrac{9}{4}x}\,dx$. Let $u = 1 + \tfrac{9}{4}x$: $du = \tfrac{9}{4}\,dx$. Then $L = \tfrac{4}{9}\!\cdot\!\tfrac{2}{3}[u^{3/2}]_{u=1}^{u=13/4} = \tfrac{8}{27}\!\left[\!\left(\tfrac{13}{4}\right)^{3/2} - 1\right]$.

Volumes of Revolution

Disk Method

The volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region under $y = f(x) \ge 0$ over $[a,b]$ around the $x$-axis is: $$V = \pi\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx.$$ Each cross-section is a disk of radius $f(x)$, so $dV = \pi[f(x)]^2\,dx$.

Shell Method

The volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region under $y = f(x) \ge 0$ over $[a,b]$ around the $y$-axis is: $$V = 2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx.$$ Each shell has radius $x$, height $f(x)$, and thickness $dx$, so $dV = 2\pi x\,f(x)\,dx$.

Example — Volume of a Sphere

Rotate $y = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$ around the $x$-axis. By the disk method: $$V = \pi\int_{-r}^r (r^2 - x^2)\,dx = \pi\left[r^2 x - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-r}^r = \pi\cdot\frac{4r^3}{3} = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3.$$

Surface Area of Revolution

Surface Area Formula

The surface area generated by rotating $y = f(x) \ge 0$ over $[a,b]$ around the $x$-axis is: $$S = 2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}\,dx.$$

Average Value and MVT for Integrals

Average Value of a Function

The average value of $f$ on $[a,b]$ is: $$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx.$$

Theorem — Mean Value Theorem for Integrals

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then there exists $c \in [a,b]$ such that $f(c) = f_{\text{avg}}$, i.e.: $$f(c) = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx.$$ Proof: $f_{\text{avg}}$ lies between $\min f$ and $\max f$; by IVT, $f$ attains this value.

Work

Work Done by a Variable Force

If a particle moves along the $x$-axis from $x = a$ to $x = b$ under a force $F(x)$, the total work done is: $$W = \int_a^b F(x)\,dx.$$ Example: Hooke's Law $F(x) = kx$ gives $W = \int_0^d kx\,dx = \tfrac{1}{2}kd^2$ to stretch a spring by $d$.
