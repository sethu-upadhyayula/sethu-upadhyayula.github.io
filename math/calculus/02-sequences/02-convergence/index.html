2.2 Convergence

The central question in the study of sequences is: does a sequence approach a fixed value as $n$ grows large? We now make this precise using the $\varepsilon$-$N$ definition, which is the rigorous formulation of the intuitive idea that a sequence "gets close to $L$ and stays there."

Definition 2.2.1 - Convergence of a Sequence

A sequence $(a_n)$ converges to the limit $L \in \mathbb{R}$, written $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$ or $a_n \to L$, if: $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N},\ \forall n \ge N :\ |a_n - L| < \varepsilon.$$

In words: for every tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$, all but finitely many terms of the sequence lie within distance $\varepsilon$ of $L$.

Theorem 2.2.2 - $\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0$

The sequence $a_n = 1/n$ converges to $0$.

Proof

Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. We need to find $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for all $n \ge N$, $|1/n - 0| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $1/n < \varepsilon$, i.e., $n > 1/\varepsilon$.

By the Archimedean property, there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\varepsilon$. Then for all $n \ge N$: $$\left|\frac{1}{n} - 0\right| = \frac{1}{n} \le \frac{1}{N} < \varepsilon.$$

Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $\lim_{n\to\infty} 1/n = 0$. $\square$

Theorem 2.2.3 - $\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{2n+1}{n+1} = 2$

The sequence $a_n = (2n+1)/(n+1)$ converges to $2$.

Proof

We compute: $$\left|\frac{2n+1}{n+1} - 2\right| = \left|\frac{2n+1 - 2(n+1)}{n+1}\right| = \left|\frac{-1}{n+1}\right| = \frac{1}{n+1}.$$

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, we need $1/(n+1) < \varepsilon$, i.e., $n > 1/\varepsilon - 1$. Choose $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\varepsilon$. Then for $n \ge N$: $$\left|\frac{2n+1}{n+1} - 2\right| = \frac{1}{n+1} \le \frac{1}{N+1} < \frac{1}{N} < \varepsilon. \quad \square$$

Uniqueness of Limits

Theorem 2.2.4 - Uniqueness of Limits

If $a_n \to L$ and $a_n \to M$, then $L = M$.

Proof

Suppose $a_n \to L$ and $a_n \to M$. Let $\varepsilon > 0$. There exist $N_1, N_2 \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon/2$ for $n \ge N_1$ and $|a_n - M| < \varepsilon/2$ for $n \ge N_2$.

For $n \ge N = \max(N_1, N_2)$, by the triangle inequality: $$|L - M| \le |L - a_n| + |a_n - M| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon.$$

Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary and $|L - M| \ge 0$, we conclude $|L - M| = 0$, i.e., $L = M$. $\square$

Divergent Sequences and Infinite Limits

Definition 2.2.5 - Divergence

A sequence $(a_n)$ is divergent if it does not converge to any real number $L$.

We say $a_n \to +\infty$ if for every $M > 0$ there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $a_n > M$ for all $n \ge N$. Similarly, $a_n \to -\infty$ if for every $M < 0$ there exists $N$ such that $a_n < M$ for all $n \ge N$. Note that these are not convergence statements - the limits $\pm\infty$ are not real numbers.

Example - Divergence of $(-1)^n$

The sequence $a_n = (-1)^n$ diverges. To see this, suppose for contradiction that $a_n \to L$. Taking $\varepsilon = 1/2$, there exists $N$ such that $|a_n - L| < 1/2$ for all $n \ge N$. In particular, both $|a_N - L| < 1/2$ and $|a_{N+1} - L| < 1/2$. But $a_N$ and $a_{N+1}$ are $1$ and $-1$ in some order, so: $$2 = |a_N - a_{N+1}| \le |a_N - L| + |L - a_{N+1}| < \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1,$$

a contradiction. Therefore $(-1)^n$ diverges.

Remark

The $\varepsilon$-$N$ definition requires that $N$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on which term we examine. This is what makes the definition capture uniform eventual closeness. A common beginner error is choosing $N$ that depends on some other variable - always check that your $N$ depends only on $\varepsilon$.
