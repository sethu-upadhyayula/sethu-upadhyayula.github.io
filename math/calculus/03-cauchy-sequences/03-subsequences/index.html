3.3 Subsequences

A subsequence is formed by selecting an infinite, ordered subset of terms from a sequence. Subsequences allow us to zoom in on particular behavior - for example, the even-indexed terms or a rapidly growing selection - and are central to characterising convergence via cluster points.

Definition 3.3.1 - Subsequence

A subsequence of $(a_n)$ is a sequence $(a_{n_k})_{k=1}^\infty$ where $(n_k)$ is a strictly increasing sequence of natural numbers: $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$.

The indices $n_k$ must be strictly increasing so that we always move forward through the original sequence. Note that $n_k \ge k$ for all $k$ (since the $n_k$ are distinct positive integers in increasing order).

Theorem 3.3.2 - Subsequences of Convergent Sequences

If $a_n \to L$ then every subsequence $(a_{n_k})$ also converges to $L$.

Proof

Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $a_n \to L$, $\exists N$ such that $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ for all $n \ge N$. Since $n_k \ge k$, for $k \ge N$ we have $n_k \ge k \ge N$, so $|a_{n_k} - L| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

Example - Divergence via Subsequences

Consider $a_n = (-1)^n$. The even-indexed subsequence $(a_{2k}) = (1, 1, 1, \ldots)$ converges to $1$. The odd-indexed subsequence $(a_{2k-1}) = (-1, -1, -1, \ldots)$ converges to $-1$.

By the contrapositive of Theorem 3.3.2: if two subsequences have different limits, the original sequence diverges. Since $1 \ne -1$, $(-1)^n$ diverges - a clean alternative proof to the one in Section 2.2.

Limsup and Liminf

Definition 3.3.3 - Limsup and Liminf

For a bounded sequence $(a_n)$, define: $$\limsup_{n\to\infty} a_n = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left(\sup_{k \ge n} a_k\right), \qquad \liminf_{n\to\infty} a_n = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left(\inf_{k \ge n} a_k\right).$$

The sequences $s_n = \sup_{k \ge n} a_k$ and $i_n = \inf_{k \ge n} a_k$ are monotone and bounded (so they converge by MCT). We always have $\liminf a_n \le \limsup a_n$.

Theorem 3.3.4 - Convergence via Limsup and Liminf

A bounded sequence $(a_n)$ converges if and only if $\limsup a_n = \liminf a_n$, in which case the common value is the limit.

Example - Limsup and Liminf of $(-1)^n$

For $a_n = (-1)^n$: $\sup_{k \ge n} a_k = 1$ for all $n$ (since even indices give $a_k = 1$), and $\inf_{k \ge n} a_k = -1$ for all $n$.

Therefore $\limsup a_n = 1$ and $\liminf a_n = -1$. Since $1 \ne -1$, the sequence diverges - consistent with what we know.

Remark - Subsequential Limits

The $\limsup$ is the largest subsequential limit of $(a_n)$ (the largest value that some subsequence converges to), and the $\liminf$ is the smallest. For $a_n = \sin(\pi n / 2)$, the subsequential limits include $0, 1, -1$, with $\limsup = 1$ and $\liminf = -1$. The Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem guarantees that any bounded sequence has at least one subsequential limit.
