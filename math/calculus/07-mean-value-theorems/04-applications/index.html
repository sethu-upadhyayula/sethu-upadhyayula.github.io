7.4 Applications of the MVT

The Mean Value Theorem is not merely an existence statement — it is a powerful tool for deriving quantitative inequalities, establishing analytic bounds, and proving Taylor's theorem, which is arguably the most useful approximation result in analysis.

1. Proving Inequalities

Example 1 — Sine Inequality

Prove: $|\sin x - \sin y| \le |x-y|$ for all $x,y\in\mathbb{R}$.

Apply the MVT to $f(t)=\sin t$ on the interval between $x$ and $y$. There exists $c$ between $x$ and $y$ with $$\sin x - \sin y = \cos(c)(x-y).$$ Since $|\cos c|\le 1$, taking absolute values gives $|\sin x-\sin y|\le|x-y|$.

This shows $\sin$ is Lipschitz with constant 1.

Example 2 — Exponential Inequality

Prove: $e^x \ge 1+x$ for all $x\in\mathbb{R}$.

Case $x\ge0$: Apply MVT to $f(t)=e^t$ on $[0,x]$: $\exists c\in(0,x)$ with $$e^x - e^0 = e^c(x-0) = e^c x.$$ Since $c>0$, $e^c>1$, so $e^x-1=e^c x\ge x$, giving $e^x\ge 1+x$.

Case $x<0$: Apply MVT to $f(t)=e^t$ on $[x,0]$: $\exists c\in(x,0)$ with $$1-e^x=e^c(-x).$$ Since $c<0$, $e^c<10$), so $1-e^x=-e^c x < -x$, giving $e^x>1+x$.

In both cases, $e^x\ge1+x$, with equality only at $x=0$.

2. Taylor's Theorem

The MVT says $f(x)\approx f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)$ (linear approximation). Taylor's theorem extends this to a polynomial approximation of arbitrary degree.

Theorem 1 — Taylor's Theorem with Lagrange Remainder

Suppose $f$ is $n+1$ times differentiable on an open interval containing $a$ and $x$. Then $$f(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k + R_n(x),$$ where the Lagrange remainder is $$R_n(x)=\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}$$ for some $c$ strictly between $a$ and $x$.

The sum $T_n(x)=\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k$ is the $n$-th degree Taylor polynomial of $f$ at $a$.

Proof Sketch

Define $F(t)=f(x)-\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{f^{(k)}(t)}{k!}(x-t)^k$ and $G(t)=(x-t)^{n+1}$. Both satisfy $F(x)=G(x)=0$. Applying the Cauchy MVT (Section 8.1) to $F$ and $G$ on $[a,x]$ repeatedly, or applying Rolle's theorem $n+1$ times to a suitably constructed auxiliary function, yields the existence of $c\in(a,x)$ with $F'(c)/G'(c)$ equal to the desired expression. Unwinding gives the Lagrange remainder formula.

$\square$

Example 3 — Taylor Polynomial for $e^x$ at 0

Since $f(x)=e^x$ satisfies $f^{(k)}(0)=1$ for all $k$, the Taylor polynomial of degree $n$ at $a=0$ is $$T_n(x)=1+x+\frac{x^2}{2!}+\cdots+\frac{x^n}{n!},$$ and the Lagrange remainder is $R_n(x)=\frac{e^c}{(n+1)!}x^{n+1}$ for some $c$ between 0 and $x$.

For $x=1$: $|R_n(1)|\le\frac{e}{(n+1)!}\to0$ as $n\to\infty$, confirming that $e=\sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{1}{k!}$.

This gives a rigorous method to compute $e$ to any desired accuracy.

3. Second Derivative Test

Theorem 2 — Second Derivative Test

Suppose $f$ is twice differentiable near $c$ and $f'(c)=0$.

  • If $f''(c)>0$, then $f$ has a local minimum at $c$.
  • If $f''(c)<0$, then $f$ has a local maximum at $c$.
  • If $f''(c)=0$, the test is inconclusive.
Proof (minimum case)

Apply the degree-1 Taylor expansion at $c$: for $x$ near $c$, $$f(x)=f(c)+f'(c)(x-c)+\frac{f''(d)}{2}(x-c)^2=f(c)+\frac{f''(d)}{2}(x-c)^2,$$ where $d$ is between $c$ and $x$. Since $f''$ is continuous and $f''(c)>0$, for sufficiently small $|x-c|$ we have $f''(d)>0$, so $f(x)\ge f(c)$. Hence $c$ is a local minimum.

$\square$

Example 4 — Classifying Critical Points

Classify the critical points of $f(x)=x^4-4x^3$.

$f'(x)=4x^3-12x^2=4x^2(x-3)$. Critical points: $x=0$ and $x=3$.

$f''(x)=12x^2-24x$. At $x=3$: $f''(3)=108-72=36>0$, so $x=3$ is a local minimum with $f(3)=81-108=-27$. At $x=0$: $f''(0)=0$, so the test is inconclusive. Checking the sign of $f'$: $f'(x)=4x^2(x-3)<0$ for $x\in(-\infty,0)\cup(0,3)$ and $>0$ for $x>3$, so $f$ is decreasing through $x=0$. Hence $x=0$ is an inflection point, not a local extremum.

The only local (and global on bounded intervals) minimum is at $x=3$.
