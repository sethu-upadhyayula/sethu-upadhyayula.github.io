7.2 Rolle's Theorem

Rolle's theorem is the stepping stone to the Mean Value Theorem. It handles the special case where a differentiable function takes equal values at the two endpoints of an interval, and concludes that the derivative must vanish somewhere in between.

Theorem 1 — Rolle's Theorem

Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfy:

  1. $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$,
  2. $f$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$,
  3. $f(a)=f(b)$.

Then there exists $c\in(a,b)$ such that $f'(c)=0$.

Proof

Since $f$ is continuous on the closed bounded interval $[a,b]$, the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that $f$ attains its maximum value $M$ and its minimum value $m$ on $[a,b]$.

Case 1: $M=m$. Then $f$ is constant on $[a,b]$, so $f'(x)=0$ for every $x\in(a,b)$. Any $c\in(a,b)$ works.

Case 2: $M>m$. Since $f(a)=f(b)$, both $a$ and $b$ deliver the same value; therefore at least one of the extremes $M$ or $m$ is attained at some interior point $c\in(a,b)$. At such a point $f$ has a local extremum, and since $f$ is differentiable at $c$, Fermat's theorem (Section 7.1) gives $f'(c)=0$.

$\square$

Geometric Interpretation

Remark — Tangent Line is Horizontal

The condition $f(a)=f(b)$ means the two endpoints lie at the same height. Rolle's theorem asserts that the graph must have a horizontal tangent line at some intermediate point $c$. Intuitively, if the curve rises and falls (or stays flat) between two equally high endpoints, there must be a peak or a trough in between where the slope is momentarily zero.

When Hypotheses Fail

Each of the three hypotheses is necessary. Removing any one of them can cause the conclusion to fail.

Example 1 — Differentiability Fails at One Interior Point

Let $f(x)=|x|$ on $[-1,1]$. Then $f(-1)=f(1)=1$, $f$ is continuous everywhere, but $f$ is not differentiable at $x=0$. Indeed $f'(x)=-1$ for $x<0$ and $f'(x)=1$ for $x>0$; the derivative is never zero on $(-1,1)$. The conclusion of Rolle's theorem fails.

This shows that differentiability on the open interval is essential — continuity alone is not enough.

Example 2 — Endpoints are Not Equal

Let $f(x)=x$ on $[0,1]$. Then $f(0)=0\ne1=f(1)$, and $f'(x)=1\ne0$ for all $x$. The conclusion fails because hypothesis (3) is violated.

Example 3 — Applying the Corollary

Show that $p(x)=x^3+3x-5$ has exactly one real root.

Existence: $p(0)=-5<0$ and $p(2)=8+6-5=9>0$. By the Intermediate Value Theorem, there is a root in $(0,2)$.

Uniqueness: $p'(x)=3x^2+3>0$ for all $x\in\mathbb{R}$, so $p$ is strictly increasing and can cross zero at most once. By the corollary, there is at most one zero.

Combining both, $p$ has exactly one real root.
