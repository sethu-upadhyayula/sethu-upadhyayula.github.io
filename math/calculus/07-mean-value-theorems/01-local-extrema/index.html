7.1 Local Extrema

Before proving Rolle's theorem and the Mean Value Theorem, we need to understand what happens at the points where a differentiable function achieves its maximum or minimum value locally. Fermat's theorem provides the key link between local extrema and critical points.

Definition 1 — Local Maximum and Minimum

Let $f: D \to \mathbb{R}$. We say $f$ has a local maximum at $c \in D$ if there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$f(x) \leq f(c) \quad \text{for all } x \in D \text{ with } |x-c| < \delta.$$ Similarly, $f$ has a local minimum at $c$ if $f(x) \geq f(c)$ for all such $x$.

A local extremum is either a local maximum or a local minimum.

Theorem 1 — Fermat's Theorem

If $f$ has a local extremum at $c$ and $f$ is differentiable at $c$, then $f'(c) = 0$.

Proof

Suppose $f$ has a local maximum at $c$ (the minimum case is analogous). Then for small $h > 0$: $$\frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h} \leq 0 \quad \text{(since } f(c+h) \leq f(c)\text{)}.$$ Taking $h \to 0^+$: $f'_+(c) \leq 0$.

For small $h < 0$: $$\frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h} \geq 0 \quad \text{(negative over negative)}.$$ Taking $h \to 0^-$: $f'_-(c) \geq 0$.

Since $f$ is differentiable at $c$, $f'_+(c) = f'_-(c) = f'(c)$. The inequalities $f'(c) \leq 0$ and $f'(c) \geq 0$ force $f'(c) = 0$.

$\square$

Critical Points

Definition 2 — Critical Point

A point $c$ in the domain of $f$ is a critical point if either $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist. Fermat's theorem says every interior local extremum of a differentiable function is a critical point.

Remark — Converse is False

$f'(c) = 0$ does not guarantee a local extremum. The standard example is $f(x) = x^3$ at $c = 0$: $f'(0) = 0$, but $f$ is strictly increasing through $0$, so $0$ is neither a local max nor a local min — it is an inflection point.

Thus the procedure for finding extrema is: find all critical points (candidates), then use additional criteria (sign change of $f'$, or the second derivative test) to classify them.

Example

Find the local extrema of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x^2-1) = 3(x-1)(x+1)$. Critical points: $x = \pm 1$.

  • For $x < -1$: $f'(x) > 0$ (increasing). For $-1 < x < 1$: $f'(x) < 0$ (decreasing). So $x = -1$ is a local maximum with $f(-1) = 2$.
  • For $x > 1$: $f'(x) > 0$ (increasing). So $x = 1$ is a local minimum with $f(1) = -2$.

Alternatively, $f''(x) = 6x$: $f''(-1) = -6 < 0$ confirms local max; $f''(1) = 6 > 0$ confirms local min.
