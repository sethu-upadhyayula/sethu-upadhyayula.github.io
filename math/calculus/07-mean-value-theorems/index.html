Chapter 7: Mean Value Theorems

The Mean Value Theorems are among the most powerful and frequently used results in differential calculus. They connect the local behaviour of a function (its derivative at a point) to its global behaviour (its average rate of change over an interval). The chain of results - Fermat's theorem on extrema, Rolle's theorem, and the Mean Value Theorem itself - builds naturally and culminates in applications including Taylor's theorem.

Chapter Overview

7.1 - Local Extrema

We define local maxima and minima and prove Fermat's theorem: a local extremum at an interior differentiable point forces the derivative to vanish there. This identifies critical points as the candidates for extrema.

7.2 - Rolle's Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $f(a) = f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a,b)$ with $f'(c)=0$. The proof uses the Extreme Value Theorem to find where $f$ attains its maximum or minimum.

7.3 - Mean Value Theorem

The MVT generalises Rolle's theorem: if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then $\exists c$ with $f'(c) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$. Geometrically, some tangent line is parallel to the secant through $(a,f(a))$ and $(b,f(b))$.

7.4 - Applications

We apply the MVT to prove inequalities, derive criteria for monotonicity, and state Taylor's theorem with Lagrange remainder - the ultimate generalisation of the linear approximation from Section 6.2.
