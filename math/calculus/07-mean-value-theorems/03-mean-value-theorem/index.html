7.3 Mean Value Theorem

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT), also known as Lagrange's theorem, is one of the central results of differential calculus. It generalises Rolle's theorem by dropping the requirement that the endpoints share the same function value, replacing it with the weaker conclusion that the derivative equals the average rate of change somewhere in the interval.

Theorem 1 — Mean Value Theorem (Lagrange)

Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfy:

  1. $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$,
  2. $f$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$.

Then there exists $c\in(a,b)$ such that $$f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}.$$

Proof

Define the auxiliary function $$h(x)=f(x)-f(a)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a).$$ This is the difference between $f$ and the secant line through $(a,f(a))$ and $(b,f(b))$. Observe:

  • $h(a)=f(a)-f(a)-0=0$,
  • $h(b)=f(b)-f(a)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(b-a)=f(b)-f(a)-(f(b)-f(a))=0$.

So $h(a)=h(b)=0$. Since $h$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ (as a difference of continuous functions) and differentiable on $(a,b)$, Rolle's theorem applies: there exists $c\in(a,b)$ with $h'(c)=0$.

Differentiating: $h'(x)=f'(x)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$. Setting $h'(c)=0$ gives $f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.

$\square$

Geometric Interpretation

Remark — Tangent Parallel to Secant

The quantity $\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$ is the slope of the secant line joining $(a,f(a))$ to $(b,f(b))$. The MVT guarantees that somewhere on the curve there is a point $c$ where the tangent line has exactly this slope — that is, the tangent at $c$ is parallel to (or coincides with) the secant. No matter how the curve winds between the two endpoints, it must at some moment be "moving at the average rate."

Important Corollaries

Corollary 1 — Zero Derivative Implies Constant

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $f'(x)=0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, then $f$ is constant on $[a,b]$.

Proof

Pick any $x\in(a,b]$. Apply the MVT on $[a,x]$: there exists $c\in(a,x)$ with $$f(x)-f(a)=f'(c)(x-a)=0\cdot(x-a)=0.$$ So $f(x)=f(a)$ for every $x\in[a,b]$.

$\square$

Corollary 2 — Monotonicity from the Derivative

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$.

  • If $f'(x)>0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, then $f$ is strictly increasing on $[a,b]$.
  • If $f'(x)<0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, then $f$ is strictly decreasing on $[a,b]$.
  • If $f'(x)\ge0$ for all $x\in(a,b)$, then $f$ is non-decreasing on $[a,b]$.

Example — Applying the MVT

Show that $|\sin x - \sin y| \le |x-y|$ for all $x,y\in\mathbb{R}$.

Let $f(x)=\sin x$. Then $f'(x)=\cos x$ and $|\cos x|\le1$ for all $x$. By Corollary 3 with $M=1$, $$|\sin x-\sin y|\le 1\cdot|x-y|=|x-y|.$$

This holds on any interval, hence on all of $\mathbb{R}$.
