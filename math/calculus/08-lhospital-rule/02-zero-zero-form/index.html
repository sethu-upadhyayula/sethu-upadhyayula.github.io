8.2 L'Hôpital's Rule: 0/0 Form

When both the numerator and denominator of a limit tend to zero, we have the indeterminate form $0/0$. L'Hôpital's rule resolves this by replacing the ratio with the ratio of the derivatives, which may be easier to evaluate.

Theorem 1 - L'Hôpital's Rule (0/0 Form)

Suppose $f$ and $g$ are differentiable on $(a-\delta,a+\delta)\setminus\{a\}$ for some $\delta>0$, with $g'(x)\ne0$ near $a$ (except possibly at $a$). Assume:

  1. $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=0$ and $\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=0$ (the $0/0$ form),
  2. $\lim_{x\to a}\dfrac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}=L$ (where $L\in\mathbb{R}$ or $\pm\infty$).

Then $\lim_{x\to a}\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}=L$.

Proof (one-sided, $x\to a^+$)

Extend $f$ and $g$ by setting $f(a)=g(a)=0$ (so both are continuous at $a$ from the right). For $x>a$ near $a$, apply the Cauchy MVT on $[a,x]$: there exists $c\in(a,x)$ with $$\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{g(x)-g(a)}=\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}.$$ Since $f(a)=g(a)=0$, this gives $\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$. $\square$

Examples

Example 1 - $\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$

Both $\sin 0=0$ and $0=0$, giving the $0/0$ form. Apply L'Hôpital: $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x}=\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos x}{1}=\cos 0=1.$$

This confirms the fundamental trigonometric limit rigorously.

Example 2 - $\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{e^x-1}{x}$

Both numerator and denominator vanish at $x=0$. Apply L'Hôpital: $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{e^x-1}{x}=\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{e^x}{1}=e^0=1.$$

This is a foundational limit used to establish $\frac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$ from first principles.

Example 3 - $\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$ (Apply Twice)

At $x=0$: numerator $1-\cos0=0$, denominator $0$. First application: $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{2x}.$$ Again $0/0$. Second application: $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{2x}=\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\cos x}{2}=\frac{1}{2}.$$

So $\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}=\frac{1}{2}$. Note: each application requires re-checking the $0/0$ form.

Example 4 - $\lim_{x\to 1}\dfrac{x^3-1}{x^2-1}$

Both vanish at $x=1$. Apply L'Hôpital: $$\lim_{x\to1}\frac{x^3-1}{x^2-1}=\lim_{x\to1}\frac{3x^2}{2x}=\frac{3}{2}.$$

(Alternatively, factor: $\frac{(x-1)(x^2+x+1)}{(x-1)(x+1)}=\frac{x^2+x+1}{x+1}\to\frac{3}{2}$. Both methods agree.)

Warning - Verify the Form First

L'Hôpital's rule applies only when the limit is a genuine $0/0$ (or $\infty/\infty$) indeterminate form. Before differentiating, always verify that both $f(x)\to0$ and $g(x)\to0$. Applying the rule to a non-indeterminate limit gives wrong answers.
