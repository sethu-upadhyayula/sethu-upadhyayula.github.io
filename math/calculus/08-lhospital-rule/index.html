Chapter 8: Cauchy MVT and L'Hôpital's Rule

The Cauchy Mean Value Theorem is a natural two-function generalisation of the Mean Value Theorem. Its most celebrated application is L'Hôpital's rule, which provides a systematic method for evaluating limits of indeterminate forms such as $0/0$ and $\infty/\infty$ by differentiating numerator and denominator separately.

Chapter Overview

8.1 - Cauchy Mean Value Theorem

For two functions $f$ and $g$ continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, there exists $c\in(a,b)$ with $\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}$. This reduces to the ordinary MVT when $g(x)=x$ and is the key tool for proving L'Hôpital's rule rigorously.

8.2 - L'Hôpital's Rule: 0/0 Form

If $f(c)=g(c)=0$ and $\lim_{x\to c}f'(x)/g'(x)=L$, then $\lim_{x\to c}f(x)/g(x)=L$. The proof applies the Cauchy MVT on small intervals near $c$. Classic examples: $\lim\sin x/x=1$ and $\lim(e^x-1)/x=1$.

8.3 - L'Hôpital's Rule: ∞/∞ Form

When both numerator and denominator diverge to $\pm\infty$, the same conclusion holds under the same hypothesis on the ratio of derivatives. This version handles limits like $\lim x/e^x=0$ and $\lim\ln x/x=0$ as $x\to\infty$.

8.4 - Other Indeterminate Forms

Forms $0\cdot\infty$, $\infty-\infty$, $1^\infty$, $0^0$, and $\infty^0$ are reduced to $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$ by algebraic manipulation or by writing $f^g=e^{g\ln f}$ and evaluating the exponent.

8.5 - Examples and Warnings

L'Hôpital's rule has important limitations: it only applies when an indeterminate form is present, it is inconclusive when $f'/g'$ has no limit, and applying it incorrectly can lead to nonsensical answers. Several worked examples illustrate both correct use and common mistakes.
