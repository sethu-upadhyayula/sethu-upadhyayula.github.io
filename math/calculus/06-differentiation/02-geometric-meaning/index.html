6.2 Geometric Meaning

The derivative has a compelling geometric interpretation: it is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(c, f(c))$. This interpretation motivates both the definition and the usefulness of the derivative in approximation theory.

Tangent and Normal Lines

Definition 1 — Tangent Line

If $f$ is differentiable at $c$, the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at $(c, f(c))$ is $$y - f(c) = f'(c)(x - c),$$ i.e., the unique line through $(c, f(c))$ with slope $f'(c)$.

The tangent line is the limit of secant lines: as the second point $(c+h, f(c+h))$ approaches $(c, f(c))$, the secant slope $\frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h}$ converges to $f'(c)$.

Definition 2 — Normal Line

The normal line at $(c, f(c))$ is perpendicular to the tangent line. If $f'(c) \neq 0$, its equation is $$y - f(c) = -\frac{1}{f'(c)}(x - c).$$

Example

For $f(x) = x^2$ at $c = 3$: $f'(x) = 2x$, so $f'(3) = 6$.

  • Tangent line: $y - 9 = 6(x-3)$, i.e., $y = 6x - 9$.
  • Normal line: $y - 9 = -\frac{1}{6}(x-3)$.

For $f(x) = \sin x$ at $c = 0$: $f'(0) = \cos 0 = 1$. Tangent line: $y = x$.

Monotonicity and the Sign of $f'$

Theorem 1 — Sign of Derivative and Monotonicity

Let $f$ be differentiable on an open interval $I$.

  • If $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in I$, then $f$ is strictly increasing on $I$.
  • If $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x \in I$, then $f$ is strictly decreasing on $I$.
  • If $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in I$, then $f$ is constant on $I$.
Remark

The converse of the first statement is false in general: $f(x) = x^3$ is strictly increasing on $\mathbb{R}$ but $f'(0) = 0$. However, if $f$ is increasing and differentiable, then $f'(x) \geq 0$ everywhere.

Linear Approximation

Definition 3 — Linear Approximation

The linear approximation (or linearisation) of $f$ at $c$ is $$L(x) = f(c) + f'(c)(x-c).$$ This is simply the tangent line, viewed as a function. The approximation $f(x) \approx L(x)$ is accurate for $x$ near $c$ in the sense that $$\lim_{x \to c} \frac{f(x) - L(x)}{x - c} = 0.$$

In other words, the error $f(x) - L(x)$ is $o(x-c)$ as $x \to c$ — it goes to zero faster than $x - c$ itself.

Example — Approximating $\sqrt{1+x}$

Let $f(x) = \sqrt{1+x}$, $c = 0$. Then $f(0) = 1$ and $f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1+x}}$, so $f'(0) = \frac{1}{2}$.

Linear approximation: $\sqrt{1+x} \approx 1 + \frac{x}{2}$ for $x \approx 0$. For instance, $\sqrt{1.04} \approx 1.02$ (exact: $1.0198\ldots$).
