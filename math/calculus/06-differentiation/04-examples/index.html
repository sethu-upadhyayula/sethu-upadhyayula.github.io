6.4 Examples

We compile the derivatives of the elementary functions and work through illustrative examples using the differentiation rules. We also encounter two important pathological examples that reveal the subtlety of differentiability.

Standard Derivatives

Theorem 1 — Derivatives of Elementary Functions

The following hold for all $x$ in the natural domain:

  • $(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}$ for $n \in \mathbb{R}$ (power rule)
  • $(\sin x)' = \cos x$
  • $(\cos x)' = -\sin x$
  • $(e^x)' = e^x$
  • $(\ln x)' = \dfrac{1}{x}$ for $x > 0$
  • $(\tan x)' = \sec^2 x$
Proof — $(\sin x)' = \cos x$

Using the addition formula $\sin(x+h) = \sin x \cos h + \cos x \sin h$: $$\frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin x}{h} = \sin x\cdot\frac{\cos h - 1}{h} + \cos x \cdot \frac{\sin h}{h}.$$ As $h \to 0$: $\frac{\cos h - 1}{h} \to 0$ and $\frac{\sin h}{h} \to 1$ (standard limits).

Therefore $(\sin x)' = \sin x \cdot 0 + \cos x \cdot 1 = \cos x$. $\square$

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Product Rule

Differentiate $f(x) = x^3 \sin x$. By the product rule: $$f'(x) = 3x^2 \sin x + x^3 \cos x = x^2(3\sin x + x\cos x).$$

Differentiate $g(x) = e^x \ln x$ for $x > 0$: $$g'(x) = e^x \ln x + e^x \cdot \frac{1}{x} = e^x\!\left(\ln x + \frac{1}{x}\right).$$

Example 2 — Chain Rule

Differentiate $h(x) = \ln(\sin x)$ for $x \in (0,\pi)$. With $f(u) = \ln u$ and $g(x) = \sin x$: $$h'(x) = \frac{1}{\sin x} \cdot \cos x = \cot x.$$

Differentiate $k(x) = e^{x^2}$: $$k'(x) = e^{x^2} \cdot 2x = 2xe^{x^2}.$$

Pathological Examples

Theorem 2 — Weierstrass Function (statement only)

The function $W(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)$, where $0 < a < 1$, $b$ is a positive odd integer, and $ab > 1 + \frac{3\pi}{2}$, is continuous everywhere on $\mathbb{R}$ but differentiable nowhere. This example, due to Weierstrass (1872), demolished the intuition that continuity should imply differentiability "almost everywhere."

Example 3 — Differentiable but $f'$ Discontinuous

Define $$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 \sin(1/x) & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0. \end{cases}$$ At $x = 0$: $$f'(0) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{h^2\sin(1/h)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} h\sin(1/h) = 0$$ since $|h\sin(1/h)| \leq |h| \to 0$. So $f'(0) = 0$.

For $x \neq 0$, by the product and chain rules: $$f'(x) = 2x\sin(1/x) - \cos(1/x).$$ As $x \to 0$, the term $\cos(1/x)$ oscillates between $-1$ and $1$, so $\lim_{x\to 0} f'(x)$ does not exist. Thus $f$ is differentiable everywhere, but $f'$ is not continuous at $0$.

This shows that derivatives need not be continuous — they cannot have jump discontinuities (by Darboux's theorem), but they can have essential discontinuities.
