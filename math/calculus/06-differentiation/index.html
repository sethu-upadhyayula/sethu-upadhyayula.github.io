Chapter 6: Differentiation

Differentiation is the process of finding the instantaneous rate of change of a function. Built on the limit machinery developed in previous chapters, the derivative captures how a function behaves locally — its slope, its linear approximation, and its tendency to increase or decrease. This chapter develops the theory carefully, starting from the epsilon-delta definition and building up to the standard differentiation rules.

The key insight is that differentiability is a stronger condition than continuity: a differentiable function must be continuous, but a continuous function need not be differentiable. Understanding this distinction, and the wealth of examples that illuminate it, is a central goal of the chapter.

Chapter Overview

6.1 — Definition

The derivative $f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$ is defined as a limit of difference quotients. We develop left and right derivatives, establish that differentiability implies continuity, and derive the linearity, product, chain, and quotient rules.

6.2 — Geometric Meaning

The derivative is the slope of the tangent line to the graph at a point. The tangent line equation $y - f(c) = f'(c)(x-c)$ gives the best linear approximation to $f$ near $c$. We also study the relationship between the sign of $f'$ and the monotonicity of $f$.

6.3 — Differentiability and Continuity

We prove rigorously that differentiability implies continuity, and exhibit counterexamples (e.g., $|x|$ at the origin) showing the converse fails. We introduce higher-order derivatives $f^{(n)}$ and the class $C^\infty$ of infinitely differentiable functions.

6.4 — Examples

We compute derivatives of elementary functions — $x^n$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$, $e^x$, $\ln x$ — and work through examples using the product and chain rules. We also encounter pathological examples such as the Weierstrass nowhere-differentiable function and $x^2 \sin(1/x)$.
