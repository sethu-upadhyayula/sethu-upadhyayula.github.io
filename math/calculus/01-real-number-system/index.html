Chapter 1: The Real Number System

The real number system $\mathbb{R}$ is the foundation upon which all of calculus is built. While we use real numbers intuitively every day, the rigorous development of their properties is essential for understanding why calculus works the way it does. This chapter carefully constructs the key properties of $\mathbb{R}$ that distinguish it from the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ and make it the correct setting for analysis.

The central theme is completeness: the real line has no gaps. Every bounded set has a least upper bound. Every Cauchy sequence converges. These are not obvious facts - they must be established through careful axioms and theorems.

Chapter Overview

1.1 - Rationals and Irrationals

We define rational numbers as quotients $p/q$ of integers and irrational numbers as reals that cannot be so expressed. We prove that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational and discuss the countability of $\mathbb{Q}$ versus the uncountability of $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$.

1.2 - Order and Bounds

We introduce the ordered field axioms, the concepts of upper and lower bounds, and the absolute value $|x|$. We prove the triangle inequality $|x + y| \leq |x| + |y|$, which is indispensable throughout analysis.

1.3 - Supremum and Infimum

The supremum (least upper bound) and infimum (greatest lower bound) of a set are introduced. We show that these need not exist in $\mathbb{Q}$ and establish the useful $\varepsilon$-characterisation of the supremum.

1.4 - Completeness

The Completeness Axiom states that every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ bounded above has a supremum in $\mathbb{R}$. This single axiom distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from all other ordered fields and is the engine behind the major theorems of calculus.

1.5 - Archimedean Property

A consequence of completeness: for any real number $x$, there is a natural number larger than $x$. Equivalently, for any $\varepsilon > 0$, we can find $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $1/n < \varepsilon$. This is the backbone of limit arguments.

1.6 - Density

Between any two distinct real numbers there is both a rational and an irrational. Thus $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ are both dense in $\mathbb{R}$, meaning every open interval contains infinitely many of each type.
