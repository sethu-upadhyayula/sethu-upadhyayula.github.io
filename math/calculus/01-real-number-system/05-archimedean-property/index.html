1.5 Archimedean Property

The Archimedean property captures the intuition that there are no "infinitely large" or "infinitely small" positive real numbers. Given any positive real, we can always find a natural number larger than it, and given any positive $\varepsilon$ however small, we can find $1/n$ smaller than it.

Theorem 1.5.1 - Archimedean Property

For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n > x$. Equivalently, $\mathbb{N}$ is not bounded above in $\mathbb{R}$.

Proof

Suppose for contradiction that $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above. Then by the Completeness Axiom, $s = \sup \mathbb{N}$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$.

Since $s$ is the supremum, $s - 1$ is not an upper bound for $\mathbb{N}$, so there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > s - 1$. Then $n + 1 > s$, but $n + 1 \in \mathbb{N}$ (since $\mathbb{N}$ is closed under the successor operation). This contradicts $s$ being an upper bound for $\mathbb{N}$.

Therefore $\mathbb{N}$ is not bounded above, and for any $x \in \mathbb{R}$ there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > x$. $\square$

Corollaries

Corollary 1.5.2

For every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $\dfrac{1}{n} < \varepsilon$.

Proof

Apply Theorem 1.5.1 to $x = 1/\varepsilon$: there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > 1/\varepsilon$, so $1/n < \varepsilon$. $\square$

Corollary 1.5.3

For every $x > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $nx > 1$ (i.e., $n > 1/x$). More generally, for any $x, y > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $nx > y$.

Remark

The Archimedean property is used constantly in analysis. Whenever we need to find $N$ such that $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ for all $n \geq N$, we typically express $N$ in terms of $\varepsilon$ using the Archimedean property. For instance, to show $1/n \to 0$: given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N > 1/\varepsilon$ (Archimedean); then for $n \geq N$, $|1/n - 0| = 1/n \leq 1/N < \varepsilon$.

Non-Archimedean ordered fields do exist (e.g., the field of formal Laurent series), but they do not satisfy the Completeness Axiom. Thus the Archimedean property is a theorem in $\mathbb{R}$, not an additional axiom.

Example - Floor Function

For any $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there is a unique integer $\lfloor x \rfloor$ (the floor of $x$) satisfying $\lfloor x \rfloor \leq x < \lfloor x \rfloor + 1$.

Existence: The set $\{n \in \mathbb{Z} : n \leq x\}$ is nonempty (by Archimedean property applied to $-x$) and bounded above by $x$; it has a maximum, which is $\lfloor x \rfloor$. Uniqueness: if $m, m'$ both satisfy the condition, then $|m - m'| < 1$ with $m - m' \in \mathbb{Z}$, so $m = m'$.
