6.2 Primal–Dual Interior Point Methods

Primal–dual interior point methods simultaneously update primal and dual variables by applying Newton's method to the perturbed KKT conditions. They are the dominant approach for large-scale LP and SDP, achieving $O(\sqrt{n})$ iterations with polynomial complexity.

KKT System for LP

Definition 6.2.1 — LP KKT Conditions

For the LP $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax = b$, $x \ge 0$ with dual $\max b^\top y$ s.t. $A^\top y + s = c$, $s \ge 0$, the KKT conditions at optimality $(x^*, y^*, s^*)$ are: $$Ax^* = b \quad\text{(primal feasibility)},$$ $$A^\top y^* + s^* = c \quad\text{(dual feasibility)},$$ $$x^*_j s^*_j = 0,\; x^*, s^* \ge 0 \quad\text{(complementary slackness)}.$$

In matrix form, writing $X = \mathrm{diag}(x)$ and $S = \mathrm{diag}(s)$: $XSe = 0$ where $e = (1,\ldots,1)^\top$. The primal–dual method replaces $XSe = 0$ with the perturbed condition $XSe = \mu e$ for $\mu > 0$ (the complementarity parameter).

Newton Step on Perturbed KKT

Definition 6.2.2 — Newton Step for Perturbed KKT

Linearise the system $F(x, y, s) = 0$ where $$F = \begin{pmatrix} Ax - b \\ A^\top y + s - c \\ XSe - \mu e \end{pmatrix}.$$ The Newton step $(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta s)$ satisfies: $$\begin{pmatrix} A & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & A^\top & I \\ S & 0 & X \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \Delta x \\ \Delta y \\ \Delta s \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} b - Ax \\ c - A^\top y - s \\ \mu e - XSe \end{pmatrix}.$$

The third block row gives $S\Delta x + X\Delta s = \mu e - XSe$. Eliminating $\Delta s = S^{-1}(\mu e - XSe - S\Delta x) \cdot X^{-1}$ reduces the system to the augmented system in $(\Delta x, \Delta y)$ involving the scaled matrix $\Theta = X S^{-1}$.

Path-Following and Predictor-Corrector

Definition 6.2.3 — Path-Following Method

A path-following IPM tracks the central path by decreasing $\mu$:

  1. Start with strictly primal–dual feasible $(x_0, y_0, s_0)$ with $x_0, s_0 > 0$ and complementarity gap $\mu_0 = x_0^\top s_0 / n$.
  2. Choose $\mu_{k+1} = (1 - \alpha/\sqrt{n})\mu_k$ (or a fixed fraction) and compute the Newton step.
  3. Step $(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta s)$ with step size $\theta$ chosen to maintain $x, s > 0$ (stay in interior).
  4. Stop when $\mu_k \le \varepsilon$.

The total number of iterations is $O(\sqrt{n}\log(1/\varepsilon))$, establishing LP as a polynomially solvable problem (Karmarkar 1984).

Definition 6.2.4 — Mehrotra Predictor-Corrector

The predictor-corrector method (Mehrotra 1992) performs two Newton steps per iteration:

  • Predictor (affine scaling): Newton step with $\mu = 0$ to estimate how far we can go.
  • Corrector: Adjust $\mu$ based on the predictor step length, then compute a corrected Newton step incorporating second-order terms.

Predictor-corrector requires roughly half the iterations of a pure path-following method in practice, typically $20$–$50$ iterations for large LPs regardless of problem size. It is the basis for virtually all production LP solvers.

Advantages Over Simplex

Remark — IPM vs Simplex

Interior point methods and the simplex method have complementary strengths:

  • Polynomial complexity: IPMs have provably polynomial iteration counts $O(\sqrt{n})$; simplex is exponential in the worst case.
  • Large-scale LPs: IPMs handle problems with millions of variables well, exploiting sparsity in $A$ through sparse Cholesky factorisation of the normal equations.
  • Warm starting: Simplex can exploit a good starting BFS (e.g., from a nearby problem). IPMs require an interior starting point and do not warm-start as naturally.
  • MIP: Simplex is preferred inside branch-and-bound because of better warm-starting. IPMs are used to obtain tight LP relaxation bounds at the root node.

Modern solvers (Gurobi, CPLEX, HiGHS) implement both methods and choose automatically based on problem structure.
