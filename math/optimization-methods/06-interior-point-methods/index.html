Chapter 6: Interior Point Methods

Interior point methods (IPMs) traverse the interior of the feasible region rather than its boundary, as simplex does. By incorporating a logarithmic barrier that penalises proximity to the constraint boundary, IPMs follow a smooth central path to the optimum. They achieve polynomial-time complexity - the first polynomial algorithm for LP - and are extremely competitive in practice for large-scale problems.

Chapter Overview

6.1 - Barrier Functions

The log-barrier approximation $B(x,t)=tf(x)-\sum_{i=1}^m\log(-g_i(x))$ has a unique minimiser $x^*(t)$ for each $t>0$, forming the central path. As $t\to\infty$, $x^*(t)\to x^*$, the true optimum. The duality gap along the central path is exactly $m/t$. The analytic centre of the feasible region corresponds to $t=0$ (equivalently, $f\equiv0$).

6.2 - Primal-Dual Methods

Rather than explicitly following the central path, primal-dual IPMs apply Newton's method to the perturbed KKT system where $\mu_ig_i=0$ is replaced by $\mu_ig_i=-\varepsilon$. Predictor-corrector strategies (Mehrotra) dramatically reduce iteration counts. The iteration complexity is $O(\sqrt{m}\log(1/\varepsilon))$, giving polynomial time. In practice, IPMs solve LP in 20-50 iterations independent of problem size.
