1.3 Separation Theorems

Separation theorems are the geometric engine of duality theory: they guarantee that disjoint convex sets can be "pulled apart" by a hyperplane. Farkas' lemma translates this geometric insight into an algebraic criterion for feasibility that directly yields LP duality.

Separating Hyperplane Theorem

Theorem 1.3.1 — Separating Hyperplane

Let $C,D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$ be disjoint convex sets. Then there exists a nonzero vector $a\in\mathbb{R}^n$ and scalar $b$ such that: $$a^Tx \le b \quad\forall x\in C \qquad\text{and}\qquad a^Tx \ge b \quad\forall x\in D.$$

The hyperplane $\{x:a^Tx=b\}$ separates $C$ and $D$. This follows from the projection theorem: the nearest point in a closed convex set to an external point exists and is unique.

Theorem 1.3.2 — Strict Separation

If $C$ is closed convex and $D$ is compact (closed and bounded) convex, and $C\cap D=\emptyset$, then there exists $a\ne0$ and $b$ such that $a^Tx < b$ for all $x\in C$ and $a^Tx > b$ for all $x\in D$ (strict inequalities). The compactness hypothesis on one set is essential.

Supporting Hyperplane

Theorem 1.3.3 — Supporting Hyperplane

Let $C$ be a convex set and $x_0\in\partial C$ (on the boundary). Then there exists $a\ne0$ such that $a^Tx\le a^Tx_0$ for all $x\in C$.

The hyperplane $\{x:a^Tx=a^Tx_0\}$ supports $C$ at $x_0$ — it touches $C$ at $x_0$ and $C$ lies entirely on one side. This generalises the tangent hyperplane of smooth sets to non-smooth convex sets.

Farkas' Lemma

Theorem 1.3.4 — Farkas' Lemma

Let $A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ and $b\in\mathbb{R}^m$. Exactly one of the following holds:

  1. There exists $x\ge0$ such that $Ax=b$.
  2. There exists $y\in\mathbb{R}^m$ such that $A^Ty\ge0$ and $b^Ty<0$.

Geometrically: $b$ is in the conic hull of the columns of $A$ (case 1), or there is a hyperplane through the origin separating $b$ from that conic hull (case 2). Farkas' lemma is the key step in proving LP strong duality.

Proof Sketch

Let $K=\{Ax:x\ge0\}$, the conic hull of columns of $A$. $K$ is a closed convex cone. If $b\notin K$, by the separating hyperplane theorem applied to $b$ and $K$, there exists $y$ with $y^T(Ax)\ge0$ for all $x\ge0$ (i.e., $A^Ty\ge0$) and $y^Tb<0$. If $b\in K$, case 1 holds and case 2 is impossible (substituting any solution of case 1 into case 2 gives $0=y^T(Ax)=y^Tb<0$, contradiction). $\square$

Remark — Applications to Duality

Farkas' lemma implies LP duality directly: applying it to the KKT optimality conditions shows that primal infeasibility of the optimality system is equivalent to dual feasibility, yielding $p^*=d^*$. It also underpins constraint qualification theory: LICQ and Slater's condition are essentially conditions ensuring the Farkas-type separation argument applies at the problem optimum.
