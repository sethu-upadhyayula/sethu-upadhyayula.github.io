Chapter 1: Convex Analysis

Convex analysis provides the geometric and algebraic foundations for all of modern optimization theory. The central objects — convex sets and convex functions — enjoy rich structural properties that make optimisation tractable: local minima are global, gradients give global under-estimates, and feasible sets are characterised by supporting hyperplanes.

This chapter develops these foundations systematically, culminating in the separation and Farkas lemma results that underpin duality theory.

Chapter Overview

1.1 — Convex Sets

A set $C\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$ is convex if for all $x,y\in C$ and $\lambda\in[0,1]$ the point $\lambda x+(1-\lambda)y\in C$. We study operations that preserve convexity — intersections, affine images, the Minkowski sum — and introduce the convex hull, extreme points, and polyhedra $\{x:Ax\le b\}$, plus the recession cone characterising unboundedness.

1.2 — Convex Functions

A function $f:C\to\mathbb{R}$ is convex if $f(\lambda x+(1-\lambda)y)\le\lambda f(x)+(1-\lambda)f(y)$. Equivalent characterisations via the epigraph, first-order inequality $f(y)\ge f(x)+\nabla f(x)^T(y-x)$, and positive semidefiniteness of $\nabla^2f$ are developed. We also cover strong convexity, Jensen's inequality, and the Fenchel conjugate $f^*(y)=\sup_x(y^Tx-f(x))$.

1.3 — Separation Theorems

Disjoint convex sets can be separated by a hyperplane; a convex set admits a supporting hyperplane at each boundary point; one compact set allows strict separation. Farkas' lemma gives an algebraic certificate for infeasibility: exactly one of $Ax=b,x\ge0$ and $A^Ty\ge0,b^Ty<0$ has a solution. These results are the geometric engines of LP duality.
