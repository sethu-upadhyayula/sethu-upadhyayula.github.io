5.2 Strong and Weak Duality

Weak duality is a universal lower-bound result requiring no special structure. Strong duality - the equality of primal and dual optima - holds under constraint qualifications and is the cornerstone of LP theory and convex optimisation.

Weak Duality

Theorem 5.2.1 - Weak Duality

Let $x$ be primal feasible and $y$ dual feasible for the LP pair (P) $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax \ge b$, $x \ge 0$ and (D) $\max b^\top y$ s.t. $A^\top y \le c$, $y \ge 0$. Then $$b^\top y \le c^\top x.$$

Proof. Since $y \ge 0$ and $Ax \ge b$: $y^\top b \le y^\top Ax$. Since $x \ge 0$ and $A^\top y \le c$: $y^\top A x \le c^\top x$. Thus $b^\top y \le c^\top x$. $\square$

Consequences: (i) Any dual feasible solution gives a lower bound on $p^*$. (ii) If $c^\top x = b^\top y$ for some feasible $x, y$, then both are optimal (certificate of optimality). (iii) If (P) is unbounded below ($p^* = -\infty$), then (D) is infeasible.

Definition 5.2.2 - Duality Gap

The duality gap at a pair $(x, y)$ of primal and dual feasible solutions is $c^\top x - b^\top y \ge 0$. The optimal duality gap is $p^* - d^* \ge 0$. Strong duality means the optimal duality gap is zero.

Strong Duality for LP

Theorem 5.2.3 - LP Strong Duality

If (P) has an optimal solution $x^*$, then (D) has an optimal solution $y^*$ and $c^\top x^* = b^\top y^*$. More precisely:

  • If (P) is feasible and bounded: both (P) and (D) are solvable and $p^* = d^*$.
  • If (P) is unbounded ($p^* = -\infty$): (D) is infeasible.
  • If (P) is infeasible: either (D) is unbounded ($d^* = +\infty$) or also infeasible.

Proof via simplex. At optimality, the simplex multipliers $y^* = A_B^{-\top} c_B$ satisfy $A^\top y^* \le c$ (reduced costs $\bar{c} \ge 0$) and $b^\top y^* = c_B^\top A_B^{-1} b = c^\top x^*$. So $y^*$ is dual feasible with $b^\top y^* = p^*$, giving $d^* \ge p^*$. By weak duality $d^* \le p^*$. Hence $d^* = p^*$. $\square$

This proof shows that the LP dual is not an abstract construction - the dual solution $y^*$ is directly computed by the simplex method as the multipliers at optimality.

Farkas' Lemma

Theorem 5.2.4 - Farkas' Lemma

For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$, exactly one of the following systems has a solution:

  • System I: $Ax = b$, $x \ge 0$.
  • System II: $A^\top y \ge 0$, $b^\top y < 0$ (equivalently $b^\top y \le -1$).

Proof. If System I has a solution $x \ge 0$, then for any $y$ with $A^\top y \ge 0$: $b^\top y = (Ax)^\top y = x^\top (A^\top y) \ge 0$, so System II fails. Conversely, if System I is infeasible, the LP $\min 0$ s.t. $Ax = b$, $x \ge 0$ is infeasible; by LP duality (or the supporting hyperplane theorem), a separating hyperplane $y$ exists with $b^\top y < 0$ and $A^\top y \ge 0$. $\square$

Farkas' lemma is the fundamental theorem of alternatives for linear systems, equivalent to LP strong duality and to the separating hyperplane theorem for polyhedral cones.

Geometric Interpretation

Remark - Separating Hyperplane Interpretation

Strong duality can be understood geometrically. Consider the set $\mathcal{G} = \{(g, t) : \exists x \ge 0,\; Ax \ge b + g,\; c^\top x \le t\} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m \times \mathbb{R}$. The optimal value $p^*$ is the infimum over $t$ such that $(0, t) \in \mathcal{G}$.

Strong duality says a supporting hyperplane to $\mathcal{G}$ at $(0, p^*)$ exists with a normal vector $(y^*, -1)$ where $y^* \ge 0$. This hyperplane gives $b^\top y^* = d^* = p^*$. The dual variable $y^*$ is literally the slope of the supporting hyperplane - it measures how much $p^*$ changes when the right-hand side $b$ is perturbed.
