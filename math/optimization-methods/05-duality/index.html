Chapter 5: Duality

Every optimisation problem has a dual — a related problem whose optimal value provides a lower bound on the primal. Under suitable conditions (Slater's condition for convex problems, always for LP), the duality gap is zero: primal and dual optimal values coincide. Duality theory unifies optimality conditions, sensitivity analysis, and the design of algorithms.

Chapter Overview

5.1 — Lagrangian Duality

The Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}(x,\mu,\lambda)=f(x)+\mu^Tg(x)+\lambda^Th(x)$ yields the dual function $g(\mu,\lambda)=\inf_x\mathcal{L}$, which is always concave regardless of problem convexity. The dual problem maximises $g$ over $\mu\ge0$. For LP $\min c^Tx$ s.t. $Ax=b$, $x\ge0$, the dual is $\max b^Ty$ s.t. $A^Ty\le c$.

5.2 — Strong and Weak Duality

Weak duality: the dual value always lower-bounds the primal ($g(\mu,\lambda)\le p^*$). Strong duality ($p^*=d^*$) holds for LP by the fundamental theorem, and for convex programs under Slater's condition. A zero duality gap is equivalent to the existence of KKT multipliers satisfying all KKT conditions.

5.3 — Complementary Slackness

At primal-dual optimality, $\mu_i^*g_i(x^*)=0$ for all $i$ — either the constraint is active or its multiplier is zero, but not both. For LP: $x_j(c_j-(A^Ty)_j)=0$ and $(b_i-(Ax)_i)y_i=0$. Primal-dual feasibility plus stationarity plus complementary slackness is necessary and sufficient for joint optimality under strong duality.
