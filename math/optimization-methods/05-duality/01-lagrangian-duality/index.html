5.1 Lagrangian Duality

Lagrangian duality associates to every constrained optimisation problem a dual problem that provides a lower bound on the primal optimal value. The duality gap measures the difference; under suitable constraint qualifications it closes to zero (strong duality). The LP dual, derived here, gives the simplex multipliers a precise economic interpretation.

LP Primal and Dual

Definition 5.1.1 — LP Primal and Dual

The primal LP in inequality form is $$\text{(P)}\quad p^* = \min_{x \ge 0}\; c^\top x \quad\text{s.t.}\quad Ax \ge b.$$ Multiplying the $i$-th constraint by $y_i \ge 0$ and summing gives $y^\top Ax \ge y^\top b$. For any feasible $x$, $c^\top x \ge y^\top A x \ge y^\top b$ whenever $A^\top y \le c$ and $y \ge 0$. This motivates the dual LP: $$\text{(D)}\quad d^* = \max_{y \ge 0}\; b^\top y \quad\text{s.t.}\quad A^\top y \le c.$$

The dual variables $y_i$ are the prices of the $i$-th constraint. They equal the Lagrange multipliers of the primal.

The Lagrangian and Dual Function

Definition 5.1.2 — Lagrangian and Dual Function

For a general problem $\min f(x)$ s.t. $g_i(x) \le 0$, $i=1,\ldots,m$, the Lagrangian is $$L(x, \lambda) = f(x) + \sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i g_i(x), \quad \lambda \ge 0.$$ The Lagrangian dual function is $$g(\lambda) = \inf_{x}\; L(x, \lambda).$$ For each $\lambda \ge 0$, $g(\lambda) \le f(x^*)$ for any feasible $x^*$ (since $\lambda_i g_i(x^*) \le 0$). Thus $g$ provides a lower bound on $p^*$.

The dual function $g$ is always concave (as an infimum of affine functions of $\lambda$), regardless of convexity of the primal. The dual problem is $d^* = \max_{\lambda \ge 0} g(\lambda)$.

Example — LP Dual Function

For the LP $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax \ge b$, $x \ge 0$, the Lagrangian is $L(x, y) = c^\top x - y^\top(Ax - b) = (c - A^\top y)^\top x + b^\top y$ with $y \ge 0$. The infimum over $x \ge 0$ is $$g(y) = \inf_{x \ge 0} L(x, y) = \begin{cases} b^\top y & \text{if } c - A^\top y \ge 0, \\ -\infty & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

So the dual problem $\max_{y \ge 0} g(y)$ is $\max b^\top y$ s.t. $A^\top y \le c$, $y \ge 0$ — exactly the LP dual of Definition 5.1.1.

Slater's Condition and Strong Duality

Theorem 5.1.3 — Slater's Condition (Strong Duality for Convex Problems)

Consider a convex problem: $f$ convex, $g_i$ convex, $i = 1,\ldots,m$. Suppose Slater's condition holds: there exists a strictly feasible point $\tilde{x}$ with $g_i(\tilde{x}) < 0$ for all $i$ (strict interior). Then: $$d^* = p^*,$$ i.e., strong duality holds and the dual optimum is attained.

Slater's condition is the canonical constraint qualification for convex optimisation. For LP (linear constraints), Slater's condition reduces to primal feasibility, and strong duality holds whenever both primal and dual are feasible.

Remark — Duality Gap and Non-Convex Problems

For non-convex problems, the duality gap $p^* - d^* \ge 0$ can be strictly positive. The Lagrangian relaxation still provides a lower bound and is widely used in branch-and-bound algorithms for integer programming (Section 7.1): the dual function $g(\lambda)$ gives a lower bound on the IP optimum, and the best lower bound $d^*$ is obtained by maximising $g$ (the Lagrangian dual).

For quadratic programs with equality constraints (QPs), strong duality also holds under rank conditions on the constraint matrix. For semidefinite programmes (SDPs), Slater's condition is again the standard qualification.
