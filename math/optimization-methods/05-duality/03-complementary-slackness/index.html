5.3 Complementary Slackness

Complementary slackness (CS) conditions are necessary and sufficient optimality conditions expressed in terms of the primal-dual pair. They state that at optimality, a constraint's slack and its dual variable cannot both be positive simultaneously - one must be zero.

CS Conditions

Theorem 5.3.1 - Complementary Slackness

Let $x^*$ be primal feasible and $y^*$ dual feasible for the LP pair (P) $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax \ge b$, $x \ge 0$ and (D) $\max b^\top y$ s.t. $A^\top y \le c$, $y \ge 0$. Then $x^*$ and $y^*$ are both optimal if and only if: $$y_i^*(A_i x^* - b_i) = 0 \quad \forall i, \qquad x_j^*(c_j - (A^\top y^*)_j) = 0 \quad \forall j.$$

Proof. By weak duality, $c^\top x^* \ge b^\top y^*$. Expanding the gap: $$c^\top x^* - b^\top y^* = (c - A^\top y^*)^\top x^* + y^{*\top}(Ax^* - b).$$ Both terms are non-negative (dual feasibility and primal feasibility with $x^*, y^* \ge 0$). The gap equals zero (strong duality) if and only if each term is zero, which gives the CS conditions. $\square$

The CS conditions are the LP specialisation of the KKT conditions from Section 3.2.

Interpretation: Shadow Prices

Definition 5.3.2 - Shadow Prices

The dual variable $y_i^*$ is the shadow price (or marginal value) of the $i$-th constraint: it equals the rate of change of the optimal primal objective with respect to the right-hand side $b_i$: $$y_i^* = \frac{\partial p^*(b)}{\partial b_i}\bigg|_{b = b^*},$$ provided the optimal basis does not change (i.e., we are within the range of the parametric analysis).

By CS: $y_i^* > 0$ implies $A_i x^* = b_i$ (the constraint is active/binding). Conversely, if a constraint is slack ($A_i x^* > b_i$), its shadow price is zero - relaxing it further has no effect on the optimal value.

LP Complementary Slackness in Standard Form

Proposition 5.3.3 - CS for Standard Form LP

For the standard form LP $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax = b$, $x \ge 0$ with dual $\max b^\top y$ (unconstrained $y$) s.t. $A^\top y \le c$, define reduced costs $\bar{c} = c - A^\top y$. The CS conditions reduce to: $$x_j^* \bar{c}_j^* = 0 \quad \forall j.$$

This means: a non-basic variable ($x_j = 0$) can have any $\bar{c}_j \ge 0$, and a basic variable ($x_j > 0$) must have $\bar{c}_j = 0$. At a BFS, the basic variables automatically satisfy $\bar{c}_j = 0$ (by definition of reduced costs), so CS reduces exactly to the simplex optimality condition $\bar{c}_j \ge 0$ for non-basic $j$.

Sensitivity Analysis

Remark - Sensitivity Analysis via Shadow Prices

Sensitivity analysis uses the shadow prices and CS conditions to answer: "How does the optimal value change if we perturb the problem data?" For the LP $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax \ge b$, $x \ge 0$:

  • RHS ranging: If $b_i$ increases by $\delta$, then $p^* \approx p^* - y_i^* \delta$ (since $y_i^*$ measures decrease per unit of $b_i$; a larger $b_i$ tightens the constraint, so $p^*$ increases for minimisation).
  • Cost ranging: The optimal basis remains unchanged as long as all reduced costs remain non-negative. The range of $c_j$ for which the current basis is optimal can be computed from the simplex tableau.

These analyses are performed without re-solving the LP - they use the basis inverse $A_B^{-1}$ already computed at optimality.

Example - Verifying Optimality via CS

Consider $\min\; x_1 + 2x_2$ s.t. $x_1 + x_2 \ge 3$, $x_2 \ge 1$, $x_1, x_2 \ge 0$. Dual: $\max\; 3y_1 + y_2$ s.t. $y_1 \le 1$, $y_1 + y_2 \le 2$, $y_1, y_2 \ge 0$.

Claim: $x^* = (2, 1)$ is primal optimal with $p^* = 4$. Dual solution: $y_1^* = 1$, $y_2^* = 1$, $d^* = 4$. Check CS: $$y_1^*(x_1^* + x_2^* - 3) = 1 \cdot 0 = 0\;\checkmark,\quad y_2^*(x_2^* - 1) = 1 \cdot 0 = 0\;\checkmark.$$ $$x_1^*(1 - y_1^*) = 2 \cdot 0 = 0\;\checkmark,\quad x_2^*(2 - y_1^* - y_2^*) = 1 \cdot 0 = 0\;\checkmark.$$

All CS conditions hold and $p^* = d^* = 4$, confirming optimality.
