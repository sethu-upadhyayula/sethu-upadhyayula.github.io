3.1 Equality Constraints and Lagrange Multipliers

The method of Lagrange multipliers transforms an equality-constrained optimisation problem into an unconstrained system by appending the constraints to the objective with multipliers. The key geometric insight is that at a constrained minimum, the gradient of the objective must be a linear combination of the constraint gradients.

Problem Setup

Definition 3.1.1 — Equality-Constrained Problem

The standard equality-constrained problem is: $$\min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n} f(x) \quad\text{subject to}\quad h(x)=0,$$ where $h:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ with $m\le n$. The feasible set is the manifold $\mathcal{M}=\{x:h(x)=0\}$.

The Lagrangian is $\mathcal{L}(x,\lambda) = f(x) + \lambda^T h(x)$, where $\lambda\in\mathbb{R}^m$ are the Lagrange multipliers.

First-Order Conditions

Theorem 3.1.2 — Lagrange Multiplier Theorem (First-Order)

Suppose $x^*$ is a local minimum of $f$ on $\mathcal{M}=\{x:h(x)=0\}$, and the LICQ holds at $x^*$ (the Jacobian $\nabla h(x^*)$ has full row rank). Then there exists a unique $\lambda^*\in\mathbb{R}^m$ such that: $$\nabla_x\mathcal{L}(x^*,\lambda^*) = \nabla f(x^*) + \nabla h(x^*)^T\lambda^* = 0.$$

Geometrically: $\nabla f(x^*)$ lies in the row space of $\nabla h(x^*)$ — the gradient of $f$ has no component in the tangent space of $\mathcal{M}$ at $x^*$. Any tangential perturbation cannot improve $f$.

Example — Minimise $f(x,y)=x^2+y^2$ subject to $x+y=1$

Lagrangian: $\mathcal{L}=x^2+y^2+\lambda(x+y-1)$. Stationarity: $2x+\lambda=0$, $2y+\lambda=0$, so $x=y=-\lambda/2$. From the constraint: $x+y=1$ gives $-\lambda=1$, so $\lambda=-1$, $x=y=1/2$. Minimum value: $f(1/2,1/2)=1/2$.

The multiplier $\lambda^*$ equals minus the rate of change of the optimal value with respect to the right-hand side: $\partial f^*/\partial b = -\lambda^*$ (sensitivity interpretation).

Second-Order Conditions

Theorem 3.1.3 — Second-Order Conditions

At a feasible stationary point $(x^*,\lambda^*)$ of the Lagrangian, let $T=\ker(\nabla h(x^*))$ be the tangent space of $\mathcal{M}$.

  • Second-order necessary: $d^T\nabla^2_{xx}\mathcal{L}(x^*,\lambda^*)d\ge0$ for all $d\in T$.
  • Second-order sufficient: if $d^T\nabla^2_{xx}\mathcal{L}(x^*,\lambda^*)d>0$ for all $d\in T\setminus\{0\}$, then $x^*$ is a strict local minimum.

The projected Hessian $\nabla^2_{xx}\mathcal{L}|_T$ replaces the full Hessian from the unconstrained theory. Only directions tangent to the constraint manifold matter.
